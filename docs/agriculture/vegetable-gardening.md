A survival garden isn't about growing pretty tomatoes. It's about producing the most calories and nutrition per square foot with the least effort and risk. That means prioritizing different crops than a hobby garden.

## Highest-Calorie Crops

If you're growing food to survive, these earn their space:

| Crop | Calories per 100 sq ft | Days to Harvest | Difficulty |
|------|----------------------|----------------|-----------|
| Potatoes | 15,000–20,000 | 70–120 | Easy |
| Sweet potatoes | 12,000–15,000 | 90–120 | Easy (warm climates) |
| Dry beans | 8,000–12,000 | 80–100 | Easy |
| Corn | 8,000–10,000 | 60–100 | Moderate |
| Winter squash | 4,000–6,000 | 80–110 | Easy |
| Carrots | 3,000–4,000 | 60–80 | Easy |
| Cabbage | 2,000–3,000 | 70–100 | Moderate |
| Tomatoes | 1,500–2,000 | 60–85 | Moderate |

Potatoes are king. Nothing else comes close in calories per square foot for the effort involved. A 200 square foot potato patch can produce 300+ pounds of food.

Lettuce and herbs are nice but produce almost zero calories. Grow them only after your calorie crops are established.

## The "Three Sisters" — Best Survival Planting

Indigenous peoples across North America figured this out centuries ago. Corn, beans, and squash planted together:

- **Corn** provides a structure for beans to climb.
- **Beans** fix nitrogen in the soil, feeding the corn and squash.
- **Squash** spreads along the ground, shading out weeds and holding moisture.

Together they form a complete protein (corn + beans) and a long-storing staple (winter squash).

### How to Plant Three Sisters

1. Build mounds of soil about 4 feet apart, each mound roughly 12 inches across and 6 inches high.
2. Plant 4–6 corn seeds in the center of each mound. Wait until soil is 60°F+ (16°C).
3. When corn is 6 inches tall (about 2–3 weeks), plant 4 bean seeds around the base of each corn stalk, about 6 inches out.
4. At the same time, plant 2 squash seeds on the side of each mound.
5. As beans grow, guide them toward the corn stalks if needed. They'll usually find it themselves.

## Planting Calendar Basics

Every region has different frost dates. You need to know two numbers:

- **Last spring frost date:** When it's safe to plant warm-season crops outdoors.
- **First fall frost date:** When cold will kill tender crops. Your growing season is the number of days between these two dates.

Without internet to look this up, ask local farmers or observe: when the last hard frost happens in spring, note the date. Count backward from when things die in fall.

### Cool-Season Crops (Plant 2–4 Weeks Before Last Frost)

These handle light frosts and actually grow better in cool weather. Direct sow unless noted:

- Peas: 1 inch deep, 2 inches apart, needs something to climb
- Lettuce: surface sow, thin to 6 inches apart
- Spinach: 1/2 inch deep, 4 inches apart
- Radishes: 1/2 inch deep, 1 inch apart (ready in 25–30 days — fastest food you can grow)
- Carrots: 1/4 inch deep, 2 inches apart, keep surface moist until germination (can take 2–3 weeks)
- Onion sets: push into soil 1 inch deep, 4 inches apart
- Cabbage, broccoli, kale: start indoors 6 weeks early, transplant out 2 weeks before last frost
- Potatoes: plant seed potatoes (cut pieces with 2+ eyes) 4 inches deep, 12 inches apart, 2–4 weeks before last frost

### Warm-Season Crops (Plant After Last Frost, Soil 60°F+)

These die at the first frost. No exceptions.

- Tomatoes: transplant only (start indoors 6–8 weeks early), space 24 inches apart, need support (cage, stake, trellis)
- Peppers: transplant, 18 inches apart, slower to mature than tomatoes
- Beans (bush): direct sow 1 inch deep, 4 inches apart. Bush beans produce all at once. Pole beans produce over a longer period but need something to climb.
- Corn: direct sow 1 inch deep, 8 inches apart, in blocks of at least 4 rows (corn is wind-pollinated — single rows produce empty ears)
- Squash and pumpkins: direct sow 1 inch deep, give them space — vining types need 6+ feet between hills
- Cucumbers: direct sow or transplant, 12 inches apart, grow on a trellis to save space

## Companion Planting

Some crops help each other. Some sabotage each other.

**Good combinations:**
- Tomatoes + basil (basil may repel some pests, doesn't compete for space)
- Corn + beans + squash (Three Sisters — see above)
- Carrots + onions (onion smell confuses carrot fly)
- Cabbage + dill (dill attracts wasps that eat cabbage worms)

**Bad combinations:**
- Tomatoes + fennel (fennel inhibits growth of most nearby plants)
- Beans + onions or garlic (alliums stunt bean growth)
- Potatoes + tomatoes (same family, share diseases, compete for the same nutrients)

Don't overthink this. Companion planting helps at the margins. Spacing, water, and soil matter 10x more.

## Spacing and Layout

**Row gardening** is traditional but wastes space on walkways. Fine if you have room.

**Intensive/square foot gardening** eliminates walkways and plants in dense grids. Produces 4–5x more food per area than row gardening. Requires better soil (more compost) because you're asking more from each square foot.

Intensive spacing guidelines (per plant):
- Lettuce: 6 inches
- Carrots: 3 inches
- Bush beans: 4 inches
- Tomatoes: 18–24 inches
- Peppers: 12–18 inches
- Cabbage: 15 inches
- Corn: 8 inches (in blocks)
- Squash: 24+ inches (or trellis vertically)
- Potatoes: 12 inches

Maximum bed width: 4 feet. You need to reach the center without stepping in the bed — compacting soil around roots kills production.

## Succession Planting

Don't plant everything at once. Stagger plantings every 2–3 weeks so you have a continuous harvest instead of a glut followed by nothing.

This works especially well for fast crops: radishes, lettuce, beans, and greens. Plant a new short row every 2 weeks from spring through late summer.

For crops with a long season (tomatoes, peppers, squash), start a second round 3–4 weeks after the first to extend your harvest window.

## Watering

Most vegetables need about 1 inch of water per week. That's roughly 60 gallons per 100 square feet.

1. Water deeply and less often rather than a little every day. Deep watering encourages roots to grow down. Shallow watering keeps roots at the surface where they dry out fast.
2. Water at the base of plants, not overhead. Wet leaves invite fungal disease.
3. Best time to water: early morning. Evening watering leaves foliage wet overnight, promoting disease. Midday watering loses a lot to evaporation.
4. Mulch 2–4 inches thick cuts water needs by 30–50%. Straw, leaves, wood chips — whatever you have.
5. Signs of underwatering: wilting in the afternoon (some wilting in midday heat is normal — check again in the morning), dry soil 2 inches below the surface, leaf edges turning brown.
6. Signs of overwatering: yellowing lower leaves, constantly wet soil, mushy stems at ground level, fungus gnats.

## Extending the Season

**In spring:** Use row covers (fabric draped over hoops) to trap heat and protect from late frosts. Plastic sheeting works too but needs to be vented on warm days or it cooks the plants.

**In fall:** The same row covers protect from early frost. Many cool-season crops (kale, carrots, turnips, spinach) actually taste better after a light frost — the cold converts starches to sugars.

**Cold frames:** A bottomless box with a clear lid (old window, clear plastic) set over a garden bed. Creates a mini greenhouse. Can extend your growing season 4–8 weeks on each end.

## Harvest and Storage

- Pick beans, cucumbers, and squash regularly — leaving overripe fruit on the plant signals it to stop producing.
- Tomatoes can be picked when they show any color and ripened indoors. This is smart if frost is coming or birds are eating them.
- Potatoes: wait until foliage dies back, then dig. Cure in a dark, ventilated space for 1–2 weeks before long-term storage.
- Winter squash: harvest when the stem is dry and the skin can't be dented with a fingernail. Cure in a warm spot for 10 days, then store cool and dry. Properly cured butternut squash stores 6+ months.
- Root crops (carrots, beets, turnips): can be left in the ground and harvested as needed well into winter. Mulch heavily over them to prevent the ground from freezing solid.

> **Reality check:** A family of four needs roughly 4,000–8,000 square feet of productive garden to approach self-sufficiency in vegetables. That's a lot of work. Start with 200–400 square feet your first season, learn what grows in your soil and climate, and expand from there. Growing all your own food takes years of practice.
