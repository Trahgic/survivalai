Growing grain by hand is labor-intensive — there's no way around that. But grain is stored calories. A bushel of wheat (60 pounds) contains about 90,000 calories, stores for years with no refrigeration, and can be ground into flour for bread, tortillas, porridge, and pasta. If you're thinking beyond a garden and toward actual food self-sufficiency, grain is how you get there.

## How Much Land Do You Need

Rough rule of thumb: one person needs about 200–400 pounds of grain per year as a dietary staple. That's 4–8 bushels of wheat or equivalent.

Yields vary wildly by climate, soil, and variety, but for small-scale hand-harvested grain:

| Grain | Yield per 1,000 sq ft | Yield per Acre | Growing Season |
|-------|-----------------------|----------------|---------------|
| Wheat | 25–60 lbs | 1,000–2,500 lbs | 90–120 days (spring) or fall-planted |
| Corn | 30–60 lbs shelled | 1,500–3,000 lbs | 70–100 days |
| Oats | 20–40 lbs | 1,000–2,000 lbs | 70–90 days |
| Barley | 20–40 lbs | 1,000–2,000 lbs | 60–90 days |
| Rye | 20–50 lbs | 1,000–2,000 lbs | 90–120 days |
| Rice | 15–30 lbs (dry) | 800–1,500 lbs | 120–150 days |

A dedicated 1/10-acre plot (roughly 4,400 sq ft, about a 66x66 foot square) can produce 100–250 pounds of wheat — enough for one person's annual bread needs.

## Wheat

The most important grain for bread-making because of its gluten content.

### Types

- **Hard red winter wheat:** Planted in fall (September–October), overwinters, harvested in early summer. Best for bread. Needs a cold period (vernalization) to set grain.
- **Hard red spring wheat:** Planted in early spring, harvested late summer. Good for bread. Use in areas with extremely harsh winters where fall-planted wheat doesn't survive.
- **Soft wheat:** Lower protein, less gluten. Better for pastry, crackers, and flatbread. Easier to mill by hand.

### Planting

1. Prepare the soil: remove weeds, rake smooth. Wheat isn't fussy about soil but does best in well-drained loam.
2. Broadcast seed by hand at a rate of about 1.5–2 pounds per 100 square feet (roughly 60–80 lbs per acre). Scatter evenly.
3. Rake or drag a board across the surface to cover seeds 1–2 inches deep.
4. For winter wheat: plant 4–6 weeks before the first hard freeze. Seeds need to establish roots before winter.
5. For spring wheat: plant as soon as the ground can be worked in spring. Wheat is frost-hardy once sprouted.

### Harvest

Wheat is ready when the heads turn golden brown and the kernels are hard. Bite one — it should crack between your teeth, not dent or feel doughy.

1. **Cut** the stalks near the base with a scythe, sickle, or heavy knife. Bundle into sheaves (handfuls tied with a straw band) and stand them upright in shocks (teepee arrangements of 6–8 sheaves) to finish drying for 1–2 weeks.
2. **Thresh** to separate grain from stalks. Small batches: put heads in a pillowcase and beat against a hard surface. Or spread on a tarp and stomp on them. Medium batches: beat sheaves against the inside of a barrel or trash can. The grain falls to the bottom.
3. **Winnow** to separate grain from chaff. On a breezy day, slowly pour the threshed grain from one bucket into another, letting the wind blow away the lighter chaff. Or use a fan. Repeat 2–3 times until mostly clean grain remains.
4. **Dry** the grain to below 12% moisture before storage. Spread in thin layers on clean tarps in sun and air for 2–3 days, stirring occasionally. Bite a kernel — it should crack cleanly, not flex.

## Corn

Easiest grain to grow on a small scale. Produces the most calories per area. Requires less processing than wheat.

### Types for Grain

- **Dent corn (field corn):** The standard grain corn. Not sweet corn — it's starchy and intended for grinding into cornmeal, masa, or animal feed. Plant the same way as sweet corn but let it dry on the stalk.
- **Flint corn:** Harder kernel, stores better, more disease resistant. Good for cornmeal and hominy.

Don't try to save seed from sweet corn for grain — it has too much sugar and not enough starch.

### Planting

1. Plant after last frost when soil is 60°F+ (16°C). One inch deep, 8–10 inches apart, in blocks of at least 4 rows. Corn is wind-pollinated and needs dense planting for good pollination.
2. Side-dress with compost or manure when plants are knee-high. Corn is a heavy feeder — especially nitrogen.
3. Watch for drought stress (leaves curling inward). Water deeply during tasseling and silking — this is when the ears fill.

### Harvest for Grain

1. Leave ears on the stalk well past eating stage. The husks should be brown and papery, kernels hard and dry.
2. Pull ears, peel back husks. If kernels dent when pressed with a thumbnail, they need more drying. If they don't dent at all, they're ready.
3. **Shelling:** Twist two dried ears against each other — kernels pop off. For larger quantities, scrape kernels off with a board, dowel, or the edge of a bucket.
4. Spread shelled corn on tarps to dry below 13% moisture.

### Nixtamalization

Raw ground corn (cornmeal) is missing niacin in a form your body can absorb. Eating corn as a staple without treating it causes pellagra — a nasty vitamin deficiency. Indigenous peoples across the Americas solved this thousands of years ago.

1. Add 1 tablespoon of food-grade calcium hydroxide (cal, pickling lime) or 2 tablespoons of hardwood ash to 1 quart of water.
2. Add 1 pound of dried corn kernels.
3. Bring to a boil, simmer 30–60 minutes. Kernels should be tender and skins should peel off easily.
4. Remove from heat, let soak overnight (8–12 hours).
5. Drain and rinse, rubbing kernels to remove loosened skins.
6. The result is hominy (nixtamal). Grind it wet for fresh masa (for tortillas, tamales). Or dry it and grind later.

> **This is not optional.** If corn is your primary calorie source, you must nixtamalize it. Pellagra starts with skin lesions, diarrhea, and confusion, and it kills if untreated. Lime or ash treatment unlocks niacin and improves protein availability.

## Oats and Barley

Both are cool-season crops, easier to grow than wheat in many areas.

**Oats:** Plant early spring, harvest mid-summer. Tolerates poor soil and wet conditions better than wheat. Hull-less oat varieties are much easier to process — standard oats have a tough hull that requires specialized dehulling.

**Barley:** Very short growing season (60–70 days). Plant early spring. Tolerates dry conditions. Hull-less varieties available. Barley is excellent as a porridge grain and for animal feed.

Both are planted and harvested the same way as wheat — broadcast, rake, cut, thresh, winnow.

## Rice

Rice requires specific conditions: standing water or very wet soil for most of the growing season, and 120+ frost-free days with warm temperatures (70–85°F / 21–30°C).

**Upland rice** varieties grow in regular garden soil without flooding — they just need consistent moisture. These yield less than paddy rice but are far more practical for small-scale growing.

If you're in the right climate (Southeast US, Gulf Coast, or similar):

1. Start seeds indoors 4–6 weeks before last frost.
2. Transplant into flooded beds or very wet soil. Maintain 2–4 inches of standing water through the growing season (paddy rice) or keep soil constantly wet (upland rice).
3. Drain beds 2–3 weeks before harvest.
4. Harvest when stalks turn golden and grain heads droop. Cut, dry, thresh, and winnow like wheat.
5. Rice needs an extra step: **hulling** (removing the outer husk). A mortar and pestle works — pound gently to crack hulls without smashing the grain. Winnow out the chaff. The result is brown rice.
6. For white rice, continue pounding and sifting to remove the bran layer. This removes nutrients but improves storage life.

## Milling Grain into Flour

Without a mill, your options:

**Mortar and pestle:** Works for small quantities. Pound rather than grind. Makes coarse meal, not fine flour.

**Flat stones (quern):** Two flat stones, the top one rotated on the bottom one. Pour grain through a hole in the top stone. This is how flour was made for thousands of years. Works, but it's slow.

**Hand-crank grain mill:** If you have one or can find one, these produce good flour with manageable effort. Corona-style mills are common, inexpensive, and can process 1–2 pounds per hour.

**Converted meat grinder:** A manual meat grinder with fine plates can grind grain. Run it through twice — once coarse, once fine.

Flour goes rancid within weeks to months because grinding exposes the oils in the grain. Grind only what you'll use in the next few days. Store whole grain, not flour.

## Grain Storage

Whole, dry grain stores for years if protected from moisture, insects, and rodents.

1. **Moisture:** Must be below 12%. If you can't measure, the "bite test" should produce a sharp crack, not a dent.
2. **Containers:** Metal trash cans with tight lids (rodent-proof). 5-gallon buckets with gamma seal lids. Mylar bags inside buckets for longest storage.
3. **Oxygen absorbers:** Drop 2–3 300cc oxygen absorbers into a 5-gallon bucket of grain before sealing. The oxygen-free environment kills weevil eggs and extends storage life to 10–25 years.
4. **Diatomaceous earth:** Mix food-grade DE through the grain at 1 cup per 5-gallon bucket. It kills grain weevils by scratching their exoskeletons.
5. **Temperature:** Cool and stable. Avoid attics and garages with wild temperature swings.
6. **Check regularly:** Open and inspect stored grain every few months. If you see webbing, live insects, or off smells, the grain may still be usable — sift out the bugs, spread the grain in sun for a day to drive out pests, re-store with fresh oxygen absorbers.

## Small-Scale Harvesting Tools

If you're growing grain beyond a small test plot:

- **Scythe:** Cuts 1/10 acre per hour with practice. Austrian-style scythes are lighter and easier to use than American patterns. Keep the blade razor sharp — a dull scythe is miserable.
- **Grain cradle:** A scythe with fingers that catch the stalks and lay them in neat windrows for bundling. Much faster than a plain scythe for grain.
- **Flail:** Two sticks joined by a short chain or leather strap. Swing the free end onto the grain spread on a threshing floor. Traditional and effective for small batches.

> **Realistic expectations:** Growing grain by hand is hard work. It's pre-industrial agriculture. Don't try to feed your family entirely from hand-harvested grain in year one. Start with a 10x10 foot test plot, learn the process, and scale up as your skills and tools improve. Even a small grain patch produces valuable seed stock and supplements your stored supplies.
