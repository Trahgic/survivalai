Bees give you three things: honey (long-term stored calories and medicine), beeswax (candles, waterproofing, sealant), and pollination (30–50% higher yields on fruits and vegetables). A single healthy hive can produce 30–60 pounds of surplus honey per year in a good area. Two hives is the minimum recommendation — if one dies, you still have bees.

## Hive Types

### Langstroth Hive

The standard in commercial and hobby beekeeping. Stackable rectangular boxes with removable frames inside. Bees build comb on the frames, which you can pull out individually to inspect, harvest, or manage.

- **Advantage:** Most resources, guides, and equipment are designed for Langstroths. Easy to expand by adding boxes. Frames are interchangeable. Honey harvest is straightforward.
- **Disadvantage:** Requires purchasing or building standardized equipment. Heavier to manage (a full honey super weighs 40–60 pounds).

### Top-Bar Hive

A horizontal trough with bars across the top. Bees build natural comb hanging from the bars. No frames, no foundation. You manage the hive by lifting bars one at a time.

- **Advantage:** Cheaper and easier to build from scrap materials. Lighter management — no heavy boxes to lift. Bees build natural comb (some beekeepers believe this is healthier for bees).
- **Disadvantage:** Lower honey yield than Langstroth. Comb is fragile — can't spin it in an extractor. Harder to find resources and mentors.
- **Best for survival situations:** If you can't buy equipment, a top-bar hive can be built from a single sheet of plywood and some scraps. The critical dimension: bars must be 1-3/8 inches wide for European honey bees. This matches their natural comb spacing.

### Building a Simple Top-Bar Hive

1. Build a trough-shaped box about 4 feet long, 12 inches deep, with sloping sides (wider at top, narrower at bottom — this discourages bees from attaching comb to the walls).
2. Top opening should be about 18–20 inches wide.
3. Cut bars from 1x2 lumber, each 1-3/8 inches wide and long enough to span the top of the trough with a little overhang.
4. Melt a thin bead of beeswax along the center bottom of each bar as a comb guide. Bees will build comb hanging from this line.
5. Drill a 1-inch entrance hole at one end of the box, about 2 inches from the bottom.
6. Set on legs or blocks at waist height.

## Getting Bees

### Capturing a Swarm

Free bees. Swarms happen in spring when a colony gets crowded and the old queen leaves with about half the workers. A swarm is a ball of bees hanging on a branch, fence post, or wall. They're looking for a new home and are generally very docile — they have no hive to defend.

1. Place an open hive body or box directly below the swarm.
2. Give the branch a sharp shake — the ball drops into the box.
3. Set a lid on top loosely. If the queen is inside, the rest will march in.
4. Move the box to your hive location at dusk (bees don't fly at night, so none get left behind).
5. Transfer into your permanent hive.

If the swarm is on a wall or flat surface, brush them gently into a box using a soft brush or a piece of cardboard.

### Bait Hive (Swarm Trap)

Set up an empty hive body with a few old frames (or bars rubbed with beeswax) and a drop of lemongrass oil at the entrance. Place it 8–15 feet off the ground in a visible location near trees. Scouts from swarming colonies often choose bait hives as their new home. Check weekly in spring.

### Buying Bees

If commerce exists: a 3-pound package of bees with a mated queen costs around $150–200. A nucleus colony ("nuc" — 5 frames with brood, bees, and a laying queen) costs $200–250 and has a higher success rate.

## Basic Colony Management

### What's Inside the Hive

- **Queen:** One per hive. She lays all the eggs — up to 2,000 per day at peak. She lives 2–5 years. If she dies or fails, the colony is doomed unless they raise a new one.
- **Workers:** Female bees that do everything — forage, build comb, nurse brood, guard the entrance, regulate temperature. They live 4–6 weeks in summer, several months in winter.
- **Drones:** Male bees. Their only job is mating with queens from other colonies. They eat resources and contribute nothing to the hive otherwise. The colony kicks them out before winter.

### Inspections

Check hives every 2–3 weeks during the active season (spring through fall). Less often in winter — don't open the hive when it's below 55°F (13°C).

**What you're looking for:**

1. **Eggs:** Tiny white grains standing up in cells. If you see eggs, the queen was alive within the last 3 days.
2. **Brood pattern:** Capped brood (tan caps over developing pupae) should be in a solid, compact pattern. Spotty, scattered brood may indicate a failing queen or disease.
3. **Food stores:** Bees need honey and pollen. In spring and fall, check that stores are adequate. A colony needs 60–80 pounds of honey to survive winter in cold climates.
4. **Queen cells:** Peanut-shaped cells hanging from the bottom of comb. This means the colony is preparing to swarm or replace the queen. If you want to prevent a swarm, remove queen cells (but this doesn't always work — it treats the symptom, not the cause).
5. **Pests and disease:** See below.

### Using a Smoker

Smoke calms bees by triggering a gorging response (they eat honey in case they need to abandon the hive) and masks alarm pheromones.

1. Light your smoker with newspaper, then add fuel: pine needles, burlap, dried grass, wood chips, cotton. You want cool, white smoke — not flames or hot smoke.
2. Puff 2–3 gentle puffs at the entrance before opening.
3. Puff a little smoke across the top of the frames as you open the hive.
4. Don't over-smoke — too much stresses the bees and taints honey.

### Seasonal Management

**Spring:** Check for queen activity (eggs), add space (more frames or bars) as the colony grows, watch for swarm preparations, consider splitting strong colonies.

**Summer:** Peak honey production. Add honey supers (extra boxes) on top for surplus storage. Ensure water is available nearby — bees consume a lot of water in hot weather.

**Fall:** Harvest surplus honey (leave at least 60–80 pounds for the colony's winter stores, depending on your climate). Treat for mites. Reduce entrance size to deter robber bees and mice.

**Winter:** Leave them alone. Don't open the hive. The colony clusters into a ball around the queen and shivers to generate heat — maintaining about 93°F (34°C) in the center of the cluster even in sub-zero weather. Make sure the upper ventilation hole isn't blocked (moisture buildup inside the hive kills more colonies than cold).

## Harvesting Honey

### Langstroth Method

1. Remove frames from the honey super. Brush or shake bees off each frame.
2. Cut the wax cappings off the filled cells with a serrated knife or heated uncapping knife.
3. Place uncapped frames in an extractor (a centrifuge that spins honey out of the cells). Or, if you don't have an extractor, crush the comb and strain through cheesecloth — you'll get wax and honey, but can't reuse the comb.
4. Strain honey through a mesh filter to remove wax bits.
5. Let it settle for 24 hours, then bottle.

### Top-Bar Method

1. Remove a full bar of capped honey. Brush off bees.
2. Cut the comb from the bar.
3. Crush the comb in a bucket and let it drain through cheesecloth or a fine strainer.
4. The honey drips through, the wax stays in the cloth. Press gently to extract remaining honey.
5. Save the wax — it's valuable.

### How Much to Take

Never take all the honey. The colony needs stores to survive, especially through winter. In cold climates, leave 60–80 pounds (a full deep Langstroth box weighs about 90 pounds when full). In mild climates, 30–40 pounds is usually enough. If in doubt, leave more — you can always harvest a frame or two later if the colony is strong. A starved colony is a dead colony.

## Honey Storage

Raw honey stores almost indefinitely if kept sealed, dry, and at room temperature. Edible honey has been found in Egyptian tombs thousands of years old. It crystallizes over time — this is normal and doesn't mean it's bad. Warm the jar gently in hot water to re-liquify.

Don't store honey in the refrigerator — it crystallizes faster. Don't feed raw honey to infants under 12 months — it can contain botulism spores that their immature gut can't handle.

## Beeswax

Save all the wax cappings and crushed comb. Melt it in a double boiler (never direct heat — wax is flammable) and strain through cheesecloth. Pour into molds or containers. Uses: candles, leather waterproofing, wood finish, lubricant for tools and drawers, sewing thread coating, fire starter, lip balm, salve base.

## Common Problems

### Varroa Mites

The #1 killer of managed colonies worldwide. Tiny reddish-brown parasites that feed on developing bees and transmit viruses. Every colony has them — the question is whether the mite load is under control.

**Monitoring:** Do an alcohol wash or sugar roll every 1–2 months during active season. Count mites per 100 bees. Above 3 mites per 100 bees, treatment is needed.

**Treatment options (without commercial chemicals):**
- Oxalic acid vaporization (most effective, requires a vaporizer tool)
- Oxalic acid dribble (dissolved in sugar syrup, trickled between frames during broodless periods)
- Formic acid pads (organic-approved)
- Powdered sugar dusting (roll bees in powdered sugar — mites lose grip and fall through the screen bottom board). Less effective than acid treatments but better than nothing.
- Brood breaks: cage the queen for 3 weeks so no brood is present, then treat with oxalic acid. This kills the mites that have nowhere to hide.

Without treatment, most colonies collapse within 1–3 years from varroa. Don't skip this.

### Starvation

Colonies starve in late winter/early spring when stored honey runs out before new flowers bloom. If the colony feels light (lift one side — it should feel heavy), feed 2:1 sugar syrup (2 parts sugar to 1 part water by weight) or place a fondant patty on top of the frames.

### Queenlessness

If you stop seeing eggs and young larvae, the queen may be dead or failing. A queenless colony becomes agitated and loud. They may try to raise a new queen from young larvae (emergency queen cells). If they succeed, the new queen takes 2–3 weeks to mate and start laying. If they don't have young larvae, the colony is doomed without your intervention — you'll need to introduce a new queen or combine the colony with a queened colony.

## Bee Stings

They're going to happen. A few stings per inspection is normal.

- Remove the stinger immediately by scraping sideways with a fingernail or hive tool. Don't pinch it — that squeezes more venom in.
- Ice and antihistamine for normal reactions (local swelling, pain, itching).
- Watch for anaphylaxis: difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat, dizziness, widespread hives. This is life-threatening. See **allergic-reactions.md** for epinephrine dosing and management.

> **Getting started:** If this is your first time, start with two hives, read everything you can, and find a local beekeeper to mentor you. Beekeeping has a steep learning curve in the first year. Expect to make mistakes. Your bees are more forgiving than you'd think — they've been managing themselves for millions of years.
