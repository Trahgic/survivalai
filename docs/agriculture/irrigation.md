Gardens die without water. In a grid-down scenario, you can't run a sprinkler off the tap. You need systems that move water to plants using gravity, hand power, or stored rainwater. The best systems are simple, fixable with basic tools, and don't depend on electricity or pumps.

## How Much Water Your Garden Needs

Most vegetables need about 1 inch of water per week during the growing season. That translates to roughly 0.6 gallons per square foot per week.

Do the math for your garden:
- A 200 sq ft garden needs about 120 gallons per week
- A 500 sq ft garden needs about 300 gallons per week
- A 1,000 sq ft garden needs about 600 gallons per week

Those numbers assume no rain and no mulch. Mulching 3–4 inches thick cuts water needs by 30–50%. Rain obviously reduces what you need to supply.

In hot, dry, or windy conditions, double the estimate. Sandy soil drains fast and needs more frequent watering. Clay holds water longer but absorbs it slowly.

## Gravity-Fed Systems

The simplest powered irrigation: put water above your garden and let gravity do the work. No electricity, no pumps, no moving parts to break.

### Rain Barrel to Drip Line

1. Set rain barrels on a platform at least 3–4 feet above the garden beds. Higher is better — every foot of elevation gives you about 0.43 PSI of pressure. You need at least 2 feet of elevation for drip emitters to work.
2. Install a spigot at the bottom of the barrel. Use a standard garden hose fitting.
3. Connect a garden hose or 3/4-inch poly tubing from the barrel to the garden.
4. At the garden, run 1/2-inch drip irrigation tubing (or soaker hose) along each row of plants.
5. Punch holes or install drip emitters every 12–18 inches for row crops, or place one emitter at the base of each plant for widely spaced crops (tomatoes, squash).
6. Open the spigot and let it drip. Gravity pressure is low, so the flow will be slow — that's fine. Slow and steady soaks in better than fast and shallow.

**Key numbers:**
- A standard 55-gallon rain barrel waters about 90 square feet at 0.6 gallons per sq ft
- Connect multiple barrels in series for larger gardens
- A 1,000 sq ft roof captures about 600 gallons per 1 inch of rain

### Gravity-Fed from a Creek or Pond

If you have a water source uphill from your garden:

1. Run poly pipe (1-inch or larger) from the source to the garden. Bury it below frost line if winter freezing is a concern.
2. At the source, install a basic filter (screen or mesh) to keep debris out.
3. The pipe outlet at the garden will have pressure proportional to the elevation difference. 10 feet of head gives about 4.3 PSI — enough for drip irrigation.
4. Use a valve at the garden end to control flow.
5. From the valve, distribute through drip tubing as above.

### Elevated Tank System

For a more permanent setup:

1. Build or install a tank (IBC tote, large barrel, or cistern) on a tower or hillside 8–15 feet above the garden.
2. Fill the tank by hand (bucket brigade), pump (hand or solar), or rainwater collection.
3. Run pipe to the garden with a valve. This gives 3.5–6.5 PSI — enough for most drip systems and even some sprinklers at the lower end.

## Hand Watering Methods

When gravity systems aren't set up, these keep your garden alive.

**Bucket watering:** Slow and labor-intensive but works anywhere. Water at the base of each plant, not overhead. One person can hand-water about 500 square feet in 30–45 minutes with a bucket.

**Watering can with rosette:** Better than dumping from a bucket. The rosette (the sprinkler head) breaks the water into gentle streams that don't erode soil away from roots.

**Ladle or dipper:** For precise watering of seedlings and transplants. A soup ladle delivers exactly where you want it without disturbing small plants.

## Drip Irrigation Details

Drip is the most water-efficient method — 90–95% of water reaches roots. Sprinklers lose 30–50% to evaporation and overspray.

### Building a Drip System

**Materials:** 1/2-inch poly drip tubing (sometimes called "mainline"), drip emitters (1 or 2 gallon per hour), 1/4-inch micro tubing for branches, end caps, T-fittings, hole punch.

1. Run mainline tubing along the length of each bed.
2. Punch holes in the mainline every 12–18 inches and insert drip emitters. Or use pre-punched "emitter tubing" that has emitters built in at regular intervals.
3. For large plants (tomatoes, trees), run 1/4-inch micro tubing from the mainline to a drip emitter at each plant's base.
4. Cap the ends of all tubing.
5. Flush the system before capping — run water through to clear debris.
6. Connect the mainline to your water source (barrel, tank, hose).

### Improvised Drip Irrigation

No drip supplies? These work:

**Plastic bottle drip:** Poke 2–4 small holes near the bottom of a plastic bottle. Bury it next to a plant with the holes at root level and the top above ground. Fill with water. It slowly seeps out over hours. A 2-liter bottle waters one plant for 1–3 days depending on hole size and soil type.

**Soaker hose from old garden hose:** Poke small holes every 6 inches along an old garden hose with a nail. Lay it along rows. Cap the far end. Connect to a water source. It weeps water along its length.

**Olla irrigation:** An ancient method. Bury an unglazed clay pot (terra cotta) up to its neck in the garden bed. Fill it with water and cap the top. Water seeps through the porous clay walls into surrounding soil. Roots grow toward the olla and pull moisture as needed. A single olla waters a 3-foot radius. Refill every 2–5 days.

## Rain Barrel Setup

Catching roof runoff is free water.

1. Position the barrel under a downspout. Cut the downspout to aim into the barrel's top opening.
2. Install a screen over the opening to keep out leaves, mosquitoes, and debris.
3. Install an overflow outlet near the top — when the barrel is full, excess water needs somewhere to go that isn't your foundation. Route it to another barrel or away from structures.
4. Install a spigot near the bottom for hose connection.
5. Elevate the barrel on cinder blocks or a platform for gravity pressure.
6. In mosquito season, keep the barrel sealed or screened. Mosquitoes breed in standing water.

**Capacity planning:** A 55-gallon barrel fills fast — a 1,000 sq ft roof generates 600 gallons per inch of rain. One inch of rain fills 10 barrels. Consider larger containers: IBC totes (275 gallons), multiple barrels linked in series, or a proper cistern for serious volume.

## Swale-Based Water Harvesting

Swales capture rainwater and sink it into the ground where plant roots can access it (see **permaculture-basics.md** for construction details). A well-placed swale system can dramatically reduce irrigation needs by keeping the soil profile charged with water between rains.

Swales work best in climates with periodic heavy rain followed by dry spells. The swale captures the burst and meters it into the soil over days.

## Greywater Reuse

Used water from sinks, showers, and laundry can water fruit trees, ornamentals, and some crops. Do not use greywater on root vegetables or leafy greens eaten raw.

**Basic greywater rules:**
1. Don't store it — use it the same day. Greywater grows bacteria fast.
2. Use biodegradable, plant-friendly soap. No bleach, no boron-based detergents, no antibacterial soaps.
3. Distribute below mulch, not on the surface. Soil filters and breaks down contaminants.
4. Rotate application areas so no single spot gets saturated.
5. Don't use kitchen sink water that has grease, meat juices, or dairy in it.

**Simple setup:** Route the washing machine drain hose to a garden. Use a 3-way valve so you can switch between sewer/septic and garden depending on what you're washing. A single load of laundry produces 15–30 gallons — enough to water several fruit trees.

## Mulch as Water Conservation

This isn't irrigation — it's the single most effective thing you can do to reduce how much irrigation you need.

1. Apply 3–4 inches of organic mulch (straw, wood chips, leaves, grass clippings) around all plants. Keep it 2 inches away from stems to prevent rot.
2. Mulch reduces evaporation from the soil surface by 50–70%.
3. It moderates soil temperature, keeping roots cooler in summer and warmer in shoulder seasons.
4. As it decomposes, it feeds soil organisms and improves water-holding capacity.
5. Replenish as it breaks down — usually once or twice per season.

A heavily mulched garden in a moderate climate with occasional rain may need no supplemental irrigation at all for established perennials. Annual vegetables still need regular water, but mulch cuts the amount in half.

## Seasonal Water Strategy

**Spring:** Soil is usually wet from snowmelt and spring rains. Don't overwater — waterlogged soil prevents root growth and promotes disease. Focus on setting up irrigation infrastructure.

**Summer:** Peak demand. Water deeply every 3–5 days rather than lightly every day. Early morning is best. Check soil moisture by sticking your finger 2–3 inches into the soil — if it's dry at that depth, water.

**Fall:** Reduce watering as crops mature and days shorten. Give fruit trees and perennials a deep soaking before first hard freeze — this "winter watering" protects roots.

**Winter:** No irrigation needed for dormant plants. Drain and store drip tubing and hoses to prevent freeze damage.

> **Priority call:** If water is scarce, prioritize calorie crops (potatoes, beans, squash, corn) over greens and herbs. A tomato plant that doesn't get watered produces less fruit. A potato plant that doesn't get watered produces nothing.
