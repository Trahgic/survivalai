Good soil is the difference between a garden that feeds you and a patch of dirt that wastes your seeds. You don't need a lab to figure out what you're working with — your hands and eyes will tell you most of what you need to know.

## Testing Soil by Hand

Grab a handful of moist soil and squeeze it.

1. **Sandy soil** crumbles apart immediately when you open your hand. Water runs straight through it. Plants dry out fast and nutrients wash away. Feels gritty.
2. **Clay soil** holds its shape like modeling clay and stays in a tight ball. Water pools on the surface and roots struggle to push through. Feels slick and sticky.
3. **Loam** holds together loosely but breaks apart when you poke it. This is what you want — it holds water and nutrients but still drains and lets roots breathe.

Most soil is a mix. You're trying to figure out which direction it leans so you know what to add.

### The Jar Test

This gives you a rough percentage of sand, silt, and clay.

1. Fill a quart jar one-third full with soil. Remove rocks, roots, and debris.
2. Fill the rest with water, leaving an inch of air at the top.
3. Add a tablespoon of dish soap or salt — this helps the particles separate.
4. Shake hard for 2–3 minutes, then set it on a flat surface and don't touch it.
5. After 1 minute, mark the settled layer on the jar with a marker. That's sand.
6. After 2 hours, mark again. The new layer is silt.
7. After 24–48 hours, the water should be mostly clear. The top layer is clay.

Measure each layer and divide by the total to get your percentages. Ideal garden soil is roughly 40% sand, 40% silt, 20% clay — but anything close works fine.

### pH Testing Without a Kit

Most vegetables prefer pH between 6.0 and 7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral). Without a test kit:

1. **Vinegar test:** Put a tablespoon of dry soil in a cup. Pour white vinegar on it. If it fizzes, the soil is alkaline (above pH 7).
2. **Baking soda test:** Put a tablespoon of moist soil in a cup. Sprinkle baking soda on it. If it fizzes, the soil is acidic (below pH 7).
3. If neither fizzes, you're somewhere near neutral — which is fine for most crops.

To lower pH (make more acidic): work in pine needles, coffee grounds, or sulfur. To raise pH (make more alkaline): work in wood ash or crusite limestone. Changes take weeks to months — don't expect instant results.

## Fixing Sandy Soil

Sandy soil drains too fast and doesn't hold nutrients. The fix is organic matter — lots of it.

1. Spread 3–4 inches of compost, rotted manure, or leaf mold over the surface.
2. Work it into the top 8–10 inches by turning with a shovel or fork.
3. Repeat every season. Sandy soil eats organic matter fast because it decomposes quickly in well-aerated soil.
4. Mulch heavily on top (straw, leaves, wood chips) to slow evaporation.

## Fixing Clay Soil

Clay holds water and nutrients well but suffocates roots and turns into concrete when dry.

1. Spread 3–4 inches of coarse compost over the surface. Avoid fine-textured amendments — they fill the gaps between clay particles and make it worse.
2. Add coarse sand or perlite — about 2 inches mixed into the top 10 inches. Don't add sand without compost or you'll basically make poor concrete.
3. Work it in when the soil is moist but not wet. Tilling wet clay destroys the structure and creates hard clumps that take years to break down.
4. Grow cover crops with deep roots (daikon radish, clover) to break up compaction naturally.
5. Build raised beds on top of clay rather than fighting it. Fill with imported soil mix.

## Composting

Compost is the single best soil amendment. It fixes drainage in clay, improves water retention in sand, feeds soil organisms, and provides slow-release nutrients.

### Hot Composting (Fast — 4 to 8 Weeks)

1. Build a pile at least 3 feet by 3 feet by 3 feet. Smaller piles won't heat up enough.
2. Alternate layers of "greens" (nitrogen-rich: food scraps, fresh grass clippings, manure, coffee grounds) and "browns" (carbon-rich: dry leaves, straw, cardboard, sawdust).
3. Aim for roughly 3 parts brown to 1 part green by volume.
4. Water each layer as you build — the pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge. Not dripping, not dry.
5. After 3–5 days the center should be hot enough you can't hold your hand in it (130–160°F / 55–70°C). This heat kills weed seeds and pathogens.
6. Turn the pile with a fork every 4–7 days, moving the outside material to the center.
7. If it stops heating: add more greens (nitrogen). If it smells like ammonia: add more browns (carbon). If it smells like rot: it's too wet — add browns and turn it.
8. It's done when it looks like dark crumbly soil, smells earthy, and you can't recognize any of the original materials.

### Cold Composting (Slow — 6 to 12 Months)

Just pile it up and wait. Add materials as you have them, keep it loosely moist, turn it occasionally if you feel like it. It works — it just takes longer and won't kill weed seeds.

### What NOT to Compost

- Meat, bones, dairy, oils — attract animals, create smell problems
- Dog or cat feces — carry parasites that survive composting
- Diseased plants — spread the disease back to your garden
- Treated wood, glossy paper — chemical contamination
- Coal or charcoal ash (wood ash is fine in small amounts)

## No-Till Gardening

Tilling destroys soil structure, kills beneficial fungi, brings weed seeds to the surface, and accelerates erosion. No-till methods build soil over time instead of mining it.

### Sheet Mulching (Lasagna Method)

This builds a new garden bed on top of existing ground — works on grass, weeds, even compacted soil.

1. Mow or stomp down whatever's growing. Don't remove it.
2. Lay overlapping sheets of cardboard or 8–10 layers of newspaper directly on the ground. Wet them thoroughly.
3. Add 2–3 inches of compost or manure on top of the cardboard.
4. Add 4–6 inches of mulch on top (straw, leaves, wood chips).
5. Water the whole thing well and wait. In 2–3 months the cardboard decomposes, the grass underneath dies, worms move in, and you've got plantable soil.
6. To plant immediately: cut holes through the cardboard layer, fill with compost, plant into the compost pockets.

### Chop and Drop

Instead of pulling weeds and removing crop residue, cut them at the base and leave them on the surface as mulch. They decompose in place and feed the soil. This mimics what happens on a forest floor.

## Building Raised Beds

If your native soil is terrible, skip the amendment battle and build on top of it.

1. Frame: Use untreated lumber (avoid pressure-treated — the chemicals leach into soil). Cedar and redwood resist rot naturally. Even scrap pallets work for a season or two.
2. Size: 4 feet wide maximum — you need to reach the center from either side without stepping in the bed. Length is whatever you want. 10–12 inches deep minimum for most vegetables, 18+ inches for root crops.
3. Bottom: No need for a liner on most ground. If you're building on concrete or heavy clay, add a few inches of gravel at the bottom for drainage.
4. Fill mix: 60% topsoil, 30% compost, 10% coarse sand or perlite. If buying bagged soil, avoid anything labeled "garden soil" without checking — some is just dyed wood mulch.

## Soil Amendments Quick Reference

| Problem | Amendment | How Much | Notes |
|---------|-----------|----------|-------|
| Low nitrogen | Composted manure, blood meal, fish meal | 2–4 inches manure, or 5 lbs blood meal per 100 sq ft | Nitrogen is the nutrient most crops burn through fastest |
| Low phosphorus | Bone meal, rock phosphate | 5–10 lbs per 100 sq ft | Crucial for root development and fruiting |
| Low potassium | Wood ash, kelp meal, granite dust | Wood ash: 5 lbs per 100 sq ft max | Too much ash raises pH fast — go easy |
| Compacted soil | Compost, gypsum, cover crops | 3–4 inches compost | Gypsum loosens clay without changing pH |
| Poor drainage | Coarse sand + compost mixed together | 2–3 inches each | Never add sand alone to clay |
| Low organic matter | Any compost, cover crops, mulch | As much as you can get | This fixes almost everything over time |

## Cover Crops

These are planted not for harvest but to improve soil between growing seasons.

- **Clover** (crimson or white): Fixes nitrogen from the air into the soil. One of the best things you can grow for free fertilizer.
- **Winter rye:** Grows in cold weather, massive root system breaks up compaction, produces a lot of biomass to chop and drop.
- **Daikon radish (tillage radish):** Roots drill 12+ inches into compacted soil, creating channels for water and future roots. They die in hard frost and decompose in place.
- **Buckwheat:** Fast summer cover. Ready to chop in 4–6 weeks. Attracts pollinators.

Cut cover crops before they set seed (or they become weeds). Chop at the base and leave the residue on the surface as mulch.

## Seasonal Soil Care

**Spring:** Pull back mulch to let soil warm up. Add compost before planting. Don't work soil when it's wet — squeeze a handful, and if it sticks together in a ball, wait.

**Summer:** Keep mulch 2–4 inches thick around plants to retain moisture and suppress weeds. Side-dress heavy feeders (tomatoes, corn, squash) with compost mid-season.

**Fall:** After harvest, plant cover crops or add 3–4 inches of mulch. Don't leave soil bare over winter — rain erodes exposed soil fast.

**Winter:** Leave it alone. The freeze-thaw cycle naturally breaks up clumps. If you sheet-mulched in fall, the bed will be ready to plant in spring.
