The goal isn't to kill every bug. Most insects in your garden are either neutral or helpful. The goal is to keep the damaging ones below the level where they destroy your crop. Healthy soil, diverse plantings, and physical barriers handle 90% of pest problems. You don't need a chemical arsenal.

## Prevention First

Most pest problems start with stressed, weak plants in monoculture rows. Fix the conditions and you fix most of the pests.

1. **Healthy soil** grows strong plants that resist damage better. Compost, mulch, and cover crops build soil that produces resilient crops.
2. **Crop rotation:** Don't plant the same family in the same spot two years in a row. Tomato pests overwinter in the soil and hit hard if you keep planting tomatoes there. Rotate: nightshades (tomatoes, peppers) → legumes (beans, peas) → brassicas (cabbage, broccoli) → roots (carrots, beets) → back to nightshades.
3. **Diversity:** Mixed plantings confuse pests. A solid block of cabbage is an all-you-can-eat buffet. Cabbage mixed with onions, herbs, and flowers is much harder for cabbage moths to find.
4. **Clean up:** Remove diseased plants immediately. Don't compost them — burn or trash them. Clear spent crop debris at season's end so pests don't overwinter in it.
5. **Timing:** Plant early or late to miss peak pest cycles. Many pests have a specific window. If you can get your crop past that window, damage drops.

## Physical Barriers

The most reliable pest control. No pests get through if the barrier is solid.

**Row covers (floating row cover / reemay):** Lightweight fabric draped over hoops or directly on plants. Lets in light, water, and air. Keeps out moths, beetles, flies, and most flying insects. Remove when plants need pollination (squash, cucumbers) or use hand-pollination.

**Netting:** Bird netting protects berries and fruit. Drape over frames — don't lay directly on plants or birds get tangled. 1/4 inch mesh also excludes cabbage moths.

**Collars:** Cut a 4-inch square of cardboard, slit to the center, and place around the stem of transplants at soil level. This blocks cutworms — soil-dwelling caterpillars that chew through stems at ground level, killing transplants overnight.

**Copper tape:** Strips of copper around raised bed edges or pot rims give slugs and snails a mild electrical shock on contact. They won't cross it. Actual copper tape from electrical suppliers is cheaper than garden-store versions.

**Fencing:** For deer, you need 8-foot fencing — they jump anything shorter. For rabbits, 3-foot fence with wire buried 6 inches below ground. For groundhogs, same as rabbits but bend the buried wire outward in an L-shape.

## Hand-Picking

Unglamorous but effective. Check plants early morning or late evening when pests are sluggish.

- **Tomato hornworms:** Big green caterpillars with white stripes. They can strip a tomato plant in days. Pick them off and kill them (drop in soapy water). If you see one covered in small white cocoons — leave it. Those are parasitic wasp eggs that will kill the hornworm and produce more wasps that kill more hornworms.
- **Colorado potato beetles:** Yellow-orange striped beetles on potato, tomato, and eggplant. Crush adults, scrape orange egg clusters off leaf undersides, pick and drown the red larvae.
- **Japanese beetles:** Metallic green and copper, eat almost everything. Pick them off into soapy water early morning when they're sluggish. Pheromone traps often attract more beetles than they catch — skip them.
- **Slugs and snails:** Hunt them at night with a flashlight or set beer traps — bury a cup so the rim is at soil level, fill halfway with cheap beer. They crawl in and drown. Or lay boards between rows — slugs hide underneath during the day, flip and collect them every morning.
- **Squash vine borers:** Moth larvae that burrow into squash stems at the base. Check stems for small holes with sawdust-like droppings. Slit the stem lengthwise with a razor blade, remove the larva, and bury the damaged section of stem — it'll root.

## Biological Controls

Use nature's pest eaters.

**Beneficial insects you want in your garden:**

- **Ladybugs:** Both adults and larvae eat aphids by the hundreds. Don't kill the larvae — they look like tiny alligators and eat more aphids than adults.
- **Green lacewings:** Larvae are voracious aphid predators. Also eat mites, thrips, and whiteflies.
- **Parasitic wasps (tiny, non-stinging):** Lay eggs inside hornworms, aphids, and caterpillars. Plant dill, fennel, yarrow, and small-flowered herbs to attract them.
- **Ground beetles:** Eat slugs, cutworms, and other soil pests. Provide habitat with mulch and ground cover.
- **Praying mantis:** Generalist predators. They eat pests and beneficials equally, so they're not targeted pest control, but they help with overall balance.

**How to attract beneficials:** Plant flowers and herbs among your vegetables. Marigolds, zinnias, sweet alyssum, dill, cilantro (let it flower), fennel, and yarrow all attract predatory and parasitic insects. A garden with zero flowers has zero beneficials.

**Chickens and ducks:** Free-range poultry eat enormous quantities of insects, slugs, and grubs. Ducks are especially effective against slugs. Don't let them into the garden when crops are young — they'll eat those too. Let them in after harvest or in fallow beds.

## Companion Planting for Pest Control

These combinations have some evidence behind them — they're not magic but they help:

| Companion | Planted Near | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Basil | Tomatoes | May repel aphids and hornworms, attracts pollinators |
| Marigolds (French) | Everywhere | Root exudates suppress soil nematodes, flowers attract beneficials |
| Onions/garlic | Carrots, brassicas | Strong scent confuses carrot fly and cabbage pests |
| Nasturtiums | Squash, beans | Trap crop — aphids and beetles go for nasturtiums first |
| Dill | Brassicas, tomatoes | Attracts parasitic wasps that eat caterpillars |
| Radishes | Squash | Trap crop for flea beetles |
| Borage | Tomatoes, squash | Attracts pollinators, may deter hornworms |

## Homemade Sprays

Use as a last resort after prevention, barriers, and hand-picking.

**Soap spray:** 1 tablespoon of pure liquid castile soap (not detergent) per quart of water. Spray directly on aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites. It breaks down the waxy coating on soft-bodied insects. Rinse plants after a few hours — soap can burn leaves in direct sun.

**Garlic spray:** Blend 4–5 garlic cloves in a quart of water. Strain through cloth. Add a few drops of soap as a surfactant. Spray on plants — the smell repels many chewing insects for a few days. Reapply after rain.

**Neem oil:** If you have access to it. Mix per label directions (usually 1–2 tablespoons per gallon of water with a drop of soap). Disrupts insect feeding and reproduction. Effective against a wide range of pests. Spray in the evening — neem breaks down in UV light and can harm bees if wet.

**Hot pepper spray:** Blend hot peppers in water, strain, add a drop of soap. Spray on plants to deter chewing insects and some mammals. Wear gloves and don't touch your eyes.

**Diatomaceous earth (DE):** Fine powder made from fossilized algae. Sprinkle around plant bases. It scratches the waxy coating on insects, causing them to dehydrate. Effective against slugs, ants, beetles, and many crawling pests. Must be reapplied after rain. Use food-grade only. Wear a mask when applying — don't breathe the dust.

> **WARNING:** Even organic sprays kill indiscriminately. Soap spray kills ladybugs just as easily as aphids. Neem affects beneficials too. Spray targeted and spray late in the day when pollinators are less active. Don't spray when plants are flowering if possible.

## Dealing with Larger Pests

**Deer:** 8-foot fence is the only reliable solution. Motion-activated noisemakers and scent deterrents (soap bars, human hair, predator urine) work temporarily — deer habituate to everything eventually. If fencing isn't possible, grow deer-resistant crops: garlic, onions, hot peppers, most herbs, rhubarb.

**Rabbits:** 36-inch wire fence, buried 6 inches with an L-shaped footer. Hardware cloth or welded wire with 1-inch openings max.

**Groundhogs:** Same fencing as rabbits but add an outward-leaning extension at the top or an electric wire at 4 inches above ground. They're strong diggers — bury fence deep.

**Voles and moles:** Voles eat roots and bulbs — line raised bed bottoms with hardware cloth. Moles eat grubs and worms (not plants) but their tunnels disturb roots. Reduce grub populations and mole activity usually drops.

**Birds:** Netting over berry bushes and newly seeded beds. Reflective tape or old CDs on strings provide temporary deterrent.

## Disease vs. Pests

Not every plant problem is a pest. Fungal diseases (powdery mildew, blight, rust) look different from insect damage:

- Insect damage: holes in leaves, chewed edges, visible insects or frass (droppings)
- Fungal disease: spots, powdery coating, wilting without holes, dark lesions on stems

For fungal problems: improve air circulation (space plants wider), water at the base instead of overhead, remove affected leaves immediately, and rotate crops. Baking soda spray (1 tablespoon per gallon of water plus a drop of soap) helps suppress powdery mildew on squash and cucumbers.
