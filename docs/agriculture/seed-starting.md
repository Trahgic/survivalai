Seeds are the most important supply in long-term survival. A $3 packet of tomato seeds produces enough fruit to feed a family for a season — and if you save seeds from that harvest, you never need to buy again. But seeds are also fragile. Store them wrong and they're dead. Start them wrong and they rot or grow into spindly junk that falls over in the first breeze.

## Starting Seeds Indoors

Starting indoors gives plants a head start before outdoor conditions are safe. Most vegetables need 4–8 weeks of indoor growth before transplanting.

### What You Need

- Containers: anything with drainage holes. Egg cartons, yogurt cups, cut-down milk jugs, plastic trays. Poke holes in the bottom if there aren't any.
- Growing medium: seed-starting mix is ideal (light, sterile, holds moisture). In a pinch, use sifted compost mixed 50/50 with sand or perlite. Don't use straight garden soil — it's too dense and carries disease.
- Light: a south-facing window works in a pinch, but seedlings really need 12–16 hours of direct light. Without enough light they stretch tall and thin ("leggy") trying to reach it. If you have power, a shop light hung 2–3 inches above the seedlings is cheap and effective.
- Warmth: most seeds germinate best between 65–85°F (18–30°C). On top of a refrigerator, near a wood stove, or on a heat mat if you have one.

### Step by Step

1. Fill containers with moist (not soggy) growing medium. Press it down lightly — firm enough to hold shape, loose enough for roots to push through.
2. Plant seeds at the depth listed on the packet. General rule: plant at a depth of 2–3 times the seed's diameter. Tiny seeds (lettuce, basil) just press into the surface — don't bury them.
3. Cover with a thin layer of medium or vermiculite. Mist the surface.
4. Cover the containers with plastic wrap, a plastic bag, or a clear lid to hold moisture. Remove it as soon as you see green.
5. Keep the medium moist but not wet. A spray bottle works better than pouring water, which displaces seeds. Bottom-watering (setting containers in a tray of water for 20 minutes) is even better.
6. Once seedlings emerge, get them into maximum light immediately. Rotate containers daily if using window light so they don't lean.
7. When seedlings have their second set of leaves (the "true leaves" — the first pair are seed leaves and look different), thin to one plant per cell. Cut extras at the base with scissors instead of pulling — pulling disturbs the roots of the one you're keeping.
8. Feed weekly with dilute compost tea or fish emulsion once true leaves appear. Seedlings in sterile starting mix have no nutrients to draw from.

### Common Problems

- **Leggy seedlings** (tall, thin, floppy): Not enough light. Move closer to light source or add hours. You can bury the stem deeper when transplanting — tomatoes especially root along buried stems.
- **Damping off** (seedlings keel over at soil line, stem looks pinched): Fungal disease from too much moisture and poor air circulation. Don't overwater. A small fan on low helps.
- **Seeds don't germinate:** Too cold, too old, planted too deep, or medium dried out. Check viability (see below).

## Hardening Off

Indoor seedlings are soft. Direct sun, wind, and temperature swings will kill them if you just stick them outside. Hardening off toughens them up over 7–10 days.

1. **Day 1–2:** Set seedlings outside in a sheltered, shady spot for 2–3 hours. Bring them back in.
2. **Day 3–4:** Increase to 4–5 hours. Introduce some morning sun (not midday).
3. **Day 5–6:** Full sun exposure for most of the day. Leave out longer.
4. **Day 7–8:** Leave outside overnight if nighttime temps are above 50°F (10°C) for most crops. Cold-hardy crops (brassicas, lettuce) can handle down to 40°F (4°C).
5. **Day 9–10:** Transplant into the garden. Water immediately and thoroughly.

If a cold snap or storm hits during hardening off, bring them back in. Better to delay than lose a flat of seedlings.

## Transplanting

1. Water seedlings thoroughly an hour before transplanting. Dry root balls tear when you pull them from containers.
2. Dig a hole slightly larger than the root ball.
3. Pop the seedling out of its container — push from the bottom, don't pull by the stem.
4. Set it in the hole at the same depth it was growing (exception: tomatoes — bury 2/3 of the stem, it grows roots along the buried section).
5. Fill soil around it, press gently to eliminate air pockets.
6. Water immediately at the base. Not overhead — wet leaves in direct sun can scorch.
7. If it's sunny, shade transplants for 2–3 days with a board propped at an angle, cardboard, or row cover. They need time to recover from root disturbance.

## Direct Sowing

Some crops don't transplant well and should go straight in the ground. These include: corn, beans, peas, carrots, beets, turnips, radishes, squash, melons, and cucumbers.

1. Wait until soil temperature is right. Cold soil rots seeds. Most warm-season crops need soil above 60°F (16°C). Stick your finger in 2 inches deep — if it feels cold, wait.
2. Plant at the depth on the packet. Large seeds (corn, beans) go 1–2 inches deep. Small seeds (carrots, lettuce) go 1/4 inch or just pressed into the surface.
3. Keep the soil surface moist until germination. This might mean daily watering in dry conditions. Once seedlings are up and growing, reduce to deep watering every few days.

## Seed Saving

This is how you become self-sufficient. One generation of saved seeds makes you independent of supply chains forever — as long as you do it right.

### Critical Rule: Open-Pollinated vs. Hybrid

- **Open-pollinated (OP) and heirloom** varieties breed true. Save seeds from these — the offspring will match the parent.
- **Hybrid (F1)** varieties are crosses between two different parent lines. Their seeds either won't germinate, will produce inferior plants, or will revert unpredictably. Don't waste time saving hybrid seeds.

Check the packet. If it says "F1" or "hybrid," don't save from it. If it says "heirloom," "OP," or "open-pollinated," you're good.

### Saving from Common Crops

**Tomatoes:**
1. Pick fully ripe fruit from your best plant.
2. Scoop out seeds and pulp into a jar with a splash of water.
3. Let it sit 2–3 days at room temperature. A mold layer will form on top — this fermentation breaks down the gel coating that inhibits germination.
4. Stir, pour off the mold and floating debris. Good seeds sink.
5. Rinse seeds in a strainer, spread on a plate (not paper towel — they stick), and dry for a week.

**Peppers:**
1. Let peppers fully ripen on the plant (past eating stage — they should be deeply colored and slightly wrinkled).
2. Cut open, scrape seeds onto a plate, dry for 1–2 weeks. No fermentation needed.

**Beans and peas:**
1. Leave pods on the plant until they're dry and brown and rattle when shaken.
2. Shell them. Spread on a screen and dry another week indoors.
3. Freeze for 48 hours to kill any bean weevil eggs, then store.

**Squash and melons:**
1. Let the fruit fully mature on the vine — winter squash should be hard-shelled, melons fully ripe.
2. Scoop out seeds, rinse off pulp, spread and dry for 1–2 weeks.
3. Warning: squash cross-pollinates easily between varieties. If you grew multiple types of the same species near each other, the saved seeds may produce something weird next year. Isolate varieties by 1/4 mile, or hand-pollinate and tape flowers shut.

**Lettuce and greens:**
1. Let one or two plants "bolt" (go to flower). Don't eat these — they'll be bitter.
2. Wait for seed heads to form and dry on the plant.
3. Shake heads into a paper bag. Separate chaff from seeds by gently blowing across a plate.

### Crops to Avoid Saving (Beginners)

- **Corn:** Wind-pollinated, crosses with any corn within a mile. Requires large populations (50+ plants) to maintain genetic diversity.
- **Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale):** Biennial — they don't make seeds until year two. Also cross-pollinate readily.
- **Carrots, beets, onions:** Biennial. Require overwintering the root and replanting for seed production.

Start with tomatoes, peppers, beans, peas, and lettuce. These are the easiest and most reliable.

## Seed Storage

Seeds are alive. They're just dormant. Heat, moisture, and light are what kill them in storage.

### Storage Rules

1. **Dry them thoroughly.** Seeds should snap when bent, not flex. Incompletely dried seeds mold in storage.
2. **Cool and dark.** A basement, root cellar, or the back of a closet works. Ideal temperature is 40–50°F (4–10°C). A refrigerator is great if you can keep humidity controlled.
3. **Airtight container.** Mason jars, ziplock bags with the air squeezed out, or vacuum-sealed bags. Throw in a small packet of silica gel or a tablespoon of powdered milk wrapped in tissue as a desiccant.
4. **Label everything.** Variety, date saved, any notes. Unlabeled seeds become mystery seeds within a month.

### Viability by Crop

Seeds don't last forever. Here's how long they're reliably viable under good storage conditions:

| Crop | Years Viable |
|------|-------------|
| Onion, parsnip, parsley | 1–2 |
| Corn, pepper, leek | 2–3 |
| Bean, pea, carrot, spinach | 3–4 |
| Tomato, brassicas, beet | 4–5 |
| Cucumber, melon, squash, lettuce, radish | 5–6 |

These are conservative numbers. Seeds stored in ideal conditions (cool, dry, dark) often last longer. Seeds stored in a hot shed or garage may die in a single summer.

### Germination Test

If you're not sure whether old seeds are still good:

1. Wet a paper towel, wring it out so it's damp but not dripping.
2. Place 10 seeds on the towel, fold it over, put it in an open ziplock bag.
3. Keep it warm (70–80°F / 21–27°C). Check daily and keep the towel moist.
4. After the expected germination time for that crop (usually 5–14 days), count how many sprouted.
5. If 7 out of 10 sprouted, you have 70% germination — still usable, just plant more densely. Below 50%, get fresh seeds if possible.

## Building a Seed Bank

If you're thinking long-term, build a collection of open-pollinated varieties adapted to your region.

1. Focus on calorie crops first: beans, corn, squash, potatoes (saved as tubers, not seeds), wheat, and root vegetables.
2. Add nutritional variety: tomatoes, peppers, greens, herbs.
3. Get varieties from local seed swaps or regional seed companies — they're already adapted to your climate.
4. Store duplicates in separate locations. One set in your house, one in a cache somewhere else. Fires, floods, and theft are real.
5. Rotate your stock. Grow out a portion each year, harvest fresh seed, replace the old stored seed. This keeps your bank genetically alive and viable.

> **Long-term thinking:** A well-maintained seed bank of 20–30 open-pollinated varieties, properly stored and rotated, can feed a family indefinitely. This is the single most valuable agricultural asset you can own when supply chains don't exist.
