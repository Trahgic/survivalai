Not every survival situation calls for livestock — they're a commitment. Animals need daily care, daily feed, shelter, and attention even when you're sick or exhausted. But the payoff is real: fresh protein, eggs, milk, manure for gardens, and eventually meat. These three animals are the most practical for small-scale survival situations because they're small, productive, and don't need a lot of land.

## Chickens

The best starter livestock. A flock of 6–8 hens gives you 4–6 eggs per day in peak season, pest control, and manure. They eat table scraps, bugs, weeds, and grain. They're hardy, relatively quiet (hens — roosters are loud), and manageable even for kids.

### Housing

1. **Space:** Minimum 4 square feet per bird inside the coop, 10 square feet per bird in an outdoor run. Overcrowding causes pecking, disease, and stress.
2. **Roosts:** Chickens sleep on elevated bars. Provide 8–10 inches of roost space per bird, set 2–4 feet off the ground. Round dowels or 2x4s with the wide side up work well.
3. **Nesting boxes:** One box per 3–4 hens. Set them lower than the roosts or the birds will sleep in the boxes and foul them. Size: about 12x12x12 inches, with a lip to keep bedding and eggs from rolling out.
4. **Ventilation:** Coops need airflow near the roof — ammonia from droppings builds up fast and causes respiratory disease. Don't seal the coop airtight even in winter. Provide vents near the roofline that don't create a draft on the roost.
5. **Predator proofing:** Hardware cloth (1/2 inch welded wire), not chicken wire — raccoons reach right through chicken wire. Bury wire 12 inches below ground or bend it outward in an L-shape to stop digging predators. Lock the coop at night.

### Feeding

- Commercial layer feed (16% protein) is ideal if available. About 1/4 pound per hen per day.
- Without commercial feed: mix cracked corn or grain (60%), protein source like dried insects, sunflower seeds, or soybean meal (20%), and greens/kitchen scraps (20%).
- Chickens need calcium for eggshells. Crush their used eggshells and feed them back, or provide crushed oyster shell in a separate dish.
- They need grit (small stones) to grind food in their gizzard. Free-range birds find their own. Confined birds need a dish of coarse sand or small gravel.
- Fresh clean water daily. A hen drinks about a pint a day, more in heat.

### Egg Production

Hens start laying at 18–24 weeks old. Production peaks in the first two years and slowly declines. A good laying breed (Rhode Island Red, Plymouth Rock, Leghorn) produces 250–300 eggs per year in year one.

Egg production drops in winter because it's triggered by daylight hours. Under 14 hours of light, laying slows or stops. You can add artificial light (a single low-watt bulb on a timer) to maintain production, but many people let hens rest in winter.

Eggs are safe to eat for 3–5 weeks without refrigeration if unwashed. The natural coating ("bloom") on a fresh egg seals the pores. Once washed, refrigerate or eat within a week.

### Breeding

You need a rooster to get fertile eggs. One rooster per 8–12 hens is plenty. If you don't want chicks, don't keep a rooster — hens lay fine without one.

To hatch chicks, you need a "broody" hen — one that wants to sit on eggs for 21 days. Some breeds go broody reliably (Orpingtons, Silkies), others almost never do (Leghorns). A broody hen does all the incubation work for you.

Without a broody hen, you need an incubator (99.5°F / 37.5°C, 50–65% humidity, eggs turned 3 times daily for 18 days, then stop turning for the last 3 days).

### Butchering Chickens

Most people butcher surplus roosters and older hens past laying age.

1. Don't feed the bird for 12–24 hours before slaughter (empties the crop and intestines, making processing cleaner).
2. Hold the bird upside down by the feet — this calms them. Place in a killing cone (a traffic cone with the bottom cut out works) head-down.
3. Pull the neck down, make a quick cut across both sides of the neck just below the jaw, severing the jugular veins and carotid arteries. Let bleed out completely — 1–2 minutes.
4. Dip in 145–150°F (63–65°C) water for 30–60 seconds to loosen feathers. Don't go hotter or the skin cooks and tears.
5. Pluck feathers starting from the breast, pulling in the direction of growth. The scald makes this easy if temperature was right.
6. Cut around the vent (cloaca) carefully. Make a horizontal cut across the belly below the breastbone. Reach in and pull out the entrails in one mass. Don't puncture the gallbladder (small green sac on the liver) — bile ruins any meat it touches.
7. Save the liver, heart, and gizzard (cut open the gizzard, peel out the lining and contents). Discard everything else or compost it.
8. Rinse thoroughly with clean water. Chill immediately if possible.

Older hens are tough — stew or pressure cook them. Young roosters (12–16 weeks) are tender enough for roasting.

## Rabbits

Rabbits are the most efficient meat animal for survival situations. They're quiet, small, breed fast, eat weeds and garden waste, and produce excellent manure. A trio (1 buck, 2 does) can produce 100+ pounds of meat per year.

### Housing

1. **Wire cages** raised off the ground are standard. Size: 30x36 inches for a doe with litter, 24x30 for a buck. All-wire floors let droppings fall through (easier cleaning, fewer diseases).
2. **Wire gauge:** 14-gauge for floors, 16-gauge for sides and tops. Floor openings: 1/2 x 1 inch (large enough for droppings, small enough for feet).
3. **Shade is critical.** Rabbits die from heat faster than cold. They can't sweat. Keep cages in shade, provide frozen water bottles in summer if temps exceed 85°F (30°C). Many rabbits die above 90°F (32°C).
4. In cold climates, they do fine down to about 20°F (-7°C) with a windbreak and dry bedding in the nesting box.
5. **Nesting box:** Provide a wooden box (18x10x10 inches) for does 3 days before kindling (giving birth). Fill with straw or hay.

### Feeding

- Hay (timothy, orchard grass, or meadow hay) should be the foundation — unlimited supply. Hay keeps the gut moving. A rabbit that stops eating hay is getting sick.
- Pellets (commercial rabbit feed, 16–18% protein) if available: 1/4 cup per 5 lbs body weight daily.
- Without pellets: fresh greens (dandelion, clover, plantain, grass), vegetable scraps, some grain. Introduce new greens slowly — sudden diet changes cause fatal digestive issues.
- Fresh water daily. A doe with a litter drinks up to a quart per day.
- **Never feed:** iceberg lettuce (almost no nutrition, causes diarrhea), potatoes, onions, rhubarb leaves, or any plant you're not sure about.

### Breeding

- Does are ready to breed at 5–6 months (medium breeds). Bucks at 6–7 months.
- Always bring the doe to the buck's cage, never the other way around — does are territorial and may attack.
- Breeding takes about 30 seconds. The buck falls off sideways when done. You'll know.
- Gestation: 28–32 days (usually 31). Average litter: 6–10 kits.
- The doe pulls fur from her belly to line the nest — this is normal.
- Don't handle kits for the first week unless one falls out of the nest (put it back — does don't carry babies).
- Kits start eating solid food at 3–4 weeks and can be weaned at 6–8 weeks.
- Re-breed the doe 2–4 weeks after weaning for sustainable production without exhausting her.
- Keep records: which doe, which buck, date bred, litter size, survival rate. Cull does that consistently have small litters or don't care for their young.

### Butchering Rabbits

Fryer age is 8–12 weeks (3–5 pounds live weight for medium breeds).

1. Hold the rabbit by the back legs. Strike firmly at the base of the skull with a heavy stick, or use cervical dislocation — grip the head, pull and twist sharply to break the neck.
2. Hang by the back legs. Cut the head off and let bleed out.
3. Cut the skin around both hind legs just below the hocks. Peel the skin downward like pulling off a sock — rabbit skin comes off easily.
4. Cut through the belly, remove entrails. Save the liver and kidneys if they look healthy (no spots, normal color).
5. Rinse and chill. Rabbit is lean and mild — similar to chicken. Don't rely on rabbit as your only meat source — it's too lean for sole-source nutrition (see "rabbit starvation" — you need fat from other sources).

> **WARNING:** Rabbit starvation (protein poisoning) is real. Rabbit meat is extremely lean — almost no fat. If rabbit is your only food source for weeks, you'll develop diarrhea, headache, and fatigue from protein overload without adequate fat. Always supplement with fat from other sources: cooking oils, other animals, nuts, or fatty fish.

## Goats

Goats are the most versatile larger livestock for homesteads. Dairy goats produce 1–3 quarts of milk daily. Meat goats grow fast on brush and weeds. They clear land, provide manure, and are tough enough to thrive in marginal conditions that would starve cattle.

### Breeds for Survival

- **Dairy:** Nigerian Dwarf (small, rich milk), Nubian (high butterfat), LaMancha, Alpine. A good doe produces 1–3 quarts per day for 10 months of the year.
- **Meat:** Boer (fast growth), Kiko, Spanish. Cross a Boer buck with dairy does for dual-purpose offspring.
- **General purpose:** Kinder (Nubian x Nigerian Dwarf cross), good for both milk and meat on a small frame.

### Housing

1. Goats need a dry shelter — they hate rain. A three-sided shed open to the south works in most climates. Minimum 20 square feet per goat.
2. Fencing is the biggest challenge. Goats test every fence constantly. Minimum 4-foot woven wire or cattle panels. Electric fencing works well if you have power. They'll go through, over, or under anything weaker.
3. Provide an elevated sleeping area if possible — goats prefer to sleep off the ground.
4. Keep bedding dry. Straw or wood shavings, cleaned regularly. Wet bedding causes hoof rot and respiratory problems.

### Feeding

- Goats are browsers, not grazers — they prefer weeds, brush, leaves, and bark over grass. They're excellent for clearing overgrown land.
- Provide good quality grass or legume hay (alfalfa for dairy does, grass hay for others). About 2–4% of body weight per day in forage/hay.
- Milking does need supplemental grain — about 1 pound of grain per 3 pounds of milk produced daily.
- Goats need loose minerals (not mineral blocks designed for cattle — goat mineral formulations have copper, which sheep can't have but goats need).
- Fresh water — a dairy doe drinks 2–4 gallons per day.
- **Poisonous plants:** Rhododendron, azalea, mountain laurel, cherry leaves (wilted — fresh are less toxic), and yew are all deadly to goats. Contrary to myth, goats don't eat everything, but they will try most things.

### Milking

1. Milk twice daily, 12 hours apart, for best production. Once daily is possible but production drops 30–40%.
2. Clean the udder with warm water and a clean cloth before milking.
3. Squeeze from the top of the teat downward, trapping milk in the teat with your thumb and forefinger, then squeezing with your lower fingers to express it. Don't pull — squeeze.
4. Strip each teat until empty. Incomplete milking leads to mastitis (udder infection).
5. Strain milk through a filter or clean cloth immediately. Chill as fast as possible.
6. Goat milk is naturally homogenized — the cream doesn't separate as easily as cow milk. It tastes slightly different from cow milk but most people adjust quickly.

### Breeding

- Does cycle every 18–21 days in fall and winter (they're seasonal breeders in temperate climates).
- Gestation: 145–155 days (about 5 months). Average: 1–3 kids.
- Does can be bred at about 8 months or 60–70% of adult weight, whichever comes later.
- Keep bucks separate except for breeding — they smell terrible (it's a gland thing, not poor hygiene) and the smell transfers to the milk if they're housed together.
- Kids can be weaned at 8–12 weeks. Dam-raise for simplicity, or bottle-raise for friendlier goats and maximum milk harvest.

### Common Health Issues

- **Hoof rot:** Trim hooves every 6–8 weeks. If they go soft, white, and smelly, trim back the affected tissue and treat with zinc sulfate solution or dilute copper sulfate. Keep bedding dry.
- **Internal parasites (worms):** The #1 health problem. Check the inner lower eyelid — bright pink/red is healthy, pale pink or white means anemia from worm load. Use the FAMACHA scoring system. Rotate pastures. Don't dose with dewormers on a fixed schedule — it breeds resistant worms. Only treat when parasite load is confirmed.
- **Bloat:** Sudden distension of the left side. Usually from too much grain or lush legume pasture. Walk the goat to encourage belching. In an emergency, dose with 1/4 cup vegetable oil. Severe bloat (can't breathe, left side hard as a drum) may require puncturing the rumen with a trocar or large-bore needle in the upper left flank — this is a last resort.
- **Mastitis:** Hot, hard udder, clumpy or off-color milk. Milk the affected side out completely, multiple times daily. Infuse the teat with intramammary antibiotic if available.

### Butchering Goats

Young goats (3–10 months) have the best meat. Older does and bucks are tougher and stronger-flavored.

Processing is similar to deer on a smaller scale. Hang, skin, eviscerate, and age 2–5 days in a cool (34–40°F / 1–4°C) environment if possible. The meat is lean — cook with moisture (stew, braise) or add fat.

## Which Animal to Start With

If you can only pick one: **chickens.** Lowest startup cost, easiest care, immediate return (eggs within 5–6 months), and manageable even for complete beginners.

If you have more space and someone with daily commitment: **add rabbits** for meat production. They breed fast enough to build up a significant protein supply within a year.

If you have pasture, fencing, and are thinking long-term: **add goats** for milk and land clearing. But don't start with goats if you've never kept animals — the learning curve is steeper and the consequences of mistakes are more expensive.
