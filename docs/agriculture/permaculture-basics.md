Permaculture is a design system for setting up land so it produces food, water, and resources with less and less work over time. The core idea: instead of fighting nature with annual tilling, planting, weeding, and watering — design a system that works like a forest, where plants support each other, water is captured and stored, and soil builds itself.

It's not a religion or a lifestyle brand. It's a practical toolkit for arranging plants, water, and structures so you spend less energy maintaining them.

## Zone Planning

Permaculture organizes your property into zones based on how often you visit each area. Things you check daily go closest to where you live. Things you rarely touch go farthest away.

**Zone 0 — The house.** Energy systems, food storage, rainwater collection off the roof. Design for efficiency: south-facing windows for passive solar, thermal mass for heat storage, greywater routing to gardens.

**Zone 1 — Right outside the door.** Herbs, salad greens, the kitchen garden, compost bin, chicken coop. Anything you visit every day. This zone gets the most attention and the best soil.

**Zone 2 — Intensive food production.** Larger vegetable beds, berry bushes, small fruit trees, beehives, main irrigation systems. Visited every day or two. Mulch heavily to reduce maintenance.

**Zone 3 — Farm scale.** Field crops (corn, wheat, potatoes), orchards, larger livestock pastures. Visited weekly. Designed for lower maintenance — cover crops, perennials, managed grazing.

**Zone 4 — Semi-wild.** Managed woodland, forage areas, firewood harvest, wild edibles, hunting grounds. Minimal intervention.

**Zone 5 — Wild.** Unmanaged natural area. Leave it alone. It provides habitat for beneficial insects and predators, erosion control, and a seed bank for wild plants.

Not every property has all five zones. A suburban lot might only have Zones 0–2. The point is to put the most labor-intensive things closest to where you spend time.

## Water Management

Water is the most important element to design for. Capture it high, store it, slow it down, spread it across the landscape.

### Swales

A swale is a shallow ditch dug along a contour line (level across the slope). Water collects in the ditch during rain, soaks into the ground instead of running off, and hydrates the soil downhill.

1. Find the contour: Use an A-frame level (two sticks joined at the top with a plumb line) to mark a level line across the slope.
2. Dig a trench along this line — 12–18 inches deep, 18–24 inches wide.
3. Pile the excavated dirt on the downhill side to form a berm.
4. Plant trees and perennials on the berm. Their roots drink from the stored water.
5. The swale fills during rain, drains slowly into the surrounding soil over days.

On a steep slope, build multiple swales every 15–30 feet of elevation change. On a gentle slope, one or two swales may be enough.

### Rainwater Harvesting

Every 1,000 square feet of roof captures roughly 600 gallons per inch of rain. A modest roof in a moderate climate can collect thousands of gallons per year.

See the **rainwater-harvesting.md** article in the water section for detailed collection and storage methods.

### Keyline Design

For larger properties: keyline plowing follows specific patterns across the landscape to move water from wet valleys to dry ridges, evening out moisture across the whole property. It requires understanding your land's topography, but the payoff is dramatically more even soil moisture without irrigation.

## Guilds

A guild is a group of plants that support each other, mimicking natural plant communities. The classic example:

### Apple Tree Guild

- **Apple tree** (or any fruit tree) — the central producer
- **Comfrey** planted at the drip line — deep taproots mine minerals from subsoil, huge leaves make excellent mulch when chopped, attracts pollinators
- **Clover or vetch** as groundcover — fixes nitrogen, living mulch
- **Chives or garlic** in a ring around the trunk — repels borers, deters some pests
- **Dill or fennel** nearby — attracts predatory wasps that eat codling moth larvae
- **Nasturtiums** — trap crop for aphids (they eat the nasturtiums instead of the tree)

Every plant has a job. Nothing is decorative. The tree produces fruit, the companions fix nitrogen, mine nutrients, repel pests, attract pollinators, and suppress weeds.

You can build guilds around any central productive plant — nut trees, berry bushes, grape vines. The principles are the same: nitrogen fixer, dynamic accumulator (deep-rooted nutrient miner), pest repellent, pollinator attractor, groundcover.

## Sheet Mulching and Soil Building

Permaculture rarely tills. Instead, soil is built from the top down using sheet mulching (detailed in **soil-preparation.md**). Layers of cardboard, compost, and mulch smother weeds and create rich planting soil over one to two seasons.

Once established, permaculture beds are maintained by "chop and drop" — cutting plants back and leaving the material where it falls as mulch. Comfrey is the star here: you can cut it 4–5 times per growing season and each cutting decomposes into nutrient-rich mulch within weeks.

## Perennial vs. Annual Thinking

Annual crops (tomatoes, corn, beans) need replanting every year. They need tilled soil, lots of fertilizer, and constant attention.

Perennial crops produce year after year with minimal input once established:

- **Fruit trees:** Apple, pear, plum, cherry, peach — 5–15 years to full production but then produce for decades
- **Nut trees:** Walnut, chestnut, hazelnut, pecan — slower start but massive long-term calorie producers
- **Berry bushes:** Blueberry, raspberry, blackberry, currant, gooseberry — producing within 1–3 years
- **Perennial vegetables:** Asparagus (20+ year lifespan), rhubarb, Jerusalem artichoke, sorrel, walking onions
- **Herbs:** Rosemary, thyme, oregano, sage, mint, chives — plant once, harvest for years

A smart long-term strategy: grow annual gardens for immediate food while establishing perennial plantings that take over food production in 3–10 years. By year 10, a well-designed perennial system produces significant calories with a fraction of the labor.

## Stacking Functions

Every element in a permaculture system should serve at least two functions:

- A pond: stores water, grows fish, moderates temperature, attracts wildlife, irrigates downhill gardens
- A chicken coop: produces eggs, produces manure (fertilizer), controls insects, turns compost
- A hedge: windbreak, wildlife habitat, privacy, firewood, fruit or nut production
- A greenhouse attached to the house: grows food, heats the house in winter through the shared wall

If something only does one thing, ask if it can do more — or if something else can replace it while doing multiple jobs.

## Getting Started With Permaculture

You don't need to redesign your whole property at once. Start with one project and expand:

1. **Observe for a full year first.** Watch where water flows during rain. Note where sun hits in each season. See where wind comes from. Observe what's already growing well. Don't make permanent changes until you understand the land.
2. **Start closest to the house (Zone 1).** Build one guild around a fruit tree. Sheet mulch one garden bed. Set up rainwater collection from one downspout.
3. **Plant perennials early.** Trees and bushes take years to produce. The best time to plant them was 5 years ago. The second best time is now.
4. **Build soil everywhere.** Mulch, compost, cover crops. Whatever you do, stop leaving bare soil exposed.
5. **Connect systems.** Route kitchen greywater to a fruit tree. Put the compost bin where it's easy to reach from both the kitchen and the garden. Let chickens clean up the garden after harvest.

> **The real payoff is time.** A conventional garden takes roughly the same amount of work every year — plowing, planting, weeding, watering, repeat. A permaculture system takes more work upfront but less work each subsequent year as the perennials establish, the soil improves, and the water systems do their job. By year 5–7, a well-designed system needs a fraction of the maintenance of a traditional garden for comparable or greater output.
