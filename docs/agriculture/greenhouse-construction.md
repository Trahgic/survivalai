A greenhouse extends your growing season by weeks or months on each end. In cold climates, the difference between a 90-day season and a 180-day season is the difference between subsistence and surplus. You don't need fancy materials — a basic hoop house made from bent pipe and plastic sheeting works.

## Cold Frames (Simplest Build)

A cold frame is a bottomless box with a transparent lid. It's a mini greenhouse that sits right on the ground over a garden bed.

### Construction

1. Build a rectangular frame from lumber, cinder blocks, straw bales, or any solid material. Size: 3–4 feet deep (front to back), whatever length you want.
2. Make the back wall 4–6 inches taller than the front. This creates an angled top that faces south, catching more winter sun.
3. Set an old window, storm door panel, or clear plastic sheeting stretched over a frame as the lid. Hinge it at the back so it opens for ventilation.
4. Place the cold frame against a south-facing wall if possible — the wall acts as thermal mass, absorbing heat during the day and releasing it at night.

### Using a Cold Frame

- Start cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, radishes, kale) 4–6 weeks earlier than normal in spring.
- In fall, plant a last round of greens inside the cold frame. They'll produce well into December or beyond in moderate climates.
- Open the lid when daytime temps exceed 50°F (10°C) inside — heat buildup can cook plants fast even in winter. Prop it with a stick. Close it before evening.
- A jug of water inside acts as thermal mass — absorbs heat during the day and releases it at night.

## Hoop House (Best Balance of Cost and Function)

A hoop house (also called a high tunnel or polytunnel) is a series of arched hoops covered in greenhouse plastic. No heating, no electrical required. It's the workhorse of season extension.

### Materials for a 10x20 Foot Hoop House

- 10 pieces of 1/2-inch or 3/4-inch EMT electrical conduit, 10 feet long (or 3/4-inch PVC — cheaper but less durable)
- 20 pieces of 2-foot rebar (1/2 inch diameter) for ground anchors
- 1 roll of 6-mil greenhouse plastic (UV-stabilized — regular poly degrades in one season), at least 16 feet wide and 24 feet long
- Lumber for baseboards: 2x6 or 2x8, 20 feet and 10 feet lengths
- Wiggle wire channel and wire (or wooden lath strips and screws) to attach plastic to frame
- Clamps, zip ties, or strap material

### Step by Step

1. **Layout:** Mark a 10x20 foot rectangle. Level the ground as much as possible.
2. **Baseboards:** Lay 2x6 lumber along both 20-foot sides, staked to the ground. This anchors the structure and provides an attachment point for the plastic.
3. **Ground anchors:** Drive 2-foot rebar stakes 12 inches into the ground along both sides, every 4 feet, right next to the baseboards. You should have about 12 inches of rebar sticking up.
4. **Bend the hoops:** Slide one end of a 10-foot conduit over a rebar stake on one side. Walk to the other side and bend the conduit into an arch, sliding the other end over the opposite rebar stake. Repeat for each pair.
5. **Ridge pole:** Run a straight piece of conduit along the top of all the hoops (the ridge). Attach with zip ties, wire, or conduit clamps. This prevents the hoops from swaying.
6. **Purlins (optional but recommended):** Run additional straight pieces of conduit along each side at about 3–4 feet high. These stiffen the whole structure against wind.
7. **Cover with plastic:** Drape the greenhouse plastic over the hoops on a calm day. Pull it taut. Attach to the baseboards using wiggle wire channel (a metal strip that wedges the plastic into a C-channel) or by sandwiching the plastic between the baseboard and a lath strip, then screwing through. Leave excess at both ends.
8. **End walls:** Frame end walls from scrap lumber. Cover with plastic. Leave one end as a door — build a simple frame and staple plastic to it, or hang a heavy tarp flap. Good ventilation matters — add a door at both ends if possible.

### PVC vs. Conduit vs. Cattle Panels

- **PVC pipe:** Cheapest. Bends easily. Degrades in UV light within 3–5 years (paint it white to help). Breaks in heavy snow or wind if undersized. Use Schedule 40, 3/4 inch minimum.
- **EMT conduit:** Galvanized steel. Lasts 15+ years. Stronger in wind and snow. Harder to bend without a conduit bender but 10-foot lengths flex into a natural arch.
- **Cattle panels (16-foot welded wire panels):** Very sturdy. Bend them into an arch and anchor the feet. Cover with plastic. Handles heavy snow loads. More expensive but bombproof.

### Ventilation

This is the thing most first-time builders get wrong. A closed hoop house on a sunny day — even a sunny winter day — can exceed 100°F (38°C) inside. Plants cook.

1. Open doors at both ends for cross-ventilation whenever daytime temps inside exceed 75°F (24°C).
2. Roll up the sides: build the plastic attachment so you can roll the side walls up 2–3 feet on warm days. This is the easiest and most effective ventilation method.
3. In summer, you may need shade cloth (30–50% shade rating) draped over the top.

## Passive Solar Greenhouse (Year-Round in Cold Climates)

A passive solar greenhouse uses thermal mass and insulation to maintain temperatures without any heating system. It's more work to build but can grow food through hard winters in Zone 4–5.

### Design Principles

1. **South-facing glazing:** The entire south wall is transparent (glass, polycarbonate, or double-layer greenhouse plastic). This catches maximum winter sun.
2. **Insulated north, east, and west walls:** These walls are solid and insulated — framed with plywood and stuffed with fiberglass, straw bales, or rigid foam. The north wall is often painted dark or stacked with water containers for thermal mass.
3. **Thermal mass:** Dark-colored water containers (55-gallon drums painted black, rows of water-filled jugs) along the north wall absorb heat during the day and release it at night. One gallon of water stores about 8 BTUs per degree Fahrenheit of temperature change.
4. **Angled glazing:** The south-facing glass should be angled perpendicular to the winter sun's angle for maximum solar gain. In the northern US (40° latitude), that's about 50–55 degrees from horizontal.
5. **Insulated floor or raised beds:** Cold ground sucks heat out of the air. Insulate the floor perimeter with rigid foam, or use raised beds that sit above the cold ground.

### Temperature Performance

A well-built passive solar greenhouse in Zone 5 (winter lows of -20°F / -29°C) can typically maintain interior temperatures above 32°F (0°C) on most nights without supplemental heat. On sunny winter days, interior temps often reach 60–70°F (16–21°C).

This is enough for cold-hardy greens: lettuce, spinach, kale, chard, Asian greens, carrots, beets, and herbs. Not enough for tomatoes or peppers without adding heat.

### Supplemental Heat (When Needed)

- Compost pile inside the greenhouse generates steady warmth — a 4x4x4 foot actively composting pile can raise greenhouse temps 5–10°F.
- Rocket mass heater: burns small wood very efficiently, stores heat in a thermal mass bench. Burns 80% less wood than a regular wood stove. See **fire** section articles for construction principles.
- Simple wood stove as last resort. Effective but requires venting through the wall and fire monitoring.

## What to Grow in an Unheated Greenhouse

### Spring (4–8 Weeks Before Last Frost)

Start warm-season transplants inside: tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, squash. These go into the garden after last frost.

Direct sow cool crops in greenhouse beds: lettuce, spinach, radishes, peas, Asian greens.

### Summer

Most people open the greenhouse fully or use it for heat-loving crops that struggle outdoors: tomatoes, peppers, and cucumbers often produce better under cover because of the consistent warmth.

### Fall (After First Frost)

Plant a second round of cool-season greens in September (Northern Hemisphere). The greenhouse protects them from frost while day length remains long enough for growth.

### Winter

Growth slows dramatically as day length drops below 10 hours. Plants go semi-dormant. But crops planted in September and October will hold in the greenhouse through winter and can be harvested fresh even when everything outside is frozen and dead.

Hardy picks for winter greenhouse growing: mâche (corn salad), claytonia (miner's lettuce), spinach, kale, tatsoi, scallions, carrots (planted in summer, harvested through winter).

## Maintenance

- Replace plastic sheeting every 3–5 years (UV degrades it even with UV-stabilized film).
- Check for rips and patch with greenhouse repair tape.
- Clean glazing annually — dirt blocks light.
- Reinforce before winter storms. Heavy wet snow is the main killer of hoop houses — keep a broom nearby to sweep snow off the roof before it accumulates.
- Check ground anchors annually. Frost heave can lift rebar. Re-drive if needed.

> **Cost perspective:** A basic 10x20 hoop house costs roughly $200–400 in materials and a weekend of labor. A passive solar greenhouse of the same size runs $500–1,500 depending on materials. Both pay for themselves in a single season through extended production and earlier harvests.
