A fireplace with a working chimney transforms a shelter from a smoky survival hut into a livable home. The principles are simple — hot air rises, pulling smoke up and out — but the details matter. A poorly built chimney fills your shelter with smoke or catches fire.

## How Chimneys Work

Hot combustion gases are lighter than cool air. The temperature difference between inside the chimney and outside creates draft — suction that pulls air through the fire and smoke up the chimney. Taller chimneys draft better. Wider flues draft worse (counterintuitive — the gas cools too fast and loses buoyancy).

## Simple Stone/Mud Fireplace

1. Build the firebox against an exterior wall. Interior dimensions: 24-36 inches wide, 18-24 inches deep, 24-30 inches tall. The wider the firebox relative to its depth, the more heat radiates into the room.
2. The back wall should angle forward slightly starting halfway up. This reflects heat outward into the room.
3. Build walls from stone, brick, or cob. Line the interior with stone or firebrick if possible — regular clay bricks crack from direct flame.
4. The smoke shelf: at the top of the firebox opening, create a flat horizontal shelf 4-6 inches deep before the flue narrows. This catches downdrafts and prevents cold air from pushing smoke into the room.
5. Above the smoke shelf, narrow to the flue opening — roughly 1/10 to 1/12 of the firebox opening area.

## Chimney Construction

1. The flue (interior opening) should be roughly 8×8 inches to 12×12 inches for a residential-sized fireplace.
2. Build with stone, brick, or cob. Minimum wall thickness: 4 inches for stone/brick, 8 inches for cob.
3. The chimney must extend at least 2 feet above the roof peak or any structure within 10 feet. Short chimneys draft poorly and create fire risk.
4. Line the interior with a smooth clay wash — rough surfaces collect creosote and impede draft.
5. Install a damper (flat stone or metal plate) at the throat (where firebox meets flue) to close the chimney when not in use. Prevents heat loss.

## Improvised Chimney (Mud and Sticks)

For quick shelters where stone isn't available:

1. Build a frame of green (wet) sticks in a square or round cross-section above the fire.
2. Plaster thickly with mud and clay — minimum 3 inches thick. The wet wood won't burn because the mud insulates it, and by the time the mud dries and hardens, the stick framework is encased.
3. Apply mud in layers, letting each layer partially dry before adding the next.
4. These chimneys work but are temporary — they crack and need constant maintenance. Inspect for cracks before every use. A crack in a mud-and-stick chimney is a house fire waiting to happen.

> Clean your chimney regularly. Creosote (the tar-like buildup from wood smoke) is highly flammable. Chimney fires burn at 2000°F and can destroy the chimney and ignite the roof. Burn hot, dry wood to minimize creosote. A bundle of burning pine branches pushed through the flue cleans creosote in an emergency.

## Rocket Mass Heater (Most Efficient Design)

A rocket mass heater burns a small amount of wood at extreme efficiency and stores heat in a thermal mass bench.

1. Feed tube: vertical or angled tube where you insert wood sticks vertically.
2. Burn tunnel: horizontal, insulated with firebrick or cob. The fire burns at the junction of feed and tunnel.
3. Heat riser: vertical insulated column. Combustion gases get so hot they undergo secondary combustion — burning the smoke itself. Exhaust should be nearly clear.
4. Barrel/bell: an inverted metal barrel or dome captures the rising heat and radiates it.
5. Mass bench: exhaust runs through a long horizontal duct embedded in a cob bench before exiting through a low chimney. The bench absorbs and slowly releases heat for 12-24 hours after a 2-hour fire.

Rocket mass heaters use 80-90% less wood than an open fireplace. The downside: they're complex to build, require precise proportions, and need more space.

## Safety Rules

- Never burn pressure-treated lumber, painted wood, or plastics. The fumes are toxic.
- Keep all flammable materials 36 inches from the fireplace opening.
- Install a spark screen (wire mesh) across the fireplace opening.
- Always have a damper and use it — an open chimney in a heated cabin acts like a vacuum, sucking all your warm air out.
- Carbon monoxide kills without warning. Always ensure adequate ventilation. If anyone gets a headache and drowsiness near the fire, ventilate immediately.
