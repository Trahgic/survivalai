A log cabin is the classic long-term wilderness structure. Two people with axes can build a small livable cabin in 1-2 weeks. The key skills are tree selection, notching, and chinking.

## Site Preparation

1. Pick level ground at least 50 feet from water (flooding, mosquitoes). Slight slope for drainage.
2. Clear a footprint at least 2 feet larger than your cabin on each side.
3. Remove topsoil and vegetation from the footprint down to mineral soil.
4. Level the ground. Use a water level (clear tube filled with water between two sticks) if you don't have a proper level.

## Foundation

Do NOT set logs directly on soil — they rot in 2-3 years.

1. Place large flat rocks at each corner and every 4-6 feet along the walls. These are your piers.
2. First course (sill logs) sits on the rocks. Use your largest, straightest, most rot-resistant logs for sill logs. Cedar, locust, and oak are best for ground contact.
3. Sill logs should be at least 6 inches above ground level.

## Log Selection and Preparation

Ideal logs: 8-12 inch diameter, straight, consistent taper, no major branches on the lower trunk. Softwoods (pine, spruce, fir) are easier to work but rot faster. Hardwoods (oak, ash) are harder to cut but last longer.

1. Fall trees, limb them, and peel bark immediately. Bark traps moisture and harbors insects.
2. Let logs dry 2-4 weeks before building if possible. Green logs shrink as they dry, opening gaps.
3. Cut to length: wall logs should extend 12-18 inches past the corner notch on each end (overhang).

## Notching

The notch locks logs together at corners. Several types exist — saddle notch is the most forgiving for beginners.

**Saddle notch:**
1. Place the new log on top of the wall at the corner, perpendicular to the log below.
2. With the log sitting in position, use a compass or scribe to trace the shape of the lower log onto the bottom of the upper log. If you don't have a scribe, eyeball a semicircle.
3. Roll the upper log over. Chop and carve the traced semicircle out of the bottom of the log. The notch should be roughly half the diameter deep.
4. Set the log back in place. It should sit snugly on the lower log. Fine-tune with an axe or chisel.
5. The notch should face DOWN to shed water. Water pooling in notches causes rot.

## Raising Walls

1. Alternate butt ends and tip ends of logs at each corner to keep walls level (logs taper).
2. Use ramps (two poles leaned against the wall) to roll heavy logs up as walls get higher.
3. Pin logs vertically every 4-6 feet with wooden dowels (18-inch hardwood pegs driven through pre-drilled holes) to prevent shifting.
4. Door and window openings: leave gaps in the log courses. Cut the opening after walls are up. Frame with vertical boards spiked into the log ends, leaving 2 inches at the top for settling.

## Chinking and Daubing

Gaps between logs must be sealed against wind and rain.

1. Chinking: stuff gaps with moss, grass, strips of wood, rope, or fiberglass insulation if available. This is the bulk filler.
2. Daubing: seal over the chinking with a mud/clay mixture. Recipe: 1 part clay, 1 part sand, handful of grass or straw (fiber reinforcement). Mix with water to a thick paste.
3. Apply daubing 1/2 inch thick over the chinking. Press firmly into gaps.
4. Daubing cracks as it dries and needs reapplication. Two coats minimum. Adding a small amount of lime (wood ash soaked in water) makes it more durable.

## Roof

The simplest roof is a ridge-and-purlin design:

1. Ridge pole: heaviest log, runs the length of the cabin at the peak. Support on gable-end walls built up in a triangle.
2. Purlins: logs running from ridge to side walls, spaced 18-24 inches apart.
3. Covering: split shakes (hand-split wood shingles), bark sheets, or thatch layered from eaves upward.
4. Minimum roof pitch: 30 degrees for rain shedding, 45 degrees for heavy snow.

## Floor

Packed earth floor works fine. For a wood floor, lay split-log halves flat-side up on sleeper logs running perpendicular. Pin or notch in place. A wood floor is warmer but requires more effort.

## Realistic Expectations

A 12×14 foot single-room cabin takes 60-80 logs and roughly 100-200 hours of labor for two experienced people. A first attempt takes longer. The cabin will settle 2-4 inches in the first year as logs dry. Build doors and windows with settling gaps.
