Earthen construction uses the ground under your feet as building material. Structures built this way have sheltered humans for 10,000 years and still house a third of the world's population. The materials are free, fireproof, excellent thermal mass, and require no specialized tools.

## Testing Your Soil

Not all dirt works. You need clay content between 15-30%.

Jar test: fill a clear jar 1/3 full of soil, add water to the top, shake vigorously, and let settle for 24 hours. Sand settles in seconds (bottom layer), silt in hours (middle), clay stays suspended longest (top). If the clay layer is less than 15% of the total, add clay. Over 30%, add sand.

Ribbon test: wet a handful of soil, roll into a ball, squeeze between thumb and fingers into a flat ribbon. If the ribbon extends 2+ inches before breaking, you have enough clay.

## Cob (Monolithic Sculpted Walls)

Cob is a mixture of clay-rich soil, sand, straw, and water built up in courses like a giant clay sculpture.

Mix recipe: roughly 1 part clay soil, 2-3 parts sand, straw mixed throughout. The final mix should hold together when squeezed but not be sticky.

1. Mix on a tarp: pile soil and sand, add water, stomp and fold with feet. Add straw gradually — it should poke out in all directions.
2. Form into loaves (cobs) about the size of a football.
3. Build walls on a stone or rubble foundation (cob must never sit on bare ground — moisture wicks up and destroys it).
4. Place cobs in courses, smooshing each one into the previous course. Knit them together with your fingers — no mortar needed.
5. Build 12-18 inches per day maximum. Let each course firm up overnight before adding more.
6. Wall thickness: minimum 12 inches for a non-load-bearing wall, 18-24 inches for structural walls.
7. Form window and door openings as you build by placing temporary wooden frames.
8. Trim and sculpt walls with a machete or flat spade as they firm up.
9. Total drying time: 4-8 weeks depending on thickness and climate.

> Cob's worst enemy is water. Protect walls with roof overhangs (minimum 12 inches), raised foundations, and lime plaster or earthen plaster on exterior surfaces.

## Adobe (Sun-Dried Bricks)

Adobe bricks are standardized, stackable, and faster to build with than cob.

1. Build a brick mold from wood: 4×10×14 inches is traditional. Open top and bottom.
2. Mix same recipe as cob but wetter — should pour into the mold.
3. Fill mold on flat ground, strike off the top level, lift the mold straight up.
4. Dry bricks in the sun for 3-5 days, flipping once. Fully cured in 2-4 weeks.
5. Lay bricks with mud mortar (same mix without straw, thinner consistency).
6. Stagger joints like standard brickwork. Walls are typically 2 bricks thick (minimum 10 inches).
7. Place a wooden bond beam (continuous beam of wood) on top of the walls to distribute roof load.

Adobe production rate: one person can make 50-80 bricks per day. A small building needs 500-1000 bricks.

## Rammed Earth

Strongest earthen method. Soil is compacted in forms to create dense, concrete-like walls.

1. Build formwork: two parallel boards (plywood or planks) held apart by spacers at the desired wall thickness (minimum 12 inches). Braced firmly.
2. Fill with slightly damp soil (same mix minus straw) in 4-6 inch lifts.
3. Compact each lift with a rammer (heavy flat-bottomed log, 20-30 lbs) until the soil rings and won't compress further.
4. Repeat lifts until the form is full. Remove formwork after 24 hours, move up.
5. Rammed earth walls are extremely strong (1000+ PSI) and last centuries.

## Plaster and Finish

Bare earthen walls erode in rain. Protect with plaster:

- Earthen plaster: fine clay soil, sand, chopped straw. Apply 1/2 inch thick. Needs reapplication every 1-3 years.
- Lime plaster: slaked lime, sand, optional animal hair for fiber. More durable, more waterproof. Apply in 2-3 thin coats.
- Whitewash: slaked lime and water. Cheapest waterproofing. Reapply annually.

## Thermal Performance

Earthen walls have excellent thermal mass — they absorb heat during the day and release it at night. In hot climates, thick earthen walls keep interiors 15-20°F cooler than outside. In cold climates, they store heat from fire or sun exposure. They are not insulating by themselves — in very cold climates, add an insulating layer (straw bales, earth-sheltering) on the exterior.
