In a post-disaster environment, your supplies make you a target. Perimeter security buys you the most valuable commodity: time to react. Even simple alarm systems give you seconds to minutes of warning that turn a surprise into a prepared response.

## Layered Defense Concept

Think in concentric rings:

1. **Outer ring (300+ feet):** Observation and early warning. Trip alarms, noise makers, cleared sight lines.
2. **Middle ring (100-300 feet):** Obstacles that slow approach. Thorny brush, tangled wire, ditches, blocked paths.
3. **Inner ring (0-100 feet):** Fortified living space. Reinforced doors, window shutters, safe room.

You don't need all three layers on day one. Start with early warning and work inward.

## Improvised Trip Alarms

**Can alarm:** String or fishing line 6-8 inches above ground between trees or posts. Attach empty cans with pebbles inside. When the line is disturbed, the cans rattle. Simple, effective, weatherproof.

**Mouse trap alarm:** Attach a firecracker or cap gun cap to a mouse trap. Rig the trip wire to the trigger bar. When pulled, the snap ignites the cap. Extremely loud. Use sparingly — you need to rearm after each trigger.

**Chemical light alarm:** Attach a chemical light stick (glow stick) to a tripwire with the activation bend point at the wire. When pulled, the stick bends and activates, marking the intruder's location with light.

**Electronic:** A battery-powered driveway motion alarm (available at hardware stores) has a 100+ foot wireless range, runs on batteries for months, and is weather-resistant. Stock a few and batteries. These are the best value early warning device that exists.

## Sight Lines and Fields of View

Clear brush, tall grass, and concealment within 100 feet of your structures. This is called a "clear zone." An approaching person should be visible for at least 5-10 seconds before reaching your walls.

Position lookout points where you can observe approaches without being seen. A second-floor window, a tree stand, or an elevated platform gives enormous advantage.

## Night Security

Most threats approach at night. Options:

- Motion-activated solar lights at approaches (requires pre-stocking or salvaging)
- Dogs — the best motion sensor ever invented. Even a small dog's hearing and alertness outperforms any electronic system.
- Noise discipline — your group stays quiet at night so ambient sounds (footsteps, snapping twigs) are audible.
- Watch rotation: 2-4 hour shifts. The person on watch stays awake, stays quiet, stays in a covered position with a view of the most likely approach.

## Obstacle Placement

Slow people down without actively harming them (legal and ethical considerations apply even in emergencies):

- Tangle foot: wire, rope, or fishing line strung at ankle height in a web pattern across approach routes. Not visible at night, causes tripping and noise.
- Thorn barriers: plant osage orange, hawthorn, or wild rose in hedge rows. Takes a season to establish but creates an impenetrable wall.
- Ditch: a 3-foot-deep, 4-foot-wide trench is a significant obstacle, especially at night.
- Vehicle barriers: disabled vehicles, large logs, earth berms across road approaches.

## Communication Within the Group

Establish challenge and password for night encounters: "Thunder" → "Lightning" (response). Change daily. Anyone who can't give the correct response is treated as unknown.

Pre-arranged signals: flashlight patterns (2 short flashes = all clear, 3 rapid = alert), whistle codes (1 blast = attention, 3 blasts = emergency).

> The goal of perimeter security is NOT to stop a determined assault. It's to give you enough warning to wake up, arm up, gather your people, and decide whether to defend, negotiate, or withdraw. Seconds of warning save lives.
