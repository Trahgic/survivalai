Concealment keeps you alive when you can't fight or don't want to be found. The human eye evolved to spot humans — your shape, movement, shine, and color all betray you. Effective camouflage defeats all four.

## The Five S's of Detection

**Shape:** The human silhouette is instantly recognizable. Break your outline with irregular attachments — branches, cloth strips, netting with local vegetation woven in. Avoid standing on ridgelines or hilltops where your shape is silhouetted against the sky.

**Shine:** Anything reflective — glasses, watch faces, knife blades, phone screens, buckles. Tape, mud, or cloth over all reflective surfaces. Skin reflects light — use mud, charcoal, or face paint on exposed skin.

**Shadow:** You cast a shadow. Stay in shadows rather than stepping into sunlight. Under overhead concealment (trees, overhangs), your shadow is absorbed. In open ground, move when clouds cover the sun.

**Silhouette:** Against sky, water, or open ground your outline is obvious. Stay low. Crawl on ridgelines. Move through the base of terrain features, not across their tops.

**Movement:** The eye is wired to detect motion before shape or color. Move slowly. Stop frequently. When you stop, freeze completely — don't fidget. Use available cover to mask your movement. Cross open ground quickly and at irregular intervals, never at a steady pace.

## Natural Camouflage

1. Study the local palette: is the dominant color green, brown, gray, or mixed? Match it.
2. Mud, charcoal, and plant stains on skin and clothing. Apply in irregular patches, not solid coats. Dark colors in hollows (eye sockets, under jaw), lighter on prominent features (forehead, nose, cheekbones). This flattens the 3D appearance of your face.
3. Attach local vegetation to a hat, pack, and shoulders. Replace every few hours as plants wilt and change color.
4. A ghillie-style concealment: tie strips of burlap, old fabric, or natural cordage to a net or mesh worn over clothing. Weave in local vegetation. The ragged texture breaks your outline completely.

## Camp Concealment

- No fires during daylight in areas where you might be observed. Even a small fire produces visible smoke that carries for miles.
- At night, shield fire from all directions with screens or dig a Dakota hole (fire below ground level). Use hardwood for low-smoke fuel.
- Avoid disturbing natural ground cover around your camp. Walk on rocks and hard surfaces. Don't create trails with repeated foot traffic.
- Latrine, fire, and water collection should be approached from different directions.
- Remove all trash and signs of occupation if you move on. Discipline about ground sign is the difference between an invisible camp and an obvious one.

## Observation Without Being Observed

- Use terrain to mask your approach: gullies, tree lines, walls, brush.
- Observe from an unexpected position. If you were looking for someone, where would you NOT look? That's where you should be.
- Binoculars reflect light. Cup your hands around the lenses. Observe from shadow.
- At night, observe from the lowest point. Silhouettes are visible against the sky from below, but a low observer is nearly invisible.
