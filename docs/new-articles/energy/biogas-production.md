Biogas is methane produced by bacteria digesting organic material in an oxygen-free environment. It burns clean enough to cook with, run a generator, or fuel a gas lamp. The inputs are things you'd otherwise discard — manure, food scraps, crop waste.

## How It Works

Anaerobic bacteria break down organic material in a sealed container, producing a mix of methane (50-70%) and carbon dioxide (30-50%). The process takes 15-40 days depending on temperature. Warmer = faster. Below 60°F, production drops dramatically.

## Simple Drum Digester

1. Get two 55-gallon drums. One fits inside the other (or use one drum and a large water-filled trough).
2. Inner drum (gas holder): inverted, open bottom, acts as a floating cap that rises as gas fills it.
3. Outer drum (digester): filled with a slurry of manure and water (roughly 1:1 ratio by volume).
4. Seal the outer drum except for an inlet pipe (to add fresh material) and an outlet pipe (for spent slurry — excellent fertilizer).
5. Gas collects under the inverted inner drum. Attach a gas line from the top of the inner drum. The weight of the drum provides constant low pressure.
6. Run the gas line to a burner. A simple propane-style burner jet works. Biogas burns at lower pressure than propane — the jet opening may need to be enlarged slightly.

## Feeding the Digester

Best inputs: cow manure, pig manure, chicken manure mixed with water. Manure from herbivores (cows, horses, goats) is ideal. Human waste works but requires longer digestion and careful handling of the output.

Also works: food scraps, vegetable waste, grass clippings, crop residues. Chop material fine — smaller pieces digest faster.

Do NOT add: wood, bones, large amounts of citrus (too acidic), antibiotics (kill the bacteria), oils and fats (slow digestion), meat (attracts pests and produces foul odors).

Daily feeding: add 1-2 buckets of fresh slurry and drain the same amount of spent material from the outlet. Continuous flow keeps production steady.

## Gas Yield

One cow's daily manure produces roughly 1-1.5 hours of cooking gas per day. A family of four's food waste plus two goats' manure can produce enough for 2-3 hours of cooking daily. That's enough to cook two meals.

## Safety

Methane is flammable and explosive in a 5-15% concentration with air. Never use open flames near the digester. Vent any leaking gas outdoors. The gas is odorless (unlike natural gas which has odorant added), so leaks aren't detectable by smell. Test all connections with soapy water — bubbles reveal leaks.

> The spent slurry from a biogas digester is one of the best fertilizers available — all nutrients are retained but pathogens are killed. Apply directly to garden beds.
