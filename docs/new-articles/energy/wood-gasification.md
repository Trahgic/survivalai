Wood gasification converts solid wood into a combustible gas (syngas) that can fuel internal combustion engines, cook food, or heat spaces more efficiently than an open fire. During WWII, over a million vehicles ran on wood gas when petroleum was unavailable. The technology is proven, simple, and buildable from scrap.

## How It Works

Wood heated above 700°F without enough oxygen to fully combust releases volatile gases — carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and methane. This gas mixture (syngas) burns cleanly when mixed with air. A gasifier controls this process in a contained vessel.

## TLUD Stove (Simplest Build — Cooking)

A Top-Lit Updraft stove is a gasifier you can build in 30 minutes from two cans.

1. Get two cans — a large one (like a paint can or #10 food can) and a smaller one that fits inside with a gap.
2. Punch holes around the bottom of the outer can (primary air intake).
3. Punch holes around the top of the inner can (secondary air intake for gas combustion).
4. Fill the inner can with small dry wood chunks, chips, or pellets. Pack loosely — air must flow between pieces.
5. Light the fuel from the top. As the top layer burns down, it heats the wood below, releasing gas. The gas rises, mixes with secondary air at the top holes, and burns in a clean blue flame above the fuel.
6. Set your cooking pot on top. Burns 30-60 minutes per load with minimal smoke.

A TLUD uses 50-70% less wood than an open fire for the same cooking output.

## Imbert-Style Gasifier (Engine Fuel)

For powering a gasoline engine or generator:

1. **Reactor:** A cylindrical vessel (steel drum, propane tank, or fabricated from sheet metal) where wood is loaded from the top.
2. **Hearth restriction:** A narrow zone (4-6 inch diameter for a small engine) where temperatures reach 1800°F+. This is where tar compounds crack into clean gas. The restriction diameter must match the engine displacement — too large and gas is dirty, too small and flow is restricted.
3. **Grate:** Below the hearth, a grate supports the charcoal bed and allows ash to fall through.
4. **Filter train:** Hot gas exits the reactor and passes through: a cyclone separator (removes ash particles), a cooler (radiator or long pipe run), and a filter (packed with wood chips, cloth, or straw) to remove remaining tar and particulates.
5. **Mixing valve:** Clean cool gas mixes with air at roughly 1:1 ratio before entering the engine's air intake.

A gasoline engine runs on wood gas with no modifications except plumbing the gas-air mixture into the carburetor or throttle body intake. Expect 30-50% power reduction compared to gasoline — wood gas has lower energy density.

## Fuel Requirements

Best fuel: dry hardwood chunks 1-3 inches, uniform size. Moisture content below 20% is critical — wet wood produces tar that clogs filters and destroys engines.

Wood consumption: roughly 2-3 lbs of dry wood per horsepower-hour. A 5kW generator running on wood gas uses about 15-20 lbs of wood per hour.

Do NOT use: green wood, resinous softwoods (produce excessive tar), painted or treated wood, leaves or grass (form clinkers).

## Safety

Carbon monoxide in wood gas is lethal. Syngas is roughly 20% CO. Never operate a gasifier indoors or in enclosed spaces. All connections must be sealed — test with soapy water. If you smell exhaust or feel dizzy, get to fresh air immediately.

The reactor vessel gets extremely hot (1000°F+). Keep all flammable materials well clear. Have a fire extinguisher or water source nearby.

> Wood gasification is not a weekend project. It requires metalworking skills, a good understanding of airflow dynamics, and extensive testing. But for long-term grid-down situations where gasoline runs out, a working gasifier converts your local forest into electricity.
