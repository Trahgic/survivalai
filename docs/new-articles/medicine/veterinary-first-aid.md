When veterinary care isn't available, your livestock and working dogs are critical survival assets. A dead milk goat or injured guard dog is a serious loss. Most animal first aid uses the same principles as human first aid, with some key differences.

## Universal Animal Wound Care

1. Restrain the animal safely. Muzzle dogs (even friendly ones bite when in pain). Halter and tie livestock. Have a second person hold.
2. Control bleeding with direct pressure, same as humans.
3. Clip or shave hair around the wound — hair in wounds causes infection.
4. Flush with clean water or dilute betadine (weak tea color). Pressure irrigation removes debris.
5. Leave most animal wounds OPEN — do not suture. Animals lick and tear sutures. Open wounds heal well from the inside out (secondary intention) in most animals.
6. Apply antibiotic ointment (triple antibiotic is fine for animals) and bandage if the location allows.
7. Change dressings daily. Watch for swelling, heat, discharge, odor.

## Antibiotic Dosing for Common Animals

These are standard veterinary doses. Fish antibiotics are the same compounds.

| Animal | Amoxicillin | Metronidazole | Dosing Frequency |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Dog | 10-20 mg/kg | 15 mg/kg | Every 8-12 hrs |
| Cat | 10-20 mg/kg | 10 mg/kg | Every 12 hrs |
| Goat | 10 mg/kg | 15 mg/kg | Every 12 hrs |
| Chicken | 100 mg/kg in water | — | Daily in drinking water |

Duration: 7-10 days for most infections. Do not stop early.

## Dog-Specific Issues

**Bloat (GDV):** Large breeds, deep chest. Stomach fills with gas and rotates. Signs: swollen hard belly, retching without producing vomit, restless, drooling, rapid breathing. This kills in hours. There is no field treatment — it requires surgery. Prevention: feed 2-3 small meals instead of one large one, don't exercise for an hour after eating.

**Porcupine quills:** Pull straight out with pliers. Do not cut quills — this does not make them easier to remove (myth). Check inside the mouth. Embedded quills migrate and cause abscesses. Remove them all.

**Heatstroke:** Panting hard, drooling, staggering, bright red gums. Cool with wet towels on belly, armpits, groin. Offer cool (not cold) water. Do NOT submerge in cold water — causes shock.

**Paw injuries:** Clean, bandage, and cover with a sock or boot to keep clean. Pad injuries heal slowly because of constant pressure.

## Livestock-Specific

**Goat/sheep bloat:** Left side of belly is swollen and drum-tight (rumen distension). Mild: walk the animal, massage the left flank, drench with 2-4 oz vegetable oil orally. Severe/emergency: trocar or 14-gauge needle inserted into the left flank at the highest point of distension to release gas. This is a last resort but saves lives.

**Egg binding in chickens:** Hen strains, stops eating, may have swollen vent. Warm water bath (100°F) for 20 minutes relaxes muscles. Apply lubricant (vegetable oil) around vent. Calcium supplement if available. Most pass the egg within hours after treatment.

**Foot rot in goats/sheep:** Limping, foul smell between toes, swollen tissue. Trim affected hoof tissue with sharp knife, soak in zinc sulfate solution (10%) or copper sulfate, keep on dry ground. Treat for 5-7 days.

## Pain Management for Animals

- Dogs: aspirin 10 mg/kg every 12 hours with food (NOT acetaminophen — toxic to dogs). Ibuprofen is risky but 5 mg/kg once daily with food in emergencies only.
- Cats: NO aspirin, NO acetaminophen, NO ibuprofen. All are toxic to cats. There is no safe OTC pain medication for cats.
- Livestock: aspirin 50-100 mg/kg orally every 12 hours. Flunixin (Banamine) 1.1 mg/kg if available.

> Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is FATAL to cats. Even one regular-strength tablet can kill an adult cat. Never give any human pain medication to a cat without veterinary guidance.

## When to Euthanize

The hardest decision. An animal with a compound fracture of a weight-bearing leg, evisceration (intestines outside the body), severe burns over more than 50% of the body, or untreatable pain has no path to recovery in a field setting. A bullet or sharp knife to the brainstem (base of skull where it meets the neck) is the most humane field method. This is a leadership decision that should be made clearly and without delay once the assessment is certain.
