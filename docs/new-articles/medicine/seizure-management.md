Seizures happen when electrical activity in the brain misfires. In survival situations, seizures can be triggered by head injury, high fever, low blood sugar, dehydration, heat stroke, withdrawal from alcohol or medications, infection, or pre-existing epilepsy when medications run out.

## During a Seizure

Most seizures last 30 seconds to 2 minutes and stop on their own.

1. Clear the area of anything hard or sharp the person could hit. Move furniture, rocks, tools away.
2. Ease them to the ground if they're standing.
3. Place something soft under their head — folded jacket, towel, your hands.
4. Turn them on their side (recovery position) if possible. This keeps the airway clear.
5. Time the seizure. Duration matters for treatment decisions.

**Do NOT:**
- Put anything in their mouth. They will not swallow their tongue. Inserting objects breaks teeth and can cause choking.
- Restrain them or hold them down. You will not stop the seizure and may cause injury.
- Give them water or food until fully alert.

## After the Seizure (Postictal Phase)

The person will be confused, exhausted, and may not know where they are. This phase lasts 5-30 minutes.

1. Keep them on their side until fully awake.
2. Check for injuries — bitten tongue, bumps, cuts. Tongue bleeding usually stops on its own.
3. Speak calmly. Tell them where they are, what happened, that they're safe.
4. Do NOT leave them alone until they are fully oriented and can answer questions.
5. Let them rest. Post-seizure exhaustion is normal.

## When a Seizure Is an Emergency

Call for help or prepare for evacuation if:

- Seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes (status epilepticus — brain damage risk)
- Second seizure follows without full recovery between them
- Person doesn't wake up or resume breathing after seizure stops
- Seizure happens in water
- Person is pregnant
- First-ever seizure (underlying cause needs investigation)
- Seizure after head injury

## Seizures from Medication Withdrawal

When anticonvulsant medications (phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproic acid, levetiracetam) are suddenly unavailable, seizure risk increases dramatically within 24-72 hours.

If you know someone in your group takes seizure medication, that medication is the single highest priority for stockpiling. Even partial doses reduce risk.

Without medication, reduce seizure triggers: ensure adequate hydration, maintain blood sugar with regular meals, prioritize sleep, avoid alcohol, reduce physical exhaustion and heat exposure.

## Seizures from Fever (Febrile Seizures)

Common in children 6 months to 5 years. Triggered by rapid temperature rise, not the peak temperature.

1. Do NOT put the child in cold water — this can cause shivering which raises core temperature.
2. Remove excess clothing. Tepid (lukewarm) sponging of armpits and groin.
3. Acetaminophen after the seizure ends: 15 mg/kg.
4. Most febrile seizures are brief and not harmful. But a child who has one needs monitoring for the underlying infection causing the fever.

## Head Injury Seizures

A seizure within minutes of a head impact may not indicate severe brain injury. A seizure hours or days after head trauma is more concerning — it suggests bleeding or swelling inside the skull.

Post-traumatic seizure signs that indicate serious brain injury: one pupil larger than the other, weakness on one side of the body, worsening headache, repeated vomiting, worsening confusion. These require evacuation to surgical care if any option exists.
