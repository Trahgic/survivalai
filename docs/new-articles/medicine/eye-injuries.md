Eye injuries in survival situations are disproportionately dangerous because vision loss cripples your ability to do everything else. Most eye problems in the field come from foreign objects, UV exposure, chemical splash, or blunt trauma.

## Foreign Object in the Eye

Most common field injury. Dust, metal shavings, wood splinters, insects.

1. Do NOT rub the eye — this drives the object deeper and scratches the cornea.
2. Pull the upper eyelid out and down over the lower lid. The lower lashes may sweep the object out.
3. Flush the eye with clean water for 5 minutes. Tilt the head so water flows from the inner corner outward. Use a squeeze bottle, syringe, or cupped hands.
4. If you can see the object on the white of the eye, touch it gently with the moistened corner of a clean cloth or a cotton swab. Do NOT touch the colored part (iris) or the pupil.
5. If the object is embedded or on the cornea, do NOT try to remove it. Patch the eye loosely, keep both eyes closed (eyes move together), and get to care.

## Chemical Burns to the Eye

Splash from battery acid, bleach, lye, fuel, or any caustic substance.

1. Flush IMMEDIATELY with water. Seconds matter. This is the single most important step.
2. Continue flushing for at least 20 minutes. Hold the eye open with your fingers.
3. Flush from the inner corner outward so contaminated water doesn't flow into the other eye.
4. Remove contact lenses if present — chemicals trap behind them.
5. After flushing, cover loosely with damp cloth. Do NOT patch tightly.

> Alkali burns (lye, ammonia, cement dust) are worse than acid burns. They keep penetrating. Flush longer — 30 minutes minimum.

## Snow Blindness (Photokeratitis)

UV sunburn of the cornea. Happens from bright sun on snow, water, or sand. Symptoms start 6-12 hours after exposure: intense pain, tearing, swollen lids, gritty sensation, light sensitivity.

1. Get into shade or darkness. Cover both eyes with damp cool cloths.
2. Pain management: ibuprofen 400mg every 6 hours. Cool compresses.
3. Do NOT rub eyes. Do NOT wear contacts.
4. Recovery takes 24-72 hours. Usually heals without permanent damage.
5. Prevention: improvised sunglasses — cut narrow horizontal slits in cardboard, bark, or duct tape. Soot under the eyes reduces glare.

## Blunt Trauma to the Eye

Hit by branch, fist, rock, or blast debris.

Signs of serious injury: blood visible inside the eye (hyphema), pupil shape irregular, severe pain, vision dramatically reduced, eye feels soft or hard.

1. Do NOT apply pressure to the injured eye.
2. Cover with a rigid shield (bottom of a paper cup taped over the eye socket). This prevents further damage.
3. Patch the uninjured eye too — eyes move together, and movement of the good eye moves the injured one.
4. Keep the person sitting up at 30-45 degrees to reduce pressure in the eye.
5. Do NOT give aspirin or ibuprofen — they increase bleeding. Acetaminophen only for pain.

## When You Cannot Get to Medical Care

A scratched cornea (corneal abrasion) heals in 24-72 hours on its own. Keep the eye clean, avoid rubbing, and use a loose patch.

Antibiotic eye drops (fish antibiotics are NOT appropriate for eyes) can prevent infection in a scratched cornea. If you have erythromycin ophthalmic ointment, apply a thin strip inside the lower lid every 6 hours for 3 days.

Any injury where you can see fluid leaking from the eye, the pupil is irregular, or vision is severely reduced is a surgical emergency. Stabilize, shield the eye, and evacuate.
