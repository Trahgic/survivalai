Drowning is the third leading cause of unintentional death worldwide. In a survival context, river crossings, flooding, and improvised water collection create constant risk. Most drowning victims die within arm's reach of safety.

## Recognizing Drowning

Drowning does not look like drowning in movies. Real drowning is quiet.

Signs: head low in the water with mouth at surface level, head tilted back with mouth open, eyes glassy and unfocused, hair over forehead or eyes, not using legs (vertical in water), hyperventilating or gasping, trying to swim but not making progress, trying to roll onto back. The person cannot call for help — their body is using all air for breathing.

A person who is splashing and yelling is in distress but not yet drowning. They can still assist in their own rescue. Once silent drowning begins, you have 20-60 seconds before submersion.

## Rescue Priorities — Don't Become a Second Victim

Follow this order. Each step is safer than the next:

1. **Reach** — extend a pole, branch, paddle, towel, or clothing from shore. Lie flat to avoid being pulled in.
2. **Throw** — throw a rope, life ring, cooler, sealed jug, or anything that floats. Aim beyond the person and pull toward them.
3. **Row** — use a boat, raft, surfboard, or inflatable. Approach from downstream.
4. **Go** — swimming rescue is a last resort. Untrained swimmers attempting rescue have a high drowning rate themselves.

> Panicking drowning victims will climb on top of a rescuer and push them under. If you must swim to them, bring something buoyant they can grab instead of grabbing you. Approach from behind.

## If You Must Swim to a Victim

1. Take a buoyant object — sealed water jug, cooler lid, log, sealed dry bag.
2. Approach from behind. Push the flotation device into their hands.
3. If they grab you, tuck your chin, dive down, and push away. They will let go when submerged.
4. Tow from behind: reach under their armpits, grip their jaw or collar, sidestroke to shore.
5. In current, aim upstream of the victim — the current will carry you to them.

## After Removal from Water

1. Check for breathing. If not breathing, start CPR immediately. Do NOT try to drain water from lungs — stomach thrusts waste time and don't work.
2. Standard CPR: 30 compressions, 2 breaths. Rescue breaths are especially important in drowning (unlike cardiac arrest where compressions-only is acceptable).
3. Expect vomiting — turn the head to the side to clear the airway, then continue CPR.
4. If they're breathing, place in recovery position (on their side) and monitor.
5. Remove wet clothing and treat for hypothermia — even warm water draws heat 25 times faster than air.

## Secondary Drowning

A person who was submerged but revived can develop delayed lung problems 1-24 hours later. Water in the lungs causes inflammation that progressively impairs breathing.

Watch for: increasing cough, difficulty breathing, chest pain, extreme fatigue, confusion, foamy sputum.

If any of these develop after a submersion event, the person needs higher medical care. Keep them sitting upright, monitor breathing rate, and prepare for possible CPR.

## Self-Rescue in Current

If you fall into moving water:

1. Do NOT try to stand up in fast water deeper than knee-height — foot entrapment kills.
2. Float on your back, feet downstream, toes up. Use feet to push off rocks.
3. Angle toward shore by backstroking at 45 degrees to current.
4. Once in slower water near shore, roll onto stomach and crawl out.
5. In cold water, swim aggressively for the first minute before your muscles weaken. Cold shock is the immediate killer, not hypothermia.
