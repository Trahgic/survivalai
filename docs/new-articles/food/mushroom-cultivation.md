Growing mushrooms is one of the highest-value food production methods for survival situations. Mushrooms convert waste materials (logs, straw, coffee grounds, cardboard) into protein-rich food in spaces where nothing else can grow — shade, basements, under trees.

## Easiest Species to Grow

Oyster mushrooms are the beginner species. They're aggressive colonizers, grow on almost anything, tolerate temperature swings, and produce within weeks. Shiitake are second-easiest but require hardwood logs and more patience.

## Log Cultivation (Shiitake and Oyster)

1. Cut fresh hardwood logs (oak, maple, beech, sweetgum) 3-8 inches diameter, 3-4 feet long. Cut in late winter while dormant. Do NOT use conifer/softwood.
2. Wait 2-4 weeks after cutting. The tree's natural anti-fungal compounds need to dissipate, but the log needs to stay moist.
3. Drill holes in a diamond pattern: 6 inches apart along the log, rows 2 inches apart. Drill 1 inch deep, 5/16 inch diameter.
4. Insert mushroom spawn plugs (inoculated wooden dowels). Tap in with a hammer.
5. Seal each plug with melted food-grade wax (beeswax, cheese wax, or soy wax).
6. Stack logs in a shady area with good airflow. Lean against a fence or stack in a log cabin pattern.
7. Keep moist — soak for 24 hours every 2 weeks during dry periods.
8. Colonization takes 6-18 months depending on species and temperature.
9. Mushrooms fruit in spring and fall, triggered by rain and temperature changes. Each log produces for 3-6 years.

## Straw or Cardboard Cultivation (Oyster Mushrooms)

Faster than logs. Can produce mushrooms in 3-4 weeks.

1. Pasteurize your substrate: soak chopped straw or torn cardboard in hot water (160-180°F) for 1 hour. This kills competing organisms. Drain and cool to room temperature.
2. Layer damp substrate and grain spawn in a clean bucket or plastic bag with holes poked for air exchange. Alternate layers: 2 inches substrate, sprinkle of spawn, repeat.
3. Keep in a warm dark place (65-75°F) for 2-3 weeks. White mycelium will colonize the substrate.
4. When fully white, move to indirect light and increase fresh air. Mist daily.
5. Mushrooms pin (tiny bumps appear) within days. Harvest when cap edges begin to flatten — before they fully open and drop spores.
6. Most substrates produce 2-3 flushes of mushrooms, 1-2 weeks apart.

## Growing Spawn from Existing Mushrooms

If you have fresh oyster mushrooms, you can clone them:

1. Tear a fresh mushroom in half with clean hands.
2. Use a sterilized knife to cut a small piece from the INSIDE of the stem (cleanest area).
3. Place on damp cardboard in a sealed container. Poke a small air hole.
4. In 1-2 weeks, white mycelium grows out from the tissue onto the cardboard.
5. Use this colonized cardboard as spawn for larger substrates.

This isn't as reliable as commercial spawn but works well enough for oyster mushrooms.

## Nutritional Value

Mushrooms provide protein (2-4g per cup), B vitamins, potassium, selenium, and vitamin D (if sun-dried gill-side up for 6 hours). They're one of the only non-animal sources of vitamin D. In a diet heavy on carbohydrates from grain and root crops, mushrooms fill important nutritional gaps.
