In long-term survival, nutritional deficiencies kill slowly. Most people fixate on calories while ignoring vitamins and minerals. A diet of rice and rabbit meat will keep you alive for weeks but leaves you sick and weakened within months. Knowing what deficiencies look like — and how to fix them with available food — is critical.

## Scurvy (Vitamin C Deficiency)

Appears after 4-12 weeks without vitamin C. Historically killed more sailors than storms and combat combined.

Signs: fatigue and weakness first, then bleeding gums, loose teeth, slow wound healing, easy bruising, old wounds reopening, joint pain, small red dots on skin (petechiae).

Treatment: any fresh plant material contains some vitamin C. Pine needle tea (steep fresh green needles in hot water 10 minutes), rose hips, dandelion greens, spruce tips, cattail shoots, wild berries, raw cabbage, peppers. Even sprouted grains and beans contain enough. Cooked foods lose vitamin C — eat raw when possible.

Dose to treat: 100 mg/day reverses scurvy within days. An orange has 70 mg. A cup of pine needle tea has 50-80 mg. Even a handful of fresh grass provides some.

## Iron Deficiency Anemia

Common in women of childbearing age, especially with poor diet. Takes weeks to months to develop.

Signs: extreme fatigue, pale skin (check inside lower eyelid — should be red, pale pink = anemia), dizziness on standing, rapid heartbeat with minimal exertion, shortness of breath, craving non-food items (ice, dirt — called pica), brittle nails.

Sources: red meat (best absorption), liver (extremely rich), dark leafy greens (spinach, nettles), beans, blackstrap molasses, cooking in cast iron (yes, this works — iron leaches into acidic foods). Pair with vitamin C foods — vitamin C doubles iron absorption. Avoid drinking tea or coffee with iron-rich meals (tannins block absorption).

## Protein Deficiency

More common than most people expect in survival diets heavy on foraging and grains.

Signs: muscle wasting, swollen belly (edema from low albumin), hair loss, skin changes (flaky, pale), slow wound healing, weakened immune system (getting sick frequently), apathy and irritability.

Complete protein sources: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, insects, quinoa, soybeans. Most plant proteins are incomplete — combine grains with legumes (rice and beans, corn and squash) to get all essential amino acids. This combination is why every traditional civilization independently invented a grain-plus-legume staple.

## Vitamin A Deficiency

Signs: night blindness (first symptom — can't see in dim light), dry eyes, frequent infections, dry scaly skin.

Sources: liver (one serving provides weeks of vitamin A), egg yolks, dairy, orange/yellow vegetables (sweet potato, carrots, squash), dark leafy greens. The body converts beta-carotene from plants into vitamin A, but inefficiently — animal sources are 10-20x more effective.

## B Vitamin Deficiency (Beriberi, Pellagra)

Beriberi (B1/thiamine): common with diets of white rice or refined grains. Numbness in hands/feet, muscle weakness, heart problems. Treatment: whole grains, legumes, pork, seeds, liver.

Pellagra (B3/niacin): common with corn-based diets. The 4 D's: dermatitis (scaly skin rash in sun-exposed areas), diarrhea, dementia, death. Treatment: meat, fish, legumes, nuts. Traditional corn processing (nixtamalization — soaking corn in lime/wood ash water) releases niacin. This is why pellagra was unknown in indigenous American populations who processed corn this way.

## Iodine Deficiency

Signs: goiter (swollen thyroid gland in neck), fatigue, weight gain, cold sensitivity, mental fog. In pregnant women, causes severe developmental problems in the baby.

Sources: seaweed (kelp, nori), ocean fish, iodized salt. Inland populations without access to seafood are most at risk. Historically this was solved by iodized salt — if you're stockpiling salt, make sure it's iodized.

## Practical Approach

The single best defense against deficiency is dietary diversity. Even small amounts of many foods beat large amounts of few foods. Liver is the most nutrient-dense food that exists — one serving per week prevents most deficiencies. Wild greens, sprouts, and organ meats together cover nearly every gap.

Sprouting grains and beans is the easiest way to add vitamins to a monotonous diet. Soak seeds overnight, drain, rinse twice daily for 3-5 days. Sprouts contain vitamin C, folate, and more bioavailable minerals than the dry seed.
