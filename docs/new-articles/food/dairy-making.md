If you have a milk animal (goat, cow, sheep), dairy products are the best way to preserve that milk without refrigeration. Cheese lasts months, butter weeks, and yogurt days — all far longer than raw milk.

## Butter (Simplest Dairy Product)

1. Let fresh milk sit covered in a cool place for 12-24 hours. Cream rises to the top.
2. Skim the cream off with a spoon or ladle.
3. Let cream warm to about 60°F (cool room temperature works).
4. Shake vigorously in a sealed jar for 15-30 minutes. The cream goes through stages: sloshing → thick whipped cream → suddenly separates into butter chunks and buttermilk.
5. Pour off the buttermilk (drink it or use for baking).
6. Rinse the butter in cold water, kneading it, until the water runs clear. This removes residual buttermilk which causes spoilage.
7. Add 1/2 teaspoon salt per pound of butter and knead in.
8. Press into a mold or wrap in cloth. Store in the coolest place available.
9. Salted butter lasts 2-3 weeks without refrigeration, longer in cool conditions.

## Simple Farmhouse Cheese (Paneer/Queso Fresco Style)

No aging required. Ready to eat in 1 hour.

1. Heat 1 gallon of milk to 190°F (just below boiling — small bubbles forming).
2. Remove from heat. Add 1/4 cup of vinegar or lemon juice. Stir gently.
3. The milk will separate into white curds and yellowish whey within 1-2 minutes. If it doesn't separate, add more acid.
4. Let sit 10 minutes.
5. Pour through cheesecloth (or a clean cotton t-shirt) draped over a colander.
6. Gather the cloth and squeeze out excess whey.
7. Add 1 teaspoon salt, mix into the curds.
8. Press into a shape: tie the cloth tight and set a weight on top for 30-60 minutes.
9. Unwrap. You have a firm, sliceable cheese. Eat within 3-5 days, or salt-brine it for longer storage.

## Aged Hard Cheese (Weeks to Make, Months of Storage)

This requires rennet (an enzyme that curdles milk differently from acid). Sources: animal rennet from a calf or kid stomach lining (traditional), vegetable rennet from certain thistle species, or commercial rennet tablets if you stocked them.

1. Warm 2 gallons of milk to 90°F.
2. Add mesophilic culture (or 1/4 cup of buttermilk as a crude substitute). Stir. Wait 1 hour.
3. Add rennet dissolved in 1/4 cup cool water. Stir gently for 30 seconds, then stop.
4. Wait 45-60 minutes. The milk sets into a solid gel (clean break test: insert finger, lift — it should break cleanly around your finger).
5. Cut the curd into 1/2-inch cubes with a long knife.
6. Slowly heat to 102°F over 30 minutes, stirring gently. Curds shrink and firm up.
7. Drain whey through cheesecloth. Salt the curds — 2 tablespoons per gallon of original milk.
8. Pack curds into a mold (tin can with holes punched in bottom works). Press with 15-20 lbs weight for 12 hours. Flip, press another 12 hours.
9. Air dry the wheel for 2-3 days, turning daily, until a rind starts forming.
10. Age in a cool (50-60°F), humid location. Turn daily for the first week, then weekly. Wipe any mold with a cloth dipped in vinegar.
11. Minimum 2 months aging. Longer = stronger flavor. Properly aged hard cheese lasts 6-12 months.

> Save the whey from cheesemaking. It's rich in protein and can be used in bread, soup stock, animal feed, or fermented into whey drinks. Nothing is wasted.

## Troubleshooting

- Milk won't curdle: not hot enough (for acid method) or rennet is expired. Raw milk curdles easier than pasteurized.
- Cheese is rubbery: overheated or over-stirred the curds.
- Cheese is crumbly: too much acid or too little pressing.
- Mold during aging: wipe with vinegar cloth. Some surface mold is normal and harmless on hard cheese. Black or pink mold should be cut away with 1 inch margin.
