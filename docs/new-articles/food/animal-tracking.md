Tracking is the foundation of hunting and trapping. Without it, you're wandering the woods hoping to stumble onto an animal. With it, you know what animals are in your area, where they travel, when they're active, and where to set traps or wait with a weapon.

## Reading Tracks

**Track components:** Toe pads, heel pad (or hoof), claw marks (or lack of), stride length, straddle (width between left and right feet), and gait pattern.

**Key identification features:**

| Animal | Track Shape | Size | Claw Marks | Notes |
|--------|-----------|------|-----------|-------|
| Deer | Split hoof, heart-shaped | 2-3.5 in | No | Most common large game track |
| Dog/coyote | 4 toes, oval shape | 2-3 in | Yes, always | Symmetrical, tight toe grouping |
| Cat/bobcat | 4 toes, round shape | 1.5-3 in | No claws visible | Asymmetrical, spread toes |
| Raccoon | 5 toes, hand-like | 2-3 in | Yes | Rear track looks like a tiny human hand |
| Rabbit | 4 toes, oblong | 1-3 in | No | Rear feet land AHEAD of front feet when hopping |
| Bear | 5 toes, wide | 4-7 in | Yes, long | Flat-footed like a human, massive |
| Turkey | 3 toes forward, 1 back | 4-5 in | No | Large bird track, straight middle toe |
| Squirrel | 4 front, 5 rear toes | 0.5-1 in | Yes | Paired tracks, rear feet flank front |

## Aging Tracks

Fresh tracks have sharp, crisp edges. As hours pass, edges crumble, rain softens detail, leaves and debris fall in, and spider webs may form across the impression. In soft mud, a fresh track has a wet sheen; a 12-hour-old track is dull and dry.

Practice in your own area: make a clear bootprint, then check it at 1 hour, 6 hours, 12 hours, and 24 hours. Notice how the edges change. This calibrates your eye for local soil and weather conditions.

## Sign Beyond Tracks

**Scat (droppings):** Tells you what the animal eats, how recently it passed, and species. Deer scat = piles of oval pellets. Coyote = twisted rope with hair and bone fragments. Bear = large irregular piles with berries, insect parts, or fish bones. Rabbit = round dry pellets. Fresh scat is moist and dark; old scat is dry, gray, and crumbly.

**Browse and feeding signs:** Deer tear vegetation (ragged edge — they have no upper front teeth). Rabbits cut at 45-degree angles (sharp, clean cut). Stripped bark indicates porcupine (high) or rabbit (low). Digging indicates skunk, raccoon, or bear after grubs.

**Rubs and scrapes:** Deer rub antlers on saplings (shredded bark, 2-4 feet high). Bear claw marks on trees (parallel scratches, sometimes very high). Scrapes in dirt indicate territorial marking (deer, cats).

**Trails:** Animals are creatures of habit. They use the same paths repeatedly between bedding, water, and feeding areas. Game trails are worn paths through vegetation, often 6-12 inches wide, with tracks and droppings concentrated.

## Where to Find Animals

**Water:** Every animal needs water daily. Set up observation or traps within 50 yards of a reliable water source during early morning or dusk.

**Transitions:** The edge between two habitat types (forest and field, brush and creek) is called an edge or ecotone. Animals concentrate at edges because they offer food, cover, and escape routes.

**Bedding areas:** Deer bed in dense cover on slopes where they can see downhill and smell wind from above. Pressed-down grass or leaf litter shows where animals have rested.

**Funnels:** Anywhere terrain forces movement into a narrow path — a gap in a fence, a narrow creek crossing, a saddle between two hills. These are the highest-value trap and ambush locations.

## Tracking Wounded Game

After a shot or trap injury, tracking becomes critical.

Bright red blood with bubbles = lung hit. The animal dies within 100-200 yards. Wait 30 minutes, then follow.

Dark red blood = liver or muscle hit. The animal may go 200-500 yards. Wait 1-2 hours before following.

Pink frothy blood = heart or major artery. Short trail.

No blood trail but the animal reacted to a hit: look for scuffed leaves, broken branches, and hair at the impact point. Follow disturbed vegetation. Mark each sign with a piece of cloth so you can backtrack if you lose the trail.

## Practical Tracking Exercise

Start with domestic animals — your dog, a neighbor's cat, local squirrels. Watch them move across soft ground, then examine their tracks. Graduate to wild animal tracks at a muddy creek bank — this is where the most tracks concentrate. Visit the same mud bank daily and you'll learn your local wildlife inventory within a week.
