Fermentation is controlled rotting — beneficial bacteria or yeast consume sugars and produce acid or alcohol that prevent harmful bacteria from growing. Requires no electricity, no special equipment, and produces foods that last months.

## Sauerkraut

1. Shred cabbage. Add 2% salt by weight (1 tablespoon per pound).
2. Massage 5-10 minutes until it releases liquid.
3. Pack tightly into a clean jar. Cabbage must be BELOW the brine level.
4. Weight it down with a clean rock or water-filled bag.
5. Cover with cloth, not airtight. Store at 60-75°F.
6. Taste after 3 days. Ferment 1-4 weeks. Lasts 6+ months sealed in cool storage.

> The golden rule: submerge below the brine. Above the liquid = mold. Below = safe.

## Simple Fruit Wine

1. Crush 4-5 pounds fruit in a clean bucket.
2. Add 2 pounds sugar dissolved in 1 gallon warm water.
3. Add yeast — bread yeast works, wine yeast is better.
4. Cover with cloth, stir daily for 5-7 days.
5. Strain out solids. Transfer liquid to a jug with an airlock (or balloon with a pinhole).
6. Ferment 2-6 weeks until bubbling stops.
7. Siphon clear liquid off sediment. Bottle. Roughly 8-14% alcohol.

## Mead (Honey Wine)

3 pounds honey in 1 gallon warm water. Cool, add yeast. Airlock 4-8 weeks. Siphon and bottle.

## Vinegar

1. Start with any alcohol — wine, cider, mead.
2. Pour into wide-mouthed container (bacteria need air contact).
3. Add unpasteurized vinegar as starter if available.
4. Cover with cloth. Warm dark place. Wait 2-8 weeks.
5. A rubbery disc (the "mother") forms on the surface — this is normal.

Essential for pickling, cleaning, and wound care.

## Yogurt

1. Heat milk to 180°F. Cool to 110°F.
2. Stir in 2 tablespoons existing yogurt per quart.
3. Keep warm (100-115°F) for 6-12 hours — wrap in towels near heat source.
4. Done when thick and tangy. Save 2 tablespoons to start next batch.

## Kimchi, Pickles, Hot Sauce

Same principle as sauerkraut: vegetable + 2-3% salt + submerge below brine + wait. Works with cucumbers, peppers, radishes, green beans, garlic — almost any vegetable. Add spices to taste.

## Safety

Signs of a bad ferment: pink/black/fuzzy mold, putrid smell (sour is fine), slimy texture. White surface film (kahm yeast) is ugly but harmless. When in doubt, discard and start over. Properly fermented foods are extremely safe — the acid kills pathogens.
