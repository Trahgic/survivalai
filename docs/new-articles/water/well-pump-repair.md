When the grid goes down, electric well pumps stop. Your well still has water — you just can't get it out. This article covers emergency methods to extract water from an existing well, basic electric pump troubleshooting, and manual pump options.

## Emergency Water from an Existing Well

If you have a drilled well with a standard 6-inch casing and no power:

**Bailer method (simplest):**
1. A bailer is a long narrow tube with a check valve at the bottom. Lower it into the well on a rope, it fills with water, pull it up, dump into a bucket.
2. Improvised bailer: 3-4 foot section of PVC pipe (1.5-3 inch diameter) with a rubber flap valve or marble seated in the bottom. Drill a small hole at the top for the rope.
3. Slow but works for any depth. One bailer load = roughly 1/2 to 1 gallon depending on pipe diameter.
4. For a 100-foot-deep well, plan on 10-15 minutes per gallon including lowering, filling, and hauling up.

**Inertia pump (hand-powered):**
1. Drop a length of polyethylene tubing (1-inch diameter) down the well to below the water level, with a foot valve at the bottom.
2. Rapidly pump the tube up and down 6-8 inches. The water's inertia keeps it moving upward through the tube with each stroke.
3. Water flows continuously once primed. Output depends on depth and effort — typically 1-5 gallons per minute for shallow wells.

## Electric Pump Basics

Most residential wells use a submersible pump sitting at the bottom of the well, connected to a pressure tank in the house.

**Common failure points:**
- No power to the pump: check the breaker, pressure switch (on the pressure tank — the switch with a lever), and wiring. A tripped pressure switch is the most common "dead pump" cause.
- Pressure switch failure: the switch (usually a Square D brand gray box on the pressure tank) has contact points that burn over time. Remove the cover, check for burnt/pitted contacts. File clean or replace.
- Pressure tank waterlogged: the tank has an air bladder. If it fails, the pump short-cycles (turns on/off rapidly). Check air pressure at the valve stem on top — should be 2 PSI below the cut-in pressure (usually 28 PSI for a 30/50 system).
- Pump motor burned out: no field repair. Replace with a spare submersible pump if available, or switch to manual extraction.

## Installing a Hand Pump Alongside an Electric Pump

The most valuable prep for well-dependent homesteads. A hand pump can be installed in the same well casing as the submersible pump.

**Simple Pitcher Pump:** Works for wells where the water level is within 25 feet of the surface. Bolts onto the well cap. Cast iron, no moving parts except a plunger and check valve. $50-150 purchased pre-grid-down. Produces 2-5 gallons per minute with manual pumping.

**Deep Well Hand Pump (e.g., Simple Pump, Bison Pump):** Works to 300+ feet. Uses a cylinder at the bottom of a drop pipe with a long rod connecting to the handle at the surface. $500-1500 but worth every dollar if your well is deep. Produces 1-3 gallons per minute.

## Rope Pump (Build Your Own)

An ancient design still used worldwide. A continuous loop of rope with rubber discs (washers) spaced every 12 inches passes through a PVC pipe that descends into the well. Turning a wheel at the top pulls the rope upward — each disc pushes a slug of water up the pipe.

1. PVC pipe: 1.5-2 inch diameter, long enough to reach below water level.
2. Rope: continuous loop, twice the well depth plus extra for the wheel.
3. Discs: cut from rubber (old tires) to fit snugly inside the pipe. Attach to rope every 12 inches.
4. Wheel: bicycle wheel works well. Mount at the top of the well. Rope wraps over the wheel.
5. Crank by hand or attach to a windmill or bicycle drive.
6. Output: 1-5 gallons per minute depending on depth and crank speed.

## Water Level and Yield

Your well has a static water level (where water sits when not pumping) and a pumping water level (drops as you pump — recovery rate depends on the aquifer). If your manual extraction rate exceeds the aquifer's recharge rate, the well runs dry temporarily. Let it recover for a few hours and try again.

Mark your rope or tube at the static water level so you know how deep to lower your bailer or pump.

## Maintaining an Unpowered Well

- Keep the well cap sealed to prevent contamination from surface water, insects, and animals.
- After a flood or contamination event, disinfect by pouring 1/2 gallon of bleach down the casing, waiting 12 hours, then pumping until bleach smell clears.
- If the well produces muddy or sandy water after sitting unused, pump several hundred gallons to clear the sediment before drinking.
