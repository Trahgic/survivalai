Greywater is wastewater from sinks, showers, and laundry — everything except toilet water (which is blackwater). In water-scarce survival conditions, greywater recycling can cut your fresh water needs by 30-50% by routing used wash water directly to your garden.

## What Is and Isn't Greywater

**Greywater (reusable):** sink water, shower/bath water, laundry rinse water, dishwater (with caution — high grease/food particles).

**Blackwater (NOT reusable without treatment):** toilet waste, diaper rinse water, water from washing raw meat, water contaminated with chemical cleaners.

## Simplest System: Bucket Carry

1. Collect dishwater, hand-wash water, and rinse water in a basin.
2. Let food particles settle. Skim off floating grease.
3. Carry directly to fruit trees, bushes, or non-root-crop garden beds.
4. Pour at the base of plants, not on leaves or fruit.

This works immediately with zero construction. The limitation is labor — you're carrying buckets.

## Gravity-Fed Greywater System

1. Route your sink or wash station drain through a pipe to outside. The drain must be higher than the garden (gravity flow). Even 12 inches of elevation is enough.
2. Run the pipe (garden hose, PVC, or improvised bamboo) to a mulch basin in the garden.
3. **Mulch basin:** dig a shallow depression (6 inches deep, 3 feet wide) around a tree or garden area. Fill with wood chips, straw, or coarse mulch. Route the greywater pipe to discharge into the mulch.
4. The mulch filters solids, prevents pooling, and prevents direct contact with soil surface.
5. Plants' root zones absorb the water and nutrients. The soil biome breaks down soap and organic matter.

## Branched Drain System (Multiple Outlets)

For distributing greywater across a larger garden:

1. Main drain line from the source (1-2 inch pipe).
2. Split into branches using Y-fittings. Each split halves the flow.
3. Each branch terminates at a different mulch basin around different plants.
4. Use a 3-way valve at the first split to alternate flow between areas, preventing waterlogging.

## What Soap Is Safe

Standard soaps and detergents are fine for greywater irrigation in moderate amounts. The soil biome breaks them down.

**Avoid:** bleach (kills soil organisms), boron-containing detergents (toxic to plants at high concentrations), strong antibacterial soaps (kill beneficial soil bacteria), solvents and harsh chemicals.

**Best:** biodegradable camp soaps, castile soap, homemade soap. These break down fastest and add the least harmful residue.

## Safety Rules

1. Never store greywater for more than 24 hours. Bacteria multiply rapidly in warm nutrient-rich water. Use it the day it's produced.
2. Never spray greywater through a sprinkler or onto edible leaf crops (lettuce, herbs). Subsurface or mulch-basin application only.
3. Greywater is safe for fruit trees, berry bushes, ornamentals, and root crops where the edible part doesn't contact the water.
4. Keep greywater outlets covered with mulch to prevent mosquito breeding and direct contact.
5. Do NOT use greywater from washing soiled diapers, cleaning up vomit, or anyone with an infectious illness.

## Winter Considerations

In freezing climates, above-ground greywater lines freeze. Options: insulate pipes, bury below frost line (requires more construction), or revert to bucket carry during winter. Greywater should never be allowed to pool and freeze on the surface — ice sheets become slip hazards and the water doesn't infiltrate.

> A household generating 30-50 gallons of greywater daily has enough to irrigate 200-500 square feet of garden. That's a meaningful food production area sustained entirely on recycled water.
