When manufactured weapons and ammunition are unavailable, primitive weapons built from natural materials provide hunting capability and personal defense. These technologies kept humans at the top of the food chain for 200,000 years before firearms existed.

## Simple Spear

The fastest weapon to make. Functional in under an hour.

1. Select a straight hardwood shaft 6-8 feet long, 1-1.5 inches in diameter. Green wood is fine.
2. Sharpen one end to a point with a knife. Fire-harden the tip by rotating it slowly over coals (not in flame) until the surface browns and dries. This makes the tip much harder.
3. For a more effective hunting spear, split the tip 6 inches and insert a sharpened stone or metal flake. Lash tightly with cordage below the split to prevent further splitting.

**Fishing spear (gig):** Split the tip into 3-4 prongs by making two perpendicular splits. Spread the prongs with small wedges and sharpen each one. Barb the prongs by cutting a backward-facing notch near each tip. Most effective for spearing fish in shallow water at night with a torch.

## Bow and Arrow

A functional bow takes 4-8 hours to make. Arrows take longer — plan on a full day for a bow and 6 usable arrows.

**Bow wood selection:** Best species in order: Osage orange, yew, black locust, ash, hickory, elm, maple, oak. The ideal stave is a straight branch or split from a trunk, 5-6 feet long, free of knots, with even grain.

**Quick survival bow (tillering not required):**
1. Find a dead-standing sapling 2 inches diameter at chest height, straight, no side branches in the working section. Cut a 5-foot length.
2. Shave the belly (side facing you when shooting) so it bends evenly along its length. Leave the back (outer surface, bark side) completely untouched — cutting into the back breaks growth rings and the bow will snap.
3. Narrow the limb tips for string nocks. Cut a notch on each side of each tip, 1 inch from the end.
4. String: paracord, bankline, natural cordage (rawhide or sinew is traditional). The string should be 4-5 inches shorter than the bow.
5. The bow should bend evenly when drawn. If one spot bends more, shave material from the stiff spots (not the bending spots) until the curve is uniform.

**Arrows:**
1. Shaft: straight shoots from dogwood, viburnum, willow, reed, or any straight-grained wood. 26-30 inches long, pencil-thick.
2. Straighten by heating over coals and bending while hot. Hold in position until cool.
3. Nock: cut a shallow notch in the back end to receive the bowstring.
4. Fletching: split feathers and tie 3 vanes 120 degrees apart near the nock using thin cordage or sinew. Glue with pine pitch. Fletching stabilizes flight — without it, accuracy past 10 feet is poor.
5. Point: sharpened hardwood tip (fire-hardened), knapped stone point, bone point, or any sharp metal lashed to the shaft.

## Sling

One of the most effective primitive ranged weapons. Devastating force — a skilled slinger can kill at 100+ yards. Used in warfare through the Middle Ages.

1. Cut a pouch from leather, canvas, or woven cloth, roughly 3×5 inches.
2. Attach two cords 24-30 inches long to each side of the pouch. One cord has a loop for your middle finger (retention cord). The other has a knot at the end (release cord) held between thumb and finger.
3. Place a round stone (egg-sized, smooth, dense) in the pouch.
4. Swing overhead 1-2 rotations to build speed, then release the knotted cord at the forward point of the swing.
5. Practice extensively. Accuracy takes hundreds of throws. But the weapon is free, ammunition is everywhere, and it's silent compared to firearms.

## Atlatl (Spear Thrower)

Extends effective spear range from 15 feet to 100+ feet by adding leverage.

1. A flat piece of wood 18-24 inches long with a hook or notch at one end and a handle at the other.
2. The dart (a light, flexible spear 5-7 feet long) rests on the atlatl with its butt end engaging the hook.
3. Throw overhand like a baseball throw. The atlatl acts as an extension of your arm, multiplying the force.
4. Effective hunting weapon for medium game. Used by indigenous peoples worldwide.

## Practical Expectations

None of these weapons are pick-up-and-use. All require significant practice. A first-time bow shooter will miss a deer-sized target at 15 yards. A first-time slinger can barely control direction. Start practicing NOW, not when you need to eat. Dedicate a practice target and throw/shoot daily.

> For hunting, traps and snares almost always outperform primitive weapons in calorie return per effort. Weapons are best for defense and opportunistic hunting. Set snares for reliable protein.
