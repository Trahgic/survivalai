Woodworking is the foundational building skill. With an axe, a saw, and a knife you can build shelters, furniture, tools, vehicles, and infrastructure. Understanding wood grain, basic joints, and tool use turns raw trees into functional structures.

## Understanding Wood

**Grain direction matters for everything.** Wood is strong along the grain (lengthwise) and weak across it. A board splits easily along the grain but resists breaking across it. Always orient structural members so the load runs along the grain, not across it.

**Green vs. dry wood:** Freshly cut (green) wood is easier to work — softer, splits cleaner, carves easier. But it shrinks as it dries (up to 8% across the grain, almost zero along it). This means joints tighten or loosen as wood dries. For permanent structures, use dry wood or plan for movement.

**Hardwood vs. softwood:** Hardwoods (oak, maple, ash, hickory) are stronger and more durable but harder to work. Softwoods (pine, spruce, cedar) are easier to work, lighter, and adequate for most construction. Use hardwood for tools, furniture joints, and high-stress applications.

## Splitting Logs into Lumber

Without a sawmill, split lumber is how you get flat boards.

1. Select straight-grained logs free of knots and twists.
2. Drive wedges (wooden or metal) into the end grain to split the log in half. Use a sledgehammer or mallet.
3. Split halves into quarters, quarters into eighths.
4. Hew (flatten) split faces with a broad axe or adze. Work with the grain, not against it.
5. A froe (L-shaped blade) and mallet split thin boards (shingles, staves) from short bolts of straight-grained wood.

## Essential Joints

**Butt joint:** Two pieces butted end-to-end or end-to-face. Weakest joint. Reinforce with pegs, nails, or lashing. Adequate for rough construction.

**Lap joint:** Both pieces notched halfway so they overlap flush. Much stronger than butt joint. Good for frames, furniture, gate construction.

**Mortise and tenon:** A rectangular hole (mortise) in one piece receives a shaped projection (tenon) from the other. The strongest wood joint. Used for door frames, timber framing, furniture. Cut the mortise first with a chisel, then shape the tenon to fit.

**Dado/housing joint:** A channel cut across the grain of one board receives the end of another board. Good for shelving, drawer construction.

**Dovetail:** Interlocking fan-shaped cuts. Extremely strong, resists pulling apart. Traditional for drawers and boxes. Requires precise cutting — practice on scrap first.

**Pegged joint:** Any joint reinforced with a wooden peg (treenail/trunnel) driven through a drilled hole. The peg expands as it absorbs moisture, locking permanently. Use a slightly oversized peg of hardwood in a softwood joint for maximum holding.

## Improvised Tools

**Mallet:** Short log with a branch left as a handle. Or bore a hole through a dense hardwood block and insert a handle.

**Chisel:** File or rasp shaped from a car leaf spring, lawnmower blade, or flat piece of steel. Grind an edge on a stone.

**Drawknife:** Any curved blade with two handles for pulling toward you. Excellent for peeling bark and shaping round stock. Can be improvised from a car spring.

**Measuring:** A story stick (straight stick with notch marks at consistent intervals) replaces a tape measure. Mark your body measurements — hand span, forearm (cubit), stride length — and use these consistently.

## Wood Selection by Application

| Use | Best Species | Why |
|-----|-------------|-----|
| Structural beams | Oak, ash, Douglas fir | High strength-to-weight ratio |
| Tool handles | Hickory, ash | Absorbs shock without breaking |
| Firewood | Oak, maple, locust | High BTU, long-burning coals |
| Carving | Basswood, pine, poplar | Soft, consistent grain |
| Fence posts | Black locust, cedar, osage | Naturally rot-resistant |
| Shingles/shakes | Cedar, pine, oak | Splits cleanly, weather-resistant |
| Bows/flexible items | Yew, ash, hickory, osage | High elasticity |
