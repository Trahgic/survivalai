Rivers are the most dangerous terrain feature in wilderness travel. More hikers and survivors die from water crossings than from animal attacks, falls, and exposure combined. The first rule is to avoid crossing if any alternative route exists, even if it adds hours.

## Assessing a Crossing

Before entering water, scout upstream and downstream for a better spot.

Good crossing signs: wide and shallow (current is slower), visible bottom, gentle downstream terrain (if you wash downstream, you want a beach — not rapids or a waterfall), even current without visible turbulence.

Bad crossing signs: narrow and deep (water compresses = fast current), murky water hiding depth and obstacles, current above knee height, downed trees or strainers (branches that let water through but trap bodies), rapids or drops downstream.

> Knee-deep fast water can knock you down. Thigh-deep fast water will knock anyone down. Do NOT attempt to walk across thigh-deep moving water. Ever.

## Walking Ford (Shallow Crossing)

1. Unbuckle your pack's chest strap and hip belt. If you go under, you need to shed the pack instantly — drowning with a pack on is common.
2. Use a sturdy pole (6+ feet) as a third leg. Plant it upstream and lean on it.
3. Face upstream at a 45-degree angle. Side-step across. Never cross your feet.
4. Shuffle slowly. Feel each step before committing weight. Rocks are slippery.
5. Group crossing: link arms in a line perpendicular to current. Strongest person upstream. Move one person at a time while others brace.
6. Cross at a slight downstream angle — fighting the current upstream wastes energy.

## Improvised Raft

For deeper crossings where swimming is too risky.

**Log raft:**
1. Gather 6-10 dry logs, 8-12 inches diameter, 8-10 feet long. Dead standing timber floats better than green wood.
2. Lay logs parallel. Lash cross-members across the top at each end and in the middle using square lashing.
3. Test in calm water near shore before loading. It should support your weight with at least 2-3 inches of freeboard.
4. Use a long pole to push in shallow water, a paddle (split board, flat branch) in deep water.
5. Stay low on the raft — standing raises the center of gravity and makes tipping easy.

**Poncho raft (gear transport):**
1. Lay poncho flat, snap side up.
2. Pile gear in the center, wrap and snap closed.
3. Tie off neck opening.
4. You swim alongside pushing the raft. Your gear stays dry.

**Trash bag float:**
1. Fill a large garbage bag with brush, grass, or trapped air.
2. Tie closed. This won't support your full weight but provides enough buoyancy to keep your head above water while you kick across.

## If You Fall In

1. Do NOT try to stand in fast current deeper than knee-height. Foot entrapment kills — your foot wedges between rocks, the current pins you face-down.
2. Float on your back, feet downstream, toes up. Use feet to push off rocks.
3. Angle toward shore using backstroke.
4. In calm water, roll to stomach and swim aggressively.
5. If caught in a strainer (downed tree), swim TOWARD it aggressively and climb up and over. Do NOT try to go under — the current pins you against the branches.

## Night Crossing

Never cross water you can't see the bottom of. If you must cross at night, choose the widest, shallowest section, probe every step with a pole, and have a partner on the far bank with a light and rope if possible.

## Cold Water

Water below 60°F causes cold shock — involuntary gasping, hyperventilation, and panic within the first 1-3 minutes. You have about 10 minutes of useful swimming before your muscles fail. Get across fast or get out.

Strip wet clothing immediately after crossing. Wring out and put back on (wet clothing is better than no clothing) or change into dry clothes if available. Build a fire.
