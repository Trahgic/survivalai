Moving at night is slower, more dangerous, and more disorienting than daytime travel. But sometimes it's necessary — evading threats, traveling in extreme heat (desert), or covering ground when time is critical. The keys are dark adaptation, route planning, and pace discipline.

## Dark Adaptation

Your eyes take 30-45 minutes to fully adapt to darkness. Rod cells in the retina handle night vision but take time to produce rhodopsin (the light-sensitive pigment).

1. Avoid all bright light for 30+ minutes before moving. If you must use light, use red light only — red wavelengths don't destroy dark adaptation.
2. If exposed to bright light (vehicle headlights, flashlight), cover one eye. When the light is gone, the covered eye retains its adaptation.
3. Use peripheral vision. Rod cells are concentrated at the edges of your retina, not the center. Look slightly to the side of what you're trying to see.
4. Scan slowly. Quick eye movements in darkness make objects disappear. Let your gaze linger.

## Planning Night Movement

1. Scout your route during daylight if possible. Memorize landmarks, terrain features, and hazards.
2. Identify catching features — large linear features (river, road, ridge) that you can't accidentally pass.
3. Set shorter legs between waypoints. Navigate in 100-200 meter segments.
4. Avoid cliffsides, water crossings, dense brush, and loose rock. Injuries in darkness are hard to treat and evacuation is nearly impossible.

## Movement Techniques

**On trails:** Walk slowly with knees slightly bent, feeling with each foot before committing weight. One arm extended in front of your face to catch branches at eye level. The other arm protecting your torso.

**Off trail:** Shuffle forward in small steps. Use a walking stick to probe the ground ahead. Move from tree to tree, pause, listen, then move again.

**In a group:** Stay close — within arm's reach. The lead person sets a slow pace. Use touch signals: tap shoulder to stop, squeeze arm for direction, tug backward to reverse. Keep conversation to whispers.

## Navigation Without Light

Stars: Polaris in the northern hemisphere gives you true north all night. The Big Dipper's pointer stars lead you there.

Moon: A first-quarter moon (right half lit) is roughly due south at sunset. A last-quarter moon (left half lit) is roughly due south at sunrise. A full moon is due south at midnight.

Terrain: Water flows downhill. Following a drainage downhill usually leads to larger streams, then rivers, then civilization. Ridges are easier to follow than valleys at night — fewer obstacles and better sky visibility.

Sound: Highway noise carries 2-5 miles on quiet nights. Dogs barking, machinery, trains — all give direction clues. Moving water indicates streams. Wind in pines sounds different from wind in bare branches — helps identify forest type.

## Pace Counting at Night

Your pace count changes at night — expect 20-30% more steps per 100 meters due to shorter, more cautious steps. Recalibrate by counting paces over a known distance before dark.

Use beads, pebbles in a pocket, or knots on a cord to track distance. Move one bead per 100 meters.

## Moon Phases and Light

Full moon on clear snow provides enough light to read a map. Full moon on bare ground gives usable shadow navigation. New moon or heavy overcast = true darkness, starlight only. Quarter moons give moderate ambient light.

Cloud cover matters more than moon phase. A full moon behind thick clouds gives less light than a new moon night with clear stars.

## When Not to Move at Night

Injury risk doubles in darkness. If you're not being pursued and don't have a time-critical reason to move, shelter in place and move at dawn. The 30 minutes before sunrise (nautical twilight) gives enough light to travel safely while retaining most of the night's concealment.
