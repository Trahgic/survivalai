Composting converts waste into the most valuable substance in a survival garden: fertile soil. When sewage systems fail, composting toilets solve two problems simultaneously — sanitation and soil fertility. Done correctly, thermophilic composting kills pathogens and produces safe, odorless humus.

## Basic Composting (Garden Waste)

The recipe: carbon + nitrogen + water + air.

**Browns (carbon):** dry leaves, straw, cardboard, wood chips, sawdust, dead plants. These provide structure and feed slow-decomposing organisms.

**Greens (nitrogen):** fresh grass, kitchen scraps, coffee grounds, manure, green plant material. These provide protein for decomposer organisms and heat the pile.

Ideal ratio: roughly 3 parts brown to 1 part green by volume. Too much green = slimy and smelly. Too much brown = slow and cold.

1. Build the pile at least 3×3×3 feet — smaller piles don't generate enough internal heat.
2. Layer browns and greens. Moisten each layer — should feel like a wrung-out sponge.
3. Turn (fork over) every 1-2 weeks to add oxygen. The pile should be hot in the center — 130-160°F is ideal. If it's not hot, add more greens or water.
4. Finished in 2-4 months when turned regularly. Finished compost is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling, and unrecognizable as its original inputs.

## Humanure Composting Toilet

When the sewer stops working, this is the long-term answer. Simpler and more hygienic than a pit latrine.

**The toilet:**
1. A 5-gallon bucket inside a wooden box with a toilet seat on top. That's it.
2. After each use, cover deposits with a generous handful of cover material — sawdust is best (absorbs moisture, controls odor, provides carbon). Rice hulls, dry leaves, or peat moss also work.
3. There should be zero odor if enough cover material is used. If it smells, add more.
4. When the bucket is full, carry it to the compost bin.

**The compost bin:**
1. Build a 4×4×4 foot bin from pallets, wire mesh, or boards.
2. Start with a 12-inch base layer of straw or coarse plant material.
3. Dump the bucket in the center. Cover immediately with a thick layer of straw, leaves, or grass. The cover layer is critical — it controls odor, flies, and rain exposure.
4. Rinse the bucket with a small amount of water. Pour rinse water on the pile. Add fresh cover material.
5. Continue adding full buckets, always covering each addition.
6. When the bin is full, start a second bin. Let the full bin sit undisturbed for one full year.

**After one year:** The internal heat (sustained 130°F+ for weeks during the active composting phase) kills all human pathogens including bacteria, viruses, parasites, and helminth eggs. The resulting material is safe, odorless humus. Apply to fruit trees and perennial gardens. For extra caution, avoid applying directly to root vegetables for the first cycle.

## Vermicomposting (Worm Bins)

Red wiggler worms convert kitchen scraps into the highest-quality fertilizer available.

1. Container: any opaque bin with drainage holes. 10-20 gallon capacity for a household.
2. Bedding: shredded newspaper, cardboard, or coconut coir moistened to sponge dampness.
3. Add 1 lb of red wiggler worms (NOT earthworms — different species).
4. Feed with kitchen scraps: fruit, vegetables, coffee, eggshells, bread. No meat, dairy, or oil.
5. Bury food under the bedding to prevent flies.
6. Worms eat roughly half their body weight daily. 1 lb of worms processes 3-4 lbs of scraps per week.
7. Harvest castings (worm poop) every 2-3 months by pushing material to one side, adding fresh bedding to the empty side, and feeding only the new side. Worms migrate to the food. Harvest the abandoned side.

Worm castings are the most potent natural fertilizer — apply a thin layer to garden beds or brew into "compost tea" by soaking in water for 24 hours and using as liquid fertilizer.

## Compost Tea (Liquid Fertilizer)

Fill a burlap sack or pillowcase with finished compost. Submerge in a bucket of water. Let steep 24-48 hours, agitating occasionally. The resulting brown liquid is rich in beneficial microorganisms and nutrients. Dilute 1:5 with water and apply to plants as a foliar spray or soil drench.
