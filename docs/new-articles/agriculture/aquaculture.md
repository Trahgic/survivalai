A small fish pond produces more protein per acre than any livestock operation. Fish convert feed to meat at roughly 1.5:1 ratio (compared to 7:1 for cattle). In a survival context, a functioning fish pond is a renewable, low-maintenance protein source.

## Best Species for Beginners

**Tilapia:** The easiest. Tolerates poor water quality, overcrowding, and low oxygen. Grows fast — harvest size in 6-9 months. Eats almost anything: algae, kitchen scraps, duckweed, insects, garden waste. Breeds readily. Downside: tropical — dies below 55°F. In cold climates, needs a greenhouse pond or indoor tank.

**Catfish (channel):** Hardy, tolerant of murky water, fast-growing. Bottom feeders — eat scraps, insects, worms, grain. Harvest size in 12-18 months. Survives cold winters. Native to most of North America.

**Bluegill/sunfish:** Small but prolific. Excellent for small ponds. Breeds aggressively. Good combined with catfish (catfish eat excess bluegill fry, controlling population).

**Trout:** Need cold, clean, well-oxygenated water. Harder to raise but thrive in mountain stream-fed ponds. Die above 70°F.

## Pond Construction

A 1/10-acre pond (roughly 20x30 feet, 4-6 feet deep) can sustainably produce 50-100 lbs of fish per year.

1. Choose a site with clay soil (holds water) near a water source but NOT in the floodplain.
2. Dig the pond. Minimum 4 feet deep in the deepest area (prevents complete freeze-through in winter and gives fish a cool refuge in summer). Shelved edges — shallow to deep.
3. If soil is sandy, line with clay (6 inches packed), bentonite, or plastic sheeting.
4. Fill from a reliable water source — spring, stream diversion, rainwater, well.
5. Let the pond stabilize for 2-4 weeks before stocking. Algae should start growing. This is good — it's the base of the food chain.

## Stocking

Start with fingerlings (2-4 inch juvenile fish) from a hatchery or wild-caught.

Stocking density for beginners: 1 fish per 3-5 gallons in tanks, or 1000-2000 fish per acre in ponds. For a small survival pond, 50-100 fingerlings is plenty.

## Feeding

Supplemental feeding dramatically increases yield:

- Kitchen scraps (vegetable waste, bread, cooked grain)
- Duckweed (grows on pond surface — fish eat it and it regrows continuously)
- Black soldier fly larvae (grow a larvae bin near the pond, drop larvae in daily)
- Worms from a compost bin
- Crushed grain (corn, wheat, barley)
- Insects attracted by a light over the pond at night

Feed 2-3% of total fish body weight per day. In a young pond, that's a handful twice daily.

## Water Quality

Fish need dissolved oxygen. Signs of low oxygen: fish gasping at the surface, especially at dawn.

Increase oxygen: agitation (waterfall, stream inflow, manual splashing), aquatic plants (produce oxygen during daylight), avoid overstocking, remove dead plant material from the bottom.

Water changes: if using tanks, replace 10-20% of water weekly. Ponds with inflow/outflow are self-renewing.

## Harvesting

Use a seine net (drag net) across the pond, or hook and line, or a fish trap at the outflow. Harvest the largest fish first. Leave breeding stock — at least 10-20% of the population.

Tilapia in a well-managed pond can provide a harvest every 2-3 months year-round in warm climates. In cold climates, harvest before winter or maintain a small heated indoor tank as breeding stock.

## Combining with Garden (Aquaponics Principle)

Fish waste water is excellent fertilizer. Route pond overflow or water changes to garden beds. The plants filter the water, removing nitrogen. The cleaned water can be returned to the pond. This creates a closed-loop food system — fish feed plants, plants clean water for fish.
