Clay vessels are among the most important survival technologies ever developed. Before pottery, you couldn't boil water, store grain, ferment food, or carry liquids efficiently. Clay is free, abundant in most soils, and the techniques are learnable in a day.

## Finding and Processing Clay

Clay is found near rivers, streams, road cuts, construction sites, and anywhere erosion has exposed subsoil layers. Look for soil that's smooth, sticky when wet, and holds a shape when formed into a ball.

**Test:** Roll a piece of wet soil into a pencil-thin coil. If you can wrap the coil around your finger without it cracking, you have usable clay.

**Processing raw clay:**
1. Dig clay and break into small chunks. Remove visible rocks, roots, and debris.
2. Soak in water for 24-48 hours until it dissolves into a slurry (slip).
3. Pour the slip through a screen or cloth to remove grit and organic material.
4. Let the screened slip settle for a few hours. Pour off excess water.
5. Spread the thick clay on a cloth or board in the sun until it reaches a workable consistency — like stiff bread dough.
6. Knead (wedge) for 5-10 minutes to remove air bubbles. Trapped air causes explosions during firing.

## Temper

Raw clay alone cracks during drying and shatters during firing. Temper is a non-clay material mixed in to control shrinkage and thermal shock.

**Best tempering materials:** fine sand, crushed rock, crusite shell, crushed fired pottery (grog), mica, chopped dry grass. Mix 15-25% temper by volume into the prepared clay.

## Hand-Building Techniques

**Pinch pot:** Start with a ball of clay. Push your thumb into the center, then pinch and rotate to thin the walls evenly. Good for small bowls and cups. Maximum size is limited by your hand span.

**Coil building (most versatile):**
1. Roll clay into ropes (coils) about 3/4 inch thick.
2. Spiral a coil for the base, pinching it flat and smooth.
3. Stack coils on top of each other, scoring and applying slip (watery clay) between each coil for bonding.
4. Smooth the interior and exterior with your fingers, a stick, or a smooth stone.
5. Build walls in stages — if the pot gets too tall before the lower walls firm up, it'll slump. Build 3-4 inches, let it firm for a few hours, then continue.

**Slab building:** Roll clay flat with a round log (like rolling dough). Cut flat pieces and join them with slip and scoring. Good for boxes, rectangular containers, and tiles.

## Drying

Slow drying prevents cracking. Cover pots with a damp cloth and let them dry slowly over 3-7 days in shade. Turn daily. Rush this step and you'll crack every piece. The clay is dry when it feels room-temperature to the touch (damp clay feels cool).

> The most common failure in primitive pottery is cracking during drying. Go slow. If you see cracks forming, rewet the surface slightly and cover more tightly.

## Firing

Unfired (greenware) clay dissolves in water. Firing transforms clay permanently into ceramic — waterproof and heat-resistant.

**Pit firing (no kiln needed):**
1. Dig a shallow pit 2 feet deep, 3-4 feet across.
2. Pre-warm the pots by a fire for 30 minutes — placing cold pots directly in a fire causes thermal shock and cracking.
3. Place pots in the pit surrounded by fuel (dry wood, dried dung, dry brush).
4. Light the fire and build it up gradually over 1-2 hours. Don't dump a bonfire on the pots — gradual heating is critical.
5. Maintain a strong fire for 2-4 hours. Target 1300-1700°F (the pottery should glow dull red in dim light).
6. Let the fire die and the pots cool completely before removing — at least 6-8 hours, preferably overnight.
7. Fired pots ring when tapped (unfired pots thud). They should be hard enough that you can't scratch them with a fingernail.

**Simple kiln:** Stack firebrick or rocks in a dome shape over the pots with an opening at the base for feeding fuel and an opening at the top for draft. This traps heat more efficiently than an open pit and reaches higher temperatures.

## Waterproofing

Low-temperature pit-fired pottery is slightly porous. For water storage:

- **Pine pitch lining:** Melt pine resin and swirl inside the hot pot. The resin coats the interior and seals pores.
- **Burnishing:** Before firing, when the clay is leather-hard, rub the surface vigorously with a smooth stone until it's glossy. This compresses the surface and reduces porosity.
- **Fat sealing:** Rub animal fat into the interior of a fired pot, then heat gently to drive it into the pores.
- **Repeated use:** Cooking pots seal naturally over time as fats and starite compounds fill the pores.

## What You Can Make

Cooking pots, water storage jars, drinking cups, grain storage containers, oil lamps (shallow dish with a fiber wick), fermentation crocks, plates and bowls, pipes (water conveyance), bricks, tiles, and kiln furniture for more advanced firing. Clay is the original do-everything material.
