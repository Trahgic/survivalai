Charcoal is wood that's been heated without enough oxygen to fully combust. The process drives off water, sap, and volatile compounds, leaving nearly pure carbon. It burns hotter, cleaner, and longer than raw wood — and it has uses beyond fuel including water filtration, soil improvement, and metal forging.

## Why Make Charcoal

**As fuel:** Charcoal burns at 1,000–2,000°F (540–1,090°C) — much hotter than wood. It produces almost no smoke. It's lighter than wood (easier to transport). It burns predictably and controllably.

**For water filtration:** Charcoal adsorbs chemicals, odors, and some contaminants from water. See gravity-filters.md. Activated charcoal is even more effective.

**For blacksmithing and metalwork:** Charcoal reaches the temperatures needed to forge iron and steel (2,200°F+ with forced air). It was the standard metalworking fuel before coal and coke.

**For soil amendment (biochar):** Charcoal worked into soil improves water retention, microbial habitat, and nutrient availability. It persists in soil for centuries.

**For medicinal use:** Activated charcoal absorbs poisons in the GI tract. See medication-reference.md.

**For gunpowder/black powder:** Charcoal is one of three ingredients in black powder (with potassium nitrate and sulfur). Willow charcoal is the traditional choice.

## Pit Method (Simplest)

The oldest and easiest method. Low yield but requires zero equipment.

1. Dig a pit 2–3 feet deep and 3–4 feet across.
2. Fill with dry hardwood cut to similar lengths (uniformity helps it burn evenly). Hardwoods make the best charcoal — oak, hickory, maple. Softwoods work but produce lighter, lower-quality charcoal.
3. Light the wood from the top and let it burn freely until the entire pit is engulfed and a thick bed of coals has formed. This takes 1–3 hours depending on wood size and quantity.
4. When the wood has burned down to glowing coals with no visible flame, cover the pit. Use sheet metal, flat rocks, or a thick layer of dirt (6+ inches). The goal is to cut off oxygen completely.
5. Seal all edges. If you see smoke escaping from a spot, pile more dirt on it. Any oxygen getting in will let the coals continue burning to ash — you want them to stop burning and cool as charcoal.
6. Leave covered for 24–48 hours. Do not open it early — oxygen rushing in will reignite the charcoal and you'll lose it to ash.
7. Uncover and collect the charcoal. It should be black, lightweight, and break with a clean snap (not crumbly like ash).

**Yield:** Roughly 20–25% by weight. 100 lbs of wood produces 20–25 lbs of charcoal.

## Mound Method (Charcoal Kiln)

Traditional commercial charcoal method used worldwide. Higher yield and better quality control than the pit method.

1. Stack wood in a dome or conical pile, 4–6 feet tall. Place a central chimney (a stack of sticks or a pole you'll pull out) in the center for air flow during the burn.
2. Cover the entire mound with a layer of green leaves or straw (prevents the next layer from filling gaps in the wood).
3. Cover with a thick layer of earth or clay (4–6 inches), leaving the chimney and a few small air holes around the base open.
4. Light the fire through the chimney at the top. The fire burns downward and outward through the pile.
5. Control the burn by opening and closing the air holes at the base. More air = faster, hotter burn. Less air = slower, more controlled carbonization. You want slow — fast burns produce too much ash.
6. Monitor the smoke color: white/grey smoke means water and volatiles are being driven off (good — the process is working). When smoke turns blue/clear and thin, carbonization is nearly complete.
7. Seal all air holes and the chimney. Let cool for 48–72 hours.
8. Uncover and harvest charcoal. Break open a few pieces — they should be fully black through the cross-section. Brown centers mean incomplete carbonization (usable but lower quality).

**Yield:** 25–35% with good technique.

## Retort Method (Metal Container)

The most controlled method using a closed container that separates the heat source from the wood being carbonized.

### Simple Retort — Two-Can Method

1. Fill a metal container (a paint can, ammo can, large coffee can, or 55-gallon drum) with wood cut to fit. Pack tightly — air gaps are wasted space.
2. Put the lid on with a small hole (¼ inch) for gases to escape. If using a drum, drill a small hole in the top.
3. Place this container inside or on top of a fire. An external fire heats the container; the wood inside pyrolyzes (thermally decomposes) without direct flame contact.
4. Gases and vapors escape through the small hole. These gases are flammable — they'll ignite at the hole, which actually provides additional heat to the process. This is a sign the retort is working.
5. When the gases stop flowing (no more flame at the hole), carbonization is complete.
6. Seal the hole. Remove the container from the fire. Let it cool completely before opening (12+ hours). Opening a hot retort allows oxygen in and the charcoal spontaneously ignites.

**Advantages:** Highest quality charcoal. Most consistent results. The wood never contacts flame directly, so you get pure, uniform charcoal with less ash contamination.

## Activating Charcoal

Activated charcoal has dramatically higher adsorption capacity than regular charcoal because the activation process creates millions of microscopic pores, increasing surface area by orders of magnitude.

### Chemical Activation (Improvised)

1. Crush charcoal into small pieces (pea-sized or smaller).
2. Soak in a calcium chloride solution (1 part calcium chloride to 3 parts water by weight) for 24 hours. Calcium chloride is available as road de-icing salt, DampRid, or from some pool supply stores.
3. Drain the solution.
4. Heat the soaked charcoal in a retort or covered container at high temperature (600°F+ / 315°C+) for 3+ hours.
5. Rinse with clean water to remove remaining chemicals.
6. Dry completely.

This produces a rough approximation of commercial activated carbon. It won't match pharmaceutical-grade activated charcoal, but it's significantly more effective than plain charcoal for water filtration and poison adsorption.

### Steam Activation (If You Have the Setup)

Pass steam through charcoal at 1,500–1,800°F (815–980°C). This requires a forge or very hot fire with steam injection. The steam reacts with the carbon to create the porous structure. This is the industrial method and produces the best results, but the temperatures are difficult to achieve in field conditions.

## Storage

- Store charcoal in a dry location. Charcoal absorbs moisture from the air — wet charcoal is harder to light and burns poorly.
- Keep in sealed containers or bags.
- Freshly made charcoal can spontaneously re-ignite if stored in a pile while still warm. Ensure it's fully cooled before storage.
- Charcoal dust is a fire hazard in enclosed spaces. Handle away from open flame.
- Properly stored charcoal lasts indefinitely — it's essentially pure carbon, which doesn't decompose.

## Safety

- Charcoal production creates carbon monoxide. Never operate a charcoal kiln or retort in an enclosed space.
- The gases released during pyrolysis are flammable and toxic. Stay upwind.
- Hot charcoal kilns can collapse. Don't stand on or lean against a burning mound.
- Never burn charcoal inside an enclosed shelter for heating — CO poisoning risk. Charcoal produces CO even more efficiently than wood because it burns with less visible smoke (giving a false sense of safety).
