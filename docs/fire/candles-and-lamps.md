When the grid goes down, darkness changes everything. Without light, productive hours shrink to daylight only. Tasks that need precision — medical care, reading, repair work — become impossible after sunset. Candles and oil lamps are the simplest, most reliable, and most sustainable lighting you can produce from raw materials.

## Candle Making

### Tallow Candles (Animal Fat)

The most historically common candle. Made from rendered animal fat (beef tallow, mutton tallow, bear fat, deer fat). See butchering-and-processing.md for fat rendering.

**Materials:**
- Rendered, strained tallow (clean, white fat with all meat particles removed)
- Wicking: cotton string, twisted cotton cloth strips, or braided plant fibers (milkweed, dogbane, or cattail leaf fibers). The wick should be loosely twisted or braided — too tight and it won't draw fuel.

**Dipping Method (Traditional):**
1. Melt tallow in a pot until liquid (around 170°F / 77°C). Don't overheat — tallow smokes and can flash above 400°F.
2. Cut wicks 2 inches longer than your desired candle length.
3. Dip the wick into the melted tallow. Pull it out and let it cool and harden (30 seconds to a minute).
4. Dip again, building up a layer each time. Repeat 20–30 times until the candle reaches desired thickness (½ to 1 inch diameter).
5. Straighten the candle between dips by rolling gently on a smooth surface while still warm.
6. Hang to fully cool and harden.

**Molding Method (Faster):**
1. Use any tube shape as a mold — a cardboard tube, metal can with the bottom cut off, PVC pipe section, or carved wood.
2. Thread the wick through the center. Tie the top end to a stick laid across the mold opening. Weight the bottom end or tie it around a small nail.
3. Pour melted tallow into the mold.
4. Let cool completely (4–8 hours). Tallow shrinks as it cools — top off with more melted tallow if a depression forms.
5. Slide the candle out of the mold (metal molds may need brief warming to release).

**Burn characteristics:** Tallow candles produce a warm, yellowish light. They smoke more than beeswax and have a faint animal smell. A ½-inch tallow candle burns approximately 1 hour per inch of length.

### Beeswax Candles

Superior to tallow in every way — brighter, cleaner burn, pleasant honey smell, no dripping if the wick is sized correctly. But beeswax requires beekeeping or finding wild hives.

Process is identical to tallow candles. Beeswax melts at a higher temperature (145°F / 63°C), burns longer, and is naturally harder.

### Improvised Candles

**Crayon candle:** A standard wax crayon burns for about 30 minutes. Stand it upright in clay or sand. The paper wrapper serves as the wick.

**Cheese wax candle:** The wax coating from wheels of cheese can be collected, melted, and cast around a wick.

**Shortening candle:** Punch a hole in the center of a can of solid shortening (Crisco). Insert a twisted cotton string as a wick. It burns for hours — the entire can is fuel.

**Butter or lard lamp:** See oil lamps below — any solid fat can fuel an improvised lamp.

## Oil Lamps

Oil lamps produce more light than candles and burn more efficiently. Any liquid fat or oil is potential lamp fuel.

### Simple Oil Lamp

1. **Container:** Any heat-resistant, non-flammable container — a bowl, cup, jar, shell, tin can, or hollowed stone. Something low and wide is more stable than tall and narrow.
2. **Fuel:** Any liquid oil — vegetable oil (olive, canola, coconut), rendered animal fat (liquid at warm temperatures), kerosene, lamp oil, even used cooking oil. Olive oil is one of the safest and cleanest-burning lamp oils.
3. **Wick:** Cotton string, twisted cotton cloth, a strip of cotton t-shirt, or a piece of natural fiber rope. The wick must be absorbent — it draws oil up from the reservoir by capillary action.
4. **Assembly:** Drape the wick over the edge of the container so one end is submerged in oil and the other end extends over the rim. Or use a wick holder — a piece of wire, a bent paperclip, or a cork with a hole punched through it that floats on the oil surface with the wick threaded through.
5. **Light the wick.** The flame should be small and steady. If it's smoky, the wick is too long — trim it. If it keeps going out, the wick isn't drawing enough oil — check that the wick end is fully submerged.

### Wick Sizing

The relationship between wick size and flame matters:
- Too thick a wick = smoky, sooty flame (too much fuel burning)
- Too thin a wick = weak flame that goes out (not enough fuel drawn)
- Start with a wick about the diameter of a thin shoelace. Adjust by adding or removing strands.

### Fuel Burn Rates and Light Output

| Fuel | Burn Rate | Light Quality | Smoke |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| Olive oil | Slow | Warm, steady | Very low |
| Vegetable oil (canola, etc.) | Moderate | Warm, steady | Low |
| Rendered tallow (liquid) | Moderate | Warm | Moderate |
| Kerosene | Moderate | Bright | Low (if trimmed well) |
| Fish oil | Moderate | Warm | High, strong odor |
| Motor oil | Slow | Dim | Very high, toxic — last resort only |

### Safety

- Place lamps on a stable, flat surface where they can't be knocked over.
- Keep flammable materials away from the flame.
- Never leave an oil lamp unattended, especially near children or in a tent/shelter.
- Ensure ventilation — oil lamps consume oxygen and produce CO in enclosed spaces. Not as much as a fire, but significant in a small sealed room.
- Don't use gasoline as lamp fuel. Its vapors are explosive.

## Rush Lights

The oldest form of household lighting. A dried rush (a common wetland plant) dipped in grease.

1. Harvest common rushes. Peel the outer green skin, leaving the white pith core. Leave one strip of green skin along the length for strength.
2. Dry the peeled rushes completely.
3. Draw the dried rush through melted tallow or grease slowly, coating the pith.
4. Repeat 2–3 times for a thicker coating.
5. Clip one end in a rush light holder (a small metal clip on a stand) at about 45 degrees.
6. Light the top end. A 2-foot rush light burns for about 15–20 minutes.

Rush lights produce a weak, flickering light — enough to navigate a room but not enough for detailed work. Their value is in simplicity — the materials are free and abundant.

## Pine Torch

A resinous stick of fatwood, lit at one end. Burns for 15–30 minutes with a bright, smoky flame. The easiest field lighting — find fatwood, light it, carry it. Traditional for moving between areas at night.

**Improved torch:** Split one end of a green stick into quarters (6 inches deep). Wedge resinous fatwood or dry bark into the split. Light the wedged material. The green stick handle doesn't burn, and the fatwood burns as a bright torch.

## Light Management

- **Position lamps at the work surface.** A single lamp 2 feet from your hands provides more useful light than three lamps across the room. Light intensity drops with the square of the distance.
- **Use reflectors.** A piece of foil, a mirror, a white-painted surface, or even a light-colored wall behind the lamp roughly doubles the effective light in one direction.
- **Protect from drafts.** A flickering flame is less useful than a steady one. Shield lamps from wind with a glass jar (improvised hurricane lamp), a can with one side cut away, or positioning in a sheltered corner.
- **Ration light.** If lamp oil or candle supply is limited, use light intentionally — for tasks that require it. Let your eyes adapt to darkness for navigation and routine movement. Full dark adaptation takes 20–30 minutes.
