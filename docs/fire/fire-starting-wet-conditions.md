Starting a fire in rain, snow, or saturated conditions is the hardest fire-starting scenario and the one where you need fire most. Everything is wet — the wood, the ground, your hands, your tinder. But fire is always possible if you understand where dry material hides and how to process wet wood into burnable fuel.

## Finding Dry Material in Wet Conditions

Water penetrates from the outside in. The interior of standing dead wood, the underside of overhangs, and the inside of bark are dry even in a downpour.

### Dry Tinder Sources

1. **Inner bark of dead standing trees.** Peel bark from a dead (not rotten) standing tree. The inner layers are protected from rain and are often dry enough to catch a spark. Cedar, birch, and poplar inner bark are excellent.
2. **Fatwood.** The resinous heartwood of dead pine and spruce stumps. Find a stump, kick it or split it open, and harvest the dark, sap-saturated core wood. Fatwood is essentially nature's fire starter — the resin makes it nearly waterproof and it burns hot enough to ignite wet wood. Look for a sweet pine smell and sticky, amber-colored grain.
3. **Dead branches still attached to trees.** Standing dead branches are off the ground and sheltered by the canopy. The thin dead twigs on the underside of low-hanging branches are often dry when everything on the ground is soaked. Snap them off — if they snap clean, they're dry. If they bend, they're wet.
4. **Bird's nests.** Old bird nests in trees are made of fine, dry material and are often sheltered enough to stay dry.
5. **Resin/pitch.** Scrape hardened resin from wounded pine, spruce, or fir trees. It's flammable regardless of moisture conditions. Glob it onto your tinder bundle.
6. **Under dense evergreen canopy.** The ground directly under thick spruce or fir trees is often dry — the canopy sheds rain away from the trunk. Check the litter layer at the tree base.
7. **Inside your pockets.** Dryer lint from your clothing, napkins, paper, cotton from medication bottles — search your pockets and pack for any dry fiber.

### Dry Kindling Sources

1. **Split wet wood.** The outside of a wet stick may be soaked but 1/4 inch beneath the surface is dry. Split sticks in half and in half again to expose dry interior wood. Use a knife baton technique: place the knife blade on the end of the stick, strike the spine of the knife with a baton (another stick) to drive it through. Split pieces dry in minutes near a heat source.
2. **Dead standing wood (not ground wood).** Wood lying on the ground wicks moisture from the soil. Standing dead wood drains and stays drier. Seek out dead standing saplings or snap dead branches from trees.
3. **Feather sticks.** Shave thin curls on a split stick — leave the curls attached. The thin shavings expose maximum dry surface area and catch fire quickly. Make several feather sticks as your first-stage kindling. This is the single most useful wet-weather fire skill.

## Feather Sticks — The Key Technique

A feather stick turns one piece of damp wood into ready-to-light tinder and kindling.

1. Split a piece of dead, standing wood to expose the dry interior. A piece about 1 inch thick and 8 inches long is ideal.
2. Hold the stick vertically, braced against a log or your knee.
3. Place your knife blade near the top at a shallow angle.
4. Push down with a smooth stroke, shaving a thin curl of wood. The curl should stay attached at the bottom — it curls outward like a feather.
5. Rotate the stick and repeat. Keep adding curls all around until the stick looks like a pom-pom of thin shavings.
6. The thin curls catch a spark or flame easily because of the high surface area. Once they light, the thicker core of the stick sustains the flame.

Make 3–5 feather sticks as your starter package. Light the finest curls, and the fire burns into the progressively thicker material on its own.

## Platform Fire

Never build a fire directly on wet or snowy ground — the moisture extinguishes it from below.

1. Lay a platform of green or wet logs side by side on the ground. These sacrifice themselves to keep the fire above the moisture.
2. Build your fire on top of the platform.
3. As the fire burns, the platform chars slowly underneath but keeps the fire elevated and dry.

In deep snow: stamp down the snow first, then lay the platform. Without a platform, a fire melts down through snow and eventually extinguishes itself in a pool of meltwater.

## Wet Weather Fire Sequence

1. **Prepare before igniting.** Get everything ready — tinder, feather sticks, split kindling, and fuel wood — before you strike a spark. Once you have flame, you can't pause to gather more wood.
2. **Create a dry workspace.** If it's actively raining, set up a tarp, poncho, or any cover over your fire-building area. Even leaning a slab of bark over the spot while you prep helps. You can remove the cover once the fire is established.
3. **Start small.** Light the finest tinder — fatwood shavings, birch bark, or resin-coated material. Shelter it from rain with your body.
4. **Feed gradually.** Feather sticks first, then split kindling (pencil thick), then thumb-thick splits. Each addition should be slightly larger than the last. Don't rush — let each stage fully ignite before adding more.
5. **Use split, not round, wood.** Split wood exposes dry interior surfaces. Round sticks present their wet bark to the fire and hiss and steam instead of burning.
6. **Lean wet wood near (not on) the fire.** Use the fire's heat to dry your next round of fuel. Once it stops steaming and hissing, it's ready to burn. Rotate wet wood through the drying zone.
7. **Don't smother it.** In eagerness to build a bigger fire, people pile too much wet wood on a small flame. The wet wood absorbs the heat and kills the fire. One piece at a time until the fire is strong enough to handle more.

## Resin and Sap as Accelerants

Tree resin is a natural waterproof fire accelerant.

- Collect hardened sap globules from pine, spruce, fir, or other conifers. They're often found where branches have broken off or bark is damaged.
- Smear resin onto your tinder bundle or feather sticks. It catches fire easily and burns hot and long.
- Liquid sap (fresh) also works but is stickier and harder to handle.
- Birch bark contains birch oil (betulin) — it's the reason birch bark burns even when wet. The oil is waterproof and highly flammable.

## Maintaining Fire in Continuous Rain

Once your fire is going, keeping it alive in rain is easier than starting it:

- Build a fire reflector on the windward side and a partial overhead cover to shield from direct rain. A lean-to of green branches over the fire (high enough to not catch) sheds most rainfall.
- Keep a supply of split, drying wood staged near the fire.
- Feed the fire consistently — don't let it burn down to nothing and try to rebuild. Maintaining a moderate fire is easier than restarting.
- A deep bed of coals is more rain-resistant than flames. Once you have good coals, rain that hits them hisses and steams off. Maintain the coal bed and feed it to keep it active.
- At night, bank the fire by covering coals with ash. This preserves the coals for morning restart without constant feeding.

## When Nothing Works

If conditions are truly impossible — sustained heavy rain, everything saturated, no standing dead wood, no resin — accept that fire may not happen.

- Focus on shelter and insulation instead. A well-built debris hut keeps you warm without fire.
- If you have a stove with solid fuel (hexamine tablets, Esbit, Sterno) or a liquid fuel stove, this is when you use it. These are guaranteed ignition regardless of conditions.
- Chemical fire starters (magnesium bar shavings, commercial fire starters, WetFire cubes) are designed for exactly this scenario. Carry them.
- Wait for a break in the rain. Even a 20-minute dry spell gives you enough time to get a fire going if your materials are prepped and ready.
