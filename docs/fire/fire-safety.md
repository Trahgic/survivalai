Fire is your most valuable survival tool and your most dangerous one. An uncontrolled fire destroys shelter, supplies, and forest. Smoke inhalation kills faster than the flames themselves. In a grid-down scenario, there's no fire department coming. Fire safety isn't optional — it's the difference between using fire and being destroyed by it.

## Campfire Safety

### Site Preparation

1. Clear a circle at least 10 feet in diameter around the fire site. Remove all leaves, pine needles, dry grass, and other combustible material down to bare mineral soil if possible.
2. If you can't clear to mineral soil, scrape away the duff (decomposing leaf/needle layer) at minimum. This layer smolders and can spread fire underground for hours before surfacing.
3. Keep the fire at least 15 feet from tents, tarps, shelters, and stored gear. Sparks travel — especially from cedar, pine, and poplar.
4. Avoid building fires under low-hanging branches. Radiant heat rises and ignites branches above. Even green branches will catch if the fire is large enough.
5. Build a fire ring from rocks if available — it contains the fire and prevents lateral spread. Avoid wet rocks near rivers (steam explosion risk).
6. Never build a fire against a living tree — you'll kill the tree and risk a crown fire.

### While Burning

1. Never leave a fire unattended. Wind shifts, sparks, and popping embers start fires when nobody's watching.
2. Keep the fire manageable. A fire that's "just a little too big" can go from controlled to uncontrolled in seconds with a wind gust.
3. Have water, dirt, or sand within arm's reach to extinguish sparks or flare-ups. A bucket of water nearby is the minimum safety measure.
4. Watch for wind. Gusty or shifting winds are the most common cause of fire escapes. Reduce fire size in windy conditions.
5. Don't burn trash with unknown contents — aerosol cans explode, plastics release toxic fumes, batteries ignite.

### Extinguishing

"If it's too hot to touch, it's too hot to leave."

1. Let the fire burn down to coals. Spread the coals with a stick.
2. Pour water on the coals slowly — steam will rise. Stir the wet coals and ash.
3. Pour more water. Stir again. Continue until no hissing or steaming occurs.
4. Feel the ash and soil around the fire with the back of your hand (not palm — less sensitive). If any spot is warm, add more water.
5. If water is scarce, cover coals with mineral soil (not organic material), mix thoroughly, and repeat.
6. Check the area around the fire ring for embers or heated soil. One missed ember can reignite hours later.

## Smoke Inhalation

Smoke kills more people in structure fires than flames do. In a survival context, chronic smoke exposure from cooking fires and poorly ventilated shelters causes long-term lung damage.

### Acute Smoke Inhalation

**Signs:** Coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing, hoarse voice, black soot around nostrils or mouth, burns on the face or neck, confusion, headache.

**Treatment:**
1. Move to fresh air immediately.
2. If breathing is labored, position sitting upright (easier to breathe than lying flat).
3. If not breathing, start rescue breathing (see cpr-and-choking.md).
4. Monitor for delayed airway swelling — smoke and heat damage to the throat can cause swelling that closes the airway 2–12 hours after exposure. Hoarseness that's getting worse is a warning sign.
5. Give fluids. Smoke exposure dehydrates the airway tissues.

### Reducing Smoke Exposure

- Use dry, well-seasoned hardwood. Wet wood and softwood produce far more smoke.
- Ensure adequate airflow to the fire. A well-oxygenated fire burns cleaner. A smoldering, oxygen-starved fire produces maximum smoke.
- Cook and warm with fires that have burned down to coals — coals produce radiant heat with minimal smoke.
- Position yourself upwind of the fire when possible.
- In enclosed shelters, ensure a clear chimney or smoke hole. The smoke opening should be directly above or near the fire, with a draft path that pulls smoke upward and out.
- Never sleep in a fully enclosed space with a fire. Carbon monoxide from incomplete combustion is odorless and accumulates to lethal levels.

## Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

CO is produced by any fire burning with insufficient oxygen. It's odorless, colorless, and tasteless. You won't know it's happening until symptoms hit.

**Symptoms (progressive):**
1. Headache — the first and most common sign
2. Dizziness, nausea
3. Confusion, impaired judgment
4. Weakness, inability to move
5. Loss of consciousness
6. Death

**In a survival shelter with a fire or stove:**
- Always maintain ventilation — a fresh air inlet low on one wall and a smoke exit high on the opposite wall creates cross-ventilation.
- If anyone in the shelter develops a headache, ventilate immediately. Open the door, create airflow.
- Never block all ventilation to "keep the heat in." The heat isn't worth dying for.
- A candle flame is a rough CO indicator — if a candle burns blue or struggles to stay lit, oxygen is being depleted. Ventilate.

## Wildfire Awareness

In a grid-down scenario, wildfire risk increases dramatically — no one is managing forests, no fire crews respond, and people with less experience are building fires.

### Prevention

- Observe fire weather: hot, dry, windy days are high-risk. Reduce or eliminate open fires during these conditions.
- Clear defensible space around permanent shelters — 30 feet minimum of reduced vegetation.
- Store fuel (firewood, kindling, flammable materials) away from structures.

### If a Wildfire Approaches

1. **Assess the situation.** Which direction is the fire moving? How fast? Do you have time to prepare or should you evacuate immediately? Fires can move faster than a person can run, especially uphill and with wind.
2. **Evacuate early if possible.** Don't wait until the fire is close. Leave perpendicular to the fire's direction of travel — go sideways, not directly away.
3. **If you can't evacuate:**
   - Get into a cleared or burned area. Burned ground won't burn again — a previously burned area is the safest place during a wildfire.
   - Get to water — a lake, river, or pond. Submerge if necessary. Cover your head with wet cloth.
   - If in a structure, close all windows and doors. Fill sinks and tubs with water. Wet down the roof if possible. Stay inside — the building provides protection from radiant heat. Most structures survive brush fire contact if they have cleared defensible space.
   - If caught in the open, find a depression or ditch. Lie face down. Cover yourself with soil or a wet blanket. Protect your airway — breathe through a wet cloth close to the ground (air near the ground is cooler and has more oxygen).
4. **Smoke is the first danger.** You'll encounter thick smoke before flames. Breathe through a wet cloth. Stay low — smoke rises. If visibility drops to near zero, orient by following fences, roads, or waterways downhill.

## Burns from Fire Accidents

See burns.md for full treatment. Quick summary for campfire burns:

1. Cool the burn with lukewarm water for 10–20 minutes.
2. Don't apply ice, butter, or toothpaste.
3. Cover with a clean, non-stick dressing.
4. Pain management: ibuprofen 400–600 mg.
5. Watch for infection starting 24–48 hours after.

## Teaching Fire Safety to Children

If children are in your group:

- Establish a clear "fire zone" boundary — children don't cross it without adult supervision
- Teach stop, drop, and roll before the first fire is built
- Show them exactly what happens when fabric catches fire (demonstrate with a scrap)
- Assign them fire-related tasks appropriate to their age — carrying kindling, staging wood — so they learn to respect fire by working with it under supervision, not by fearing it from a distance
