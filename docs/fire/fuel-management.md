Running out of fuel in cold weather can kill you. Having the wrong fuel means a smoky, weak fire that doesn't cook food or warm you. Understanding which wood burns well, how to process it, and how to manage your supply turns fire from a struggle into a reliable tool.

## Hardwood vs. Softwood

**Hardwoods** (deciduous trees — oak, hickory, maple, ash, beech, birch): Dense, burn slow and hot, produce excellent long-lasting coals. Best for cooking and overnight heat. Harder to split and ignite.

**Softwoods** (conifers — pine, spruce, fir, cedar): Less dense, ignite easily, burn fast and hot with more flame. Good for starting fires and quick heat. Produce more sparks and smoke. Resinous species (pine, spruce) pop and throw sparks. Coals don't last.

**Best strategy:** Start with softwood (easy ignition, fast heat) and transition to hardwood (sustained heat, good coals) once the fire is established.

## Wood by Burn Quality

### Excellent (Long, Hot Burns)

- **Oak** — The gold standard. Burns very hot, very long, excellent coals. Hard to split but worth the effort.
- **Hickory** — Burns hottest of all common North American woods. Fantastic cooking wood (hickory-smoked flavor). Dense and hard to split.
- **Ash** — Burns well even when somewhat green (one of the few woods that does). Easy to split. Excellent all-around firewood.
- **Hard maple** — Hot, long-burning, good coals. Moderate to split.
- **Beech** — Hot burn, good coals. Tends to spark when dry.

### Good

- **Birch** — Burns hot and fast. Bark is an excellent fire starter. The wood doesn't last as long as denser hardwoods but lights easily.
- **Cherry** — Pleasant smell, moderate heat, easy to split. Good cooking wood.
- **Elm** — Decent heat but extremely difficult to split (stringy grain). If you can break it down, it burns fine.
- **Locust** — Very dense, very hot, very hard to split. Excellent if you can process it.

### Fair (Fast-Burning, Good for Starting)

- **Pine** — Easy to split, lights quickly, burns fast. Resinous — pops and sparks. Creosote buildup in chimneys from extended pine burning. Good kindling, poor sustained fuel.
- **Spruce/Fir** — Similar to pine. Quick ignition, moderate heat, burns fast.
- **Cottonwood/Poplar** — Low density, low heat output, burns very fast. Better than nothing but you'll go through enormous quantities.
- **Willow** — Poor firewood. Low heat, high moisture content even when dead. Use only when nothing else is available.
- **Cedar** — Excellent for kindling (lights easily, splits easily, aromatic). Burns fast with low heat as fuel. Pops and throws sparks aggressively — keep it away from tents and tarps.

### Avoid

- **Green (live) wood** — 50%+ moisture content. Hisses, steams, smokes, and barely burns. Use only as a long fire log or reflector where slow, non-burning is desirable.
- **Rotted/punky wood** — Too decomposed to produce useful heat. Burns to ash almost instantly. Only useful as tinder (smoldering punk) or insect repellent (smoky, smoldering pieces).
- **Driftwood** — Salt-impregnated wood produces toxic fumes when burned. Avoid for cooking. Acceptable for signal fires or outdoor warming at a distance.
- **Treated/painted wood** — Releases toxic chemicals (arsenic from pressure-treated wood, lead from old paint). Never burn for cooking. Avoid for warming if possible.

## Processing Firewood

### Batoning

Splitting wood without an axe using a knife and a baton (a stick used as a hammer).

1. Stand a short section of wood on end (or against a flat log).
2. Place your knife blade across the top, centered.
3. Strike the spine of the knife with the baton, driving the blade into the wood.
4. Continue striking, driving the knife through until the wood splits.
5. Repeat to quarter or further split the pieces.

**Requirements:** A sturdy, full-tang fixed-blade knife. Folding knives and thin blades break under batoning stress. The baton should be a dense hardwood stick, 2–3 inches in diameter.

### Breaking Without Tools

- **Lever against a tree:** Place a dead stick against the base of a sturdy tree and push or kick to snap it at the contact point. Works for wrist-thick and smaller wood.
- **Stomp break:** Stand a stick vertically with one end on the ground and stomp the middle. The bending force snaps it.
- **Two-tree break:** Wedge a stick horizontally between two trees and push against the middle.

### Sizing

- **Tinder:** Pencil-lead to matchstick thickness
- **Kindling:** Matchstick to pencil thickness
- **Small fuel:** Pencil to thumb thickness
- **Medium fuel:** Thumb to wrist thickness
- **Sustained fuel:** Wrist to forearm thickness
- **Overnight logs:** Forearm thickness and larger

## Drying and Seasoning

Green or wet wood burns poorly. Drying it is worth the effort.

**Quick drying methods:**
- Split the wood (split pieces dry 4–10x faster than round pieces because of exposed surface area)
- Lean split pieces near (not in) the fire. The radiant heat drives moisture out. Rotate pieces.
- Stack in a crisscross pattern for airflow between pieces.
- Place on a sun-warmed rock in direct sunlight.

**Seasoning for long-term camp:**
- Cut and split firewood and stack it off the ground (on rails, rocks, or a pallet) in a covered area. Air needs to circulate around the wood.
- In temperate climates, most hardwoods need 6–12 months to properly season. Softwoods season in 3–6 months.
- Properly seasoned wood has visible cracks on the end grain, sounds hollow when two pieces are knocked together, and has a moisture content below 20%.
- Always have your next batch drying while you burn your current supply.

## Fuel Conservation

In cold weather or long-term situations, conserving fuel is critical. You'll burn more wood than you expect.

**A campfire warming one person overnight burns roughly 30–50 pounds of hardwood.** For a week, that's 200–350 pounds of firewood — just for nighttime warming of a single person. Add cooking and you're moving serious weight in wood daily.

**Conservation strategies:**
- Use a rocket stove or Dakota fire hole for cooking — they use a fraction of open-fire fuel.
- Build smaller fires. A focused, well-built small fire warms you better than a wasteful large one.
- Insulate your shelter so body heat does more of the work.
- Use a reflector wall to direct heat where you need it instead of losing it in all directions.
- Use the star fire layout for overnight burning — only the tips burn, lasting hours on a single loading.
- Bank coals under ash at night instead of feeding fresh wood.
- Collect and stack firewood during daylight — doing it in the dark and cold is miserable and inefficient.
- Process wood to proper sizes. Oversized pieces waste heat. Undersized pieces burn out too fast.

## Gathering Firewood — The Rule of Threes

Before nightfall, gather three times more firewood than you think you'll need. You will use more than you expect, every time. Gathering in the dark and cold is demoralizing and dangerous.

Dedicate time each day to firewood collection. In cold-weather camps, firewood gathering is often the single biggest daily task — taking 2–4 hours for sustained operations. Plan for it.
