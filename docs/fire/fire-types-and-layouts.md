Different situations call for different fires. A cooking fire isn't a signal fire isn't a warming fire. Choosing the right layout saves fuel, produces the heat pattern you need, and reduces your workload.

## Teepee Fire

**Best for:** Quick heat, boiling water, fast warmth.

Lean kindling and then fuel sticks against each other in a cone shape, with tinder in the center. The fire climbs upward through the cone, drawing air from all sides.

**Pros:** Lights easily, produces flame quickly, concentrates heat upward (good for hanging a pot above).
**Cons:** Burns fast, requires frequent feeding, unstable (the cone collapses as sticks burn through). Not great for sustained cooking — you're constantly rebuilding the cone.

## Log Cabin Fire

**Best for:** Cooking, producing coals, sustained heat.

Stack fuel in alternating layers — two parallel sticks, then two more perpendicular on top, continuing upward like building with Lincoln logs. Place tinder and kindling in the center hollow.

**Pros:** Burns evenly and predictably. Creates excellent coals for cooking. Flat top surface supports pots and pans. Self-feeding as upper layers fall into the fire.
**Cons:** Slower to establish than a teepee. Requires somewhat uniform stick sizes.

## Long Fire

**Best for:** Sleeping beside, warming a full-body length, long-duration heat.

Lay two large green logs (6+ inches diameter) parallel, 6–8 inches apart. Build the fire between them. The logs contain and direct the heat in a line.

**Pros:** Radiates heat evenly along your body length — lie beside it to sleep. The green logs burn very slowly, lasting hours. Pots can rest on the logs over the fire.
**Cons:** Requires large logs. Not the most fuel-efficient.

**Variation — top-down long fire:** Stack three or more logs on top of each other between stakes driven at each end to hold the stack. Build the fire on top. As the top log burns, it ignites the one below. A well-built stack burns 6–8 hours without maintenance. Excellent for overnight warmth.

## Star Fire (Indian Fire)

**Best for:** Fuel conservation, long-duration low heat.

Arrange 4–6 logs in a star pattern, all pointing inward to a central fire. Only the tips burn. Push logs inward as the tips are consumed.

**Pros:** Extremely fuel-efficient — only the tips burn at any time. A single feeding of logs can last hours. Low maintenance — just push logs in periodically. Produces a small, consistent fire.
**Cons:** Low heat output. Not great for cooking large meals. Slow to establish.

## Dakota Fire Hole

**Best for:** Concealment, wind protection, fuel efficiency.

Detailed construction in cooking-without-power.md. Summary: two connected holes — fire burns in the main hole, air feeds through a tunnel from the second hole. Below ground level, so wind-resistant and nearly invisible at night.

**Pros:** Nearly smokeless once established (excellent combustion). Wind-proof. Low light signature. Very fuel-efficient. Pot sits on top of the fire hole for cooking.
**Cons:** Requires digging (can't use in rocky ground, frozen ground, or high water table). Not great for group warming (heat goes up, not sideways). Harder to maintain than an above-ground fire.

## Keyhole Fire

**Best for:** Combined warmth and cooking.

Dig or arrange rocks in a keyhole shape — a main fire circle connected to a narrow cooking channel. Burn a large fire in the circle for warmth and coal production. Rake hot coals into the narrow channel for controlled cooking.

**Pros:** Separate heat zones — hot fire for warmth, controlled coals for cooking. Cook without reaching over the main fire.
**Cons:** Requires more setup. Needs rocks or digging.

## Reflector Fire

**Best for:** Directing heat toward a shelter opening.

Build a wall of green logs, stacked stones, or banked earth behind the fire. The wall reflects radiant heat in one direction — toward your lean-to or sleeping area.

**Construction:** Stack green logs 3–4 feet high, leaning against support stakes driven into the ground. Angle the reflector slightly forward (leaning toward the fire) to direct heat downward toward your sleeping position rather than straight up.

**Pros:** Dramatically increases effective warmth in one direction. Combined with a lean-to, you get radiant heat from the fire plus reflected heat from the wall.
**Cons:** Requires significant materials for the reflector wall.

## Signal Fire

**Best for:** Attracting rescue attention.

Build a large, ready-to-light fire in an open, visible area (hilltop, clearing, lakeshore). Prepare it in advance so it can be lit quickly when you hear or see potential rescuers.

**For smoke (daytime signaling):** Once the fire is burning hot, add green vegetation, damp leaves, or wet wood. The moisture creates thick white smoke visible for miles. Three fires in a triangle (spaced 100 feet apart) is the international distress signal.

**For flame (nighttime signaling):** Build big. Dry wood. Maximum flame height. Three fires in a triangle if possible.

**Preparation:** Keep the signal fire covered with bark or a tarp to protect from rain. Stage dry tinder and kindling underneath so it lights fast. You may only have minutes when an aircraft passes overhead.

## Choosing the Right Fire

| Situation | Best Fire Type |
|-----------|---------------|
| Quick warmth, emergency | Teepee |
| Cooking | Log cabin → cook on coals, or keyhole |
| Sleeping beside overnight | Long fire or top-down long fire |
| Limited fuel | Star fire |
| Windy conditions | Dakota fire hole |
| Concealment needed | Dakota fire hole |
| Directing heat to shelter | Reflector fire |
| Signaling for rescue | Signal fire (open area, maximum visibility) |
| Boiling water quickly | Teepee or rocket stove |

## Fire Management Tips

- **Don't build bigger than needed.** Large fires waste fuel and force you to sit far away. A small, hot fire is more useful than a large, cool one.
- **Feed from one side.** Push all new fuel in from the same direction. This creates a gradient: hot coals at one end, fresh fuel at the other, and a cooking zone in between.
- **Build a coal bed before cooking.** Flames are uneven and unpredictable. Coals provide steady, consistent heat. Let the fire burn down before putting food over it.
- **Bank the fire at night.** Cover coals with a thick layer of ash (not dirt — dirt can smother them completely). Banked coals stay alive for 8+ hours. In the morning, scrape away the ash, add tinder and kindling, and blow the coals back to life.
- **Keep fuel dry and staged.** A stack of dry wood near (not in) the fire, protected from rain, means you're never scrambling for fuel in the dark.
