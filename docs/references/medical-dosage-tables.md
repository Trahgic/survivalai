> **CRITICAL DISCLAIMER:** This reference provides standard dosing information for emergency situations when professional medical care is unavailable. Medications have interactions, contraindications, and side effects not fully covered here. Use medical references when available. Seek professional care when any option exists. Wrong dosages or inappropriate medications can kill.

## Over-the-Counter Pain and Fever

### Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

| Age/Weight | Single Dose | Frequency | Max Daily Dose |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Adult (150+ lbs) | 500–1,000 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 3,000 mg (4,000 mg absolute max) |
| Child 6–11 years (48–95 lbs) | 325 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 1,625 mg |
| Child 2–5 years (24–47 lbs) | 160 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 800 mg |
| Infant 6–23 months (12–23 lbs) | 80 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 400 mg |

**Warnings:** Liver damage at doses above 4,000 mg/day or with alcohol use. Do not combine with other acetaminophen-containing products. Liver failure from acetaminophen overdose is one of the most common causes of acute liver failure.

### Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)

| Age/Weight | Single Dose | Frequency | Max Daily Dose |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Adult (150+ lbs) | 200–400 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 1,200 mg (OTC) / 3,200 mg (prescription max) |
| Child 6–11 years (48–95 lbs) | 200 mg | Every 6–8 hours | 600 mg |
| Child 2–5 years (24–47 lbs) | 100 mg | Every 6–8 hours | 300 mg |
| Infant 6–23 months (12–23 lbs) | 50 mg | Every 6–8 hours | 150 mg |

**Pediatric weight-based:** 5–10 mg per kg of body weight per dose, every 6–8 hours.

**Warnings:** Take with food. Can cause stomach bleeding and kidney damage with prolonged use. Don't use in late pregnancy. Don't combine with aspirin (reduces aspirin's blood-thinning effect). Avoid in dehydration or kidney disease.

### Aspirin

| Who | Single Dose | Frequency | Max Daily Dose |
|-----|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Adult | 325–650 mg | Every 4–6 hours | 4,000 mg |
| Heart attack dose | 325 mg | Chew immediately | Single dose |

**Warnings:** Do NOT give to anyone under 18 (risk of Reye's syndrome — a rare but potentially fatal condition). Blood thinner — don't use before surgery or with active bleeding.

## Over-the-Counter Medications

### Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — Antihistamine

| Age/Weight | Dose | Frequency | Notes |
|-----------|------|-----------|-------|
| Adult | 25–50 mg | Every 4–6 hours | Max 300 mg/day |
| Child 6–11 years | 12.5–25 mg | Every 4–6 hours | Max 150 mg/day |
| Child 2–5 years | 6.25 mg | Every 4–6 hours | Max 37.5 mg/day |

**Uses:** Allergic reactions, hives, itching, insomnia, motion sickness. For anaphylaxis, diphenhydramine is a supplement to epinephrine, NOT a replacement.

**Warning:** Causes drowsiness. Don't combine with alcohol or other sedatives.

### Loperamide (Imodium) — Anti-Diarrheal

| Who | Initial Dose | Subsequent | Max Daily |
|-----|-------------|------------|-----------|
| Adult | 4 mg (2 caplets) | 2 mg after each loose stool | 16 mg |
| Child 9–11 years | 2 mg | 1 mg after each loose stool | 6 mg |
| Child 6–8 years | 2 mg | 1 mg after each loose stool | 4 mg |

**Warning:** Do NOT use for bloody diarrhea or diarrhea with high fever — these suggest bacterial dysentery, and stopping the diarrhea traps the pathogen. Treat the infection, not the symptom.

### Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)

**WHO Formula (mix per liter of clean water):**
- 6 level teaspoons sugar
- 1/2 level teaspoon salt

Drink in small sips throughout the day. This saves more lives from diarrheal disease than any medication. See **dehydration-and-rehydration.md** for full protocol.

## Fish Antibiotics

Fish antibiotics sold for aquarium use contain the same active compounds as human antibiotics, manufactured to the same USP standards. In a situation with absolutely no access to medical care, these are a viable option.

> **Use antibiotics only when bacterial infection is confirmed or strongly suspected.** Signs: wound redness spreading beyond the edges, red streaks, pus, increasing pain after initial improvement, fever. Don't use antibiotics for viral infections (colds, flu, most sore throats) — they don't help and breed resistance.

### Amoxicillin (Fish Mox)

**Broad-spectrum, first choice for many common infections.**

| Who | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|-----|------|-----------|----------|
| Adult | 500 mg | Every 8 hours | 7–10 days |
| Child (weight-based) | 25 mg/kg/day | Divided into 3 doses | 7–10 days |

**Example:** A 44-lb (20 kg) child: 25 × 20 = 500 mg per day, divided into three doses = ~165 mg every 8 hours.

**Used for:** Ear infections, sinus infections, dental infections, urinary tract infections, wound infections, pneumonia, strep throat.

**Contraindication:** Penicillin allergy. If the person is allergic to penicillin, do NOT use amoxicillin. Use azithromycin or ciprofloxacin instead.

### Ciprofloxacin (Fish Flox)

**Broad-spectrum, good for gut and urinary infections.**

| Who | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|-----|------|-----------|----------|
| Adult | 500 mg | Every 12 hours | 7–14 days |

**Used for:** Urinary tract infections, traveler's diarrhea (bacterial), intra-abdominal infections, bone/joint infections, severe wound infections resistant to amoxicillin.

**Warnings:** NOT for children under 18 (can damage growing cartilage). Can cause tendon rupture — stop immediately if you feel tendon pain. Avoid with dairy products and antacids (reduce absorption). Take 2 hours before or 6 hours after dairy/antacids.

### Azithromycin (Fish Zithro)

**Good penicillin alternative.**

| Who | Dose | Schedule |
|-----|------|----------|
| Adult | 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg days 2–5 | Once daily for 5 days |
| Child | 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg days 2–5 | Once daily for 5 days |

**Used for:** Respiratory infections, sinus infections, ear infections, skin infections, some STIs. First choice for people allergic to penicillin.

### Metronidazole (Fish Zole)

**Antibiotic and antiparasitic.**

| Who | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|-----|------|-----------|----------|
| Adult | 500 mg | Every 8 hours | 7–10 days |

**Used for:** Anaerobic infections (deep wound infections, dental abscesses), parasitic infections (giardia, amoebic dysentery), C. difficile.

**Warning:** No alcohol during treatment or for 48 hours after — causes violent nausea and vomiting (disulfiram-like reaction).

### Cephalexin (Fish Flex)

**First-generation cephalosporin.**

| Who | Dose | Frequency | Duration |
|-----|------|-----------|----------|
| Adult | 500 mg | Every 6 hours | 7–10 days |
| Child | 25–50 mg/kg/day | Divided into 4 doses | 7–10 days |

**Used for:** Skin and soft tissue infections, wound infections, UTIs, bone infections. Good choice for MRSA coverage (some strains).

**Note:** ~10% cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy. If the penicillin allergy was severe (anaphylaxis), don't use cephalosporins.

## Antibiotic Selection Guide

| Infection Type | First Choice | Alternative |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Wound infection | Amoxicillin or cephalexin | Ciprofloxacin |
| Dental abscess | Amoxicillin + metronidazole | Azithromycin + metronidazole |
| UTI | Ciprofloxacin | Amoxicillin |
| Ear infection | Amoxicillin | Azithromycin |
| Strep throat | Amoxicillin | Azithromycin |
| Pneumonia | Amoxicillin | Azithromycin |
| Diarrhea (bacterial) | Ciprofloxacin | Azithromycin |
| Giardia/parasites | Metronidazole | — |
| Skin infection (cellulitis) | Cephalexin | Amoxicillin |

## Medication Shelf Life

Most medications remain effective well beyond their printed expiration dates when stored properly (cool, dry, dark):

| Medication | Estimated Effective Life Past Expiration |
|------------|----------------------------------------|
| Amoxicillin (capsules) | 2–5 years |
| Ciprofloxacin (tablets) | 5–10+ years |
| Azithromycin (tablets) | 5+ years |
| Metronidazole (tablets) | 3–5 years |
| Ibuprofen (tablets) | 5–10+ years |
| Acetaminophen (tablets) | 3–5 years |
| Diphenhydramine (tablets) | 5+ years |
| Aspirin (tablets) | 3–5 years (smells like vinegar when degraded) |

**Exceptions:** Liquid formulations, insulin, nitroglycerin, and epinephrine degrade faster. Tetracycline can become toxic after expiration — discard expired tetracycline.

Potency may decrease over time, but "expired" does not mean "dangerous" for most solid oral medications. A 10-year-old ibuprofen tablet may be 80% as effective — still useful.

## Epinephrine for Anaphylaxis

See **allergic-reactions.md** for full anaphylaxis management.

| Who | EpiPen Dose | Manual Injection |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| Adult/child over 66 lbs (30 kg) | EpiPen (0.3 mg) | 0.3 mg of 1:1,000 epinephrine IM (outer thigh) |
| Child 33–66 lbs (15–30 kg) | EpiPen Jr (0.15 mg) | 0.15 mg of 1:1,000 epinephrine IM |
| Infant under 33 lbs | — | 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1,000 IM (consult medical reference) |

Repeat every 5–15 minutes if symptoms persist.

> **Keep this reference accessible.** Print or copy this table and keep it with your medical supplies. In an emergency, you won't have time to search for dosing information. Knowing the correct dose of the right medication at the right time saves lives. Guessing does not.
