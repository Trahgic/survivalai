Quick reference for radio frequencies and channels. This covers the services most likely to be useful in a survival or emergency situation.

**FRS Channel 1** is the default calling/emergency channel for many users. If you're trying to reach anyone on FRS, start here.

## GMRS (General Mobile Radio Service) — License Required (No Exam)

Higher power version of FRS, same frequencies plus repeater capabilities. Range: 2–10 miles handheld, 20+ miles with a mobile/base station and repeater.

GMRS uses the same channels as FRS (1–22) at higher power (up to 50W on some channels), plus 8 repeater input channels (467.5500–467.7250 MHz).

**Channel 20 (462.6750 MHz)** is widely recognized as the GMRS emergency/calling channel.

## CB (Citizens Band) — No License Required

27 MHz AM radio. Range: 2–10 miles typical. Up to 4W.

| Channel | Frequency (MHz) | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| 9 | 27.065 | Emergency channel (FCC designated) |
| 19 | 27.185 | Trucker/highway channel |
| 14 | 27.125 | Common calling channel |

CB radios are common, cheap, and widely available. Channel 9 is federally designated for emergencies. Channel 19 is the most monitored overall.

## MURS (Multi-Use Radio Service) — No License Required

VHF frequencies with slightly better range than FRS (1–5 miles). Less crowded because fewer people use it.

| Channel | Frequency (MHz) | Notes |

Max power: 2W. No repeaters allowed. Good for local group communication when FRS is too crowded.

## Amateur (Ham) Radio — License Required (Exam)

Much greater range than any service above. HF bands can reach worldwide.

### VHF/UHF Repeater Offsets

Repeaters extend range by receiving on one frequency and retransmitting on another.

| Band | Standard Offset |
|------|----------------|
| 2m (144–148 MHz) | ±600 kHz |
| 70cm (420–450 MHz) | ±5 MHz |

Repeaters may require a CTCSS tone (PL tone) to access. Common tones: 100.0, 110.9, 127.3, 141.3, 162.2 Hz. Without the correct tone, the repeater ignores your transmission.

### HF Propagation Basics

HF radio waves bounce off the ionosphere, allowing long-distance communication:

| Band | Best Time | Typical Range |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 80m (3.5–4.0 MHz) | Night | 50–500 miles |
| 40m (7.0–7.3 MHz) | Evening/night | 100–2,000 miles |
| 20m (14.0–14.35 MHz) | Day | 500–worldwide |
| 10m (28.0–29.7 MHz) | Day (solar max) | 500–worldwide (sporadic) |

## NOAA Weather Radio

Continuous broadcast weather information, hazard alerts, and emergency notifications.

| Channel | Frequency (MHz) |

| 1 | 162.400 |

| 3 | 162.450 |
| 4 | 162.475 |

A weather radio or scanner set to these frequencies receives alerts automatically. NOAA stations broadcast from fixed locations — the strongest signal tells you which channel to monitor. Not all channels are active in all areas.

## Emergency Beacons

| Device | Frequency | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) | 406 MHz + 121.5 MHz | Satellite-monitored, GPS-equipped |
| EPIRB (marine) | 406 MHz | Same system, boat-mounted |
| ELT (aircraft) | 406 MHz | Same system, aircraft-mounted |

406 MHz beacons are monitored globally by the COSPAS-SARSAT satellite system. Activation sends GPS coordinates to rescue coordination centers.

## Radio Etiquette Quick Reference

- **Announce before transmitting:** "This is [your callsign or identifier], calling on [channel]."
- **Use plain language.** No codes unless agreed upon by your group.
- **Listen before transmitting.** Make sure the frequency isn't in use.
- **Keep transmissions short.** Say what you need to say, then release the transmit button.
- **"Break" for emergencies.** If you need to interrupt a conversation for an emergency, say "Break, break, break" and state your emergency.
- **"Copy" = I received your message.** "Roger" = I understand. "Negative" = no. "Affirmative" = yes.
- **Phonetic alphabet** for spelling: Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta, Echo, Foxtrot, Golf, Hotel, India, Juliet, Kilo, Lima, Mike, November, Oscar, Papa, Quebec, Romeo, Sierra, Tango, Uniform, Victor, Whiskey, X-ray, Yankee, Zulu.

> **Channel 9 on CB and Channel 1 on FRS** are the two frequencies most likely to be monitored by strangers in an emergency. If you're trying to reach anyone within a few miles, start there.
