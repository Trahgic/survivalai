Fifteen knots that cover almost every situation. Learn these and you can build shelters, set snares, climb, haul loads, tie down gear, and secure anything to anything. Each knot has a specific purpose — using the wrong knot in the wrong situation gets things loose, stuck, or broken.

## Terminology

- **Standing end:** The long/main part of the rope not being used in the knot.
- **Working end:** The short end you're tying with.
- **Bight:** A U-shaped bend in the rope without crossing.
- **Loop:** A circle where the rope crosses itself.
- **Bend:** A knot that joins two ropes together.
- **Hitch:** A knot that attaches a rope to an object.
- **Stopper:** A knot that prevents a rope from pulling through a hole.

## The 15 Essential Knots

### 1. Square Knot (Reef Knot)
**Use:** Joining two ropes of equal diameter. Tying bandages. Bundling.

**How:** Right over left, then left over right. Or: form a loop with one end, thread the other end up through the loop, around behind both strands, and back down through the loop.

**Strength rating:** 45% (retains 45% of rope strength)

**Warning:** Not reliable for load-bearing. Capsizes (flips and fails) under uneven loading. Never use for critical loads. Good for: packages, first aid, non-structural tying.

### 2. Bowline
**Use:** Creating a fixed loop that doesn't slip or bind under load. The single most important rescue and rigging knot.

**How:** "The rabbit comes out of the hole, goes around the tree, and goes back in the hole." Make a small loop in the standing end (the hole). Bring the working end up through the loop (rabbit out), around behind the standing end (around the tree), and back down through the small loop (back in the hole). Dress and tighten.

**Strength rating:** 60%

**Key feature:** Easy to untie even after heavy loading. Won't slip tighter under load.

### 3. Clove Hitch
**Use:** Starting and ending lashings. Quick attachment to a post, pole, or ring. Temporary tie-off.

**How:** Wrap rope around the object. Cross over the standing part. Wrap around again. Tuck the working end under the last wrap.

**Strength rating:** 60%

**Warning:** Can slip on smooth surfaces or with fluctuating loads. Add a half-hitch or two below for security.

### 4. Taut-Line Hitch
**Use:** Adjustable loop on a line under tension. Guy lines for tents and tarps. Any situation where you need to tension and re-tension a rope.

**How:** Pass the working end around the anchor (stake, tree), then wrap around the standing part twice inside the loop (toward the anchor), then once outside the loop. Slide to adjust tension.

**Key feature:** Grips under load but slides when slack. Lets you adjust tension without untying.

### 5. Two Half-Hitches
**Use:** Attaching rope to a ring, post, or pole. Simple, reliable, quick.

**How:** Wrap around the object. Make a half-hitch (pass the working end around the standing part and through the loop). Make a second half-hitch below the first. Both hitches should spiral the same direction.

**Strength rating:** 60%

### 6. Trucker's Hitch
**Use:** Mechanical advantage for tensioning a line. Tie-downs, ridgelines, load securing. The most useful compound knot.

**How:** Tie a fixed loop in the standing part (use a slip loop for easy untying). Run the working end through/around the anchor, then back up through your fixed loop. Pull down — you get roughly 3:1 mechanical advantage. Secure with two half-hitches.

**Key feature:** Multiplies your pulling force. Essential for getting tight ridgelines on tarps and tents.

### 7. Figure Eight (Stopper)
**Use:** Prevents rope from pulling through a hole, pulley, or grommet. End-of-rope stopper.

**How:** Make a loop, bring the working end around behind the standing part, and push through the loop. The finished knot looks like an 8.

**Strength rating:** 75% — one of the strongest knots

**Key feature:** Easy to untie even after loading. Standard climbing stopper knot.

### 8. Figure Eight on a Bight (Loop)
**Use:** Creating a strong loop in the middle or end of a rope. Climbing, rigging, rescue.

**How:** Double the rope (form a bight). Tie a figure eight with the doubled rope. The result is a strong, reliable loop.

**Strength rating:** 75%

### 9. Sheet Bend
**Use:** Joining two ropes of different diameters. More reliable than a square knot for mismatched ropes.

**How:** Make a bight in the thicker rope. Pass the thinner rope up through the bight, around behind both legs of the bight, and under itself. The working end of the thin rope should exit on the same side as the standing end of the thick rope.

**Strength rating:** 55%

**Note:** For slippery ropes or very mismatched sizes, use a double sheet bend (wrap around twice before tucking).

### 10. Timber Hitch
**Use:** Dragging logs and poles. Starting a diagonal lashing. Grips tighter under load.

**How:** Wrap around the timber. Bring the working end around the standing part, then twist the working end around itself 3–4 times. Pull to tighten — the twists grip the timber.

**Key feature:** Self-tightening under drag. Easy to untie when load is removed.

### 11. Prussik Knot
**Use:** Ascending a rope. Creating an adjustable friction grip on a rope. Rescue and climbing.

**How:** Use a thin cord loop (1/3 the diameter of the main rope). Wrap the loop around the main rope 3 times (pass the end through the initial loop). Dress neatly. Grips when weighted, slides when unweighted.

**Key feature:** Grips under load in both directions. Slides when you release weight. Used for ascending fixed ropes.

### 12. Constrictor Knot
**Use:** Binding — holding hose ends, closing bags, clamping objects together. Extremely secure.

**How:** Like a clove hitch but the first wrap goes over-under (one end crosses under the other before the final tuck). Difficult to describe in words — practice from a diagram.

**Key feature:** Nearly impossible to untie once tightened under load. May need to be cut off. Use when you need something to stay put permanently.

### 13. Lark's Head (Cow Hitch)
**Use:** Attaching a loop of cord to a bar, ring, or rope. Hanging items, starting lashings.

**How:** Pass the bight through/around the attachment point, then pull both ends of the cord through the bight.

**Simple and fast.** Not the strongest hitch but quick and adequate for non-critical loads.

### 14. Overhand Knot
**Use:** Simplest stopper knot. Also the basis for many other knots.

**How:** Make a loop, pass the end through. Everyone already knows this one.

**Note:** Weakens rope significantly (reduces to about 50% strength). Hard to untie after loading. Use a figure eight stopper instead when possible.

### 15. Water Knot (Ring Bend)
**Use:** Joining two pieces of flat webbing or tape. The only reliable knot for flat material.

**How:** Tie a loose overhand knot in one end. Thread the other piece through the knot in reverse, following the path exactly. Dress tight. Leave 3-inch tails.

**Key feature:** Must be dressed tight and checked regularly — it can creep under repeated loading.

## Which Knot When

| Situation | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| Make a non-slip loop | Bowline |
| Join two ropes (same size) | Square knot (light duty) or double fisherman's |
| Join two ropes (different size) | Sheet bend |
| Attach rope to a post | Two half-hitches or clove hitch |
| Adjustable tension on a line | Taut-line hitch |
| Maximum tension on a line | Trucker's hitch |
| Drag a log | Timber hitch |
| Stop rope from pulling through | Figure eight |
| Bind something tight permanently | Constrictor knot |
| Climb a rope | Prussik |
| Strong loop in rope middle | Figure eight on a bight |

## Lashing

Lashing joins poles together using rope. Three types cover most construction needs:

**Square lashing:** Joins two poles crossing at right angles. Used for building frames, racks, and shelters. Start with a clove hitch on one pole, wrap alternately around both poles in a square pattern (4–6 wraps), then frap (wrap between the poles to tighten), finish with a clove hitch.

**Diagonal lashing:** Joins two poles crossing at an angle (not 90°). Start with a timber hitch, wrap diagonally around both poles in both directions (3–4 wraps each way), frap, finish with a clove hitch.

**Shear lashing:** Binds two poles side by side (for extending length or creating an A-frame). Wrap around both poles together (6–8 wraps), frap between the poles, finish with a clove hitch. Spread the poles apart at the unlashed ends.

> **Practice knots before you need them.** Tying a bowline in the dark, in the rain, with cold hands and an injured person hanging below you is not the time to learn. Carry a short piece of cord and practice until you can tie these knots with your eyes closed. Ten minutes of practice a day for two weeks makes these permanent muscle memory.
