> **ABSOLUTE RULE:** Never eat a plant you haven't positively identified. "Pretty sure" is not good enough. One wrong mushroom or one misidentified root can kill you. When in doubt, don't eat it. See **poisonous-plants.md** and **foraging-mushrooms.md** for deadly species and the universal edibility test.

This is a quick-reference for the most common, easily identified, and hard-to-confuse-with-anything-dangerous wild edibles in temperate North America. These are "beginner-safe" plants.

---

## Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

**Where:** Lawns, fields, disturbed ground. Everywhere in temperate zones.

**ID features:** Rosette of deeply toothed leaves (teeth point back toward the base). Single hollow stem per flower. Bright yellow composite flower. Stem exudes milky white sap when broken. Taproot is light brown outside, white inside.

**Edible parts:** Entire plant. Leaves (best before flowering — bitter after), flowers (sweet, remove green base), roots (roast and grind as coffee substitute).

**Lookalikes:** Cat's ear (Hypochaeris) — also edible. Hawkweed — also edible. No dangerous lookalikes.

**Season:** Spring through fall. Leaves best in early spring.

## Cattail (Typha latifolia / T. angustifolia)

**Where:** Marshes, ponds, stream banks, ditches. Standing water everywhere.

**ID features:** Tall (4–9 feet), flat sword-like leaves, distinctive brown cigar-shaped seed head on top of a stiff stalk. Grows in dense stands in wet areas.

**Edible parts:** Nearly every part. Young shoots in spring (peel to white core — tastes like cucumber). Pollen from flower spikes (yellow powder — use as flour supplement). Rhizomes year-round (peel, dry, pound into starchy flour). Immature flower heads (boil and eat like corn on the cob).

**Lookalikes:** Iris (poisonous) — similar leaves but iris has a fan-shaped arrangement and no cigar seed head. Always confirm by the seed head.

**Season:** Year-round. Best parts vary by season.

## Plantain (Plantago major / P. lanceolata)

**Where:** Lawns, trailsides, disturbed ground, cracks in pavement. Extremely common.

**ID features:** Broad-leaf plantain has oval, ribbed leaves with prominent parallel veins. Narrow-leaf plantain has lance-shaped leaves. Both produce a thin flowering stalk with a cylindrical seed head. Leaves grow in a rosette from the base.

**Edible parts:** Young leaves raw in salads, older leaves cooked (get stringy with age — strip strings like celery). Seeds can be ground as flour. Medicinal: chewed leaf poultice on insect stings and minor wounds.

**Lookalikes:** None that are dangerous. Hosta looks similar but grows from a bulb in cultivated areas — mildly toxic.

**Season:** Spring through fall.

## White Clover (Trifolium repens)

**Where:** Lawns, fields, roadsides. Ubiquitous.

**ID features:** Three round leaflets with a lighter chevron pattern. White (sometimes pinkish) round flower heads. Low-growing, creeping stems.

**Edible parts:** Flower heads (raw or dried for tea — sweet, mild). Young leaves (best cooked — raw clover can cause bloating in quantity). Dried flower heads can be ground into protein-rich flour supplement.

**Lookalikes:** Oxalis (wood sorrel) has heart-shaped leaves and yellow flowers — also edible but sour. No dangerous lookalikes for clover.

**Season:** Spring through fall. Flowers in summer.

---

## Chickweed (Stellaria media)

**Where:** Gardens, disturbed ground, shady areas, cool moist soil. Very common.

**ID features:** Small, low-growing plant. Opposite oval leaves on stems with a single line of tiny hairs running along one side (shifts sides at each leaf node — this is diagnostic). Tiny white star-shaped flowers with deeply notched petals (looks like 10 petals but it's 5 split petals).

**Edible parts:** Entire above-ground plant. Mild, pleasant flavor. Eat raw in salads or cooked like spinach. Very nutritious — high in vitamin C.

**Lookalikes:** Scarlet pimpernel and spurge can grow in similar areas. Spurge has milky sap (chickweed does not). Check for the single line of hairs on the stem — it's the definitive chickweed identifier.

**Season:** Fall through spring (goes dormant in hot summer).

## Lamb's Quarters (Chenopodium album)

**Where:** Gardens, fields, waste ground, roadsides. One of the world's most common weeds.

**ID features:** Diamond-shaped or goosefoot-shaped leaves with irregular toothed edges. Young leaves have a whitish powdery coating underneath. Grows 1–5 feet tall. No significant smell.

**Edible parts:** Young leaves and tender stem tips. Cook like spinach — it's a close relative of spinach and quinoa. One of the most nutritious wild greens available (vitamins A, C, calcium, iron).

**Lookalikes:** Nightshade species can look vaguely similar at seedling stage — nightshade has berries and a different leaf shape at maturity. Confirm goosefoot leaf shape and powdery leaf undersides.

**Season:** Late spring through fall.

## Wood Sorrel (Oxalis spp.)

**Where:** Forests, shaded gardens, disturbed ground. Common.

**ID features:** Three heart-shaped leaflets (fold along the center). Small yellow, white, or pink flowers. Sour taste (oxalic acid — like lemon).

**Edible parts:** Leaves and flowers. Eat in moderation — oxalic acid in large quantities can aggravate kidney issues. Great as a trail nibble or salad accent.

**Lookalikes:** Clover has round leaflets, not heart-shaped. Not dangerous if confused.

**Season:** Spring through fall.

---

## Violet (Viola spp.)

**Where:** Forest floors, shaded yards, moist ground. Many species across North America.

**ID features:** Heart-shaped leaves. Distinctive 5-petaled flowers (purple, blue, white, or yellow). Low-growing.

**Edible parts:** Leaves (mild, mucilaginous — good in salads or as soup thickener). Flowers (mild, sweet — edible decoration, candied, or in salads). High in vitamin C.

**Lookalikes:** Lesser celandine looks similar but has glossy, waxy leaves and yellow flowers — mildly toxic raw.

**Season:** Spring (flowers), leaves available spring through fall.

---

## Wild Garlic / Ramps (Allium spp.)

**Where:** Moist forests, stream banks (ramps). Fields and disturbed ground (wild garlic/onion).

**ID features:** Strong garlic or onion smell when crushed — THIS IS THE CRITICAL ID. If it looks like an onion/garlic but doesn't smell like one, don't eat it. Flat or cylindrical leaves from a bulb. Small star-shaped flowers.

**Edible parts:** Entire plant — bulb, leaves, flowers. Use like cultivated garlic/onion.

**Lookalikes:** CRITICAL — Death camas (Zigadenus) and lily-of-the-valley are deadly lookalikes in appearance. ALWAYS confirm by smell. If it doesn't smell like garlic/onion, it could be lethal. Crush a leaf and smell before eating.

**Season:** Spring (ramps), spring through fall (wild garlic/onion).

---

## Acorns (Quercus spp.)

**Where:** Anywhere oaks grow — most of temperate North America.

**ID features:** You know what an oak tree and an acorn look like. The key is processing — raw acorns are extremely bitter and mildly toxic due to tannins.

**Edible parts:** Nut meat — after leaching. Shell the acorns, crush the nut meat, soak in multiple changes of water (cold water leaching takes 1–2 weeks, hot water leaching takes hours with repeated boiling and draining). When the nut meat no longer tastes bitter, it's ready. Dry and grind into flour.

**White oak acorns** (rounded lobes on leaves) have less tannin and are easier to process than **red oak acorns** (pointed lobes on leaves).

**Season:** Fall harvest. Stores dried for months.

## The Universal Edibility Test

For unknown plants when you're desperate. Takes 8+ hours. Does NOT work for mushrooms.

1. Test only one plant part at a time (leaf, stem, root).
2. Don't eat anything else for 8 hours before testing.
3. Hold the plant against your inner wrist for 15 minutes. If irritation occurs, discard.
4. Touch to your lip corner for 15 minutes. If burning or irritation, discard.
5. Place on your tongue for 15 minutes. Don't chew. If bad reaction, spit and rinse.
6. Chew and hold in mouth 15 minutes. Don't swallow. If any burning, numbness, or bad reaction, spit and rinse.
7. Swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours. Any nausea, cramping, or illness = discard.
8. If no reaction, eat a small handful. Wait another 8 hours.
9. If still no reaction, the plant part is likely safe to eat.

> **Stick to the plants you know.** The 10 plants on this page are available across most of temperate North America, easy to identify, and hard to confuse with anything dangerous. Master these before experimenting with anything unfamiliar. A reliable handful of species beats a risky pantry of unknowns.
