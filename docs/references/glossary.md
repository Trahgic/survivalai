Plain English definitions for terms used across the knowledge base. Organized alphabetically.

**Allicin** — Antimicrobial compound produced when garlic is crushed or chopped. Breaks down rapidly with cooking.

**Anaerobic** — Without oxygen. Anaerobic bacteria thrive in environments with no air, such as deep wounds, sealed containers, and compost digesters.

**Anaphylaxis** — Severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. Symptoms include throat swelling, difficulty breathing, rapid pulse, and dropping blood pressure. Requires epinephrine. See *allergic-reactions.md*.

**Anvil** — Heavy metal block used as a base for hammering and shaping hot metal. Improvised from railroad track, sledgehammer heads, or thick steel plate.

**Backstitch** — The strongest basic hand stitch. Each stitch overlaps the previous one, creating a continuous line.

**Bast fiber** — Plant fiber from the inner bark of stems. Flax, hemp, nettle, and jute are bast fibers.

**Birdsmouth** — A notch cut into a rafter where it sits on the wall top plate. Prevents the rafter from sliding.

**Borax** — Sodium borate. Natural mineral used as flux for brazing, insecticide, laundry booster, and soap ingredient.

**Brazing** — Joining metals using a filler metal (usually brass or bronze) that melts above 840°F but below the melting point of the base metals. Stronger than soldering.

**Brood** — In beekeeping, the developing bees (eggs, larvae, and pupae) in the comb. A healthy brood pattern indicates a good queen.

**BTU** — British Thermal Unit. The amount of heat needed to raise 1 pound of water by 1°F. Used to measure heating capacity of fuels and appliances.

**Burr (wire edge)** — A thin strip of metal that folds over the edge of a blade during sharpening. Its presence means you've sharpened to the edge. Must be removed for a clean final edge.

**Capillary refill** — Test of circulation. Press a fingernail until it blanches white, then release. Color should return within 2 seconds. Longer than 2 seconds indicates poor circulation (shock, dehydration).

**Carding** — Combing fiber between two paddle-like brushes with fine wire teeth to align fibers for spinning.

**Cold frame** — A bottomless box with a transparent lid placed over a garden bed to extend the growing season. A mini greenhouse.

**Comfrey** — Perennial herb with deep taproots. Used in permaculture as a nutrient accumulator and mulch plant. Toxic internally due to pyrrolizidine alkaloids — external use only.

**Composting** — Controlled decomposition of organic material into nutrient-rich soil amendment.

**Contour line** — A line connecting points of equal elevation on a map. On the ground, contour lines run level across a slope.

**Cover crop** — A crop grown primarily to improve soil rather than for harvest. Clover fixes nitrogen; winter rye prevents erosion and breaks compaction.

**CTCSS (PL tone)** — Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System. A sub-audible tone transmitted along with voice on a radio that allows a repeater or radio to filter unwanted transmissions.

**Cutworm** — Soil-dwelling caterpillar that chews through plant stems at ground level overnight. Prevented by cardboard collars around transplants.

**Dead load** — The weight of a structure itself — framing, roofing, flooring. Constant and predictable. See *structural-engineering.md*.

**Decoction** — An herbal preparation made by simmering hard plant material (bark, roots, seeds) in water for 20–30 minutes. Used when simple steeping won't extract the medicinal compounds.

**Dehair** — To remove hair or fur from an animal hide during processing.

**Drawing out** — Blacksmithing operation: making a piece of metal longer and thinner by hammering.

**Emitter (drip)** — A small device in drip irrigation tubing that releases water at a controlled rate, typically 1–2 gallons per hour.

**FAMACHA** — A scoring system for assessing parasite load in goats and sheep by examining the color of the inner lower eyelid. Pale = anemic = high worm load.

**Fermentation** — Metabolic process where microorganisms (yeast, bacteria) convert sugars into alcohol, acids, or gases. Basis for bread, alcohol, sauerkraut, and vinegar production.

**Fleshing** — Removing meat, fat, and membrane from the inner surface of an animal hide.

**Flux** — A chemical applied to metal surfaces before soldering, brazing, or welding to prevent oxidation and help the filler metal flow. Borax is a common forge-welding flux. Rosin flux for electrical soldering.

**Forge welding** — Joining two pieces of metal by heating to near-melting temperature and hammering together. No filler metal used.

**FRS** — Family Radio Service. License-free two-way radio on UHF frequencies. Low power (0.5–2W), short range (0.5–2 miles typical).

**FTS5** — Full-Text Search version 5. A text search extension for SQLite databases.

**Gel phase** — In soap making, the stage where the soap heats internally during saponification, becoming translucent. Normal and desirable.

**GMRS** — General Mobile Radio Service. Licensed (no exam) two-way radio with higher power than FRS. Same frequencies, better range.

**Guild (permaculture)** — A group of plants that support each other, mimicking natural plant communities. Example: apple tree + comfrey + clover + chives.

**Hardening (steel)** — Heating steel to critical temperature and quenching rapidly to lock carbon in a hard crystalline structure (martensite).

**Hardy** — A chisel-like tool that fits in the square hole (hardy hole) of an anvil. Used for cutting hot steel.

**Head (water pressure)** — The height of water above a given point. Every foot of head provides approximately 0.43 PSI of pressure.

**Hoop house** — An arched-frame greenhouse covered in plastic sheeting. Also called high tunnel or polytunnel.

**Infusion** — An herbal preparation made by pouring boiling water over plant material and steeping for 10–15 minutes. Essentially a strong herbal tea.

**Keyline design** — A landscape design system that moves water from wet areas (valleys) to dry areas (ridges) using specific plowing and planting patterns.

**Kindling** — Small sticks and wood pieces used to build a fire from tinder to fuel-sized logs.

**Leggy** — Describing seedlings that have grown tall and thin due to insufficient light.

**Live load** — The weight of movable items on a structure — people, furniture, equipment, snow. Variable and must be designed for. See *structural-engineering.md*.

**Lye** — A strong alkaline solution. Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) produces hard soap. Potassium hydroxide (KOH) from wood ash produces soft soap.

**Mordant** — A chemical that fixes dye to fiber. Alum, iron, and copper are common mordants.

**Nixtamalization** — Treating corn with an alkaline solution (lime or wood ash water) to release niacin and improve protein availability. Without this process, corn-dependent diets cause pellagra.

**Normalize** — In metalworking, heating steel to critical temperature and cooling in still air to relieve internal stress and refine grain structure.

**On center (OC)** — Measurement between the centers of structural members (studs, joists, rafters), not the gaps between them. "16 inches on center" means centers are 16 inches apart.

**Open-pollinated (OP)** — Plant varieties that breed true from saved seed. Offspring match the parent plant. Contrast with hybrid (F1) varieties.

**PSI** — Pounds per square inch. Standard unit of pressure in the US. Atmospheric pressure at sea level is about 14.7 PSI.

**Quench** — Rapidly cooling hot steel by plunging it into oil, water, or brine. Part of the hardening process.

**Retting** — Controlled rotting of plant stems (flax, hemp, nettle) to loosen the outer bark and separate usable bast fibers. Done by dew exposure or water soaking.

**Roving** — A loosely organized strip of prepared fiber, ready for spinning.

**Saponification** — The chemical reaction between fat and lye that produces soap and glycerin.

**Scutching** — Scraping broken woody material away from bast fibers during fiber processing.

**Shear wall** — A wall designed to resist lateral (sideways) forces. Created by nailing plywood or OSB to the wall framing.

**Sheet mulching** — Building a garden bed by layering cardboard, compost, and mulch on top of existing ground. Smothers weeds and creates fertile soil.

**SODIS** — Solar disinfection. Placing water in clear PET bottles in direct sunlight for 6+ hours to kill pathogens using UV radiation.

**Span** — The unsupported distance between structural supports (posts, walls, beams). Longer spans require larger structural members.

**START triage** — Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment. A mass casualty sorting system using breathing rate, circulation, and mental status to categorize patients.

**Superfat** — Using slightly less lye than needed in soap making to ensure no free lye remains in the finished soap.

**Swale** — A shallow trench dug along a contour line to capture rainwater and allow it to soak into the ground rather than running off.

**Tannin** — Astringent compounds found in bark, leaves, and nuts. Used for tanning leather and as a mordant for dyeing.

**Tempering** — Reheating hardened steel to a controlled temperature to reduce brittleness and increase toughness. Different temperatures produce different hardness levels, indicated by surface color (straw, bronze, purple, blue).

**Tincture** — Herbal preparation made by soaking plant material in alcohol for 2–6 weeks. Concentrates and preserves medicinal compounds.

**Top plate** — The horizontal member across the top of a wall's studs. A double top plate ties walls together.

**Trace (soap making)** — The stage when the soap mixture thickens to the point where drizzled soap leaves a visible trail on the surface. Indicates saponification is underway.

**Transesterification** — The chemical reaction used to make biodiesel: fat + methanol + catalyst → biodiesel + glycerin.

**Upsetting** — Blacksmithing operation: making a piece of metal shorter and thicker by hammering the end back into itself.

**Varroa mite** — Parasitic mite (Varroa destructor) that feeds on honey bees and transmits viruses. The primary cause of managed colony losses worldwide.

**Vernalization** — A cold period required by some plants (winter wheat, biennials) to trigger flowering or grain production.

**Warp** — The lengthwise threads on a loom, held under tension. The structural framework of woven fabric.

**Weft** — The crosswise threads woven through the warp on a loom.

**Winnowing** — Separating grain from chaff by tossing into the air and letting wind blow away the lighter chaff.

**Zone (permaculture)** — Organizational system placing frequently visited elements closest to the home (Zone 1) and rarely visited elements farthest away (Zone 5).
