Knowledge that dies with the person who holds it is wasted. In a long-term survival situation, teaching is how your community survives beyond the first generation. If only one person knows how to treat an infection, stitch a wound, or repair an engine — and that person dies — the group loses that capability forever. Spreading critical knowledge across multiple people is survival insurance.

## Priority Skills to Teach

Rank by consequence of loss:

**Teach immediately (loss = people die):**
- Basic first aid, wound care, CPR
- Water purification
- Fire starting
- Food safety and preparation
- Recognizing and treating common illnesses

**Teach within months (loss = quality of life drops severely):**
- Gardening and food production
- Animal husbandry
- Basic construction and repair
- Radio operation
- Security procedures

**Teach within the first year (loss = long-term capacity drops):**
- Blacksmithing and metalwork
- Advanced medical skills
- Seed saving and crop management
- Tanning, weaving, and textile production
- Soap making, food preservation, chemistry basics

**Teach continuously (loss = civilization doesn't rebuild):**
- Reading, writing, and math
- History and science
- Governance and conflict resolution
- Music, art, storytelling

## Teaching Methods That Work Without Technology

### Demonstration and Practice

The most effective method for physical skills: show it, explain it, then have them do it while you watch and correct.

1. Demonstrate the complete skill once while explaining what you're doing and why.
2. Break it into steps. Demonstrate each step slowly.
3. Have the learner attempt each step. Correct mistakes immediately.
4. Have them do the complete skill under supervision.
5. Have them do it independently. Check the result.
6. Repeat until they can do it reliably without guidance.

### Apprenticeship Model

Pair a skilled person with 1–3 learners for extended periods. The apprentice works alongside the expert, starting with simple tasks and gradually taking on more complex ones. This is how most technical skills were transmitted for thousands of years before formal education existed.

**Key principle:** The apprentice should be doing real work, not just watching. Hands-on practice with real consequences is how skills stick. A person who helps butcher 20 chickens under supervision can do it alone. A person who watched 20 demonstrations still hesitates.

### Cross-Training

Every critical skill should be held by at least 3 people in the group. Designate primary, secondary, and tertiary people for each critical function. If the primary medical person is down, the secondary steps in. Cross-training happens during normal operations — the backup person assists regularly, not just during emergencies.

### Drills

For emergency skills (medical, security, fire response), regular drills build muscle memory and expose weaknesses in plans.

- Medical drills: practice patient assessment and triage with simulated casualties
- Security drills: practice alarm response, defensive positions, and evacuation routes
- Fire drills: practice response to structure fire, wildfire approach, and escape routes

Monthly is a good frequency for most drills. Debrief after every drill — what worked, what didn't, what needs to change.

## Teaching Children

Children in a survival situation need both practical skills and academic education. The practical skills keep them useful and safe now. The academic education ensures they can rebuild later.

### Ages 4–7

- Plant identification (safe vs. dangerous)
- Basic hygiene habits
- Simple gardening tasks (watering, weeding, harvesting)
- Counting, alphabet, reading basics
- Basic safety rules (fire, water, strangers)
- Stories and songs (cultural transmission and morale)

### Ages 8–12

- All the above plus:
- Cooking basic meals, fire management
- First aid basics (clean a wound, apply a bandage)
- Reading and writing fluently
- Basic math (addition, subtraction, multiplication, fractions)
- Animal care (feeding, egg collection, milking)
- Knot tying, tool use, basic repairs
- Navigation basics (compass, map reading)

### Ages 13+

- Full participation in group work rotations
- Apprenticeship in a chosen skill area
- Advanced reading: science, history, practical reference material
- Basic algebra and geometry (essential for construction, engineering, navigation)
- Leadership development — assign them responsibility and let them lead small projects

### Teaching Without Textbooks

- **Oral instruction and discussion** — the oldest and most natural teaching method
- **Writing materials** — even charcoal on board, scratching in dirt, or scratching on bark tablet
- **Salvaged books** — protect and share any books found. A single math textbook or medical reference serves an entire community
- **Create your own reference materials** — have knowledgeable people write down critical procedures, tables, and instructions. This creates a community knowledge base on paper

## Knowledge Preservation

### Write It Down

The most important thing you can do for knowledge preservation: create written records of critical skills and procedures. Every expert in the group should produce a written guide for their area — a document detailed enough that someone could learn the skill from reading it alone.

Format: step-by-step numbered procedures with specific measurements, warnings, and common mistakes. The same format as the articles in this knowledge base.

### Redundant Storage

Keep copies of important documents in multiple locations. Paper degrades — protect it from water, fire, and mice. Store in sealed containers, off the ground, in a cool dry place.

### Regular Teaching Rotations

Schedule regular teaching sessions — even just 30 minutes per week where a skilled person teaches their specialty to the group. This is one of the highest-value uses of community time and costs nothing.

## Motivation

Adults and children alike learn best when they understand why the skill matters, when they see progress, and when they get genuine responsibility.

- Connect every lesson to a real outcome: "You're learning this so you can do it when I'm not around."
- Give learners real tasks, not make-work. A teenager who's trusted with the morning watch takes security seriously. A teenager told to sit in a chair and watch learns to sleep with their eyes open.
- Acknowledge competence. When someone can reliably perform a skill, say so publicly. Competence recognized becomes competence reinforced.
- Accept that people learn at different speeds. Patience in teaching pays dividends for years.

> **The long view:** A community's knowledge is its most valuable asset — more valuable than food stores, tools, or weapons, because knowledge produces everything else. Every hour spent teaching is an investment in your community's future capability. The settlement that teaches its children to read, grow food, treat wounds, and build shelter will outlast the one that only fights and forages.
