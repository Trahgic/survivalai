Triage is sorting injured people by severity so you treat the ones you can actually save first. It's the hardest medical decision because it means consciously choosing not to help some people right now. But in a mass casualty situation — a building collapse, a fire, a vehicle accident with multiple victims — you can't treat everyone at once. Trying to save everyone usually means saving no one.

## START Triage (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment)

The standard civilian mass casualty system. Takes about 30 seconds per patient. You're classifying, not treating (except two quick interventions).

### Step 1: Walking Filter

Announce loudly: "If you can walk, move to [designated area]." Everyone who walks there is GREEN (Minor/Delayed). They're hurt but mobile — they can wait. This clears a large number of patients immediately.

### Step 2: Assess Non-Walking Patients

Go to each remaining patient and assess in this order:

**Check breathing:**
- Not breathing → Open the airway (tilt head back, lift chin). If they start breathing → RED (Immediate). If they still don't breathe → BLACK (Deceased/Expectant). Move on.
- Breathing rate over 30 per minute → RED (Immediate).
- Breathing rate under 30 → Move to next check.

**Check circulation:**
- Press a fingernail or the skin on the back of their hand for 2 seconds, release. If color returns in under 2 seconds = adequate circulation. If over 2 seconds (delayed capillary refill) → RED (Immediate).
- If there's major uncontrolled bleeding → apply direct pressure or tourniquet (this is one of the two treatments you DO during triage). Then tag RED.

**Check mental status:**
- "Squeeze my hand." "What's your name?" Can they follow simple commands?
- Responds appropriately → YELLOW (Delayed — serious but stable, can wait for treatment).
- Doesn't respond or confused → RED (Immediate).

### Triage Categories

| Category | Color | Meaning | Examples |
|----------|-------|---------|---------|
| Immediate | RED | Life-threatening, treatable | Severe bleeding, chest wounds, airway problems, shock |
| Delayed | YELLOW | Serious but stable, can wait hours | Fractures, moderate wounds, burns under 20% |
| Minor | GREEN | Walking wounded, can wait | Sprains, small cuts, minor burns, bruises |
| Deceased/Expectant | BLACK | Dead or injuries incompatible with survival given current resources | No pulse/breathing after airway opened, massive head trauma, burns over 90% |

### Tagging

Mark each patient clearly. Commercial triage tags exist, but in the field:
- Colored tape or cloth on the wrist or chest
- Write the category on their forehead with marker
- Use any visible marking system — consistency matters more than format

## Treatment Priority

After all patients are triaged:

1. **Treat RED first.** These are the people who will die without immediate intervention but can survive with it. Airway management, bleeding control, chest seal for sucking wounds, shock treatment.
2. **Treat YELLOW next.** Splint fractures, dress wounds, manage pain. These patients are stable but will deteriorate without care within hours.
3. **Treat GREEN last.** Self-aid, buddy-aid, and basic wound care. Walking wounded can often treat each other with guidance.
4. **BLACK receives comfort care only.** If resources are extremely scarce, black-tagged patients receive pain management and dignity. This is emotionally brutal but medically necessary.

## Retriage

Patients change categories. A YELLOW patient can become RED if they start bleeding internally. A RED patient stabilized with treatment can become YELLOW. Reassess every 15–30 minutes during active management.

## Resource Allocation

When medical supplies are limited, triage extends to how you spend supplies:

- Don't use your last tourniquet on a patient who's already lost too much blood to survive. Use it on the one who just started bleeding.
- Antibiotics go to patients most likely to develop life-threatening infections from treatable wounds, not prophylactically to everyone.
- Pain medication goes to everyone — this is the one place where you don't ration. Managing pain is basic dignity.

## Documentation

Even in a disaster, document what you can:

- Patient name (or description if unknown)
- Triage category and time
- Injuries identified
- Treatments given
- Changes in status

This matters because: multiple caregivers need to know what's been done, medical decisions may need to be reviewed later, and if organized medical care eventually arrives, your notes help them provide better care.

## Psychological Aspects

Triage breaks people emotionally. The person doing triage has to walk past someone dying to treat someone they can save. This is psychologically devastating, even for professionals.

- Accept that you can't save everyone. This isn't failure — it's reality with limited resources.
- The person doing triage shouldn't also be providing treatment if possible. Separation allows the triager to stay systematic and prevents emotional attachment from overriding medical judgment.
- After the event, debrief. Talk about what happened, what decisions were made, and why. People who carry this without processing it develop serious psychological wounds.

> **The hardest truth:** Good triage feels wrong. Skipping a screaming patient to treat a quiet one feels inhuman. But the screaming patient might have survivable injuries while the quiet one is bleeding out silently. Triage saves the most lives possible. It's not about choosing who deserves treatment — it's about choosing who benefits from it most with the resources you have right now.
