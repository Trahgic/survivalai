Cities and towns are the richest source of materials in a post-disaster environment. A single hardware store contains more fasteners, tools, and building materials than a community could manufacture in years. A pharmacy has antibiotics that can't be made from scratch. An auto parts store has batteries, wire, and components that power communication systems and lighting. Salvage is how you bridge the gap between collapse and self-sufficiency.

## Salvage vs. Looting

There's a practical and ethical distinction:

**Salvage** is recovering useful materials from abandoned, destroyed, or clearly unoccupied structures when the original supply chain is gone and recovery is not expected in the near term. It's recovery of resources that would otherwise deteriorate and be lost.

**Looting** is taking from occupied structures, from people, or taking opportunistically during a temporary disruption when normal commerce will resume. This is theft.

Your community should draw this line explicitly and early (see **legal-and-ethical.md**). Document what you take and from where. If the owner returns or society stabilizes, you can account for what was taken and negotiate restitution.

## Priority Targets (What to Salvage First)

### Pharmacies and Medical Facilities

**Highest priority.** Medications expire but many remain effective well past their printed dates (see **medication-reference.md** in the medicine section). Grab everything and sort later.

Priority items: antibiotics, painkillers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen), antihistamines, anti-diarrheal, epinephrine auto-injectors, suture kits, bandages, gauze, antiseptic, blood pressure cuffs, stethoscopes, surgical instruments, splints, prescription medications (insulin needs refrigeration — only take if you have cold storage).

### Hardware Stores

A goldmine of survival materials:

- Fasteners: nails, screws, bolts, nuts, washers — in bulk. These are impossible to manufacture at scale.
- Hand tools: hammers, saws, wrenches, pliers, files, chisels, levels
- Wire: electrical wire (copper), steel wire, baling wire
- Pipe and fittings: PVC, copper, brass fittings, valves, hose clamps
- Adhesives: epoxy, PVC cement, construction adhesive, tape
- Rope and cordage
- Tarps, plastic sheeting
- Fuel containers
- Seeds (seasonal garden centers)

### Auto Parts Stores

- Batteries (12V lead-acid — power radios, lights, small electronics)
- Jumper cables, battery cables, terminal clamps
- Wire by the spool
- Fuses and electrical components
- Hose clamps, gaskets, seals
- Motor oil, gear oil, grease
- Fuel line, vacuum hose
- Basic tools

### Grocery/Warehouse Stores

Canned goods last years. Dry goods (rice, beans, pasta) last 2–5 years. Salt, sugar, and honey last indefinitely. Cooking oils last 1–2 years. Spices last years in sealed containers.

Skip refrigerated and frozen items unless you arrive within 24–48 hours of power loss and they're still cold. Thawed and refrozen food is often unsafe.

### Sporting Goods Stores

- Camping equipment: tents, sleeping bags, pads, stoves, cookware
- Water filters and purification
- Fishing gear
- Knives, multi-tools, axes
- Binoculars
- Rope and paracord

### Libraries and Bookstores

Don't overlook information. Medical textbooks, engineering references, agricultural guides, field identification books, and repair manuals have infinite practical value. A single copy of *Where There Is No Doctor* can serve a community for years.

## Salvage Safety

Urban environments after a disaster are dangerous. Buildings may be structurally compromised, hazardous materials may be present, and other people may be territorial.

### Structural Hazards

1. **Assess before entering.** Look for: leaning walls, cracked foundations, sagging roofs, debris piles that suggest partial collapse. If the building looks unstable, don't go in.
2. **Listen.** Creaking, popping, or groaning sounds indicate ongoing structural movement. Get out.
3. **Watch for floors.** Water damage and fire weaken floor structures. Test each step before committing your weight. Stay near walls where floors are strongest (supported by wall framing).
4. **Beware of above.** Shelving, ceiling fixtures, and roof structures can fall. Wear a hard hat or helmet if you have one.
5. **Have an escape route.** Before going deep into a building, identify how you'll get out if it starts to collapse. Don't go alone — have a partner outside who knows where you are.

### Hazardous Materials

- **Gas leaks:** Smell for natural gas or propane. If you detect it, don't use any open flame or create sparks. Ventilate and leave.
- **Chemical storage:** Pool chemicals, fertilizers, cleaning products, and industrial chemicals can react with each other if mixed by spilling or structural damage. Avoid areas with visible chemical spills or strong chemical odors.
- **Asbestos and lead paint:** Older buildings may contain both. Not an immediate danger but avoid creating dust from demolition in pre-1980 buildings. Wear a mask.
- **Sewage:** If plumbing is broken, contaminated water may be pooled. Avoid contact. Wear boots and gloves.

### Human Hazards

- Other groups may have claimed a building. Approach cautiously, announce yourself, and respect territorial claims unless negotiated otherwise.
- Avoid conflict over salvage. There's usually more than enough in urban areas for multiple groups, especially early on.
- Travel in groups of 3 or more. One person watches while others work.

## Systematic Approach

Don't grab randomly. Plan salvage operations:

1. **Survey the area.** Map what businesses and facilities exist within your reach. Prioritize by content value.
2. **Assign teams.** Specialized teams are more efficient: a medical supply team knows what to look for in a pharmacy, a tools team knows hardware.
3. **Transport plan.** How are you moving heavy items back to your base? Vehicles, carts, wheelbarrows, bicycles with trailers. Plan this before you fill a store's shopping cart with 200 pounds of hardware three miles from home.
4. **Inventory what you recover.** Catalogue supplies as they come in. You can't manage what you don't know you have.
5. **Secure your stockpile.** Recovered supplies that aren't secured will be taken by others. Centralize critical supplies and control access.

## Long-Term Salvage Targets

As immediate needs are met, look for materials that support long-term self-sufficiency:

- **Solar panels** from rooftops and commercial installations
- **Copper wire** from buildings (wiring in walls) — essential for electrical projects
- **Steel and metal stock** from fabrication shops, construction sites, and junkyards — raw material for blacksmithing
- **Glass** from windows — usable for cold frames and greenhouses
- **Plumbing fixtures** — pipes, valves, fittings for water systems
- **Automotive alternators** — can be repurposed as wind or water turbine generators
- **Tires** — raised bed walls, rubber material, fuel (last resort — burning tires is toxic)

> **The clock is ticking.** Salvageable materials deteriorate. Medications expire. Food spoils. Metal rusts. Batteries discharge. Paper molds. The first months after a disaster are the window when urban salvage yields the most return. After a year, weather damage, other salvagers, and degradation sharply reduce what's available. Move early, move systematically, and store what you recover properly.
