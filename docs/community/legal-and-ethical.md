When institutions collapse, the rules don't disappear — they just stop being enforced by someone else. That makes you responsible for deciding what's right, what's fair, and what your community will tolerate. Groups that skip this conversation descend into strongman rule or chaos. Groups that address it early, even roughly, build something that can last.

## Property Rights After a Disaster

Property law doesn't evaporate because the power is out. But the practical reality shifts:

**Occupied property** remains the owner's. This doesn't change. Someone's house is their house, regardless of what's happening outside.

**Abandoned property** is a gray area. If a structure is clearly abandoned (no occupants, no signs of recent use), most post-disaster communities eventually adopt a "use it or lose it" principle. But "abandoned" and "the owner left temporarily" are different things. Before claiming abandoned property, make reasonable efforts to determine ownership and intent to return.

**Salvage vs. looting:** Taking from a destroyed, unoccupied building (salvage) is different from breaking into an occupied home (theft/burglary). Most communities draw this line intuitively. The ethics get murkier with unoccupied but intact structures — businesses, warehouses, stores. Your community needs to decide where the line is and apply the standard consistently.

**Community resource pooling:** Some groups will want to pool all resources communally. Others will want private ownership with shared security. Neither is inherently right. The key is explicit agreement — don't assume everyone has the same expectations. A formal discussion and recorded agreement about shared vs. private resources prevents fights down the road.

## Use of Force

The single most important ethical question your community will face. When is force justified? Against whom? How much?

### Self-Defense Principles

Most legal and ethical frameworks agree on these basics:

1. **Imminent threat.** You may use force to defend yourself or others against an immediate threat of serious harm. "Immediate" means happening now — not "might happen tomorrow."
2. **Proportional response.** The force used should be proportional to the threat. Shooting someone who's stealing a tomato is not proportional. Physical restraint against someone threatening violence is proportional. Lethal force is justified when the threat is lethal.
3. **Retreat if possible.** Many ethical (and legal) frameworks require you to attempt to withdraw before using force, if withdrawal is possible without greater danger. In your own home or defended position, this generally doesn't apply.
4. **Force is the last resort.** Warning, deterrence, verbal de-escalation — all should be attempted before physical force when time permits.

### Post-Event Accountability

When force is used, document what happened. Who was involved, what the threat was, what force was used, and what the outcome was. This serves two purposes:

1. **Internal accountability.** The community reviews whether force was appropriate. This prevents escalation and abuse.
2. **Future legal protection.** If organized law enforcement returns, a documented record of justified defensive actions protects individuals from prosecution.

## Community Justice

Without police, courts, or jails, your community still needs a way to handle wrongdoing.

### Minor Infractions (Theft of small items, shirking duties, rule violations)

- Mediated resolution (see **group-organization.md**)
- Restitution (the offender returns or replaces what was taken, plus performs community service)
- Public acknowledgment of the violation and agreement to change behavior

### Serious Infractions (Violence, major theft, endangering the group)

- Formal hearing before the leadership council with the accused present and able to speak
- Testimony from witnesses
- Agreed consequences: restriction of privileges, removal from positions of trust, physical separation from the group, or in extreme cases, expulsion

### What Doesn't Work

- **Mob justice.** Angry groups make terrible decisions. Never punish someone based on a crowd's emotional reaction.
- **Secret proceedings.** Justice must be visible to maintain legitimacy. Hidden punishments breed fear and resentment.
- **Disproportionate punishment.** Beating someone for stealing food destroys trust in leadership and motivates secrecy rather than compliance.
- **No process at all.** If people believe there's no recourse for being wronged, they take matters into their own hands.

## Community Agreements

The closest thing to a constitution for your group. Write it down, even if it's simple. Cover at minimum:

1. **Membership:** Who's in the group? How are new members admitted? What obligations come with membership?
2. **Leadership:** How is leadership selected? How are decisions made? How can leaders be replaced?
3. **Resource sharing:** What's communal? What's private? How are essential supplies (food, water, medical) allocated?
4. **Work expectations:** What's expected of each member? How are work disputes handled?
5. **Use of force:** When is it authorized? By whom? With what accountability?
6. **Dispute resolution:** How are conflicts between members resolved?
7. **Expulsion:** Under what circumstances? Through what process? With what provisions (or not)?

### Making It Stick

- Have every member acknowledge the agreement (verbal or signed).
- Post it publicly.
- Apply it equally — leadership included. Rules that don't apply to leaders are not rules, they're control mechanisms.
- Revise it when needed. As circumstances change, the agreement should adapt.

## Ethical Principles for Hard Decisions

You'll face decisions no peacetime framework prepared you for. Some guideposts:

**Do the most good for the most people.** When resources are scarce, triage thinking applies to more than medicine. Food rationing, shelter allocation, and work assignments should prioritize group survival.

**Protect the vulnerable.** Children, elderly, disabled, and sick members can't defend or provide for themselves equally. A community's character is measured by how it treats its weakest members.

**Preserve dignity.** Even when making hard decisions — rationing, triage, discipline — treat people as people. Dehumanization is the first step toward atrocity, and atrocity destroys communities from within.

**Think beyond the immediate.** Will this decision create enemies who come back later? Will it set a precedent that gets used against you? Will it undermine trust that took months to build?

**Be honest about your reasoning.** If you're making a hard call, explain why. "We're rationing food because we have 30 days of supply and uncertain resupply" is harder to hear than false reassurance, but it builds trust and buy-in.

> **Laws will return.** This is important to remember even in the worst situations. Governments reconstitute, legal systems rebuild, and accounts are settled. Acting ethically now isn't just morally right — it's practically smart. A documented record of fair community governance and justified use of force protects your group when law returns. A record of abuse and brutality does not.
