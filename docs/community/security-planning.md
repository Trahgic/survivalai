Security isn't about being paranoid — it's about not being caught off guard. In a breakdown situation, most people are decent. But the minority who aren't can destroy what your group has built in a single night. A realistic security plan deters opportunistic threats and buys time against organized ones.

## Threat Assessment

Before building defenses, understand what you're defending against:

**Opportunistic individuals:** Lone people or pairs looking for easy pickings. Deterred by visible security — lights, fences, dogs, people clearly on watch. They move on to softer targets.

**Small groups (3–10):** More organized, possibly armed, willing to use force. Deterred by significant visible defenses and the perception that attacking would cost them more than they'd gain.

**Large organized groups:** Beyond what a small community can repel militarily. Your strategy against this level of threat is diplomacy, alliance with other groups, or evacuation. Don't plan to fight a force that significantly outnumbers you.

**Internal threats:** Theft, conflict, and betrayal from within your own group. Addressed through governance and conflict resolution (see **group-organization.md**), not military posture.

## Perimeter Security

### Layered Defense

Think in concentric rings:

**Outer ring (observation):** As far as you can monitor. Trail cameras, tripwires with noise makers (tin cans with pebbles on fishing line), clear sight lines. The goal is early warning, not stopping anyone. You want to know someone is approaching before they're at your door.

**Middle ring (deterrence):** Fencing, locked gates, thorny hedges (osage orange, blackberry, hawthorn make excellent natural barriers over time), lighting, visible guards. The message: this is occupied, defended, and not worth the trouble.

**Inner ring (defense):** Hardened positions around critical assets — food stores, water supply, sleeping areas. Reinforced doors, fighting positions with cover, fallback points. This is where you make a stand if it comes to that.

### Watch System

Someone needs to be awake and alert at all times. A rotating watch schedule is the backbone of security.

**4-hour watches** are standard — long enough to be useful, short enough that alertness doesn't crater. In a group of 12, you can run 4-hour watches with 2 people per watch (one at each observation point) and still give everyone 8 hours off per cycle.

Post a written schedule. Missing a watch is treated as seriously as any other critical duty.

**Watch duties:**
1. Stay awake. Falling asleep on watch is the most common and dangerous failure.
2. Monitor assigned sectors. Don't stare at one spot — scan regularly.
3. Know the challenge and password (change daily). Anyone approaching at night gets challenged.
4. Have a way to raise the alarm — whistle, bell, radio, horn. Know when to raise it.
5. Know the rules of engagement — what warrants a warning call vs. waking the group vs. defensive action.

### Communication

Watches are useless if they can't communicate quickly:

- Two-way radios (FRS/GMRS) for watch-to-watch and watch-to-leadership communication
- Predetermined signals: two whistle blasts = suspicious activity, three = confirmed threat, continuous = attack
- Wired field phones (military surplus or improvised) if radio security is a concern — radio transmissions can be monitored

## Checkpoint Procedures

If your community has a road or trail approach, a checkpoint controls access:

1. **Location:** Set back from the main perimeter, preferably at a natural chokepoint (bridge, narrow road, gap in terrain). Position it where defenders have cover and the approacher is in the open.
2. **Barrier:** A vehicle, gate, or dragged obstacle that forces approaching vehicles to stop and people to approach on foot.
3. **Challenge procedure:** Who are you? Where are you coming from? What's your business? Not hostile — calm and firm. Most people approaching aren't threats.
4. **Search:** Before admitting strangers, check for concealed weapons and evaluate their story. This is uncomfortable but necessary.
5. **Escort:** New arrivals are escorted inside, not left to wander.

## Alarms and Early Warning

Low-tech solutions that work:

- **Trip lines:** Fishing line strung ankle-height across approaches, connected to tin cans or bells. Effective, nearly invisible at night.
- **Dogs:** Better alarm systems than any technology. They hear and smell threats long before humans notice. Even one alert dog is worth three extra watchstanders.
- **Gravel paths:** Crunchy gravel around your perimeter is impossible to walk on silently.
- **Geese:** Seriously. They're aggressive, loud, and react to any disturbance. Historically used as guard animals.
- **Trail cameras:** Battery-operated game cameras on approaches. Check SD cards regularly. Limited by battery life.

## Rules of Engagement

Your group needs to agree on when force is justified before a situation forces a snap decision:

1. **Warning first.** Verbal challenge, clearly audible. "Stop. Identify yourself." Most situations are resolved at this stage.
2. **Warning shot** (if applicable and agreed upon). Fires into the ground or air to demonstrate seriousness. Not everyone agrees with this practice — a missed warning shot can hit something unintended.
3. **Defensive force.** Only when a clear, immediate threat is present — someone breaching the perimeter with weapons, attacking a group member, or refusing to stop after warnings.
4. **Proportional response.** A kid stealing corn from the garden isn't a shooting situation. A group trying to breach your shelter at night is different.

Put this in writing. Brief everyone. Revisit periodically.

## Operational Security (OPSEC)

What you don't reveal is as important as what you defend:

- Don't broadcast your location, supplies, or group size to strangers.
- Control light and noise discipline at night — visible lights and audible generators tell everyone within miles that someone has resources worth taking.
- Vary patrol routes and times so observers can't predict your pattern.
- Be cautious on radio — assume others are listening. Use code words for sensitive information.
- Don't leave trash or evidence of abundance outside your perimeter.

## Evacuation Plan

Sometimes the right move is to leave. Have a plan:

1. **Triggers:** What conditions make you abandon the position? Overwhelming force, fire, contamination?
2. **Routes:** At least two pre-planned routes out. Walk them in advance. Mark rally points.
3. **Bug-out bags:** Pre-packed essentials for each person — 72 hours of food/water, medical kit, documents, ammunition, one change of clothes.
4. **Caches:** If possible, pre-position supplies along evacuation routes. A buried 5-gallon bucket with food, water, and basic tools at each rally point.
5. **Rally point:** Where does the group reassemble if separated? Primary and alternate. Make sure everyone knows both.

> **Deterrence is cheaper than defense.** Looking hard to attack is cheaper than actually being hard to attack. Most threats in a disaster scenario are opportunistic — they're looking for the easiest target, not a fight. Visible watchstanders, a maintained fence, dogs, and a community that clearly has its act together will deter 95% of potential problems without a single shot being fired.
