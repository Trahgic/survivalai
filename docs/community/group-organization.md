A group of people without organization is a mob. A mob wastes resources, makes bad decisions, and falls apart under stress. You don't need a political system or a constitution — you need clear roles, agreed rules, and a way to make decisions when people disagree. The groups that survive long-term are the ones that figure this out early.

## Leadership Structures

There's no single right structure. What matters is that everyone knows how decisions get made and who's responsible for what.

### Single Leader

One person makes final decisions. Fastest in emergencies. Risk: bad leader = bad outcomes for everyone, and removing a bad leader is hard once authority is established.

**Works best for:** Small groups (under 10), immediate crisis situations, groups with one clearly experienced person, and military-style organizations.

**Key rule:** Even a single leader should consult before big decisions. A leader who ignores input loses trust and eventually loses followers.

### Council

A small group (3–7 people) makes decisions together. Each council member may represent a functional area (medical, food, security, etc.) or a subgroup. Decisions are by majority vote or consensus.

**Works best for:** Groups of 10–50, longer-term situations, groups with multiple skilled individuals. Slower than single leadership but distributes risk and knowledge.

**Key rule:** Odd number of council members prevents tied votes. Establish what happens when the council deadlocks before it happens.

### Rotating Leadership

Different people lead based on the situation. The person with the most relevant experience takes point. The medic leads during a medical emergency. The farmer leads during planting season. The security person leads during a threat.

**Works best for:** Groups with diverse skills and mutual trust. Requires maturity — people have to accept direction from someone they outrank in other areas.

### Consensus

Everyone must agree before action is taken. No majority overruling the minority.

**Works best for:** Very small groups (under 8) with shared values. Completely impractical for larger groups or time-sensitive decisions. Sounds democratic, often results in paralysis or lowest-common-denominator decisions.

## Essential Roles

These need to be assigned early, even if informally:

**Operations/Leadership:** Who makes the call when there's no time to discuss? Who coordinates between roles?

**Medical:** Who handles injuries and illness? Even basic first aid knowledge is critical. See the medicine section articles.

**Food/Water:** Who manages food stores, rationing, hunting, gardening, water procurement?

**Security:** Who watches the perimeter? Who decides the defense plan? Who mediates disputes before they become fights?

**Logistics:** Who tracks supplies, inventory, and resource allocation? This is boring and absolutely critical. Without it, you don't know what you have or when you'll run out.

**Communications:** Who talks to other groups? Who monitors radio? Who's the designated runner if electronic comms are down?

Assign based on competence, not personality. The loudest person isn't necessarily the best leader. The person who keeps a calm head and knows what they're talking about is.

## Decision-Making Framework

Before a crisis forces you to improvise, agree on these:

1. **Who decides what?** Day-to-day operational decisions (meal planning, work assignments) shouldn't need a council vote. Strategic decisions (relocating, major resource expenditure, accepting new members) should involve broader input.
2. **How fast?** In an emergency (attack, fire, medical crisis), the designated leader decides immediately. No discussion. Debrief afterward. In non-emergencies, discuss and vote.
3. **What if we disagree?** Majority vote? Leader decides after hearing input? The mechanism matters less than having one. If there's no agreed process, disagreements spiral into power struggles.
4. **Can decisions be reversed?** Yes — but not in the middle of execution. Commit, execute, then evaluate and adjust. Changing direction mid-action gets people killed.

## Conflict Resolution

Conflict is inevitable. People under stress with scarce resources will fight over food, workload, sleeping arrangements, and personal grievances. Unresolved conflict destroys groups faster than external threats.

### Step Process

1. **Separate.** Get the parties apart to cool down. Address it within 24 hours — don't let it fester.
2. **Hear both sides.** A neutral third party (or the leader) listens to each person individually. No interrupting. The goal is understanding the problem, not assigning blame.
3. **Identify the real issue.** Most arguments aren't about what they seem to be about. A fight over who took too much food is often really about perceived unfairness, fear of scarcity, or someone feeling disrespected.
4. **Find a solution both can live with.** It doesn't have to be perfect or make both people happy. It has to be fair and enforceable.
5. **Make it public if necessary.** If the conflict involved the whole group (resource disputes, rule violations), the resolution should be communicated to everyone. Transparency prevents rumors.

### Dealing with Bad Actors

Eventually, someone will refuse to work, steal from the group, threaten others, or undermine leadership. You need a plan for this before it happens.

**Graduated response:**
1. Private conversation. Explain the problem, give them a chance to correct.
2. Public warning. The group knows the issue and the expectations.
3. Restriction. Reduced privileges, assigned specific tasks under supervision.
4. Expulsion. The last resort. Sending someone away in a survival situation is potentially a death sentence. It should require agreement from the leadership council, not just one person's decision.

Document infractions. Memory is unreliable and disputes about "what really happened" undermine fairness.

## Rules That Work

Keep rules simple, few, and enforceable. Five good rules are better than twenty nobody follows.

**Essential rules for any survival group:**

1. Everyone contributes. No one eats who doesn't work (adjusted for age, ability, and health).
2. No stealing. Taking from the common supply without authorization is treated seriously.
3. No violence against group members. Disagreements are resolved through the agreed process.
4. Follow the watch/duty rotation. Security and critical tasks are non-negotiable.
5. Information flows up. If you see a problem, report it. Don't assume someone else noticed.

Post the rules where everyone can see them. Enforce them consistently — rules that apply to some but not others breed resentment faster than anything.

## Maintaining Morale

Groups don't fall apart because of external threats. They fall apart because people lose hope, trust, or purpose. Morale maintenance is a leadership responsibility.

- **Give people meaningful work.** Idle people spiral into anxiety and conflict. Make sure everyone has tasks that matter and that they can see contributing to the group's survival.
- **Celebrate small wins.** The garden produced its first harvest. The radio made contact. The shelter is weatherproof. Acknowledge progress.
- **Maintain routines.** Predictable daily schedules reduce anxiety. Meal times, work periods, rest periods, watch rotations — a structure gives people a framework to hold onto.
- **Rest days.** Burnout is real. Schedule rest. People who work without breaks make mistakes, get sick, and resent the group.
- **Social time.** People need to talk, tell stories, play games, and connect as humans, not just as survival units. This isn't wasted time — it's what keeps people together.
- **Honesty about the situation.** Don't sugarcoat, but don't doom-spiral either. Give people accurate information and a realistic plan. Uncertainty is more demoralizing than bad news.

> **The group IS the survival strategy.** One person alone in a survival situation is fragile. Ten people with clear roles, agreed rules, and mutual trust are resilient. The social infrastructure is as important as the physical infrastructure — maybe more. A community with good leadership and poor shelter outlasts a well-stocked compound full of people who don't trust each other.
