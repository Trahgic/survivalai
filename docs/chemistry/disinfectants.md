Disinfection kills pathogens on surfaces, tools, and skin. Sterilization kills everything. In a survival situation, the ability to disinfect wounds, water, food-preparation surfaces, and medical instruments prevents the infections that kill more people than trauma does. You don't need commercial products — effective disinfectants can be made from common materials.

## Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

The most versatile disinfectant available. Cheap, effective against bacteria, viruses, fungi, and many spores.

### Dilution Ratios

| Use | Bleach Concentration | Ratio | Contact Time |
|-----|---------------------|-------|-------------|
| General surface cleaning | 500 ppm | 1 tablespoon per gallon of water | 1 minute |
| Bathroom/high contamination | 1,000 ppm | 2 tablespoons per gallon | 5 minutes |
| Blood/body fluid cleanup | 5,000 ppm | 1/2 cup per gallon | 10 minutes |
| Medical instrument soak | 5,000 ppm | 1/2 cup per gallon | 30 minutes |
| Water purification | See water-treatment-chemicals.md | — | 30 minutes |

These ratios assume standard 8.25% household bleach. For 5.25% bleach, increase amounts by about 50%.

### How to Use

1. Clean the surface first with soap and water. Bleach is deactivated by organic matter — blood, food, dirt, and grime consume the available chlorine before it can kill pathogens.
2. Apply bleach solution to the clean surface. Wipe or spray evenly.
3. Keep the surface wet for the required contact time. Don't wipe it off early.
4. Allow to air dry, or rinse with clean water after the contact time for food-preparation surfaces.

### Limitations

- Bleach corrodes metals. Don't soak steel instruments for extended periods — 30 minutes maximum, then rinse and dry.
- Bleach degrades in sunlight, heat, and with age. Make fresh solutions daily for critical disinfection.
- Toxic if mixed with ammonia (produces chloramine gas) or acids (produces chlorine gas). Both are potentially lethal. Never mix bleach with other cleaning chemicals.
- Bleach solutions are irritating to skin and eyes. Ventilate the area. Wear gloves for prolonged use.

## Alcohol

Alcohol at 60–90% concentration is an effective disinfectant for skin and surfaces. Both ethanol and isopropyl alcohol work. Below 60%, it's too dilute to be reliably effective. Above 90%, paradoxically, it's less effective because some water is needed for the alcohol to penetrate cell walls.

### Applications

**Skin antiseptic:** Rubbing alcohol (70% isopropyl) or high-proof liquor (140+ proof = 70% ethanol). Apply to skin before injections, minor procedures, or wound edges (don't pour alcohol into an open wound — it damages tissue and delays healing. Clean wounds with clean water. Use alcohol on the surrounding intact skin only).

**Surface disinfection:** Wipe surfaces with 70% alcohol. Effective against most bacteria and many viruses. Let air dry — the evaporation time IS the contact time.

**Instrument disinfection:** Soak or wipe instruments with 70% alcohol. Effective for disinfection but not sterilization — alcohol does not kill bacterial spores.

### Making Disinfectant Alcohol

If you have access to distilled spirits:
- 140-proof (70% ABV) spirits work directly as disinfectant. Most commercially available hard liquors are 80 proof (40%) — too weak. You'd need to distill to higher proof or use evaporation to concentrate.
- See **fuel-chemistry.md** for distillation basics.

If you have rubbing alcohol (isopropyl), use it at 70% concentration. Don't drink it — isopropyl alcohol is toxic even in small amounts.

## Boiling (Heat Sterilization)

Boiling is the most reliable field sterilization method. It kills all bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites, including spores (with sufficient time).

### For Medical Instruments

1. Clean instruments thoroughly with soap and water first.
2. Submerge completely in water.
3. Bring to a rolling boil. Maintain the boil for 20 minutes for disinfection or 30 minutes for near-sterilization.
4. Remove with clean tongs (not bare hands — you'll contaminate them and burn yourself). Place on a clean cloth to air dry. Don't towel-dry — the towel recontaminates them.
5. Use immediately or store in a clean, sealed container.

### For Water

A rolling boil for 1 minute kills all pathogens (3 minutes at elevations above 6,500 feet / 2,000 meters where water boils at a lower temperature). See **purification-methods.md**.

### Limitations

Not practical for surfaces, fabrics, or large objects. Reserved for water, instruments, and small items that fit in a pot.

## Dry Heat Sterilization

An oven or enclosed hot space can sterilize heat-resistant items.

- **325°F (163°C) for 2 hours:** Full sterilization for glass, metal instruments, and ceramic. Wrap items in aluminum foil to keep them sterile after removal.
- **250°F (121°C) for 30 minutes:** This is autoclave-equivalent temperature and time, but only works in a pressurized environment (like an actual autoclave or pressure cooker). In a regular oven at atmospheric pressure, use the longer/hotter method above.

### Pressure Cooker Sterilization

A standard kitchen pressure cooker reaches approximately 250°F (121°C) at 15 PSI — autoclave conditions. Wrap instruments in cloth, place on a rack above the water, bring to full pressure, and maintain for 20 minutes. This is as close to true sterilization as you can get outside a hospital.

## UV Radiation

Ultraviolet light from the sun damages DNA in microorganisms. Used for water disinfection (SODIS method — see **water-treatment-chemicals.md**) and surface disinfection.

**For surfaces:** Direct sunlight for 6+ hours significantly reduces pathogen counts on hard surfaces. This is slow and weather-dependent, but free and requires nothing.

**UV lamps:** If you have access to a UV-C germicidal lamp (254 nm wavelength), it kills surface pathogens within minutes of exposure. Used in hospitals and water treatment. Requires electricity.

## Hydrogen Peroxide

3% hydrogen peroxide (the common drugstore brown bottle) is a mild disinfectant effective against many bacteria and viruses.

**Surface disinfection:** Spray or wipe 3% hydrogen peroxide on surfaces. Let sit 1 minute. No rinsing needed — it breaks down into water and oxygen.

**Wound care:** 3% hydrogen peroxide can be used to clean a wound on initial treatment, but repeated use damages healing tissue. Use it once to flush debris, then switch to clean water for subsequent cleanings.

**Shelf life:** Hydrogen peroxide degrades over time, especially once opened. An unopened bottle lasts about 3 years. Once opened, it starts losing potency within a month. Test by pouring some on a surface — if it fizzes, it's still active.

## Improvised Disinfection

When you have nothing else:

**Vinegar (5% acetic acid):** Kills some bacteria but is NOT a reliable disinfectant by medical standards. It does not kill tuberculosis, many viruses, or spores. Useful for general surface cleaning when nothing better is available, but don't rely on it for medical disinfection.

**Salt water (saline):** A strong salt solution (3–5 tablespoons per quart of water) inhibits bacterial growth and is excellent for wound irrigation. It's not a disinfectant — it doesn't kill pathogens — but it's safe, gentle, and keeps wounds cleaner than untreated water.

**Ash water (lye):** The alkaline solution from wood ash leaching kills many bacteria and is historically used for cleaning and surface disinfection. It's caustic — rinse surfaces afterward and don't use on skin.

**Sunlight and air:** Hanging fabric, clothes, and bedding in direct sunlight kills many pathogens. UV breaks down DNA, and drying removes the moisture bacteria need. This is why airing bedding in sunlight has been a sanitation practice for centuries.

## Priority Disinfection Targets

If you're rationing disinfectant, focus on these in order:

1. **Hands** — Before wound care, food preparation, and after latrine use. Soap and water is the gold standard. Alcohol sanitizer when water isn't available.
2. **Medical instruments** — Before any procedure that breaks skin. Boil or pressure-cook.
3. **Wound edges** — Alcohol on intact skin around a wound. Clean water to irrigate the wound itself.
4. **Food preparation surfaces** — Bleach solution, wiped down after each use.
5. **Drinking water** — Chemical treatment or boiling.
6. **Toilet/latrine areas** — Bleach solution or lime (calcium hydroxide) powder.
7. **Bedding and clothing** — Wash in hot water with soap. Sun-dry.

> **The single most effective thing you can do:** Wash your hands with soap and water. Frequently. Before eating, before treating wounds, after using the latrine, after handling animals. Proper handwashing prevents more disease transmission than all other disinfection methods combined. It's not glamorous. It works.
