Chemical treatment kills bacteria, viruses, and most protozoa in water when boiling isn't practical. The three main options are chlorine (bleach), iodine, and chlorine dioxide. Each has specific dosages — too little won't kill pathogens, too much is toxic. Get the numbers right.

This article covers the chemistry. For full water purification including filtration and other methods, see **purification-methods.md**.

## Chlorine (Bleach)

The most widely available water disinfectant. Regular unscented household bleach contains sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), typically at 5.25–8.25% concentration. Check the label — you want plain bleach with no added fragrances, surfactants, or thickeners.

### Dosages for Clear Water

| Bleach Concentration | Amount per Gallon | Amount per Liter |
|---------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| 5.25% (older formulation) | 8 drops | 2 drops |
| 8.25% (current standard) | 6 drops | 1.5 drops (round to 2) |

For cloudy or cold water, double the dose.

### Procedure

1. If the water is cloudy, filter it first through cloth, coffee filter, or sand to remove sediment. Chlorine binds to organic particles and gets "used up" before it can kill pathogens.
2. Add the correct amount of bleach.
3. Stir or shake.
4. Wait 30 minutes. The water should have a faint chlorine smell. If it doesn't, add another dose and wait 15 more minutes.
5. If you can't tolerate the taste, let the water sit uncovered for a few hours — the chlorine dissipates.

### Shelf Life

Liquid bleach loses potency over time. A fresh bottle is at full strength. After 6 months, it's lost 20% of its strength. After a year, it may be at 50% or less. Increase your dosage accordingly for old bleach, or switch to calcium hypochlorite (see below).

**Storage tip:** Buy fresh bleach and rotate your stock annually. Don't rely on a bottle that's been in the garage for 3 years.

## Calcium Hypochlorite (Pool Shock)

The long-term storage solution for chlorine disinfection. Calcium hypochlorite (Ca(ClO)₂) is a dry granular powder that stores for 10+ years if kept sealed, dry, and cool. A 1-pound bag can treat roughly 10,000 gallons of water.

### Making a Stock Solution

Don't add the dry granules directly to drinking water — it's impossible to dose accurately.

1. Dissolve 1 level teaspoon (about 7 grams) of calcium hypochlorite in 1 gallon (4 liters) of water. This produces a stock solution of approximately 500 ppm chlorine.
2. Stir thoroughly and let any undissolved particles settle. Use the clear liquid.
3. This stock solution degrades the same way liquid bleach does — make fresh batches weekly.

### Using the Stock Solution

Add the stock solution to your drinking water at the same rate as regular bleach:

- 8 drops per gallon of clear water
- 16 drops per gallon of cloudy water

Wait 30 minutes. Check for faint chlorine smell.

### Buying the Right Product

Look for calcium hypochlorite with 65–73% available chlorine. This is sold as pool shock, pool chlorine granules, or "HTH." Make sure the product:

- Does NOT contain algaecides, clarifiers, or other additives
- Lists calcium hypochlorite as the active ingredient
- States 65% or higher available chlorine

> **WARNING:** Calcium hypochlorite is a strong oxidizer. It can ignite if it contacts oil, grease, brake fluid, or other organic materials. Store it in its original sealed container, away from heat and other chemicals. Never mix with acids, ammonia, or other pool chemicals.

## Iodine

Iodine is an effective water disinfectant that kills bacteria, viruses, and most protozoa (except Cryptosporidium — iodine doesn't reliably kill Crypto cysts at standard doses).

### Forms and Dosages

**Tincture of iodine (2% solution — the brown liquid in first aid kits):**
- Clear water: 5 drops per liter (20 drops per gallon)
- Cloudy water: 10 drops per liter
- Wait 30 minutes (60 minutes if water is cold, below 40°F / 4°C)

**Iodine tablets (Potable Aqua, Globaline):**
- Follow package directions (typically 1 tablet per liter, wait 30 minutes)
- Double the tablets and wait time for cold or cloudy water

**Povidone-iodine (Betadine, 10% solution):**
- 4 drops per liter of clear water
- 8 drops per liter of cloudy water
- Wait 30 minutes

### Limitations

- Iodine leaves a noticeable taste. Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) powder or a crushed vitamin C tablet added after the wait time neutralizes the taste without affecting safety.
- **Don't use iodine for long-term daily water treatment.** Extended iodine consumption (more than a few weeks) can cause thyroid problems. It's a short-term field solution, not a permanent system.
- Pregnant women and people with thyroid disorders should avoid iodine-treated water.
- Iodine does not kill Cryptosporidium reliably. If Crypto contamination is suspected, filter first with a 1-micron filter or boil.

## Chlorine Dioxide

Chlorine dioxide (ClO₂) is arguably the best chemical water treatment option. It kills bacteria, viruses, AND Cryptosporidium. It's effective across a wider pH range than chlorine. It leaves less aftertaste.

### Commercial Products

Sold as water purification drops or tablets (Aquamira, Katadyn Micropur MP1, Potable Aqua Chlorine Dioxide). Follow package directions — typically:

- Aquamira drops: mix Part A and Part B, wait 5 minutes for activation, add to water, wait 15–30 minutes (4 hours for Cryptosporidium)
- Katadyn Micropur tablets: 1 tablet per liter, wait 30 minutes (4 hours for Crypto)

### Improvised ClO₂ Generation

Chlorine dioxide can be generated by reacting sodium chlorite (NaClO₂) with an acid:

1. Sodium chlorite solution (typically 3.15% or 22.4%) is the precursor. This is different from sodium hypochlorite (bleach) — don't confuse them.
2. Add citric acid or phosphoric acid to activate. The resulting gas dissolves in water as chlorine dioxide.
3. This is the chemistry behind commercial two-part products like Aquamira.

This is more complex than bleach treatment and requires specific chemicals. For most people, buying the commercial product is practical and reliable.

## Comparison Table

| Method | Kills Bacteria | Kills Viruses | Kills Crypto | Wait Time | Taste | Long-Term Use | Shelf Life |
|--------|---------------|---------------|-------------|-----------|-------|--------------|-----------|
| Bleach (NaOCl) | Yes | Yes | No | 30 min | Slight | Yes | 6–12 months |
| Cal-hypo (dry) | Yes | Yes | No | 30 min | Slight | Yes | 10+ years |
| Iodine | Yes | Yes | No | 30 min | Strong | No (weeks only) | 5+ years |
| Chlorine dioxide | Yes | Yes | Yes | 30 min (4 hr Crypto) | Minimal | Yes | 4+ years (tablets) |
| Boiling | Yes | Yes | Yes | 1 minute at rolling boil | None | Yes | N/A |

## Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

Not a chemical method, but worth mentioning here: fill a clear PET plastic bottle with water, lay it on a reflective surface (corrugated metal roof) in direct sunlight for 6+ hours (or 2 days in cloudy conditions). UV radiation and heat kill most pathogens. Effective for bacteria and viruses. Partially effective against protozoa. Free, requires no chemicals, but slow and weather-dependent.

## Combined Approaches

No single method handles everything. The safest approach combines methods:

1. **Filter first** (cloth, sand filter, or commercial filter) to remove sediment and protozoa
2. **Chemically treat** (chlorine or ClO₂) to kill bacteria and viruses
3. Or simply **boil** if fuel is available — boiling kills everything

> **The numbers matter.** Don't eyeball chemical water treatment. Too little leaves pathogens alive. Too much makes you sick. Memorize or carry a reference card with the exact dosages for your chosen disinfectant. When the situation is uncertain — more contact time is always safer than less. Doubling the wait time costs nothing and catches edge cases.
