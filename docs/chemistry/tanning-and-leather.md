Animal hides are one of the most useful raw materials in a survival situation — clothing, shoes, bags, belts, straps, sheaths, gaskets, hinges, and lacing. But a raw hide rots within days. Tanning is the chemical process that turns a perishable skin into durable leather that lasts for years.

## Rawhide (No Tanning Required)

Rawhide is simply a dried hide with the hair removed. It's not leather — it's stiff, hard, and becomes flexible again when wet. But it has its own uses: lashings, drum heads, snowshoe webbing, knife sheaths, containers, and repair patches. It's incredibly strong when dry.

### Processing Rawhide

1. **Flesh the hide.** Lay the hide fur-side down on a smooth log or board. Scrape off all meat, fat, and membrane from the flesh side using a drawknife, dull blade, or the edge of a rib bone. Be thorough — any remaining tissue will rot.
2. **Remove hair.** Soak the hide in a wood ash and water solution (fill a bucket with hardwood ash, add water, stir) for 3–7 days. The alkaline solution loosens the hair roots. Check daily — when hair pulls out easily, it's ready. Alternatively, let the hide sit in shade for 2–3 days until the hair slips on its own (beginning decomposition loosens it — but this smells terrible and requires careful timing).
3. **Dehair.** Drape the hide over a log, fur side up. Scrape with a dull blade or fleshing tool. The hair should slide off easily. Flip and scrape the flesh side again to remove the grain layer if you want a more flexible rawhide.
4. **Rinse** thoroughly in clean water. Wring out excess water.
5. **Stretch and dry.** Lace the hide into a frame or stretch flat with stakes. Dry in shade — direct sun dries it too fast and makes it brittle. It dries into a hard, translucent sheet.

## Brain Tanning (Buckskin)

Brain tanning produces soft, supple leather using the animal's own brain as the tanning agent. The oils and lecithin in brain tissue coat the hide fibers and prevent them from bonding to each other, which is what makes untanned hide stiff. This is the traditional method used by Indigenous peoples across North America. The result — buckskin — is arguably the finest clothing leather ever made.

Every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide. That's not a coincidence.

### Full Process

**Step 1: Flesh and dehair** — Same as rawhide processing above. Ash soak for dehairing works well. After dehairing, scrape both sides aggressively to thin the hide and open the fiber structure. A thinner hide tans more evenly.

**Step 2: Prepare the brain solution.**
1. Use the brain from the animal (one brain per hide). Or use a substitute: egg yolks (a dozen per deer hide), mashed liver, or even commercial soap flakes dissolved in warm water.
2. Mash the brain thoroughly in warm water. You want a smooth, creamy slurry. Use about 1 quart of brain solution per deer-sized hide.
3. Warm it — not hot, just warm to the touch (about 100°F / 38°C).

**Step 3: Brain the hide.**
1. Wring as much water out of the hide as possible.
2. Work the brain solution into every part of the hide with your hands. Massage it in. The hide should be saturated.
3. Roll the hide up and let it soak for several hours or overnight.
4. Wring out the hide. Soak in brain solution again. Repeat 2–3 times. More brainings = softer leather.

**Step 4: Soften (the hard part).**
1. Wring the hide as dry as possible. Really wring it — twist it over a stick, pull it, squeeze every bit of moisture out.
2. Now stretch it as it dries. This is the critical step that determines leather quality. The hide must be stretched, pulled, and worked continuously as it dries. If any section dries without being stretched, it goes hard and stiff — essentially reverting to rawhide.
3. Pull it over a staking post (a vertical post with a rounded top, or a taut rope). Work every section, changing angles. Pull, stretch, rub, repeat.
4. This takes hours. For a deer hide, expect 4–8 hours of continuous work. You can't take a long break — if the hide dries stiff, you have to re-wet, re-brain, and start over.
5. The hide is done when it's completely dry AND soft throughout. It should feel like a heavy chamois cloth.

**Step 5: Smoke.**
Smoking is essential — it makes brain-tanned leather water-resistant. Without smoking, brain-tanned leather that gets wet and dries will stiffen back up.

1. Build a small, smoky fire using punky (rotting) wood. You want thick smoke, very little flame. Douglas fir, alder, or any punky hardwood works well. Don't use pine — resin turns the leather sticky.
2. Sew the hide into a tube or bag shape. Suspend it over the smoke source so the smoke fills the interior.
3. Smoke for 30 minutes to several hours until the hide turns a golden to dark brown color throughout. The smoke deposits tannins and aldehydes that cross-link with the fibers and prevent them from re-stiffening.
4. Open the hide and check that the color is even all the way through (cut a small edge piece to verify). If some areas are still white, re-smoke.

The finished product is brain-tanned, smoked buckskin — soft, flexible, breathable, washable (it softens again after washing if you work it while drying), and durable.

## Bark Tanning (Vegetable Tanning)

Bark tanning uses tannins extracted from tree bark to chemically cross-link hide proteins. This produces firmer, more water-resistant leather than brain tanning — the kind used for shoe soles, belts, holsters, saddles, and tooling leather.

### Tannin Sources

| Source | Tannin Content | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Oak bark | High | Best all-around tanning bark |
| Hemlock bark | High | Traditional in North America |
| Chestnut bark/wood | Very high | European tradition |
| Sumac leaves | High | Produces light-colored leather |
| Willow bark | Moderate | Available near water |
| Acorn (ground) | Moderate | Use crushed whole acorns |
| Black walnut hulls | High | Dyes leather dark brown |

### Process

1. **Prepare the hide.** Flesh, dehair, and scrape as above. For bark tanning, leave the grain layer intact (don't scrape the outer surface as aggressively as for brain tanning). Rinse well.
2. **Make bark liquor.** Harvest bark from your tannin source. Inner bark is richest. Chop or grind it. Soak in water — a rough ratio is 1 part bark to 3 parts water by volume. Let it steep for days to weeks, stirring occasionally. The water turns dark reddish-brown. Strain out the bark. This is your tanning liquor.
3. **Tan.** Submerge the hide completely in the tanning liquor. Use a container large enough for the hide to move freely. Weight it down so no part is above the surface.
4. **Wait.** Bark tanning is slow — 2 to 6 months for a full tan. Move the hide daily for the first few weeks to ensure even penetration. Make fresh, stronger bark liquor every few weeks and transfer the hide to it.
5. **Check progress.** Cut a small piece from the edge. If the color (tannin penetration) goes all the way through the cross-section, it's done. If there's still a light stripe in the middle, keep tanning.
6. **Rinse and oil.** Remove from the liquor, rinse in clean water for a day. While still damp, apply a mixture of tallow and neatsfoot oil (or any animal oil) by rubbing it into both sides. This replaces moisture with oil and keeps the leather flexible.
7. **Dry slowly** in shade. Work the leather periodically as it dries to keep it flexible — though bark-tanned leather won't be as soft as brain-tanned. It's meant to be firmer.

## Preservation and Care

**Oiling:** Leather dries out over time and cracks. Apply neatsfoot oil, mink oil, rendered tallow, or any animal-based oil every few months. Vegetable oils (except coconut) can go rancid and damage leather.

**Waterproofing:** Beeswax melted into the leather's surface provides good water resistance. Melt beeswax, rub into the leather, then heat the surface with a hair dryer or by holding near a fire to drive the wax into the fibers.

**Storage:** Keep leather dry. If it gets soaking wet, dry it slowly at room temperature — don't put it near a fire or in direct sun, which causes shrinking and cracking.

> **Time investment reality check:** Tanning is not fast. Brain tanning a deer hide takes 2–3 days of intense physical work. Bark tanning takes months of soaking. But the product is extraordinary — leather that you made from an animal you harvested, useful for years, repairable, and replaceable as long as you have animals and trees. It's one of the oldest and most valuable craft skills humans developed.
