Soap is fat plus a strong alkali (lye). That's it. The chemical reaction between them — saponification — produces soap and glycerin. People have made soap from animal fat and wood ash for thousands of years. It's one of the most important things you can produce for long-term survival because hygiene prevents disease, and disease kills more people in disasters than injuries do.

## Making Lye from Wood Ash

Commercial lye (sodium hydroxide, NaOH) is ideal but may not be available. You can make potassium hydroxide (KOH) lye from hardwood ash. KOH produces soft soap (liquid or paste), not hard bar soap. It still works perfectly for cleaning.

### Step by Step

1. Collect ash from hardwood fires only — oak, maple, hickory, ash, beech. Softwood (pine, spruce, cedar) ash is too low in potassium to make good lye. Don't use charcoal briquette ash — it has additives.
2. Build a leaching container: a wooden barrel, bucket, or trough with a small drain hole at the bottom. Line the bottom with a layer of straw or gravel to act as a filter.
3. Pack the ash into the container loosely. Don't compress it — water needs to flow through.
4. Pour rainwater (or any soft water — hard water with minerals interferes) slowly over the ash. Let it seep through and collect in a container below the drain. This takes hours to days depending on your setup.
5. The liquid that drains out is lye water (potassium hydroxide solution). It should be yellowish-brown.
6. **Test the strength:** The traditional test — float a feather or a small egg in the lye water. If a fresh egg floats with a quarter-sized area above the surface, the lye is approximately strong enough for soap making. If the egg sinks, the lye is too weak — pour it back through the ash again, or boil it down to concentrate.
7. Another test: dip a feather in the lye. If the barbs dissolve within a few minutes, it's strong enough.

> **WARNING:** Lye is extremely caustic. It burns skin, destroys eyes, and is fatal if swallowed. Wear gloves and eye protection. If lye contacts skin, flush with large amounts of water immediately (not vinegar — the neutralization reaction generates heat and makes it worse). Keep away from children and animals. Work outdoors or in a very well-ventilated area.

## Rendering Fat

Animal fat needs to be cleaned (rendered) before soap making. Raw fat contains meat, blood, and connective tissue that make rancid, smelly soap.

1. Cut the fat into small pieces or grind it. Smaller pieces render faster.
2. Put it in a pot with about 1/4 the volume of water.
3. Heat on low — simmer, don't boil. Stir occasionally. The fat melts out of the tissue over 2–4 hours.
4. Strain through cheesecloth or a fine mesh to remove solid bits (cracklings — these are edible).
5. Let the strained liquid cool. The clean fat solidifies on top, the water and impurities settle below.
6. Lift off the fat disk, scrape any gunk off the bottom.
7. For cleaner soap, repeat: remelt the fat with fresh water, cool, and skim again. Two or three renders produce very clean tallow.

**Best fats for soap:** Beef tallow and pork lard make hard, long-lasting bars. Chicken fat works but makes softer soap. Vegetable oils (olive, coconut, palm) make excellent soap if available. Coconut oil produces the most lather.

## Cold Process Soap Making

This is the standard method for making bar soap with commercial lye (NaOH).

### Ingredients (Basic Tallow Soap — 2 lb batch)

- 16 oz (1 lb) rendered beef tallow or lard
- 2.1 oz sodium hydroxide (NaOH) — this ratio is specific. Do not guess.
- 5.5 oz water (distilled or rain water)

The ratio of lye to fat is critical and varies by fat type. Too much lye = harsh, caustic soap. Too little lye = greasy, soft soap that doesn't clean. This is called the "saponification value" and is different for every oil and fat.

### Lye-to-Fat Ratios (NaOH per pound of fat)

| Fat/Oil | NaOH Needed per Pound |
|---------|----------------------|
| Beef tallow | 2.1 oz |
| Pork lard | 2.0 oz |
| Olive oil | 1.9 oz |
| Coconut oil | 2.5 oz |
| Vegetable shortening | 2.0 oz |

Always "superfat" by using 5% less lye than calculated. This ensures no free lye remains in the finished soap and makes it milder on skin.

### Process

1. **Mix the lye solution:** Slowly add the NaOH to the water — never water to lye (it can boil explosively). Stir with a wooden or silicone spoon (not aluminum — lye corrodes aluminum). The solution will get very hot (200°F+). Set aside to cool to about 100–110°F (38–43°C).
2. **Melt the fat** and cool it to about 100–110°F.
3. **Combine:** Slowly pour the lye solution into the melted fat while stirring constantly. Stir in one direction.
4. **Stir until trace:** Keep stirring (a stick blender speeds this up enormously, but hand-stirring works — it just takes 30–60 minutes). "Trace" is when the mixture thickens to the consistency of thin pudding and a drizzle of the mixture across the surface leaves a visible trail before sinking. This means saponification is underway.
5. **Pour into molds:** Any non-reactive container works — silicone molds, lined wooden boxes, plastic containers, even lined cardboard boxes. Avoid aluminum.
6. **Insulate and cure:** Cover with a towel or blanket. The soap generates its own heat as saponification completes (the "gel phase"). After 24–48 hours, unmold and cut into bars.
7. **Cure:** Set bars on a rack with air circulation between them. Cure for 4–6 weeks. During curing, excess water evaporates and the soap becomes harder and milder. The longer you cure, the better the soap.

### Testing for Safety

After curing, touch the tip of your tongue to the soap (carefully). If it "zaps" like touching a 9V battery, there's free lye — the soap is not safe for skin. It can still be used for cleaning clothes, surfaces, and tools. If it tastes like soap (unpleasant but no zap), it's safe for personal use.

## Soft Soap (Ash Lye Method)

If you're using homemade potassium hydroxide from ash leaching, you'll get soft soap — a paste or thick liquid. This is historically how most soap was made before commercial lye was available.

1. Heat your rendered fat in a pot.
2. Slowly add the hot lye water while stirring. The mixture will be thinner than cold-process soap because KOH doesn't produce hard soap.
3. Continue heating and stirring gently for 2–4 hours. The mixture thickens and becomes translucent.
4. Test: take a small sample, cool it. It should be a clear or translucent gel. If it's milky, cook longer.
5. When done, you have soft soap. Store in a jar. Use directly for washing — a tablespoon in a basin of water is enough.

Soft soap works just as well as bar soap for hygiene. It's actually easier to use for laundry and general cleaning.

## Laundry Soap

Grate bar soap finely (or use soft soap directly). For a basic laundry detergent:

1. 1 bar of soap, grated
2. 1 cup washing soda (sodium carbonate — made by baking baking soda at 400°F / 200°C for 1 hour)
3. 1/2 cup borax (if available)

Mix together. Use 2 tablespoons per load in hot water. Dissolve the mixture in hot water first, then add clothes.

## Troubleshooting

- **Soap is too soft after curing:** Too much water, not enough lye, or the fat was mostly unsaturated (liquid oil). Use harder fats (tallow, coconut oil) or cure longer.
- **White powdery surface (soda ash):** Harmless cosmetic issue. Scrape or wash it off. Caused by the surface reacting with air during initial setting.
- **Oily puddles or separation:** Incomplete saponification. Rebatch: chop the failed soap, add to a pot with a little water, heat gently, stir until uniform, remold.
- **Caustic/lye-heavy soap:** Can happen with imprecise measurements. Use for cleaning, not skin. Or rebatch with additional fat to consume the excess lye.

> **Why it matters:** Soap is the single most effective tool against infectious disease transmission. Proper handwashing with soap kills bacteria and viruses that spread dysentery, cholera, and respiratory infections — diseases that kill far more people in survival situations than injuries or starvation. Make soap. Use it constantly.
