This article covers herbal preparations that have actual clinical evidence or centuries of documented effective traditional use. It's not a complete herbal medicine guide — it's a short list of the things that measurably work, how to prepare them, and what their real limitations are.

> **CRITICAL:** Herbal medicine supplements real medical care — it doesn't replace it. If someone has a serious infection, a broken bone, or a medical emergency, herbs won't save them. Use what works, seek higher care if any option exists, and don't let wishful thinking about natural remedies delay treatment that actually matters. See the medicine section articles for proper emergency medical procedures.

## Preparations

### Tea (Infusion)

Pour boiling water over dried or fresh plant material. Cover and steep 10–15 minutes. Strain and drink. Use approximately 1–2 teaspoons of dried herb (or 1 tablespoon fresh) per cup of water. Teas extract water-soluble compounds.

### Decoction

For hard materials (bark, roots, seeds) that don't release their compounds in a simple steep. Add plant material to cold water, bring to a boil, simmer covered for 20–30 minutes. Strain.

### Tincture

Plant material soaked in alcohol (vodka, grain alcohol, or any drinkable spirit above 40% / 80 proof) for 2–6 weeks. The alcohol extracts and preserves medicinal compounds. Ratio: pack a jar loosely with dried herb, fill with alcohol, seal, shake daily, strain after 4–6 weeks. Dosage is typically 1–2 ml (about 30–60 drops) in water, 2–3 times daily. Tinctures store for years.

### Poultice

Fresh or dried plant material moistened and applied directly to the skin over an injury or infection. Mash fresh leaves, or rehydrate dried material with hot water. Apply warm, cover with cloth, leave for 30–60 minutes. Replace with fresh material as needed.

### Salve

Infuse dried herbs in oil (olive, coconut, or any vegetable oil) by heating gently for 2–4 hours or soaking for 4–6 weeks at room temperature. Strain. Melt beeswax into the infused oil (ratio: 1 oz beeswax per 8 oz oil). Pour into containers. The salve solidifies as it cools. Apply topically.

## Plants That Work

### Willow Bark — Pain and Fever

Willow bark contains salicin, which the body converts to salicylic acid — the precursor to aspirin. It's one of the most well-documented herbal medicines in history.

**Preparation:** Harvest inner bark from willow branches (white willow is strongest, but all willows contain salicin). Peel a few strips of bark, scrape the inner bark (the moist layer between the outer bark and the wood). Use fresh or dry for storage.

**Dosage as tea:** 1–2 teaspoons of dried inner bark, decoct (simmer) in 1 cup of water for 15 minutes. Drink up to 3 cups per day.

**Works for:** Headache, muscle pain, mild fever, joint pain, toothache.

**Limitations:** Slower onset than aspirin (30–60 minutes). Don't give to children under 16 (same Reye's syndrome risk as aspirin). Don't use if allergic to aspirin. Can irritate the stomach — take with food.

### Honey — Wound Care and Coughs

Raw honey is antibacterial. It creates a hostile environment for bacteria through low water activity, acidity, and hydrogen peroxide generation. Clinical studies have shown it's as effective as some commercial wound dressings for minor to moderate wounds.

**For wounds:** Apply a thick layer of raw honey directly to a clean wound. Cover with a dressing. Change daily. Effective against many common wound pathogens including some antibiotic-resistant strains.

**For coughs:** 1–2 teaspoons of raw honey, straight or in warm water with lemon. Clinical trials show it's as effective as dextromethorphan (standard OTC cough suppressant) for nighttime cough in children over 12 months. Never give honey to infants under 12 months (botulism risk).

### Garlic — Antimicrobial

Raw garlic contains allicin, which has measurable antibacterial, antifungal, and antiviral activity. The allicin is produced when garlic is crushed or chopped — it breaks down rapidly with cooking.

**Preparation:** Crush or finely chop raw garlic. Let sit 10 minutes before use (this maximizes allicin production). Eat 2–3 raw cloves daily for systemic antimicrobial effect. Mix with honey to make it palatable.

**Topical:** Crushed garlic in oil applied to fungal skin infections (athlete's foot, ringworm). Don't leave raw garlic on skin for more than 20–30 minutes — it can cause chemical burns.

**Limitations:** Not a substitute for antibiotics in serious infections. The antimicrobial effect is real but mild compared to pharmaceutical antibiotics. Best used as a supplement to other treatment, not a replacement.

### Ginger — Nausea and Digestion

Ginger has strong evidence for anti-nausea effects, especially motion sickness and pregnancy-related nausea.

**Preparation:** Fresh ginger root sliced thin, steeped in hot water for 10 minutes. Or chew small pieces of raw or candied ginger.

**Dosage:** 1–2 grams of fresh ginger per dose (a thumbnail-sized piece), up to 4 grams daily.

**Works for:** Motion sickness, morning sickness, post-surgical nausea, general stomach upset, mild inflammatory pain.

### Chamomile — Anxiety and Sleep

Chamomile tea has mild sedative and anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) effects supported by clinical studies.

**Preparation:** Steep dried chamomile flowers in hot water for 5–10 minutes. Drink 1–3 cups daily, or one cup 30 minutes before sleep.

**Works for:** Mild anxiety, insomnia, stress-related digestive upset. Effects are subtle — don't expect it to knock you out, but it measurably improves sleep quality in studies.

**Caution:** People with ragweed allergies may react to chamomile (same plant family).

### Plantain (Plantago major) — Stings, Bites, Skin

The common lawn weed, not the banana relative. One of the most useful field-expedient wound herbs.

**Preparation:** Pick fresh leaves, chew them to a mash (saliva helps break down the cells), apply the mash directly to insect stings, bee stings, minor cuts, rashes, or splinters.

**Works for:** Reduces pain and swelling from insect stings, draws splinters, soothes minor skin irritation. Contains allantoin (promotes skin healing) and aucubin (antimicrobial).

### Aloe Vera — Burns and Skin

The clear gel inside aloe vera leaves is well-documented for minor burn treatment and skin healing.

**Preparation:** Cut a leaf, slice it open, scrape out the clear gel. Apply directly to the burn. Reapply several times daily.

**Works for:** First-degree burns, sunburn, minor skin irritation. Provides cooling, moisture, and has anti-inflammatory properties.

**Limitations:** Not for deep burns or open wounds. Only the inner gel is useful — the yellow latex layer just under the skin is a strong laxative and should be avoided.

### Peppermint — Digestive Cramping

Peppermint oil/tea relaxes smooth muscle in the digestive tract. Clinical evidence for irritable bowel syndrome, gas, and digestive cramping.

**Preparation:** Tea from fresh or dried leaves, steeped 10 minutes. Or peppermint oil capsules if available.

**Caution:** Can worsen heartburn/GERD by relaxing the lower esophageal sphincter. Don't give peppermint oil to young children — the menthol can cause breathing spasms.

### Turmeric — Anti-Inflammatory

Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, has significant anti-inflammatory effects in clinical studies. It's poorly absorbed alone — combine with black pepper (piperine) to increase absorption by roughly 2,000%.

**Preparation:** 1/2–1 teaspoon turmeric powder in warm water, milk, or food. Add a pinch of black pepper. Take 2–3 times daily.

**Works for:** Joint pain, general inflammation, post-injury swelling. Effects are cumulative — takes days to weeks of consistent use.

## What Doesn't Work (or Lacks Evidence)

Being honest about what the evidence doesn't support:

- **Echinacea for colds:** Mixed study results at best. May shorten cold duration slightly (less than 1 day on average). Not harmful, but don't expect miracles.
- **Goldenseal as an antibiotic:** Berberine (its active compound) has antimicrobial properties in lab dishes but poor absorption when taken orally. Topical application on wounds may help marginally.
- **Comfrey internally:** Contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids that damage the liver. External use on bruises and sprains has some evidence. Never take internally.
- **Colloidal silver:** No evidence of effectiveness. Can cause permanent skin discoloration (argyria). Don't waste resources making or consuming it.
- **Essential oils as antibiotics:** Lavender, tea tree, and oregano oils have some surface antimicrobial activity, but they're not systemic antibiotics. Don't rely on them for serious infections.

> **Bottom line:** The herbs listed above are the ones with real evidence. Willow bark, honey, garlic, ginger, and aloe vera are the heavy hitters. Learn to identify and prepare these. Everything else is secondary. And none of them replace proper wound care, antibiotics, or surgical intervention when those are needed.
