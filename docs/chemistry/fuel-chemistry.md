When gasoline and diesel run out, you still need fuel — for engines, heating, lighting, and cooking. Several alternatives can be produced with basic equipment and locally available materials. None are as energy-dense or convenient as petroleum fuels, but they work.

## Biodiesel

Biodiesel is chemically modified vegetable oil or animal fat that runs in any diesel engine without modification. It's made by reacting oil with an alcohol (methanol) in the presence of a catalyst (lye).

### The Chemistry (Transesterification)

Fat/oil + methanol + NaOH catalyst → biodiesel (fatty acid methyl esters) + glycerin byproduct

### Process (Basic Small-Batch)

> **WARNING:** This process uses methanol (wood alcohol), which is toxic — it can blind or kill you if ingested or absorbed through the skin. It's also flammable. Work outdoors, wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection, avoid breathing vapors.

1. **Collect oil.** Used cooking oil (fryer grease) from restaurants is the classic source. Filter through cloth to remove food particles. Let it settle and pour off water from the bottom — water in the oil ruins the reaction.
2. **Titration test (for used oil).** Used oil contains free fatty acids (FFAs) that interfere with the reaction. Dissolve 1 gram of NaOH in 1 liter of water. Add 1 ml of oil to 10 ml of isopropyl alcohol, then add drops of the NaOH solution while stirring until the pH reaches 8–9 (test with pH strips). The number of ml used tells you how much extra NaOH to add per liter of oil.
3. **Mix the methoxide.** For 1 liter of oil: measure 200 ml of methanol. Dissolve 3.5 grams of NaOH (plus extra from titration) in the methanol. Stir until fully dissolved. This is sodium methoxide — extremely caustic.
4. **React.** Heat the oil to 130°F (55°C). Add the methoxide slowly while stirring vigorously. Continue stirring for 1 hour at temperature. A drill-powered paint mixer works well.
5. **Settle.** Pour into a tall, clear container and let sit for 8+ hours. Two layers form: biodiesel on top (amber, thin), glycerin on the bottom (dark, thick).
6. **Drain the glycerin** from the bottom.
7. **Wash the biodiesel.** Gently mist warm water over the surface. The water sinks through, carrying soap and residual chemicals. Drain the water. Repeat 3–4 times until the wash water runs clear.
8. **Dry.** Let the washed biodiesel sit uncovered in a warm place for a day or heat gently to evaporate remaining water. Water in fuel promotes microbial growth and injector problems.

Yield: roughly 1 liter of biodiesel per liter of oil.

### Using Biodiesel

- Runs in any diesel engine as-is. Can be mixed with petroleum diesel in any ratio.
- Burns cleaner than petroleum diesel. Slightly less energy per gallon (about 8–10% less).
- Gels in cold weather sooner than petroleum diesel. Blend with petro-diesel in winter, or use canola/sunflower oil (lower gel point than tallow-based biodiesel).
- Store in sealed containers out of sunlight. Shelf life: 6–12 months with no stabilizer.

## Straight Vegetable Oil (SVO)

Some diesel engines can run on straight vegetable oil without converting it to biodiesel — but the oil must be preheated to reduce viscosity. This requires a two-tank system: start on diesel, switch to heated SVO once the engine is warm, then purge with diesel before shutdown.

Simpler but less reliable than biodiesel. Long-term SVO use can coke injectors and cause carbon buildup. It's a viable emergency fuel but biodiesel is better for engine longevity.

## Ethanol (Alcohol Fuel)

Ethanol can power gasoline engines and provides a concentrated, storable fuel for cooking and lighting. Making it is straightforward — it's the same process as making liquor.

### The Process

1. **Fermentation.** Mix a sugar source with water and yeast. Sugar sources: fruit, corn, sugar beets, potatoes, grains, molasses — anything starchy or sugary. For grain, you need to convert starches to sugars first (malting: sprout the grain, dry it, grind it, mix with hot water at 150°F / 65°C for 1–2 hours).
2. Add yeast (bread yeast works, distiller's yeast is better). Ferment at 70–80°F (21–27°C) for 5–14 days in a sealed container with an airlock. The yeast converts sugar to ethanol and CO₂.
3. When bubbling stops, fermentation is done. You now have a "wash" at roughly 8–15% alcohol.
4. **Distillation.** Heat the wash in a sealed pot (still). Ethanol boils at 173°F (78.3°C), water at 212°F (100°C). The ethanol vapor rises first, travels through a condensing coil cooled by water, and drips out as liquid ethanol.
5. Discard the first small amount (the "foreshots" — about 1 oz per gallon of wash). This contains methanol and other toxic compounds.
6. Collect the "hearts" — clear, clean-smelling ethanol. Stop collecting when the temperature rises above 190°F (88°C) at the still head.
7. For fuel use, you want the highest proof possible. A single distillation run typically produces 40–60% ethanol. For fuel-grade (above 80%), distill again.

### Using Ethanol as Fuel

- Gasoline engines can run on ethanol with carburetor adjustments (larger jets — ethanol needs about 40% more fuel flow than gasoline for the same power).
- E85 (85% ethanol, 15% gasoline) runs in flex-fuel vehicles without modification.
- Lower energy content than gasoline — about 30% fewer miles per gallon.
- Excellent cooking fuel (burns clean, no soot). Used in alcohol stoves.
- For lighting, burns in any oil lamp with an appropriate wick.

## Wood Gas (Gasification)

Wood gas is a mixture of carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and methane produced by heating wood in a low-oxygen environment. It can run gasoline engines directly — this powered millions of vehicles during World War II when petroleum was unavailable.

### How It Works

1. Wood (chips, pellets, or small blocks) is loaded into a sealed reactor vessel.
2. A controlled amount of air is introduced — enough to sustain partial combustion, not enough for full burning.
3. The heat breaks down the wood through pyrolysis, producing combustible gases: CO, H₂, CH₄, and some tars.
4. The gas passes through a cooling and filtering system (to remove tars and particulates) before entering the engine's air intake.
5. The engine runs on this gas mixed with air, just like it would run on gasoline vapor.

### Limitations

- About 60% the energy output of gasoline per equivalent fuel weight.
- Requires continuous feeding of wood — a vehicle consumes roughly 2 lbs of dry wood per mile.
- Tar buildup clogs filters and can damage engines if filtration is inadequate.
- Carbon monoxide in wood gas is extremely toxic — never run a gasifier indoors or in enclosed spaces.
- Building a reliable gasifier requires welding and metal fabrication skills.

> **WARNING:** Carbon monoxide (CO) is odorless and lethal. Wood gas contains 20–25% CO. A gasifier must only operate outdoors or in extremely well-ventilated spaces. Leaks in the system can kill without warning. Treat every joint and connection as a potential CO source.

## Biogas (Methane)

Organic waste decomposing in an oxygen-free (anaerobic) environment produces methane — the same gas in natural gas. A biogas digester captures this for cooking and lighting.

### Simple Digester

1. A sealed container (55-gallon drum, IBC tote, or purpose-built tank) with an inlet for feeding organic waste and an outlet for gas collection.
2. Feed it animal manure (cow and pig manure are best), food scraps, or green plant material mixed with water to a slurry consistency.
3. Seal the container. Bacteria break down the organic matter and produce methane over 15–40 days, depending on temperature (faster in warm conditions, stalls below 60°F / 15°C).
4. Collect the gas from the top of the digester through a pipe. Route to a storage bag (inner tube, gas bag) or directly to a burner.
5. The digestate (leftover material) is excellent fertilizer — nutrient-rich and pathogen-reduced.

### Yield

A small household digester fed daily with 10–20 lbs of manure can produce enough gas for 2–4 hours of cooking per day. That's not a lot — biogas is supplemental, not a primary energy source for most small operations.

### Safety

Methane is explosive when mixed with air at 5–15% concentration. Keep flames away from the digester and storage bags. Ensure connections are gas-tight. Operate outdoors.

## Charcoal

Not a chemistry process exactly, but charcoal is a manufactured fuel that burns hotter and cleaner than raw wood. Essential for blacksmithing, water filtration, and cooking where smoke is undesirable.

## Fuel Comparison

| Fuel | Energy Density | Difficulty to Make | Engine Compatible | Best Use |
|------|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Biodiesel | High | Moderate | Diesel engines | Transport, generators |
| Ethanol | Medium | Moderate | Gasoline (w/ mods) | Cooking, lighting, engines |
| Wood gas | Low-Medium | Hard | Gasoline (direct) | Stationary engines, vehicles |
| Biogas | Low | Easy | Gas burners, some engines | Cooking, lighting |
| Charcoal | Medium | Easy | Not directly | Cooking, heating, metalworking |
| Straight wood | Low | None | Not directly | Cooking, heating |

> **Prioritize efficiency over production.** Before spending weeks building a fuel production system, reduce your fuel needs. Insulate your shelter. Use rocket stoves that burn 80% less wood. Cook in thermal cookers (haybox cookers) that finish cooking with retained heat. Solar ovens use no fuel at all on sunny days. The less fuel you need, the less you need to produce.
