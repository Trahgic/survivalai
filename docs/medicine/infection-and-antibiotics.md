Infection is the number one killer in any survival scenario where medical infrastructure is gone. Without hospitals, a simple cut that gets infected can progress to sepsis and death in days. Knowing how to recognize infection early and how to use whatever antibiotics you can get your hands on is one of the highest-value skills you can have.

## Recognizing Wound Infection

Not every wound gets infected. But without sterile conditions, the odds go up dramatically. Watch for these signs starting 24–48 hours after the injury:

**Local signs (at the wound):**
- Increasing pain after the first 24 hours — pain should be decreasing, not increasing
- Redness spreading beyond the wound edges
- Swelling that's getting worse
- The area is warm or hot to touch
- Pus — yellow, green, brown, or grey drainage
- Foul smell
- Wound edges look unhealthy — darkening, separating, or tissue looks dead (black or grey)

**Regional signs (spreading):**
- Red streaks extending from the wound toward the body (this is lymphangitis — infection tracking along the lymph system toward the heart. Very urgent.)
- Swollen, tender lymph nodes in the area — groin for leg infections, armpit for arm infections, neck for head/face infections

**Systemic signs (whole body — this is sepsis developing):**
- Fever above 101°F (38.3°C) OR temperature below 96.8°F (36°C) — low temperature with infection is actually a worse sign
- Chills, rigors (shaking so hard the bed shakes)
- Rapid heart rate (above 90 at rest)
- Rapid breathing
- Confusion, disorientation
- Severe fatigue, weakness
- General feeling of being very ill

> **CRITICAL:** Red streaks extending from a wound toward the heart mean the infection is spreading through the lymphatic system. This can become sepsis within hours. If you have antibiotics, start them immediately at the highest recommended dose. This is not a "wait and see" situation.

## Types of Infection You'll Encounter

**Cellulitis:** Bacterial infection of the skin and tissue underneath. Appears as expanding redness, warmth, swelling, and pain around a wound or skin break. Common and treatable with oral antibiotics.

**Abscess:** A pocket of pus walled off under the skin. Feels like a firm, painful lump that may become soft and fluctuant (feels like pressing on a water balloon) as pus collects. Antibiotics alone usually can't reach inside an abscess — it needs to be drained. See the drainage procedure below.

**Wound sepsis:** Infection has spread from a wound into the bloodstream. The person is systemically sick — fever, rapid pulse, confusion. Without IV antibiotics, mortality is very high.

**Gas gangrene:** A deep wound (often crush injury or dirty puncture) infected with Clostridium bacteria that produce gas in the tissue. Signs: severe pain disproportionate to the wound appearance, skin that crackles when you press on it (crepitus from gas bubbles), rapidly expanding swelling, brownish discharge with a sweet/foul smell, the person deteriorates fast. Without surgical intervention, this is usually fatal. Start the highest dose of penicillin or amoxicillin you have. This infection can kill within 24 hours.

**Tetanus:** Caused by Clostridium tetani entering through a wound, especially dirty puncture wounds. Symptoms don't start for 3–21 days. First sign is usually jaw stiffness (lockjaw), followed by muscle spasms spreading to the neck, back, and eventually the whole body. Without antitoxin and intensive care, tetanus has about a 50% mortality rate. Prevention (tetanus vaccine) is the only realistic approach. No field treatment exists once symptoms start.

## Abscess Drainage

An abscess that's "ripe" (soft, fluctuant, and coming to a point) needs to be drained. Antibiotics alone won't cure it — the pus wall blocks drug penetration.

1. Clean the skin over the abscess with alcohol, iodine, or soap and clean water.
2. If you have any local anesthetic (lidocaine), inject it around the planned incision site. If not, give the person oral pain medication 30 minutes beforehand and apply ice for 10 minutes.
3. Using the sharpest, cleanest blade you have (scalpel, razor blade, or sharp knife sterilized with flame or alcohol), make a single incision along the longest axis of the abscess. Cut deep enough to enter the cavity — you'll know because pus starts draining.
4. Make the incision at least as long as the abscess is wide. Too small an opening seals back over and the abscess refills.
5. Express as much pus as possible by gentle pressure around the edges. Don't squeeze too aggressively — you can push bacteria into the bloodstream.
6. If you have it, pack the cavity loosely with clean gauze strip or cloth strip to keep it from sealing shut. Leave a tail hanging out.
7. Cover with a clean dressing. Change the packing daily — pull the old strip out, let any remaining pus drain, repack with clean material.
8. The cavity should shrink over 5–7 days as it heals from the inside out.
9. Start oral antibiotics after draining if there's surrounding cellulitis or fever.

## Fish Antibiotics — The Backup Plan

In a collapse scenario where pharmacies are unavailable, aquarium antibiotics are the same pharmaceutical-grade compounds sold for human use, manufactured by the same companies, just labeled for fish. They're available without prescription at pet stores and online.

The key ones to stockpile:

**Amoxicillin 500 mg (Fish Mox):**
- Best general-purpose antibiotic
- Good for: skin infections, ear infections, dental infections, respiratory infections, urinary tract infections
- Adult dose: 500 mg every 8 hours (three times daily)
- Child dose: 25 mg/kg/day divided into three doses
- Duration: 7–10 days minimum
- Take with food to reduce stomach upset
- Allergic to penicillin? Do NOT use — cross-reaction is common

**Cephalexin 500 mg (Fish Flex):**
- Broad-spectrum, good for skin and soft tissue
- Good for: cellulitis, wound infections, bone infections, urinary infections
- Adult dose: 500 mg every 6 hours (four times daily)
- Duration: 7–10 days
- Mild penicillin allergy: usually safe. Severe penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis): avoid

**Ciprofloxacin 500 mg (Fish Flox):**
- Broad-spectrum, covers gram-negative bacteria well
- Good for: urinary tract infections, diarrheal illness, anthrax exposure, bone infections
- Adult dose: 500 mg every 12 hours (twice daily)
- Duration: 7–14 days depending on infection type
- Do NOT use in children or pregnant women — damages growing cartilage
- Do NOT take with dairy or calcium supplements — blocks absorption
- Can cause tendon rupture — stop if sudden joint pain occurs

**Metronidazole 500 mg (Fish Zole):**
- Covers anaerobic bacteria (deep wounds, abdominal infections) and parasites
- Good for: dental infections, abdominal infections, Giardia, C. difficile, deep wound infections
- Adult dose: 500 mg every 8 hours
- Duration: 7–10 days
- Do NOT drink alcohol while taking — causes severe vomiting
- Metallic taste is a known side effect, not an allergic reaction

**Doxycycline 100 mg:**
- Broad-spectrum tetracycline
- Good for: tick-borne illness (Lyme, Rocky Mountain spotted fever), respiratory infections, skin infections, sexually transmitted infections
- Adult dose: 100 mg every 12 hours
- Duration: 7–14 days
- Take with a full glass of water, don't lie down for 30 minutes after (causes severe esophageal irritation)
- Avoid in children under 8 and pregnant women

**Sulfamethoxazole-Trimethoprim (Bactrim/SMZ-TMP):**
- Good for: urinary tract infections, MRSA skin infections, some respiratory infections
- Adult dose: 1 double-strength tablet every 12 hours
- Duration: 7–14 days
- Sulfa allergy is common — ask about allergies before using

## Choosing the Right Antibiotic

You probably won't know the exact bacteria. Pick based on infection type:

| Infection | First Choice | Backup |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Skin/wound infection | Cephalexin | Amoxicillin-clavulanate |
| Deep wound, animal bite | Amoxicillin-clavulanate | Ciprofloxacin + metronidazole |
| Dental infection | Amoxicillin | Metronidazole |
| Urinary tract infection | Ciprofloxacin | SMZ-TMP |
| Diarrheal illness | Ciprofloxacin | Metronidazole (if parasitic) |
| Respiratory infection | Amoxicillin | Doxycycline |
| Tick-borne illness | Doxycycline | No good substitute |
| Suspected MRSA | SMZ-TMP | Doxycycline |
| Abdominal infection | Ciprofloxacin + metronidazole | Amoxicillin-clavulanate |

## Critical Antibiotic Rules

1. **Finish the full course.** Stopping early when you feel better breeds resistant bacteria. Minimum 7 days for most infections, even if symptoms improve at day 3.
2. **Don't use antibiotics for viral infections.** Colds, flu, and most sore throats are viral. Antibiotics do nothing and waste your supply.
3. **Check allergies.** Ask about penicillin allergy before giving amoxicillin or cephalexin. Ask about sulfa allergy before SMZ-TMP. Allergic reactions range from rash to fatal anaphylaxis.
4. **Watch for allergic reactions.** If a rash, hives, swelling, or difficulty breathing starts after beginning an antibiotic, stop it immediately.
5. **Store properly.** Most antibiotics lose potency in heat and humidity. Store in a cool, dry, dark place. Most tablets and capsules remain effective well past their expiration date — studies show most maintain 90%+ potency for 5–15 years past expiration when stored properly.
6. **Don't halve doses to stretch supply.** Under-dosing doesn't cure the infection and breeds resistance. Full dose for full duration, or save the antibiotics for when you really need them.

## Improvised Infection Control

When you have no antibiotics:

- **Honey (raw, unprocessed):** Apply directly to infected wounds. Honey has genuine antibacterial properties — it produces hydrogen peroxide, has low pH, and high osmolarity that dehydrates bacteria. Medical-grade manuka honey is best, but any raw honey works.
- **Clean the wound aggressively.** Irrigate with clean water under pressure (a squeeze bottle or syringe) twice daily. Remove any dead tissue you can see (dark, slimy, or loose tissue). This is basic debridement.
- **Dilute iodine solution:** Mix povidone-iodine (Betadine) to the color of weak tea (about 1% concentration). Use for wound irrigation. Full-strength iodine damages healthy tissue.
- **Dilute bleach solution:** 1 teaspoon household bleach in 1 quart (1 liter) of clean water. Use for cleaning around wounds, not directly in deep wounds.
- **Maggot therapy:** Blow fly maggots (Lucilia sericata — the common green bottle fly) eat only dead tissue and secrete substances that kill bacteria. Place them in the wound, cover with a loose mesh to keep them contained, and let them work for 48–72 hours. This sounds disgusting. It works. It was standard medical practice before antibiotics and is still used today in hospitals for non-healing wounds.

## When You've Lost the Fight

If infection progresses to sepsis despite antibiotics, survival without intensive care is poor. Signs that you're losing:

- Confusion worsening despite treatment
- Blood pressure dropping (pulse at the wrist disappearing)
- Skin becoming mottled, grey, or cold
- High fever alternating with low temperature
- Multiple organ dysfunction (decreased urine, liver dysfunction/yellow skin, breathing difficulty)

Continue antibiotics at maximum dose. Push fluids aggressively. Keep the person warm. Some people survive sepsis with supportive care alone, but the odds are against you without IV antibiotics and vasopressors.
