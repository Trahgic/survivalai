If someone's heart stops or they stop breathing, you have about 4–6 minutes before brain damage starts. 10 minutes and they're almost certainly gone. CPR isn't complicated, and bad CPR is infinitely better than no CPR. Don't freeze up worrying about doing it wrong.

## Checking If They Need CPR

1. Tap the person hard on the shoulders and shout. "Hey! Are you okay?" If they respond, they don't need CPR.
2. If no response, look at their chest. Is it rising and falling? Put your ear near their mouth — can you hear or feel breathing? Take no more than 10 seconds to check.
3. Check for a pulse at the neck. Place two fingers (not your thumb — it has its own pulse) on the side of the windpipe in the groove between the windpipe and the neck muscle. Feel for 10 seconds.

**No breathing + no pulse = start CPR immediately.**

**No breathing + has a pulse = give rescue breaths only** (see below).

**Gasping, gurgling, or irregular "fish out of water" breaths are not real breathing.** This is called agonal breathing and it means the brain is dying. Treat it the same as not breathing — start CPR.

## Adult CPR (over puberty)

### Chest Compressions

1. Place the person flat on their back on a hard surface. CPR doesn't work on a mattress or soft ground — the body just sinks. Use the floor, a table, or a board under them.
2. Kneel beside their chest.
3. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest, right between the nipples on the breastbone. Place your other hand on top, fingers interlocked.
4. Lock your elbows straight. Position your shoulders directly above your hands. You're pushing with your body weight, not your arm muscles.
5. Push hard and fast. Compress the chest at least 2 inches deep (5 cm). That's deeper than you think — you might crack ribs. That's normal and expected. Cracked ribs mean you're pushing hard enough.
6. Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute. The beat of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees is the right tempo. So is "Another One Bites the Dust" if your humor runs dark.
7. Let the chest fully recoil (come back up) between each compression. Don't lean on the chest between pushes. Full recoil lets the heart refill with blood.
8. Minimize interruptions. Every time you stop compressions, blood pressure drops to zero and you lose what you've built up. Don't stop for more than 10 seconds at a time.

### Rescue Breaths

1. After every 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths.
2. Tilt the head back by lifting the chin with one hand and pushing the forehead back with the other. This opens the airway.
3. Pinch the nose shut.
4. Seal your mouth over theirs completely.
5. Blow for about 1 second — just enough to see the chest rise. Don't over-inflate. If the chest doesn't rise, re-tilt the head and try again. If it still doesn't rise, the airway may be blocked — check the mouth for objects.
6. Give the second breath, then immediately go back to compressions.

**30 compressions : 2 breaths. Repeat until they recover, help arrives, or you physically cannot continue.**

> **If you don't want to do mouth-to-mouth** (no judgment — disease risk is real), compression-only CPR is still effective. Push hard and fast without stopping. Blood still has oxygen in it for several minutes, and circulating it is better than doing nothing. Some studies show compression-only CPR is nearly as good as full CPR for the first 5–10 minutes.

### How Long to Continue

CPR is exhausting. Two minutes of proper compressions will gas most people. If someone else is available, switch every 2 minutes (every 5 cycles of 30:2). Don't be a hero — tired compressions are shallow and useless.

Stop CPR when:
- The person starts breathing on their own and has a pulse
- Qualified medical help takes over
- You are physically unable to continue and no one can replace you
- You've been going for 30+ minutes with no response and no advanced medical care is coming (a hard call, but a realistic one)

## Child CPR (Age 1 to Puberty)

Same basic process with adjustments:

1. Use one hand for compressions instead of two (or two hands for a larger child). Same location — center of chest, between the nipples.
2. Compress about 2 inches deep (5 cm) — roughly one-third the chest depth.
3. Same rate: 100–120 per minute.
4. Same ratio: 30 compressions to 2 breaths.
5. Tilt the head back to a neutral or slightly extended position — don't over-extend a child's neck. Their airway is smaller and more flexible.
6. Use gentler breaths — just enough to make the chest rise. A full adult-force breath can rupture a child's lungs.

**For children, breathing problems are the most common cause of cardiac arrest** (not heart attacks like adults). This means rescue breaths are more important in children — don't skip them.

If you're alone with a child, do 2 minutes of CPR first, then go for help. (With adults, call for help first, then start CPR.)

## Infant CPR (Under 1 Year)

1. Place the infant on a firm, flat surface.
2. Use two fingers (index and middle finger) on the breastbone just below the nipple line. Do not use the heel of your hand.
3. Compress about 1.5 inches deep (4 cm) — one-third the chest depth.
4. Rate: 100–120 per minute.
5. 30 compressions to 2 breaths (one rescuer) or 15 compressions to 2 breaths (two rescuers).
6. For breaths: cover both the infant's mouth AND nose with your mouth. Give tiny puffs — just cheek air, not lung air. The chest should rise just slightly.
7. Do not tilt the head back as far as you would for an adult. A neutral position with a slight chin lift is enough. Over-extending an infant's neck actually closes the airway.

> **WARNING:** Never shake an infant to check responsiveness. Flick the soles of their feet or tap their shoulder instead. Shaking can cause fatal brain injuries.

## Recovery Position

When someone is breathing on their own and has a pulse but is unconscious, place them in the recovery position to keep the airway clear and prevent choking on vomit.

1. Kneel beside the person.
2. Take their arm closest to you and place it out at a right angle to their body, bent at the elbow, palm up.
3. Take their far arm and bring it across their chest, holding the back of their hand against their near cheek.
4. Grab their far knee and pull it up so the foot is flat on the ground.
5. Pull the bent knee toward you to roll them onto their side. Their head should rest on their hand.
6. Tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open.
7. Adjust the top leg so the hip and knee are both at right angles for stability.

Check breathing every 2 minutes. If they stop breathing, roll them back and start CPR.

> **WARNING:** Do not use the recovery position if you suspect a spinal injury. Keep them on their back with the head stabilized. The only exception is if they're vomiting and you can't suction the airway — roll them as a unit (head, neck, and torso together) to prevent aspiration.

## Choking — Adults and Children Over 1

A person who can cough forcefully, speak, or make noise is getting air. Encourage them to keep coughing. Don't interfere unless:

- They can't speak, cough, or breathe
- They're making high-pitched squeaking sounds on inhale
- They're turning blue (lips, face, fingertips)
- They're clutching their throat (universal choking sign)

### Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich Maneuver)

1. Stand behind the person. Wrap your arms around their waist.
2. Make a fist with one hand. Place the thumb side of the fist against their abdomen, above the navel and well below the breastbone.
3. Grab your fist with your other hand.
4. Pull sharply inward and upward — a hard J-shaped thrust. Use force. You're trying to push air out of the lungs fast enough to pop the object out.
5. Repeat until the object comes out or the person goes unconscious.

**If the person is too large for you to reach around, or if they're pregnant:**
Use chest thrusts instead. Stand behind them, place your fist on the center of the breastbone (not the abdomen), and thrust straight backward.

**If you're choking and alone:**
Make a fist against your own abdomen above the navel, grab with the other hand, and thrust inward/upward. Or lean hard over the back of a chair, table edge, or counter and drive your upper abdomen against it sharply.

### If the Person Goes Unconscious

1. Lower them to the ground on their back.
2. Open the mouth and look for the object. If you can see it, sweep it out with a finger. Do not do blind finger sweeps — you can push the object deeper.
3. Attempt 2 rescue breaths. If the chest doesn't rise, re-tilt the head and try again.
4. If air still won't go in, start CPR — 30 chest compressions. Compressions can dislodge the object.
5. After 30 compressions, open the mouth and look again. Remove anything you see.
6. Try 2 more breaths. If they go in, check for pulse and continue CPR if needed.
7. Repeat the cycle: 30 compressions → check mouth → 2 breaths.

## Choking — Infants Under 1

Do not use abdominal thrusts on infants — you can rupture internal organs.

1. Hold the infant face-down along your forearm, with the head lower than the body. Support the head by holding the jaw (not the throat).
2. Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
3. If the object doesn't come out, flip the infant face-up on your forearm (support the head).
4. Give 5 chest thrusts using two fingers on the breastbone, just below the nipple line. Same position as infant CPR compressions, but slower and more deliberate.
5. Alternate 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts until the object comes out or the infant goes unconscious.

**If the infant goes unconscious:**
1. Place on a flat surface.
2. Open the mouth — if you can see the object, remove it. No blind finger sweeps.
3. Attempt 2 breaths (mouth over mouth and nose).
4. If air won't go in, begin infant CPR: 30 compressions, check mouth, 2 breaths, repeat.

## Drowning

1. Get the person out of the water. Protect your own safety — don't become a second victim.
2. If they're not breathing, start rescue breaths immediately — even while still in shallow water if possible. Drowning victims need air more than compressions.
3. Give 5 initial rescue breaths, then check for a pulse.
4. No pulse: begin full CPR (30:2).
5. Do not try to drain water from the lungs by compressing the abdomen or flipping them over. Most swallowed water is in the stomach, not the lungs. Compressing the abdomen causes vomiting and aspiration.
6. Drowning victims who start breathing again should still be monitored for at least 24 hours. "Secondary drowning" — where water in the lungs causes delayed respiratory failure — can happen 4–24 hours after the event.
7. Keep them warm. Cold water immersion causes hypothermia fast.

> **WARNING:** Vomiting during CPR on a drowning victim is very common. Turn the head to the side, clear the mouth, and continue. This is messy and unpleasant. Do it anyway.

## Common Mistakes

- **Compressions too shallow.** You need at least 2 inches on an adult. Cracked ribs are normal.
- **Going too slow.** 100–120 per minute is fast. Most untrained people go about 60–80 without a reference beat.
- **Not letting the chest recoil.** Leaning between compressions prevents the heart from refilling.
- **Stopping too often.** Every pause drops blood pressure to zero. Check pulse only every 2 minutes and keep checks under 10 seconds.
- **Over-ventilating.** Big breaths force air into the stomach, causing vomiting. Small, 1-second breaths. Just enough to see the chest rise.
- **Giving up too early.** People have been revived after 20+ minutes of CPR. Cold water drowning victims especially — hypothermia protects the brain. Keep going.
