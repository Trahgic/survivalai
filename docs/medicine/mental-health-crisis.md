In a survival situation, mental health is a survival skill. A person having a panic attack can't make decisions. A group with destroyed morale stops working. Someone in dissociative shock after a traumatic event wanders off and dies of exposure. Your brain is your most important survival tool, and it needs maintenance like anything else.

## Acute Stress Reaction

This is the immediate response to a traumatic event — a disaster, violent encounter, death of a group member, or any overwhelming experience. It's a normal reaction to an abnormal situation.

**Signs (within minutes to hours of the event):**
- Feeling dazed, numb, or disconnected ("watching from outside my body")
- Difficulty processing what happened — repeating questions, confusion
- Physical symptoms: racing heart, shaking, nausea, hyperventilation
- Emotional flooding — crying, screaming, rage — or complete emotional flatness
- Inability to make decisions or perform tasks they normally can
- Restlessness or alternatively freezing in place

**What to do:**

1. Get the person to safety. Physical safety first, always.
2. Stay with them. The single most important thing you can do is be a calm, stable presence. Don't leave them alone.
3. Speak in short, clear, simple sentences. Their brain is overwhelmed — complex instructions won't register. "Sit here." "Drink this." "You're safe now."
4. Normalize what they're feeling without dismissing it. "Your body is reacting to what happened. That's normal." Not "calm down" or "it's fine" or "stop crying."
5. Meet basic physical needs: water, warmth, rest. Acute stress depletes energy fast.
6. Don't force them to talk about what happened. Some people need to talk immediately. Others need silence. Follow their lead.
7. Give them a simple task if they seem frozen — "Hold this" or "Help me carry this." Having purpose and physical action breaks the freeze response.
8. Do not give alcohol as a calming agent. It impairs judgment, worsens dehydration, and makes tomorrow worse.

Most people recover from acute stress reactions within hours to days with basic support and rest. It becomes a problem when it doesn't resolve.

## Panic Attacks

A panic attack feels like a heart attack or dying. The person's body launches a full fight-or-flight response with no actual threat. It's terrifying for the person experiencing it but not physically dangerous.

**Signs:**
- Sudden overwhelming fear or sense of doom
- Rapid heart rate, pounding chest
- Difficulty breathing, feeling of choking or smothering
- Chest pain or tightness
- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Trembling, shaking
- Numbness or tingling in hands, feet, face
- Hot or cold flashes
- Nausea
- Feeling of unreality or detachment
- Typically peaks within 10 minutes and resolves within 20–30 minutes

**What to do:**

1. Recognize it as a panic attack, not a medical emergency. If the person is otherwise healthy and the symptoms appeared suddenly during stress, it's almost certainly a panic attack. (That said, if someone over 40 has chest pain and shortness of breath, treat it as a potential heart attack until proven otherwise.)
2. Reassure firmly: "This is a panic attack. It feels terrible but it's not going to hurt you. It will pass."
3. Breathing exercise: have them breathe in slowly for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, breathe out for 6–8 counts. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system and physically slows the heart rate. Do it with them — match their breathing.
4. Grounding technique: "Name 5 things you can see. 4 you can touch. 3 you can hear. 2 you can smell. 1 you can taste." This pulls the brain out of the panic loop and into the present moment.
5. Don't crowd them. One calm person helping is better than five anxious people hovering.
6. After the panic attack passes, they'll be exhausted. Let them rest. Don't make them feel embarrassed or weak — panic attacks are a physiological response, not a character flaw.

**If someone is having frequent panic attacks:**
- Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25–50 mg has mild anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) effects and may help take the edge off.
- If you have benzodiazepines (diazepam/Valium 5–10 mg, lorazepam/Ativan 0.5–1 mg), they're the most effective acute treatment. Use sparingly — they're addictive and supplies will be limited.
- Regular physical activity reduces panic attack frequency over time.
- Avoid caffeine — it worsens anxiety and lowers the panic threshold.

## Grief

People in survival situations will lose people they love. Grief is not a disorder — it's the appropriate response to loss. But unprocessed grief can paralyze a person and make them non-functional.

**What to do:**

1. Let them grieve. Crying, anger, withdrawal, numbness — all normal. Don't rush them through it.
2. Acknowledge the loss simply and directly. "I'm sorry about [name]." Don't say "they're in a better place" or "at least they didn't suffer" or "you need to be strong." These are dismissals dressed up as comfort.
3. Maintain routine. The structure of daily tasks (meals, watch shifts, work) provides a framework that keeps a grieving person functional even when they feel like they can't function.
4. Watch for prolonged inability to function. If after 2–3 weeks the person can't eat, sleep, or participate in basic tasks, they need more direct support.
5. Watch for self-destructive behavior — recklessness, refusing to eat or drink, giving away supplies, isolating completely.
6. Talk about the person who died. Avoiding their name doesn't reduce pain — it signals that the loss doesn't matter.

## Group Morale

In any group survival situation, morale is as important as food and water. A demoralized group splinters, stops cooperating, makes bad decisions, and falls apart.

**Signs of declining group morale:**
- Increase in conflict and arguments over small things
- People stopping communication — withdrawal, isolation
- Refusal to do assigned tasks
- Hoarding supplies or selfish behavior
- Expressions of hopelessness — "what's the point"
- Loss of hygiene and self-care
- Sleep disruption across the group

**What to do:**

1. **Information.** People cope better when they understand the situation. Share what you know about the current state of things honestly. Uncertainty is more destructive than bad news. Don't sugarcoat, but frame realistically: "This is hard, and here's what we're doing about it."
2. **Purpose and tasks.** Everyone needs a role. People without a job deteriorate faster than people with one, even if the job is basic. Assign tasks based on ability. Rotate unpleasant tasks fairly.
3. **Small wins.** Celebrate progress, no matter how small. A successful hunt, a fire started, a shelter completed, water purified. Human brains need evidence that effort produces results.
4. **Routine.** Meal times, work periods, rest periods, watch schedules. Routine creates predictability, and predictability reduces anxiety.
5. **Rest and recovery.** Exhausted people are demoralized people. Enforce rest periods even when there's work to do. Sleep deprivation degrades judgment, emotional regulation, and immune function.
6. **Physical contact.** Handshakes, shoulder pats, hugs where appropriate. Human touch releases oxytocin and reduces stress hormones. This isn't soft — it's physiological.
7. **Stories and community.** Sharing meals, telling stories, singing, playing games — these aren't luxuries. They're social bonding activities that maintain group cohesion under stress. Every military in history has known this.
8. **Address conflict early.** Small resentments grow into group-destroying fights. See group-organization.md for conflict resolution approaches.

## When Someone Is a Danger to Themselves or Others

This is rare but real. Extreme stress can cause psychotic breaks, suicidal behavior, or violent outbursts.

**Suicidal statements or behavior:**
- Take every statement seriously. "I'd be better off dead" or "what's the point of going on" are warning signs, not just venting.
- Stay with the person. Don't leave them alone.
- Ask directly: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Asking does not plant the idea — it opens the door for honest communication.
- Remove access to means if possible — weapons, medications, heights.
- Listen without judgment. Often the act of being heard reduces the acute crisis.
- Assign someone to stay with them continuously until the crisis passes.

**Violent or psychotic behavior:**
- Safety of the group comes first. If someone is armed and threatening others, distance is priority.
- Speak calmly, slowly, and non-confrontationally. Use their name. Don't argue with delusions.
- If restraint is necessary, use the minimum force needed and only enough people to do it safely. Avoid positional asphyxia — never restrain someone face-down or with weight on their back/chest.
- Once restrained, someone must monitor their breathing continuously.
- If sedation is available and necessary: diphenhydramine 50 mg IM or oral, or lorazepam 2 mg IM if available.

## Sleep

Sleep deprivation in survival situations is epidemic and dangerous. After 24 hours without sleep, cognitive function drops to the equivalent of a 0.10% blood alcohol level. After 48 hours, hallucinations are common.

**Priorities:**
- 6 hours of sleep minimum per 24 hours, even in crisis situations
- Schedule watch shifts to allow uninterrupted sleep blocks (4 hours minimum at a stretch)
- Physical comfort matters — warmth, padding, wind protection. People don't sleep well when they're cold and miserable. Small investments in sleep comfort pay enormous returns.
- Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg can help with acute insomnia but loses effectiveness quickly with repeated use
- Melatonin 3–5 mg 30 minutes before desired sleep time if available — especially helpful when sleep schedules are disrupted

## Long-Term Psychological Health

Surviving isn't just about making it through the next day. Prolonged survival situations create chronic stress that degrades physical and mental health over weeks and months.

**Sustainable practices:**
- Maintain personal hygiene — the act of caring for yourself reinforces self-worth
- Keep a journal or log if writing materials are available — processing events through writing reduces their psychological weight
- Physical exercise — it's the single most effective anti-anxiety and anti-depression intervention known. Even a daily walk helps.
- Maintain social connections within the group
- Set goals beyond immediate survival — planning a garden, building something permanent, teaching a skill. Future-oriented thinking is protective against depression.
- Acknowledge that struggling emotionally doesn't mean you're weak. It means you're human and the situation is hard.
