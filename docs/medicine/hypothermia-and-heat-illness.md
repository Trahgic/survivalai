Your body needs to stay between about 97°F and 99°F (36.1–37.2°C) to function. Drop below 95°F and your brain starts failing. Rise above 104°F and your organs start cooking. Both ends of this spectrum kill, and both can sneak up on you.

## COLD INJURIES

### Hypothermia

Hypothermia happens when your core body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C). It doesn't require Arctic conditions — wet clothes and a 50°F (10°C) wind can do it in hours. Wet cold is far more dangerous than dry cold. Water conducts heat away from the body 25 times faster than air.

#### Stages

**Mild (90–95°F / 32–35°C):**
- Shivering — uncontrollable, intense. This is the body's last-ditch effort to generate heat.
- Poor coordination, fumbling hands, stumbling
- Difficulty with fine motor tasks (can't zip a jacket, can't light a match)
- Confusion begins — poor decisions, apathy
- Skin is pale, cold to touch

**Moderate (82–90°F / 28–32°C):**
- Shivering stops. This is a very bad sign — it means the body has given up trying to rewarm itself.
- Severe confusion, slurred speech
- Drowsiness — they want to sleep and will fight you on staying awake
- Muscle stiffness
- Slow, weak pulse
- Paradoxical undressing — the person feels burning hot and starts removing clothing. This is brain malfunction, not actual warmth.

**Severe (below 82°F / 28°C):**
- Unconscious or barely responsive
- Pulse may be undetectable (but still present — it can be very faint)
- Rigid muscles
- Dilated pupils
- May appear dead

> **CRITICAL:** "Nobody is dead until they're warm and dead." Severe hypothermia can make a person look and feel dead — no detectable pulse, not breathing, pupils fixed. But cold-preserved people have been revived after hours. Do not give up on a hypothermia victim until you've rewarmed them and they still have no signs of life. Start CPR if no pulse, and continue while rewarming.

#### Treatment — Mild Hypothermia

1. Get them out of the cold. Shelter, vehicle, tent — anything that blocks wind and wet.
2. Remove all wet clothing. Every piece. Cut it off if they can't undress — wet fabric keeps stealing heat.
3. Dry them off and insulate. Sleeping bag, blankets, dry clothes, anything. Insulate from the ground — that's where you lose the most heat. A sleeping pad or pile of pine boughs underneath matters more than blankets on top.
4. Cover the head and neck. You lose a disproportionate amount of heat from your head.
5. Give warm, sweet drinks. Hot cocoa, warm sugar water, tea. No alcohol — it opens blood vessels in the skin, feels warm but dumps core heat faster. No coffee — caffeine constricts vessels and adds dehydration.
6. Skin-to-skin contact works. Strip down and get into a sleeping bag with them. Direct body heat transfer. This is a proven treatment, not optional — core-to-core contact, chest to chest.
7. Activity can help if they're able — gentle movement generates heat. But don't push hard exercise — cold muscles are fragile and a cold heart is prone to dangerous rhythms with exertion.

#### Treatment — Moderate to Severe Hypothermia

1. Handle gently. A cold heart is electrically unstable. Rough handling, sudden movements, or jostling can trigger cardiac arrest. Move them as smoothly as possible.
2. Lay them flat. Do not let them sit up or stand — gravity pulling cold blood from the limbs into the core can cause cardiac arrest ("afterdrop").
3. Remove wet clothing by cutting — don't roll them around.
4. Insulate aggressively. Wrap in everything available. Build a "hypothermia wrap": vapor barrier (plastic trash bags, space blanket), then insulating layers (sleeping bag, blankets), then windproof outer layer.
5. Apply heat to the core only — armpits, neck, groin, and chest. Use water bottles filled with warm (not hot) water wrapped in cloth. Chemical hand warmers work. Do NOT heat the arms and legs — warming the extremities first pushes cold, acidic blood to the core and can cause cardiac arrest.
6. If they're conscious enough to swallow, give warm fluids slowly.
7. If no pulse after checking for a full 60 seconds (hypothermic hearts beat very slowly), start CPR. Continue CPR while rewarming. Do not stop until the person is warm or you've exhausted all options.

> **WARNING:** Do not put a severely hypothermic person in a hot bath or shower. Rapid surface warming causes massive vasodilation and sends cold blood from the extremities crashing into the core. This regularly kills people in rescue situations. Slow, gentle, core-first rewarming.

### Frostbite

Frostbite is actual freezing of body tissue. It usually hits the fingers, toes, nose, ears, and cheeks — the parts farthest from the core with the most surface area exposed.

#### Stages

**Frostnip:** Skin turns white or pale, feels numb and waxy. Only the surface layer is affected. Fully reversible with rewarming. No lasting damage.

**Superficial frostbite:** Skin is white or grayish-yellow, firm to touch but tissue underneath is still soft. Blisters filled with clear fluid form after rewarming. Usually heals with proper care.

**Deep frostbite:** Tissue is hard and wooden all the way through. No give when you press on it. After rewarming, blood-filled blisters form, skin turns black. Tissue death is likely. Amputation may eventually be necessary.

#### Treatment

1. Do not rewarm if there's any chance the tissue will refreeze. Thawing and refreezing causes far worse damage than staying frozen. If you're still in the field and can't guarantee warmth, leave it frozen and get to shelter.
2. Do not rub the area. No rubbing snow on it, no massaging. Ice crystals in the tissue shred cells when you rub.
3. Do not walk on frostbitten feet unless you have no other choice. If you must walk to reach shelter, don't rewarm first.
4. To rewarm: submerge in warm water (100–106°F / 37–41°C). That's warm to the touch, not hot. Test the water with a non-frostbitten hand — the person can't feel temperature in the affected area and will burn easily.
5. Keep the water at temperature — add warm water as it cools. Rewarming takes 20–40 minutes. The tissue is thawed when it becomes soft and color returns (usually red or purple).
6. Rewarming is extremely painful. Give the strongest pain medication you have before starting. Ibuprofen 600 mg helps with inflammation too.
7. After rewarming, don't break blisters. Pad between frostbitten fingers and toes with dry gauze. Wrap loosely. Elevate.
8. Give ibuprofen 600 mg every 8 hours for at least 48 hours. It reduces the chemical damage from thawing.
9. Start antibiotics if any blisters are blood-filled or tissue turns dark (cephalexin 500 mg every 6 hours, or whatever you have).

## HEAT INJURIES

### Heat Exhaustion

The body is overheating but still compensating. Core temperature is elevated but usually below 104°F (40°C). This is your warning — ignore it and heat stroke follows.

**Signs:**
- Heavy sweating (the body is still trying to cool itself)
- Weakness, fatigue, dizziness
- Headache, nausea, sometimes vomiting
- Muscle cramps (heat cramps)
- Cool, pale, clammy skin despite the heat
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Dark urine or decreased urination

**Treatment:**

1. Stop all activity. Get to shade or the coolest area available.
2. Lay down with legs elevated slightly.
3. Remove excess clothing. Loosen everything.
4. Cool aggressively — pour water over the skin, fan the wet skin. Wet towels on the neck, armpits, and groin. These are your major cooling points where blood vessels run close to the surface.
5. If they can drink, push cool water with electrolytes. Oral rehydration solution (1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt), or just water with a pinch of salt. Half a liter over 30 minutes, then keep sipping.
6. Do not give salt tablets — they concentrate sodium and can cause vomiting. Salt in solution only.
7. Monitor for 1–2 hours. Most people improve within 30 minutes with cooling and fluids. If symptoms worsen or they develop confusion, it's becoming heat stroke.

### Heat Stroke

This is a life-threatening emergency. The body's temperature regulation has failed. Core temperature is above 104°F (40°C) and the brain is cooking.

**Signs — the key difference from heat exhaustion:**
- Hot, red, DRY skin. Sweating has stopped. (Note: exertional heat stroke from heavy exercise can still have sweating — don't rely on dry skin alone.)
- Core temperature above 104°F (40°C) — use a rectal thermometer if you have one. Oral and armpit readings are inaccurate for core temperature.
- Confusion, bizarre behavior, combativeness, hallucinations
- Seizures
- Rapid, strong pulse (unlike the weak pulse of heat exhaustion)
- Loss of consciousness
- Vomiting, diarrhea

> **CRITICAL:** Heat stroke is a medical emergency on par with a heart attack. Brain damage begins within minutes at core temperatures above 106°F (41°C). Every minute of delay in cooling increases the chance of death or permanent brain damage. Cool first, transport second.

**Treatment:**

1. Cool them NOW. This is the only priority.
2. Strip all clothing.
3. If you have a tub, trough, or large container — fill it with cold water and ice and immerse them up to the neck. This is the gold standard. Cold water immersion drops core temperature faster than anything else.
4. No tub? Pour cold water over the entire body continuously. Pack ice (or any cold objects — frozen water bottles, cold cans, snow) into the armpits, groin, neck, and around the head.
5. Fan them aggressively while wet. Evaporation is powerful cooling.
6. If you have nothing cold, shade + water + fanning is still worth doing.
7. Do not give them anything by mouth while confused or seizing. Once alert and oriented, start slow sips of cool water.
8. Monitor breathing. Seizures can compromise the airway. Be ready to place in recovery position if they vomit.
9. Keep cooling until core temperature drops to 102°F (39°C) or until they become lucid and responsive. Don't overshoot into hypothermia.
10. After a heat stroke episode, the person is fragile for days. No exertion, stay hydrated, stay cool. A second heat stroke event is much more likely after the first.

### Heat Cramps

Painful muscle spasms during or after exercise in heat. Usually in the legs, abdomen, or arms. Caused by electrolyte depletion, not just dehydration.

1. Stop activity, move to shade, rest.
2. Drink electrolyte solution — water with salt and sugar. Sports drink if available.
3. Gentle stretching of the cramping muscle.
4. Do not resume heavy activity for at least several hours.
5. If cramps persist despite rest and fluids, or if the person has heart problems, treat more seriously — they may be progressing toward heat exhaustion.

## Prevention

### Cold

- Stay dry. Change wet clothes immediately. Layer with a moisture-wicking base layer if possible.
- Eat and drink. Your body needs fuel to generate heat. Dehydration accelerates hypothermia.
- Keep moving in moderate cold. Sitting still on the ground in wet clothes is the classic hypothermia setup.
- Use the buddy system. Hypothermia impairs judgment — you won't recognize it in yourself. Watch each other for confusion, stumbling, slurred speech.
- The biggest risk times: after sweating (you're wet), at night (temperature drops), during rain/wind transitions.

### Heat

- Hydrate before you're thirsty. By the time you feel thirst, you're already behind. In hot conditions, drink at least 1 liter per hour during activity.
- Work during cool hours — early morning, late evening. Rest during midday heat.
- Acclimate gradually. It takes 7–14 days for the body to adapt to heat. During the first week in a hot environment, reduce work by 50%.
- Wear loose, light-colored clothing. Cover the head and neck.
- Watch urine color. Pale yellow = hydrated. Dark yellow or brown = drink more. No urine at all = emergency.
