This is a field reference for common medications. It covers dosing, uses, contraindications, and what to use when you can't get the ideal drug. For detailed information on antibiotics specifically, see infection-and-antibiotics.md. For pediatric dosing by weight, see medical-dosage-tables.md in the references section.

## Pain Medications

### Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)

- **Use:** Pain, fever, inflammation. Best all-around field pain medication.
- **Adult dose:** 200–600 mg every 6–8 hours. Maximum 3200 mg per day.
- **Child dose (over 6 months):** 10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours. Maximum 40 mg/kg/day.
- **Take with food** to reduce stomach irritation.
- **Do NOT use if:** Active stomach bleeding or ulcer history, kidney disease, third trimester of pregnancy, allergy to aspirin/NSAIDs. Use with caution in dehydration — it can worsen kidney function when fluid is low.
- **Interactions:** Increases bleeding when combined with blood thinners or aspirin. Reduces effectiveness of some blood pressure medications.

### Acetaminophen (Tylenol, Paracetamol)

- **Use:** Pain and fever. No anti-inflammatory effect, but safe when ibuprofen can't be used.
- **Adult dose:** 500–1000 mg every 6 hours. Maximum 3000 mg per day (some references say 4000, but liver damage risk increases above 3000).
- **Child dose:** 10–15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours. Maximum 75 mg/kg/day.
- **Do NOT use if:** Liver disease, heavy alcohol use (3+ drinks/day), or other drugs being taken that affect the liver.
- **Overdose is lethal.** Acetaminophen overdose destroys the liver. Symptoms don't appear for 24–72 hours, by which time the damage is done. Maximum daily dose is not flexible. If someone ingests a large amount, activated charcoal within 1 hour may help. N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is the antidote if available.

### Aspirin

- **Use:** Pain, fever, anti-inflammatory. Also prevents blood clots (heart attack, stroke).
- **Adult dose:** 325–650 mg every 4–6 hours for pain. Maximum 4000 mg per day.
- **Do NOT give to children or teenagers** — risk of Reye's syndrome, a rare but potentially fatal condition.
- **Do NOT use if:** Bleeding disorders, active bleeding, stomach ulcers, last trimester of pregnancy.
- **Heart attack dose:** If someone is having a heart attack (crushing chest pain, jaw pain, left arm pain), have them chew (not swallow whole) 325 mg aspirin immediately. Chewing gets it into the blood faster.

### Tramadol

- **Use:** Moderate to severe pain. Weaker than opioids but stronger than OTC options.
- **Adult dose:** 50–100 mg every 6 hours. Maximum 400 mg per day.
- **Do NOT use if:** Seizure history (tramadol lowers seizure threshold), taking antidepressants (risk of serotonin syndrome), respiratory depression.
- **Warning:** Can cause dependence with prolonged use.

### Opioids (Codeine, Hydrocodone, Oxycodone)

- **Use:** Severe pain (fractures, burns, surgical procedures).
- **Codeine dose:** 30–60 mg every 4–6 hours. Maximum 360 mg per day.
- **Hydrocodone dose:** 5–10 mg every 4–6 hours.
- **Oxycodone dose:** 5–10 mg every 4–6 hours.
- **All opioids:** Cause constipation (significant issue in field settings), nausea, drowsiness, respiratory depression at high doses. Dependence develops quickly — use the minimum effective dose for the minimum duration.
- **Never combine opioids with alcohol or sedatives** — respiratory depression is additive and can be fatal.

> **WARNING:** Opioid overdose causes respiratory arrest — the person stops breathing. If someone is unresponsive with slow or absent breathing after taking opioids, give naloxone (Narcan) if available: 0.4 mg IM or 4 mg intranasal. Can repeat every 2–3 minutes. Start rescue breathing while waiting for naloxone to work.

## Anti-Nausea / Anti-Vomiting

### Ondansetron (Zofran)

- **Use:** Nausea and vomiting. The most effective anti-nausea medication available.
- **Adult dose:** 4–8 mg every 8 hours. Can dissolve the tablet under the tongue (orally disintegrating tablet) if vomiting makes swallowing impossible.
- **Child dose (over 4 years):** 4 mg every 8 hours.
- **Safe in pregnancy.** Few side effects. Excellent drug to stockpile.

### Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)

- **Use:** Allergic reactions, itching, nausea, sleep aid.
- **Adult dose:** 25–50 mg every 4–6 hours. Maximum 300 mg per day.
- **Child dose:** 1 mg/kg every 6 hours. Maximum 5 mg/kg/day.
- **Side effects:** Significant drowsiness (useful as a sleep aid), dry mouth.
- **Do NOT use in:** Children under 2 without medical guidance, elderly (increases confusion and fall risk).

### Meclizine (Dramamine Less Drowsy)

- **Use:** Motion sickness, vertigo, dizziness.
- **Adult dose:** 25–50 mg once daily or every 12 hours.
- **Less sedating** than diphenhydramine.

## Gastrointestinal

### Loperamide (Imodium)

- **Use:** Non-infectious diarrhea. Slows gut motility.
- **Adult dose:** 4 mg initially, then 2 mg after each loose stool. Maximum 16 mg per day.
- **Do NOT use for:** Bloody diarrhea, diarrhea with fever, suspected bacterial infection (the body is trying to flush the pathogen — blocking this traps it). Can be used for traveler's diarrhea without fever/blood.
- **Child dose:** Not recommended under 2 years. Ages 2–5: 1 mg three times daily. Ages 6–8: 2 mg twice daily. Ages 9–11: 2 mg three times daily.

### Bismuth Subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol)

- **Use:** Upset stomach, diarrhea, heartburn, nausea.
- **Adult dose:** 30 mL or 2 tablets every 30–60 minutes as needed. Maximum 8 doses per day.
- **Note:** Turns stool and tongue black. This is harmless.
- **Do NOT use with aspirin** (contains salicylate — combined can cause toxicity).

### Antacids (Tums, Maalox)

- **Use:** Heartburn, acid reflux, calcium supplementation.
- **Dose:** 1–2 tablets as needed after meals.
- **Note:** Short-term relief only. Calcium carbonate can interfere with absorption of certain antibiotics (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones) — space by 2 hours.

### Ranitidine / Famotidine

- **Use:** Acid reduction, stomach ulcers, allergic reaction support (H2 blocker).
- **Famotidine dose:** 20 mg twice daily or 40 mg at bedtime.
- **Good for:** Chronic heartburn, stress ulcer prevention, adjunct to antihistamines in allergic reactions.

## Respiratory

### Pseudoephedrine (Sudafed)

- **Use:** Nasal congestion, sinus pressure.
- **Adult dose:** 30–60 mg every 4–6 hours. Maximum 240 mg per day.
- **Raises blood pressure** — avoid in hypertension. Causes insomnia if taken at night.

### Guaifenesin (Mucinex)

- **Use:** Chest congestion, productive cough. Thins mucus.
- **Adult dose:** 200–400 mg every 4 hours, or 600–1200 mg extended-release every 12 hours.
- **Drink plenty of water** with it — it works by pulling fluid into the airways.

### Dextromethorphan (DM, Robitussin DM)

- **Use:** Dry cough suppression.
- **Adult dose:** 10–20 mg every 4 hours or 30 mg every 6–8 hours. Maximum 120 mg per day.
- **Do NOT combine with** SSRIs or MAOIs (antidepressants) — risk of serotonin syndrome.

## Topical

### Bacitracin / Triple Antibiotic Ointment (Neosporin)

- **Use:** Preventing wound infection. Apply thin layer to clean wounds twice daily.
- **Note:** Neomycin (in triple antibiotic) causes contact allergy in about 10% of people — if a rash develops around a treated wound, switch to bacitracin alone.

### Hydrocortisone Cream (1%)

- **Use:** Itching, rashes, insect bites, mild eczema, poison ivy.
- **Apply:** Thin layer 2–3 times daily.
- **Do NOT use on:** Open wounds, fungal infections (makes them worse), large body areas for prolonged periods.

### Clotrimazole / Miconazole Cream

- **Use:** Fungal infections — athlete's foot, jock itch, ringworm, yeast infections.
- **Apply:** Twice daily for 2–4 weeks. Continue 1 week after symptoms resolve or it comes back.

## Medication Shelf Life

Most solid-form medications (tablets and capsules) remain effective well past their expiration date when stored properly (cool, dry, dark).

**The US military's Shelf Life Extension Program tested stockpiled drugs and found:**
- 90% of tested medications retained at least 90% potency 5–15 years past expiration
- Some drugs (ciprofloxacin, amoxicillin, doxycycline capsules) were still effective 10+ years past date
- Aspirin degrades faster than most — it breaks down into salicylic acid (you'll smell vinegar). Still works but with more stomach irritation.
- Nitroglycerin and insulin degrade significantly and should not be used past expiration.
- Liquid medications (syrups, suspensions) and anything requiring refrigeration degrade faster than solid forms.

**Storage guidelines:**
- Keep in original sealed containers
- Store below 77°F (25°C) if possible
- Keep dry — moisture destroys tablets
- Keep dark — light degrades many compounds
- Desiccant packets (silica gel) in the storage container help absorb moisture

> **NOTE:** An expired medication at reduced potency is almost always better than no medication at all. If it's your only option, use it. The primary exception is tetracycline-class antibiotics (expired tetracycline can become toxic to the kidneys — but doxycycline, the most common modern tetracycline, does not carry this risk).

## Drug Interactions — The Critical Ones

- **Blood thinners (warfarin) + aspirin/ibuprofen:** Severe bleeding risk
- **Opioids + alcohol/sedatives:** Respiratory depression and death
- **Metronidazole + alcohol:** Violent vomiting reaction (disulfiram-like)
- **Ciprofloxacin + dairy/calcium/antacids:** Blocks drug absorption — space by 2 hours
- **Doxycycline + dairy/calcium/iron:** Same — blocks absorption
- **Tramadol + antidepressants (SSRIs):** Serotonin syndrome risk — confusion, rapid heart rate, high temperature, seizures
- **ACE inhibitors + potassium supplements:** Dangerous hyperkalemia
- **Acetaminophen + alcohol (chronic use):** Liver destruction

When in doubt about combining medications, use the simplest regimen you can. One drug for one problem is always safer than stacking multiple medications.
