Most allergic reactions are uncomfortable but not dangerous. Anaphylaxis — the severe kind — can kill within minutes if untreated. The difference between the two is whether the reaction stays local or goes systemic (whole body).

## Mild to Moderate Allergic Reaction

**Signs:**
- Hives (raised, itchy welts) — localized or widespread
- Itching, redness
- Swelling at the site of contact (sting, food contact, etc.)
- Mild facial swelling
- Runny nose, sneezing, watery eyes
- Mild stomach upset

**Treatment:**
1. Remove the trigger if possible — move away from the source, stop eating the food, remove the stinger.
2. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25–50 mg by mouth for adults, every 4–6 hours. This is the go-to medication for allergic reactions. It causes drowsiness — that's normal.
3. Child dose: 1 mg/kg body weight, up to 25 mg. For a 20 kg (44 lb) child, that's 20 mg.
4. Cetirizine (Zyrtec) 10 mg once daily is an alternative that causes less drowsiness.
5. For skin-only reactions: calamine lotion, hydrocortisone cream, or a paste of baking soda and water on the hives.
6. Cool compresses help with itching.
7. Monitor for 2–4 hours. A mild reaction can escalate to anaphylaxis, especially in the first hour.

## Anaphylaxis — Recognition

Anaphylaxis involves TWO OR MORE body systems reacting at once. It develops fast — usually within minutes of exposure, rarely up to a few hours.

**Skin:** Widespread hives, flushing, severe itching, swelling of lips/tongue/face

**Airway:** Throat tightness, hoarse voice, stridor (high-pitched breathing sound on inhale), feeling of throat closing, difficulty swallowing, swollen tongue

**Breathing:** Wheezing, shortness of breath, coughing, chest tightness

**Circulation:** Rapid weak pulse, low blood pressure, dizziness, fainting, pale or blue skin

**GI:** Severe abdominal cramping, vomiting, diarrhea

**Brain:** Confusion, sense of impending doom ("I feel like I'm going to die" — take this statement seriously, it's a recognized symptom)

> **CRITICAL:** If someone has throat tightness, difficulty breathing, or circulatory collapse after exposure to a known or potential allergen, treat as anaphylaxis immediately. Do not wait to see if it gets worse. Anaphylaxis can go from uncomfortable to airway closure in minutes. Epinephrine is the treatment. Everything else is secondary.

## Anaphylaxis — Treatment

### Step 1: Epinephrine (Adrenaline)

This is the ONLY drug that treats anaphylaxis. Antihistamines help with symptoms but do not reverse the anaphylactic cascade. Epinephrine does.

**EpiPen or auto-injector:**
1. Remove the safety cap.
2. Inject into the outer thigh (upper leg, halfway between hip and knee). It goes through clothing — don't waste time removing pants.
3. Hold for 10 seconds.
4. Adult EpiPen: 0.3 mg. Junior EpiPen (for children 15–30 kg / 33–66 lbs): 0.15 mg.

**Epinephrine from a vial (1:1000 concentration, which is 1 mg/mL):**
- Adult dose: 0.3–0.5 mL (0.3–0.5 mg) injected intramuscularly (IM) into the outer thigh.
- Child dose: 0.01 mg/kg, maximum 0.3 mg. For a 20 kg child: 0.2 mL.
- Use a standard syringe and needle. Inject into the outer thigh muscle.

**If symptoms aren't improving in 5–15 minutes, give a second dose.** You can repeat epinephrine every 5–15 minutes. Most people need only 1–2 doses, but severe reactions may need 3+.

**After epinephrine:**
- The effects last only 15–20 minutes. Symptoms can return (biphasic reaction). Monitor closely for at least 4–6 hours. One in five anaphylaxis cases has a second wave.
- Have a second dose ready.

### Step 2: Position

- If blood pressure is low (dizzy, faint, pale): lay flat, elevate legs.
- If breathing is the main problem: sit upright — it's easier to breathe.
- If vomiting: recovery position (on their side).
- If pregnant: lay on the left side.

### Step 3: Supplemental Treatment

After epinephrine:
1. **Diphenhydramine** 50 mg by mouth or IM injection. This helps with hives and itching but does NOT treat the life-threatening components.
2. **Ranitidine** (Zantac) 150 mg by mouth if available — it's an H2 antihistamine that adds to diphenhydramine's effect.
3. **Oral prednisone** 50 mg (or dexamethasone 10 mg) — helps prevent biphasic reactions hours later. Takes 4–6 hours to work, so it's not immediate treatment, it's prevention of recurrence.
4. **Albuterol inhaler** if wheezing is present — 4–8 puffs. Treats bronchospasm specifically.

### Step 4: Monitor

Watch for at least 6 hours. Biphasic reactions (second wave of anaphylaxis) occur in up to 20% of cases and can hit 1–12 hours after the initial reaction. Keep epinephrine within arm's reach.

## Improvised Epinephrine

If you don't have pharmaceutical epinephrine and someone is dying of anaphylaxis:

**Asthma inhalers (epinephrine/racepinephrine — like Primatene Mist):** These contain epinephrine for inhalation. In a life-threatening emergency, 10–15 puffs inhaled can provide some systemic epinephrine absorption. This is not ideal and not as effective as injection, but better than nothing.

**OTC nasal decongestant sprays containing oxymetazoline (Afrin):** These are vasoconstrictors. Spraying into the nose while anaphylaxis is occurring can help reduce nasal and pharyngeal (throat) swelling. NOT a substitute for epinephrine, but may buy time.

> **WARNING:** There is no true substitute for injectable epinephrine in anaphylaxis. If you or anyone in your group has known severe allergies, stockpile EpiPens. They work well past their expiration date — studies show they retain 80%+ potency for up to 4 years past expiration. An expired EpiPen is infinitely better than no EpiPen.

## Common Triggers

**Food:** Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, milk, eggs, wheat, soy. Food anaphylaxis usually hits within 30 minutes of eating.

**Stings:** Bee, wasp, hornet, yellow jacket, fire ant. Sting anaphylaxis usually starts within 5–15 minutes.

**Medications:** Penicillin and related antibiotics, sulfa drugs, aspirin/NSAIDs, anesthetics. Drug reactions can be immediate or delayed hours.

**Other:** Latex, exercise (exercise-induced anaphylaxis is real — usually occurs within 30 minutes of eating a trigger food then exercising), cold exposure (cold urticaria/anaphylaxis).

## Preventing Repeat Reactions

If someone has an anaphylactic event:

1. They are now at risk for future episodes. Avoid the trigger completely.
2. They should carry epinephrine at all times — two doses minimum.
3. A medical alert bracelet or tag is valuable in any group setting so others know their allergy and can act if they're unable to.
4. Teach everyone in the group how to use an EpiPen. The person having the reaction may not be able to self-inject.
5. Antihistamines preemptively (diphenhydramine 25 mg before known exposure risk) reduce the severity of reactions but do NOT prevent anaphylaxis.

## Allergic Reactions to Medications

If someone develops a reaction after starting an antibiotic or other medication:

- **Mild rash without other symptoms:** May be able to continue the medication with antihistamine support if the infection is serious enough to warrant the risk. Watch closely.
- **Hives, swelling, or any breathing difficulty:** Stop the medication immediately. Give diphenhydramine. Switch to an alternative antibiotic from a different drug class.
- **Any sign of anaphylaxis:** Stop medication, epinephrine, full anaphylaxis protocol.

Document the allergy. Mark it visibly in the person's medical information. In a group setting, make sure multiple people know who's allergic to what.
