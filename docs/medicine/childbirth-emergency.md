Most births go normally without any intervention. Humans have been doing this for hundreds of thousands of years without hospitals. Your job isn't to deliver the baby — the mother's body does that. Your job is to catch, not panic, and handle complications if they come up.

## Stages of Labor

**Stage 1 — Contractions and dilation.** The cervix opens from closed to 10 cm. This is the longest stage. First-time mothers: 12–18 hours average. Second or later babies: 6–8 hours. Contractions start irregular and mild, becoming regular, closer together, and more painful. When contractions are 3–4 minutes apart and lasting 60 seconds each, things are progressing.

**Stage 2 — Pushing and delivery.** Once fully dilated, the urge to push becomes overwhelming. This stage lasts 30 minutes to 2 hours for a first baby, sometimes just minutes for later births. The baby is born at the end of this stage.

**Stage 3 — Placenta delivery.** The placenta separates and delivers, usually within 5–30 minutes after the baby. Don't rush this.

## Preparing for the Birth

Gather before things get urgent:

- Clean towels or cloths (at least 4–5) — boil them if you can
- String, shoelaces, or strips of clean cloth for tying the cord (2 ties needed)
- Sharp, clean blade for cutting the cord — sterilize with flame or alcohol
- A clean, warm blanket or clothing to wrap the baby
- A bulb syringe if available (for suctioning the baby's airway)
- Warm water and soap for washing hands
- Something clean for the mother to deliver on — plastic sheet, clean blankets, even fresh garbage bags
- A container for the placenta

Wash your hands thoroughly. If you have gloves, wear them. If not, clean hands are more important than dirty gloves.

## Normal Delivery

### Positioning the Mother

Let her choose what's comfortable. Options:
- On her back with knees drawn up (most common for assisted delivery — gives you the best view)
- Hands and knees (reduces back labor pain)
- Squatting (gravity assists, opens the pelvis 20–30% wider than lying down)
- Side-lying (good if she's exhausted)

Whatever position, make sure you can see and access the birth area.

### As the Baby Comes

1. When the head appears at the vaginal opening ("crowning"), tell her to stop pushing hard and pant or blow. This lets the head deliver slowly and reduces tearing. A slow, controlled delivery of the head is ideal.
2. Support the head gently with your hand as it emerges. Don't pull.
3. Once the head is out, check if the umbilical cord is wrapped around the baby's neck. This happens in about 25% of births and is usually not an emergency. If the cord is loose, slip it over the baby's head. If it's tight, slip it over a shoulder or try to clamp and cut it before the body delivers (only if it's very tight and clearly restricting).
4. The baby will usually turn sideways on its own after the head comes out. This is normal — the shoulders are rotating to fit.
5. Gently guide (don't pull) the upper shoulder out by supporting the head and easing it slightly downward. Then guide upward to deliver the lower shoulder.
6. Once the shoulders are out, the rest slides out fast. Hold on — babies are slippery. Cup the head with one hand, support the body with the other.
7. Note the time of birth.

### Immediately After Birth

1. The baby should start crying within 30–60 seconds. Crying is good — it inflates the lungs. If the baby doesn't cry, rub the soles of the feet firmly and rub the back with a towel. Most babies need only stimulation to start breathing.
2. Clear the airway if needed. If you have a bulb syringe, gently suction the mouth first, then the nose. If no bulb syringe, use a clean cloth over your finger to wipe out the mouth. Don't suction deeply or aggressively.
3. Dry the baby immediately and aggressively with a towel. Drying provides stimulation AND prevents hypothermia. Newborns lose heat dangerously fast — their body surface area relative to mass is huge.
4. Throw away the wet towel and wrap the baby in a dry, warm blanket. Cover the head — most heat loss is from the head.
5. Place the baby skin-to-skin on the mother's chest, covered with a blanket. The mother's body heat is the best incubator. Skin-to-skin contact also promotes bonding and stimulates the uterus to contract (reducing bleeding).

> **CRITICAL:** The baby's body temperature is your top priority in the first hour. Hypothermia kills newborns fast. A healthy baby that dies in the first hours after birth in a field setting almost always dies from cold. Dry them, wrap them, put them on the mother's chest, cover them both.

### Cutting the Cord

There is no rush to cut the cord. Delayed cord clamping (waiting 1–3 minutes after birth) transfers extra blood from the placenta to the baby, which is beneficial.

1. Wait until the cord stops pulsating (turns white and goes limp). This usually happens within 1–5 minutes.
2. Tie the cord tightly in two places — one about 6 inches from the baby's belly, the second about 8 inches from the belly (2 inches beyond the first tie).
3. Cut between the two ties with a sterile blade.
4. Check the baby's end of the cord for bleeding. If it oozes, retie more tightly. A bleeding cord can kill a newborn quickly — their total blood volume is tiny (about a cup for a full-term baby).
5. Keep the cord stump clean and dry. It will dry up and fall off in 5–10 days.

### Delivering the Placenta

1. Don't pull on the cord. Wait for the placenta to deliver on its own, usually within 5–30 minutes.
2. Signs the placenta is separating: a gush of blood, the cord gets longer (it's descending), the uterus rises in the abdomen and becomes firm and round.
3. Have the mother push when she feels pressure. Gravity helps — sitting up or squatting.
4. Once it delivers, examine it. A complete placenta is round, about 8 inches across, and has intact membranes. If it looks like pieces are missing, the fragments left inside will cause hemorrhage and infection. There's no field fix for retained placenta fragments — note it as a serious complication.
5. Save the placenta in a container if you can. If medical care becomes available, they'll want to examine it.

> **WARNING:** If the placenta hasn't delivered within 30 minutes and the mother is bleeding heavily, this is a retained placenta emergency. Do NOT pull on the cord — you can invert the uterus, which is catastrophic. Breastfeed the baby (nipple stimulation causes the uterus to contract), massage the uterus through the abdomen (see hemorrhage section), and prepare for the possibility that manual removal may be needed.

## Postpartum Hemorrhage

Some bleeding after delivery is normal — up to about 500 mL (2 cups). Beyond that is hemorrhage, and it's the number one cause of maternal death worldwide.

**Signs of dangerous bleeding:**
- Soaking through more than one pad/cloth per hour
- Blood pooling under the mother
- Rapid pulse, dizziness, pale skin — signs of shock
- The uterus feels soft and boggy instead of firm and round

**Treatment — do all of these simultaneously:**

1. **Fundal massage:** This is the most important intervention. Place your hand on the mother's lower abdomen, find the top of the uterus (it should be around the navel after delivery), and massage it firmly in a circular motion. Press hard — this is uncomfortable but necessary. The uterus should contract and feel like a hard grapefruit. If it softens, massage again. Continue until it stays firm.
2. **Breastfeeding:** Put the baby to the breast. Nipple stimulation releases oxytocin, which causes uterine contractions.
3. **Empty the bladder:** A full bladder prevents the uterus from contracting. Have her urinate, or catheterize if you know how.
4. **Bimanual compression (if massage alone isn't working):** Place one fist inside the vagina against the front of the uterus and your other hand on the abdomen behind the uterus. Compress the uterus between your two hands. Hold for several minutes.
5. **Medications if available:**
   - Misoprostol 800 mcg placed under the tongue (sublingual) — this is the WHO-recommended drug for postpartum hemorrhage when oxytocin isn't available. Tablets are cheap and shelf-stable.
   - Oxytocin (Pitocin) 10 units IM injection if available.
6. **IV fluids** if you have them. Push aggressively — this is hypovolemic shock.
7. **Keep the mother warm.** She's losing blood and will become hypothermic.

## Complications

### Breech Presentation (Feet or Buttocks First)

If you see a foot, buttock, or anything other than the top of the head presenting:

1. Get the mother on hands and knees with her chest lower than her hips. This can sometimes encourage the baby to turn.
2. If the baby is coming buttocks first and is progressing, do NOT pull. Support the body as it delivers, letting gravity and contractions do the work.
3. Once the body is out to the chest, wrap a towel around the baby's hips for grip. Let the arms deliver by sweeping each one down across the chest.
4. For the head (which comes last in breech): flex the baby face-down over your forearm. Place two fingers on the baby's cheekbones to keep the head flexed. Use your other hand on the back of the head to guide it out slowly.
5. The head must deliver within 3–5 minutes of the body delivering, or the baby will suffocate. This is the critical window.

### Cord Prolapse (Cord Comes Before the Baby)

If you see or feel the umbilical cord protruding before the baby's head:

1. This is an extreme emergency. The baby's head will compress the cord against the pelvis and cut off blood supply.
2. Have the mother get on her knees with her chest on the ground and buttocks in the air — this uses gravity to pull the baby away from the cord.
3. With a gloved or clean hand, reach in and push the baby's head UP and away from the cord. Hold it there. Do not let go.
4. The baby must be delivered immediately, usually by cesarean. In a field setting, maintain the position and pressure until delivery occurs or help arrives.
5. Keep the cord moist with warm, wet cloths if it's protruding.

### Shoulder Dystocia (Shoulders Stuck)

The head delivers but the shoulders are stuck behind the pelvic bone. You have about 5 minutes.

1. Do NOT pull on the baby's head. This can cause nerve damage.
2. McRoberts maneuver: Push the mother's knees sharply back toward her shoulders (hyperflexion of the hips). This changes the pelvic angle and frees most stuck shoulders.
3. Apply firm pressure with the heel of your hand just above the mother's pubic bone, pushing downward and to the side. This pushes the baby's shoulder under the bone.
4. If these don't work, flip the mother to hands and knees (Gaskin maneuver) — the change in gravity and position often releases the shoulder.

### When the Baby Doesn't Breathe

If stimulation (rubbing, flicking feet) doesn't produce crying within 60 seconds:

1. Clear the airway — suction or wipe the mouth, then the nose.
2. Tilt the head to a neutral position (not tilted back like an adult — newborn airways are different). A rolled cloth under the shoulders helps.
3. Give 5 gentle rescue breaths covering the baby's mouth AND nose. Tiny puffs — just enough to see the chest rise. Newborn lungs are the size of your thumbs.
4. Check for a pulse: feel the inside of the upper arm (brachial pulse) or the umbilical cord base.
5. If pulse is below 60 per minute or absent: begin infant CPR. Two fingers on the breastbone, compress 1/3 the chest depth, 120 compressions per minute. Ratio of 3 compressions to 1 breath.
6. Reassess every 60 seconds.

Most newborns who need resuscitation respond to airway management and ventilation alone. Very few need chest compressions.
