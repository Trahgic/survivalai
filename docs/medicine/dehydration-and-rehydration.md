Water is the single most critical resource for survival. You can go weeks without food but only about 3 days without water — less in heat, at altitude, or with exertion. Dehydration is the underlying cause or a contributing factor in a huge number of wilderness and disaster deaths, often when people don't realize it's happening.

## How Dehydration Happens

Your body loses water constantly through breathing, sweating, urination, and evaporation from skin. Normally you replace it by drinking. When output exceeds input for long enough, things break down.

Common causes in a survival or disaster context:
- Not enough clean water available
- Vomiting or diarrhea (the biggest acute cause worldwide)
- Burns (plasma loss through damaged skin)
- Excessive sweating from heat or exertion
- Bleeding
- Fever (increases metabolic rate and sweating)
- Refusing to drink due to fear of contaminated water (ironically, dehydration often kills faster than most waterborne illnesses)

## Recognizing Dehydration by Severity

### Mild (1–5% body weight lost as water)

- Thirst — the earliest and most reliable sign
- Dry mouth and lips
- Slightly decreased urine output, urine darker yellow than normal
- Mild headache
- Slight fatigue

At this stage, just drinking water fixes it. Easy to manage, easy to miss.

### Moderate (6–9% body weight lost)

- Intense thirst
- Very dry mouth, thick saliva
- Markedly decreased urine — small amounts, dark amber
- Headache is worse
- Dizziness when standing up
- Rapid heart rate
- Skin turgor test positive: pinch the skin on the back of the hand. In a hydrated person it snaps back immediately. In moderate dehydration, the skin "tents" and takes 2–3 seconds to flatten back.
- Sunken eyes
- Irritability, difficulty concentrating
- Muscle cramps

### Severe (10%+ body weight lost)

- Little to no urine
- Very rapid, weak pulse
- Low blood pressure — dizzy or fainting when sitting up
- Skin is pale, cool, and the turgor test shows tenting that persists for several seconds
- Deeply sunken eyes
- Confusion, delirium
- Extreme weakness, unable to walk
- In infants: sunken fontanelle (the soft spot on top of the head is depressed), no tears when crying, dry diaper for 6+ hours

> **CRITICAL:** Severe dehydration is shock. The body doesn't have enough fluid to circulate. Treat it like any other shock — lay flat, elevate legs, rewarm, and push fluids. See shock.md. Without rehydration, severe dehydration kills within hours.

## Urine Color Chart

This is the single easiest self-check you have:

- **Clear to pale yellow:** Well hydrated. Keep doing what you're doing.
- **Yellow:** Normal, but drink a bit more if in heat or exerting.
- **Dark yellow:** Mildly dehydrated. Increase intake now.
- **Amber/honey:** Moderately dehydrated. You need to stop and drink significantly.
- **Brown or tea-colored:** Severely dehydrated OR possible muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis) from crush injuries or extreme exertion. Either way, emergency — push fluids hard and watch for kidney failure.
- **Red or pink:** Blood in urine. Not dehydration — indicates kidney injury, urinary tract injury, or severe infection. Different problem.

## Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

Plain water works for mild dehydration. But when you've lost significant fluid — especially from diarrhea or vomiting — you've also lost sodium, potassium, and other electrolytes. Drinking plain water without replacing electrolytes can actually make things worse by diluting what's left.

### WHO ORS Formula

This is the gold standard. It's been used worldwide to save millions of lives from diarrheal dehydration.

In 1 liter of clean water, dissolve:
- 6 level teaspoons of sugar (about 30 grams)
- ½ level teaspoon of salt (about 2.5 grams)

Stir until fully dissolved. Tastes like slightly salty, slightly sweet water. If it tastes saltier than tears, you used too much salt — add more water.

If you have it, adding ½ teaspoon of potassium chloride (salt substitute, sold as "No Salt" or "Nu-Salt") improves the formula significantly. Potassium loss is a major factor in weakness and muscle cramps.

### Why Sugar Matters

The sugar isn't just for calories. Glucose activates a transport mechanism in the gut that pulls sodium and water into the bloodstream. Salt water alone is absorbed much more slowly. The sugar-salt combination is what makes ORS work dramatically better than plain water for rehydration.

### Improvised ORS If You Have Nothing

- **Cereal-based:** Cook a handful of rice, wheat, or corn until it's a thin porridge. Strain out the solids. Add ½ teaspoon of salt per liter. The starch breaks down to glucose in the gut and works like sugar.
- **Fruit juice diluted:** Mix juice 50/50 with water, add a pinch of salt. Too much sugar (like straight juice or soda) pulls water INTO the gut through osmosis and worsens diarrhea.
- **Coconut water:** Already contains natural electrolytes. Add a small pinch of salt and it's close to ideal.

> **WARNING:** Do not drink seawater. Do not drink urine. Both are more concentrated than your body fluids and will accelerate dehydration. Your kidneys have to use more water to process the salt out than you get from drinking it. This applies even in desperate situations — seawater and urine will kill you faster than not drinking at all.

## Rehydration Treatment by Severity

### Mild Dehydration

1. Drink water. Any clean water. 1–2 liters over 2–4 hours.
2. ORS is ideal but not critical at this stage.
3. Rest in shade if available.
4. Resume normal drinking patterns — at least 2 liters per day at rest, 3–4 liters in heat or with activity.

### Moderate Dehydration

1. Start ORS immediately. Target 50–100 mL per kilogram of body weight over 4 hours. For a 70 kg adult that's 3.5–7 liters over 4 hours. Sounds like a lot — it is.
2. Small, frequent sips — not gulps. About a quarter cup (60 mL) every 5 minutes. Drinking too fast triggers vomiting, especially if diarrhea caused the dehydration.
3. If the person is vomiting, wait 10 minutes after a vomiting episode, then restart with even smaller sips — a tablespoon (15 mL) every 2–3 minutes. The gut absorbs more than you'd think even while vomiting if you keep the volume small enough.
4. Monitor urine output. The goal is to produce at least some pale urine within 4–6 hours of starting rehydration.
5. Continue ORS until urine color normalizes and symptoms improve.

### Severe Dehydration

1. This person needs IV fluids if you have supplies and training. Normal saline or lactated Ringer's, 1 liter wide open (as fast as it flows), then reassess.
2. If no IV supplies: oral rehydration is still the treatment, just slower. ORS by syringe, spoon, or soaked cloth dripped into the mouth if the person can't drink on their own.
3. Position flat on their back, legs elevated. This is shock treatment now.
4. If the person is unconscious, do not pour fluid into their mouth — they'll aspirate. Turn the head to the side, wet the lips, and drip tiny amounts between the cheek and gums.
5. A nasogastric tube (any flexible tubing, like an aquarium tube, passed through the nose into the stomach) can deliver fluids to an unconscious person if you know the technique. Measure from the nose to the ear to the stomach (roughly 50 cm in an adult) to know how far to insert.
6. Reassess every 30 minutes. Improving signs: pulse slowing, skin warming, becoming more alert, producing urine.

## Dehydration from Diarrhea

Diarrheal disease kills more people globally than nearly any other cause. It's not the infection that kills — it's the water loss.

1. ORS is the primary treatment. Push it constantly.
2. Do not use anti-diarrheal medications (loperamide/Imodium) for bloody diarrhea or suspected infectious diarrhea with fever. The body is trying to flush the infection out. Stopping the diarrhea traps the pathogen.
3. Loperamide is acceptable for non-bloody, non-fever diarrhea (traveler's diarrhea, food intolerance) to reduce fluid loss. Adult dose: 4 mg initially, then 2 mg after each loose stool. Maximum 16 mg per day.
4. Zinc supplementation (20 mg daily for 10 days) reduces the duration of diarrheal episodes in children. This is a WHO recommendation for pediatric diarrheal illness.
5. Continue eating if the person can tolerate food. Bland, simple food — rice, bananas, toast, broth. Food provides energy for recovery and slows gut motility.

## Dehydration from Vomiting

1. Wait 15–30 minutes after vomiting stops, then start tiny amounts of ORS — 1 tablespoon (15 mL) every 2–3 minutes.
2. If they keep it down for 30 minutes, slowly increase the volume.
3. If vomiting resumes, wait again and restart with smaller amounts.
4. If you have ondansetron (Zofran), give 4–8 mg under the tongue (dissolves and absorbs without swallowing). This is the best anti-nausea medication available. Alternatively: promethazine (Phenergan) 25 mg.
5. If nothing stays down for more than 6 hours and the person is becoming increasingly dehydrated, you need IV fluids or rectal fluids (ORS administered rectally with a small tube and syringe — the colon absorbs fluid well, and it bypasses the vomiting entirely).

## How Much Water Do People Need?

At rest in mild weather: 2–3 liters per day (about ½ gallon).
In heat with moderate activity: 4–6 liters per day.
In extreme heat with heavy labor: 8–12 liters per day.

Children need proportionally more water per body weight than adults. A child doing the same activity as an adult in the same heat needs about 50% more water per kilogram.

> **WARNING:** It's possible to drink too much water. Hyponatremia (low sodium from excessive water intake with no salt) causes confusion, seizures, and death. It looks a lot like dehydration. If someone is drinking huge quantities of water and getting worse, not better, they need salt — give ORS instead of plain water. This most commonly happens during heavy exertion in heat when people drink water without electrolytes.

## Rationing Water

If water supply is limited:

- Do not ration below 1 liter per day for survival. Below this, your body deteriorates faster than the water saves you.
- Reduce exertion. Rest burns about 1/3 the water that heavy work does.
- Stay in shade. Direct sun massively increases sweat loss.
- Breathe through your nose, not your mouth. Mouth breathing evaporates more water.
- Don't eat protein-heavy food if water is scarce — protein metabolism requires extra water.
- Cover exposed skin. Clothing actually reduces water loss compared to bare skin in hot, sunny conditions.
- Travel at night if you must move.
