A broken bone hurts, but it rarely kills you on its own. What kills is the bleeding that comes with it (a broken femur can dump 1–2 liters of blood into the thigh) or the shock that follows. Your job is to immobilize the break, manage pain, and watch for complications.

## How to Tell If It's Broken

You can't always tell without an X-ray. But these signs make a fracture likely:

- A snap or crack heard at the time of injury
- Immediate, sharp pain that gets worse with any movement
- Swelling that develops fast — within minutes
- Visible deformity — the limb looks bent, shortened, or rotated wrong
- The person can't bear weight on it or use it at all
- Crepitus — a grinding or crunching feeling when the area moves (don't go looking for this on purpose, it hurts and can worsen the injury)
- Bruising that appears quickly, especially deep purple

If you're not sure whether it's a fracture or a bad sprain, treat it as a fracture. Splinting a sprain does no harm. Not splinting a fracture does.

## Open vs. Closed Fractures

**Closed fracture:** The bone is broken but the skin isn't. Most fractures are closed.

**Open fracture:** The bone has punctured through the skin, or the wound connects to the fracture site. You can see bone, or there's a wound near the fracture with heavy bleeding. This is a much more serious injury — infection risk is very high.

> **WARNING:** Do not push a protruding bone back under the skin. Cover the wound with a moist, clean dressing, splint the limb in the position you find it, and get to medical care if any exists. An open fracture without surgical cleaning almost always gets infected.

## Splinting Basics

The goal of a splint is to stop the broken ends from moving. Movement grinds bone edges together, tears tissue, increases bleeding, and causes extreme pain.

### Rules for Every Splint

1. Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture. If the forearm is broken, lock the wrist and the elbow. If the shin is broken, lock the ankle and the knee.
2. Splint in the position you find the limb. Don't straighten a bent fracture unless you can't get a pulse below the break (see "When to Realign" below).
3. Pad the splint. Rigid material against skin causes pressure sores in hours. Use cloth, socks, leaves, moss — anything soft between the splint and the body.
4. Check circulation after splinting. Press a fingernail or toenail below the splint until it turns white, then release. Color should return within 2 seconds. Also check for feeling and the ability to wiggle fingers or toes. If circulation is gone, the splint is too tight — loosen it.
5. Recheck circulation every 30 minutes for the first few hours, then every 2 hours. Swelling increases over time and can make a good splint too tight.
6. Tie splint bindings between the fracture and the joints, not directly over the break. Minimum of 3 ties — one above the fracture, one below, and one at each immobilized joint.
7. Leave fingers and toes visible and exposed if possible, so you can check circulation.

### Improvised Splint Materials

Rigid options: sticks (straight, at least as thick as your thumb), boards, rolled newspaper or magazines, cardboard, tent poles, trekking poles, ski poles, a rolled-up sleeping pad, a rifle or shotgun.

Flexible options: SAM splints (fold into a C-shape or T-shape for rigidity), pillows, rolled blankets, heavy clothing, a backpack frame.

Ties: strips of cloth, belts, shoelaces, duct tape, paracord, bandanas, torn t-shirts. Don't use wire or thin cord — it'll cut into swollen skin.

## Specific Fractures

### Forearm (Radius/Ulna)

1. Place a rigid splint along the palm side of the forearm from the fingertips to past the elbow.
2. Place a second splint along the back of the forearm if you have material.
3. Pad well. Tie in 3–4 places — hand, mid-forearm, and above the elbow.
4. Bend the elbow to 90 degrees and support with a sling (triangular bandage, belt, or shirt pinned up). The hand should sit slightly higher than the elbow.
5. Secure the sling to the body with a swathe (a band around the chest and arm) so the arm doesn't swing.

### Upper Arm (Humerus)

1. Place a padded splint along the outer side of the upper arm from the shoulder to the elbow.
2. Bend the elbow to 90 degrees.
3. Apply a sling and swathe.
4. If you don't have splint materials, the body makes a good one — bind the upper arm to the torso with strips of cloth, with padding in the armpit, and add a sling for the forearm.

### Collarbone (Clavicle)

Collarbones break from falls onto an outstretched hand or direct impact to the shoulder. The person will hold the arm on the injured side against their body and tilt their head toward the break.

1. No rigid splint needed. Apply a sling to support the arm on the injured side.
2. Add a swathe to pin the arm against the chest.
3. Put a small pad (rolled sock) in the armpit to push the shoulder slightly outward.
4. Most clavicle fractures heal in 6–8 weeks with just immobilization.

### Wrist and Hand

1. Place a padded rigid splint along the palm side from fingertips to mid-forearm.
2. Place a rolled bandage or sock in the palm so the hand is in a natural curved position (like holding a can).
3. Wrap loosely. Sling the arm.

### Finger

1. Buddy-tape the broken finger to an adjacent healthy finger with a small pad between them.
2. If the fracture is severe or angulated, splint the finger straight using a popsicle stick, pen, or small piece of rigid material padded with tape or cloth.
3. Check that the fingertip still has circulation (pink, warm, feeling present).

### Femur (Thighbone)

This is the most dangerous common fracture. The thigh muscles are powerful and will pull the broken ends past each other, shortening the leg. Internal bleeding can easily reach 1–2 liters.

1. A femur fracture needs a traction splint if you can build one. Traction pulls the leg back to length, reduces bleeding, and dramatically reduces pain.
2. Improvised traction splint: Use two long poles (one from the armpit to past the foot on the outside, one from the groin to past the foot on the inside). Pad the top ends well — the outer pole presses against the armpit, the inner against the groin. Tie the poles to the body at the chest, hip, above the knee, and below the knee. Attach a loop of cloth to the ankle and tie it to the end of the poles with enough tension to pull the leg to approximately its normal length. The person will tell you when pain decreases — that's about the right amount of pull.
3. If traction isn't possible, splint the leg rigid from the hip to below the ankle using whatever long rigid materials you have. Pad everywhere. Tie the injured leg to the uninjured leg with padding between the knees and ankles.
4. Treat for shock — lay flat, elevate legs if possible, keep warm, push fluids.

> **CRITICAL:** A person with a femur fracture can bleed to death internally without a single drop of blood showing. If the thigh is swelling rapidly and the person shows signs of shock (rapid pulse, pale skin, confusion), they're bleeding internally. Push fluids, keep them warm, and get to surgical care by any means available.

### Lower Leg (Tibia/Fibula)

1. Place a padded splint on each side of the lower leg from above the knee to below the foot.
2. Alternatively, use an L-shaped splint along the back of the leg and under the foot.
3. A pillow wrapped around the lower leg and secured with ties works in a pinch.
4. Tie at mid-thigh, above the knee, below the knee, mid-calf, and at the ankle/foot.
5. Keep the foot at a 90-degree angle if possible to prevent foot drop during healing.

### Ankle

1. A pillow splint works best — wrap a pillow or folded blanket around the ankle and lower leg, tie in place.
2. Leave the shoe on if the foot isn't deformed — the shoe acts as a splint and taking it off through a broken ankle is agonizing. Cut the laces to allow for swelling.
3. Apply the same circulation checks — toes warm, pink, with feeling.

### Ribs

Rib fractures are common and painful but usually not dangerous unless multiple ribs are broken or a lung is punctured.

1. Do not wrap or tape the chest. Old-school rib binding restricts breathing and causes pneumonia — it kills more people than it helps.
2. The person should take deep breaths and cough periodically even though it hurts. Shallow breathing leads to lung collapse and infection.
3. Hold a pillow against the injured side when coughing to splint the ribs with pressure.
4. Pain management is the main treatment. Ibuprofen 600 mg every 8 hours. Stronger pain meds if available — the person needs to be able to breathe deeply.
5. Sleep propped up at 30–45 degrees or on the injured side (the mattress splints the ribs).

> **WARNING:** If the person develops increasing shortness of breath, one side of the chest isn't moving when they breathe, or you hear a sucking sound from the chest wall — suspect a pneumothorax (collapsed lung). See the wound care article for chest seal procedures. This is life-threatening.

### Pelvis

A pelvic fracture is a major emergency. The pelvis has huge blood vessels running through it and a fracture can cause massive internal bleeding.

1. Do not roll the person or ask them to move.
2. Bind the pelvis with a sheet, belt, or improvised binder wrapped tightly around the hips at the level of the hip bones (the bony points you can feel at the front). Tie it snug — this compresses the fracture and slows bleeding.
3. Place the knees together with a pad between them and tie the legs together.
4. Treat for shock aggressively.
5. Do not let the person walk. Carry them or drag them on a rigid surface.

### Spine

If you suspect a spinal injury (fall from height, vehicle accident, head injury with neck pain, tingling or numbness in arms/legs):

1. Do not move the person unless they're in immediate danger (fire, rising water, structural collapse).
2. Hold the head and neck still in the position you found them. Use your hands, then pack clothing, bags, or sandbags on either side of the head.
3. If you must move them, maintain spinal alignment — the head, neck, and torso move as one unit. Use a board, door, or rigid surface as a backboard. Minimum 4 people to move safely.
4. Secure the person to the board with straps across the forehead, chest, hips, and legs.

## When to Realign a Fracture

Generally, don't. Straightening a fracture is painful and can cause further damage. But there is one situation where you must try:

**If there's no pulse below the fracture site.** Press on a toenail or fingernail — if it doesn't pink back up within 2 seconds, the broken bone is pressing on or kinking an artery. Without blood flow, the limb dies within 6–8 hours.

1. Have someone hold the limb above the fracture firmly.
2. Grasp below the fracture and pull gently in line with the bone, applying steady traction. Pull slowly — not a yank.
3. As the bone ends separate slightly, guide the limb toward its natural alignment.
4. Recheck the pulse. If it returns, splint immediately in the new position.
5. If it doesn't return after one attempt, splint in the best position you achieved and get to medical care.

This is agonizing. Warn the person, give them something to bite on, and have someone hold them still. Use whatever pain management you have before attempting this.

## Pain Management

- **Mild:** Ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 6–8 hours, ice/cold compresses (15 minutes on, 15 minutes off)
- **Moderate:** Add acetaminophen 500–1000 mg between ibuprofen doses (alternating gives near-continuous coverage)
- **Severe (femur, pelvis, realignment):** You need opioids or tramadol if available. Codeine 30–60 mg every 4–6 hours, or tramadol 50–100 mg every 6 hours.
- Elevation helps — keep the break above the heart when possible
- Cold packs for the first 48 hours reduce swelling and numb pain. After 48 hours, switch to warmth.

## Complications to Watch For

**Compartment syndrome:** Pressure builds inside a muscle compartment (usually the forearm or lower leg) from swelling. The limb becomes increasingly painful, tight, shiny, and numb. Pain with passive stretching of the fingers or toes is the hallmark sign. This is an emergency — without surgical release, the muscle dies. Loosen all splints and bindings. Elevate. If symptoms don't improve within 30 minutes, the person needs surgery.

**Fat embolism:** 12–72 hours after a long bone fracture (femur, tibia), fat droplets from bone marrow can enter the bloodstream. Signs: sudden shortness of breath, confusion, petechiae (tiny red dots on the chest, armpits, or whites of the eyes). There is no field treatment. Give oxygen if available, keep the person still.

**Infection (open fractures):** Redness, swelling, warmth, pus, fever, or red streaks extending from the wound. Start antibiotics: cephalexin 500 mg every 6 hours, or ciprofloxacin 500 mg every 12 hours if available. Clean the wound twice daily.

## When It's Beyond Field Treatment

- Open fractures with visible bone
- Femur or pelvis fractures (internal bleeding risk)
- Any fracture with no pulse below the break that won't return with realignment
- Suspected spine fractures with neurological symptoms
- Compartment syndrome
- Multiple fractures from high-energy trauma (fall from height, vehicle crash)
- Joint dislocations you can't reduce (put back in place)
