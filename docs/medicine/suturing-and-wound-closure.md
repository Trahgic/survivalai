Not every wound needs to be closed. And closing the wrong wound at the wrong time can trap bacteria inside and cause a life-threatening infection. Knowing when to close is as important as knowing how.

## Should You Close This Wound?

### Close it if:

- Clean, sharp cut (knife, glass) less than 6–8 hours old
- Edges are smooth and can be brought together without tension
- Location is cosmetically important (face — close within 24 hours if possible)
- Wound is deep enough that it won't stay closed on its own
- You've irrigated it thoroughly

### Do NOT close it if:

- The wound is more than 6–8 hours old (24 hours for face wounds). Bacteria have already colonized — closing it traps them inside.
- Animal or human bite wound. Bite wounds have a very high infection rate. Clean, pack open, and let them heal from the bottom up.
- Crush injury with lots of dead tissue.
- Contaminated wound you can't clean adequately (dirt, debris embedded).
- Signs of infection already present (redness, pus, swelling, warmth).
- Puncture wounds — you can't see the bottom or clean the tract.
- Gunshot wounds — both entry and exit should be left open.

Wounds left open heal by "secondary intention" — from the bottom up, filling in with new tissue. It takes longer and scars more, but it's vastly safer than closing an infected wound.

> **WARNING:** When in doubt, leave it open. An ugly scar beats a dead patient from a sealed-in infection. You can always close a wound later (delayed primary closure) after 3–5 days if it stays clean. You can't un-close an infected wound without damage.

## Wound Preparation

Before closing by any method:

1. **Wash your hands.** Scrub with soap for 60 seconds. Wear gloves if available.
2. **Control bleeding.** Direct pressure for 10–15 minutes. You can't see what you're doing in a bleeding wound.
3. **Irrigate.** This is the single most important step in wound care. Force clean water through the wound under pressure. Use a syringe (any size, 20–60 mL is ideal) or a plastic bag/bottle with a small hole poked in it. Aim: at least 250 mL of clean water for a small wound, 500 mL–1 liter for larger wounds. The mechanical force of the water jet matters — a gentle pour doesn't dislodge bacteria.
4. **Remove debris.** Pick out any visible dirt, gravel, wood splinters, or dead tissue with clean tweezers or forceps. Anything left behind becomes a focus for infection.
5. **Trim ragged edges** if you have the tools and confidence. Clean edges close better and heal with less scarring.
6. **Numb the area** if you have local anesthetic. Lidocaine (1% or 2%) injected into the wound edges — inject directly into the tissue alongside the wound, not into the wound bed. Wait 5 minutes for full effect. Maximum adult dose: 4.5 mg/kg without epinephrine, 7 mg/kg with. For a 70 kg adult using 1% lidocaine (10 mg/mL), that's about 30 mL maximum.

## Closure Methods — Easiest to Hardest

### Butterfly Strips / Steri-Strips (Adhesive Strips)

Best for: shallow cuts with clean edges that hold together easily. Face cuts. Children. Anyone squeamish about needles.

1. Dry the skin on both sides of the wound thoroughly. Strips won't stick to wet or bloody skin. Applying tincture of benzoin (a sticky liquid) to the skin on both sides first dramatically improves adhesion.
2. Cut strips to length — they should extend at least ½ inch beyond the wound edge on each side.
3. Apply one end to one side of the wound, pull the wound edges together gently, then press the other end down on the opposite side.
4. Space strips about ¼ inch apart along the wound length.
5. Don't stretch the strips tight — just enough to hold the edges together.
6. Apply a reinforcing strip along each edge parallel to the wound to keep the perpendicular strips from peeling up.
7. Keep dry for 48 hours. After that, they can get briefly wet but pat dry immediately.
8. Leave in place for 7–10 days or until they fall off naturally.

**Improvised strips:** Duct tape cut into thin strips works. Regular adhesive tape works. Even superglue across the wound surface (not inside the wound — on the skin edges) holds clean cuts together effectively.

### Skin Staples

Best for: scalp wounds (the scalp bleeds heavily and is hard to suture in the field), long straight cuts on the torso or extremities. Fast to apply.

1. Hold the wound edges together with your fingers or forceps.
2. Position the stapler centered over the wound line.
3. Press firmly — the staple drives through both edges of skin simultaneously.
4. Space staples about ½ inch (1 cm) apart.
5. Check that the edges are everted (turned slightly outward) — flat or inverted edges heal with a worse scar and higher infection rate.
6. Removal: use a staple remover (or thin-nosed pliers in a pinch) after 7–10 days. Slide the lower jaw under the staple, squeeze to bend the staple open, lift out.

### Suturing (Stitches)

Best for: deep wounds, wounds under tension, wounds needing precise alignment (face), any wound where strips won't hold.

#### Materials

**Needle:** A curved cutting needle is ideal. Improvised: any curved sewing needle, safety pin bent into a curve, or a straight needle (harder to use but works). Sterilize with flame until red, then let cool, or soak in alcohol/iodine for 10 minutes.

**Thread:** Surgical suture is ideal. Improvised: fishing line (monofilament, 4–8 lb test is about the right thickness), dental floss (unwaxed is easier to tie), regular thread (doubles or triples for strength), thin nylon string. Sterilize by soaking in alcohol or iodine. Fishing line is actually excellent — it's monofilament, strong, and causes minimal tissue reaction.

**Needle driver/forceps:** A needle holder is ideal. Improvised: small pliers, hemostats, or even firm finger grip (harder to control).

#### Simple Interrupted Suture — The One Technique You Need

This is the most common and most forgiving suture pattern. Each stitch is independent — if one fails, the rest hold.

1. Grip the needle about 2/3 back from the tip in your needle driver.
2. Enter the skin about ¼ inch (5–7 mm) from the wound edge, perpendicular to the skin. Push through the skin in a curved arc following the needle's curve.
3. The needle should go deep enough to exit through the wound base, then re-enter the wound base on the opposite side and exit the skin ¼ inch from the opposite wound edge.
4. Equal depth and distance on both sides is important. Uneven stitches cause the edges to overlap instead of meeting flat.
5. Pull the suture through, leaving a 2-inch tail on the entry side.
6. Tie the knot: wrap the long end around the needle driver twice (instrument tie), grip the short tail with the driver, pull through. This makes the first throw. Lay it flat against the skin. Then wrap once in the opposite direction and pull through. Then one more wrap in the original direction. This gives you a square knot — flat and secure.
7. Cut the tails, leaving about ¼ inch.
8. Space stitches about ¼ inch (5–7 mm) apart.
9. Pull each stitch just tight enough to hold the edges together — not tight enough to blanch (whiten) the skin. Over-tight stitches strangle the tissue and cause worse scarring and higher infection risk.

#### Key Principles

- **Eversion:** The wound edges should be slightly rolled outward, not flat or tucked inward. This ensures proper healing. If edges are inverting, you need to go deeper with your bites.
- **Tension:** If the wound is under tension (muscles pulling it apart), place deep sutures first to take the load off the skin sutures. A deep stitch goes through the deep tissue layers (fascia, dermis) with an absorbable suture, bringing the deep layers together before you close the skin.
- **Layers:** Close deep wounds in layers. Deep tissue first, then superficial. Closing only the skin over a deep cavity leaves dead space where blood and fluid collect and bacteria breed.

## Wound Glue (Cyanoacrylate / Superglue)

Regular superglue (cyanoacrylate) works for wound closure. Medical-grade Dermabond is better (less tissue irritation), but hardware store superglue has been used successfully in the field for decades.

1. Wound must be clean, dry, and not bleeding.
2. Hold wound edges together.
3. Apply a thin line of glue across the SURFACE of the closed wound. Do NOT let glue get inside the wound — it causes tissue irritation and inflammation.
4. Hold edges together for 60 seconds while the glue sets.
5. Apply 2–3 layers, letting each dry.
6. The glue peels off on its own in 5–10 days.

Best for: small, clean, low-tension cuts. Face lacerations. Children's cuts (no needles).

Not suitable for: wounds under tension, deep wounds, joints (the glue cracks with movement), wet areas, hairy areas.

## Suture Removal

Timing depends on location:

- Face: 5 days
- Scalp: 7–10 days
- Arms and trunk: 7–10 days
- Legs: 10–14 days
- Over joints: 14 days
- Hands and feet: 10–14 days

To remove:
1. Clean the area with antiseptic.
2. Grab the knot with tweezers, lift gently.
3. Cut one side of the loop right at the skin surface.
4. Pull the suture out toward the wound (not away — pulling away can reopen the wound edge).
5. If a stitch is stuck, soak it with warm water for a few minutes first.

## After Closure — Care

1. Keep the wound clean and dry for 48 hours.
2. After 48 hours, gentle washing with soap and water once daily is fine.
3. Apply thin layer of antibiotic ointment if available.
4. Cover with a non-stick dressing for the first few days, then you can leave it open to air.
5. Watch for infection signs: increasing pain after 48 hours, expanding redness, swelling, drainage, fever.
6. If infection develops in a closed wound: remove every other stitch to allow drainage, irrigate, start antibiotics, and let it heal open.
