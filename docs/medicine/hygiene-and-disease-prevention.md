In any long-term survival situation, disease kills more people than injuries, violence, or starvation. Throughout history — every war, every disaster, every societal collapse — infectious disease has been the primary killer. Most of these diseases are preventable with basic hygiene. This isn't about comfort. It's about survival.

## Handwashing

This single practice prevents more disease than any other measure. The fecal-oral route (feces → hands → mouth) is how cholera, dysentery, typhoid, hepatitis A, and most diarrheal diseases spread.

**When to wash:**
- Before eating or preparing food
- After using the latrine
- After handling garbage, waste, or dead animals
- After caring for a sick person
- After changing diapers or cleaning a child
- After blowing your nose, coughing, or sneezing

**How:**
1. Wet hands with clean water.
2. Apply soap (any soap — antibacterial soap is not necessary and not better than regular soap).
3. Scrub all surfaces for at least 20 seconds: backs of hands, between fingers, under nails, wrists.
4. Rinse with clean water.
5. Air dry or use a clean cloth.

**No soap?** Wood ash mixed with water works as a soap substitute — the potassium carbonate (lye) in ash saponifies oils on the skin and kills bacteria. It's rough on the skin but effective. Sand rubbed on wet hands also provides mechanical cleaning.

**No running water?** A tippy-tap (a jug with a small hole hung from a stick, operated by foot pedal) creates a hands-free wash station. Build one near your latrine and cooking area.

## Water and Food Hygiene

- All drinking water must be treated if the source is uncertain. See purification-methods.md.
- Wash raw food with clean water before eating.
- Cook food thoroughly — heat kills most pathogens. Meat should be cooked until no pink remains inside.
- Don't let cooked food sit at room temperature for more than 2 hours (1 hour if above 90°F/32°C). Bacteria multiply rapidly in the "danger zone" of 40–140°F (4–60°C).
- Keep raw meat separate from ready-to-eat food. Use different cutting surfaces or wash thoroughly between uses.
- Store food in sealed containers to keep flies, rodents, and insects out. A single fly can carry millions of bacteria from a latrine to your food.
- Wash dishes with hot water and soap. Air dry rather than using a shared towel.

## Waste Management

Human waste is the single largest source of epidemic disease in disaster and survival settings. Deal with it properly or it will deal with you.

- Use a designated latrine, never the open ground near camp. See sanitation-and-latrines.md for construction details.
- Latrine should be at least 100 feet (30 meters) from any water source and downhill from camp.
- Cover feces with dirt, ash, or lime after each use. This reduces fly contact and smell.
- Wash hands after every latrine visit without exception.
- Dispose of garbage in a designated area away from living spaces. Burn what you can. Bury the rest.
- Menstrual waste, used bandages, and medical waste should be burned or buried deep.

## Personal Hygiene Without Running Water

When showers aren't available, you still need to clean yourself regularly. Body hygiene prevents skin infections, parasites, fungal infections, and camp diseases.

**Body washing:**
1. Heat a pot of water if possible. A warm washcloth bath uses very little water.
2. Focus on high-bacteria areas: armpits, groin, feet, skin folds, face, hands.
3. Dry thoroughly, especially between toes and in skin folds. Moisture breeds fungal infections.
4. If water is too scarce for washing, a dry cloth wipe-down of the key areas still helps.
5. Change underwear and socks as often as possible. If you can't change them, hang them in sunlight to air out — UV kills some bacteria.

**Dental hygiene:**
- Brush teeth twice daily. If no toothbrush, use a cloth wrapped around your finger with salt or baking soda as an abrasive.
- A twig chewed into a brush (miswak/chewing stick) works well — willow, oak, and neem are traditionally used.
- Rinse with salt water (1 teaspoon salt in a cup of water) daily.
- Dental disease leads to infection, which leads to sepsis. Dental hygiene is medical prevention.

**Hair:**
- Keep hair clean when possible. Dirty hair harbors lice.
- If lice become a problem, cutting hair short reduces the infestation surface dramatically.
- Washing hair with diluted vinegar (1 part vinegar to 2 parts water) helps remove nit (louse egg) adhesive from hair shafts.

**Nails:**
- Keep fingernails short. Long nails trap dirt and bacteria.
- Clean under nails when washing hands.

## Lice

Lice spread through close contact and shared bedding/clothing. There are three types: head, body, and pubic. Body lice are the most dangerous because they transmit typhus, relapsing fever, and trench fever — diseases that have killed millions in wartime and disaster settings.

**Signs:** Intense itching, visible small white or brown insects (2–3 mm), tiny white eggs (nits) attached to hair shafts or clothing seams.

**Treatment:**
1. **Body lice live in clothing, not on the body.** Wash or boil all clothing and bedding. If you can't wash, seal clothing in a plastic bag for 2 weeks (lice and nits die without a blood meal). Hot iron the seams where lice hide.
2. **Head lice:** Apply permethrin cream or lotion (5%) to dry hair, leave for 10 minutes, rinse. Repeat in 7–10 days to kill newly hatched nits. Comb with a fine-toothed nit comb daily. If no permethrin, soak hair in vinegar for 30 minutes (loosens nits), then comb thoroughly.
3. **Improvised treatment:** Coat hair in olive oil, petroleum jelly, or mayonnaise overnight (smothers the lice), cover with a bag or wrap, wash out in the morning, then comb. Repeat in 7 days.
4. Wash all bedding and hats in hot water (130°F/54°C+) or seal in plastic for 2 weeks.
5. Check all group members — lice spread fast in close quarters.

## Scabies

Caused by microscopic mites that burrow under the skin. Spread by prolonged skin-to-skin contact or shared bedding.

**Signs:** Intense itching (worse at night), small red bumps, thin grey lines on the skin (burrow tracks), commonly between fingers, on wrists, waistband area, elbows.

**Treatment:**
1. Permethrin cream (5%) applied to the entire body from the neck down. Leave on for 8–14 hours (overnight), then wash off. Repeat in 1 week.
2. All household/group members should be treated simultaneously, even if asymptomatic — scabies takes 2–6 weeks to show symptoms after initial infection, and people are contagious during this period.
3. Wash all clothing, bedding, and towels in hot water and dry on high heat. Items that can't be washed should be sealed in a plastic bag for 72 hours (scabies mites die without a host faster than lice).
4. Itching may continue for 2–4 weeks after successful treatment. This is the body's allergic reaction to dead mites and is normal. Antihistamines (diphenhydramine 25–50 mg) and hydrocortisone cream help.

## Fungal Infections

Athlete's foot, jock itch, and ringworm thrive in warm, moist conditions — exactly what you get when people are wearing the same boots and clothes for extended periods.

**Prevention:**
- Keep feet dry. Change socks daily if possible. Dry feet thoroughly after washing.
- Air out boots and shoes. Remove them whenever resting.
- Use foot powder (antifungal if available, plain cornstarch or baking soda as alternatives).
- Don't share towels, socks, or footwear.

**Treatment:**
- Clotrimazole or miconazole cream twice daily for 2–4 weeks. Continue 1 week after symptoms clear.
- If no antifungal cream: soak affected area in dilute vinegar (1 part vinegar to 4 parts water) for 15 minutes daily. Keep dry between treatments.
- For widespread or resistant cases: oral fluconazole 150 mg once weekly for 2–4 weeks if available.

## Menstrual Hygiene

Menstruation doesn't stop for emergencies. Lack of supplies is a real issue that affects health and dignity.

**Improvised supplies:**
- Cloth pads: cut absorbent fabric (cotton, flannel) into rectangles about 3 x 10 inches. Layer 4–6 pieces thick. Secure in underwear with safety pins. Wash with soap and water after use, dry in sunlight (UV helps sterilize). Rotate between sets.
- Rags: any clean, absorbent cloth folded and used as a pad. Wash and reuse.
- Natural materials: dry moss, cotton from seed pods, or soft plant fibers can be wrapped in cloth as an absorbent core.
- Menstrual cups (if available pre-disaster) are the ideal survival option — reusable for years, require only boiling water for sterilization between cycles.

**Hygiene:**
- Change pads every 4–6 hours to prevent bacterial growth.
- Wash the genital area with clean water at least twice daily during menstruation.
- Wash reusable pads thoroughly with soap and hot water. Dry fully in sunlight before reuse.
- Dispose of single-use products by burning or deep burial.

## Camp Disease Prevention

### Fly Control
Flies are disease vectors. They land on feces, then on food, carrying cholera, dysentery, and typhoid bacteria.

- Cover all food.
- Cover latrine holes when not in use.
- Bury or burn garbage.
- Improvised fly traps: a bottle with sugar water and a drop of dish soap (breaks surface tension — flies drown). Or a cone of paper inserted into a jar with bait — flies enter the cone but can't find the small hole to leave.

### Rodent Control
Rodents spread hantavirus, leptospirosis, plague, and other diseases.

- Store food in sealed containers — metal or thick plastic, not bags rodents can chew through.
- Keep living and cooking areas clean. No food scraps on the ground.
- Seal gaps in shelter walls larger than ¼ inch — mice can squeeze through very small openings.
- Traps are more effective and safer than poison in a camp setting (poison gets into unintended places).

### Mosquito Prevention
Mosquitoes transmit malaria, dengue, Zika, West Nile, and other diseases.

- Sleep under a mosquito net if available. Treat nets with permethrin for added effectiveness.
- Wear long sleeves and long pants at dawn and dusk (peak mosquito activity).
- Eliminate standing water near camp — any container, tire, or puddle is a breeding ground.
- Smoke from a fire helps repel mosquitoes.
- DEET-based repellent is the gold standard. In its absence, oil of lemon eucalyptus provides some protection. Mud applied to exposed skin provides a physical barrier.

## Epidemic Warning Signs

If multiple people in your group develop the same symptoms within a short period, you may be dealing with an outbreak. Act fast:

1. Isolate sick individuals from the healthy population.
2. Identify the common exposure — shared water source, shared food, insect vector, person-to-person contact.
3. Address the source — treat the water, destroy contaminated food, control the vector.
4. Increase hygiene standards for everyone.
5. Treat the sick (see specific disease articles).
6. Track cases — who, when, symptoms. This helps identify the source and whether your interventions are working.

The most common epidemic killers in disaster/survival settings are diarrheal diseases (cholera, dysentery), respiratory infections, typhus (louse-borne), and malaria. All are preventable with the practices described above.
