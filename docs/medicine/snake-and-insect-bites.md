Most snake bites and insect stings are painful but not lethal. The ones that are lethal give you time — usually hours, not minutes. Staying calm and doing the right things (and NOT doing the wrong things) makes the difference.

## SNAKE BITES

### Immediate Response — All Snake Bites

1. Move away from the snake. Don't try to catch or kill it — that's how second bites happen. If you can safely take a photo for identification, do it. If not, note the color, pattern, head shape, and size.
2. Keep the person calm and still. Panic increases heart rate and spreads venom faster.
3. Remove jewelry, watches, and tight clothing near the bite before swelling starts.
4. Position the bitten limb at or below heart level. Don't elevate it — that speeds venom absorption into central circulation.
5. Mark the edge of the swelling with a pen or marker and write the time. Check every 15 minutes and re-mark. Swelling that's rapidly advancing means significant envenomation.
6. Immobilize the limb with a splint if possible — treat it like a fracture. Less movement means slower venom spread.

### What NOT to Do

- **Do not cut the wound and suck out venom.** It doesn't work. It damages tissue and introduces mouth bacteria into the wound.
- **Do not apply a tourniquet.** Trapping all the venom in one limb concentrates it and causes severe tissue death. When the tourniquet is released, the bolus of venom hits the system all at once.
- **Do not apply ice.** Increases tissue damage.
- **Do not apply electric shock.** This myth persists but has zero evidence.
- **Do not give aspirin.** It thins the blood and worsens bleeding from venom that causes coagulopathy.

### Identifying Venomous vs. Non-Venomous Bites

Not all snakes are venomous. Roughly 20–25% of snake bites by venomous species are "dry bites" with no venom injected. Signs that venom was injected:

- Progressive swelling beyond the immediate bite area
- Severe, increasing pain
- Bruising or discoloration spreading from the bite
- Nausea, vomiting, dizziness
- Metallic taste in the mouth
- Tingling or numbness of the face or affected limb
- Difficulty breathing (neurotoxic venom)
- Bleeding from gums, nose, or wound that won't stop (hemotoxic venom)

If 4–6 hours pass with nothing beyond local pain and mild swelling at the bite itself, it was likely a dry bite or a non-venomous snake.

### North American Venomous Snakes

**Pit vipers (rattlesnakes, copperheads, cottonmouths/water moccasins):**
- Hemotoxic venom — destroys tissue and disrupts blood clotting
- Signs: immediate pain, rapid swelling, bruising, blistering around the bite over hours
- Rattlesnake bites are the most serious. Copperhead bites are rarely fatal to adults.
- Treatment: immobilize, get to antivenom if possible. Supportive care without antivenom: fluids, wound care, antibiotics for secondary infection, monitor for compartment syndrome.

**Coral snakes:**
- Neurotoxic venom — attacks the nervous system
- Signs may be delayed 2–12 hours. Then: ptosis (drooping eyelids), difficulty speaking and swallowing, double vision, respiratory paralysis.
- "Red touches yellow, kills a fellow. Red touches black, friend of Jack." This rhyme only works for North American species and has exceptions. When in doubt, treat as venomous.
- Treatment: pressure immobilization bandage (see below), be ready to do rescue breathing if respiratory paralysis occurs. Without antivenom, sustaining breathing for 24–48 hours may be enough for the venom to wear off.

### Pressure Immobilization Technique

Use this for neurotoxic snake bites (coral snakes, cobras, mambas, sea snakes, kraits) and funnel-web spider bites. Do NOT use for pit viper bites — it concentrates hemotoxic venom in the limb and causes worse tissue destruction.

1. Wrap the entire bitten limb (start at the bite site and wrap toward the body) with an elastic bandage, cloth strips, or even a torn shirt. Wrap as firmly as you would wrap a sprained ankle — tight enough to compress the lymphatic system but not so tight that you cut off blood flow.
2. You should still be able to feel a pulse at the wrist or ankle below the wrap. If fingertips or toes turn blue, it's too tight.
3. Splint the limb to prevent any movement.
4. Leave the wrapping on until you reach medical care. Removing it releases a bolus of venom.

### Long-Term Snake Bite Management Without Antivenom

If you're days from medical care:

1. Keep the person at rest. Bedrest for at least 24–48 hours.
2. Push oral fluids. Dehydration worsens venom effects.
3. Monitor for compartment syndrome: a swollen limb that becomes rock-hard, extremely painful, pale or blue, and numb. The pressure needs to be released surgically — without this, the limb dies.
4. Wound care: the bite will likely become infected. Clean twice daily with dilute iodine or clean water. Start antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate or cephalexin) at the first sign of infection.
5. Tissue around the bite may die and slough off. This is normal for pit viper bites. Keep the dead tissue clean and let it separate naturally — don't pull it off.
6. Monitor blood clotting: have the person spit into a clean container. Check if the saliva is bloody. A simple "clotting test" — prick a finger and see if the blood clots within 10 minutes. If bleeding won't stop from minor wounds, the venom has disrupted clotting.
7. Pain management: ibuprofen is fine for neurotoxic bites but use acetaminophen only for hemotoxic bites (ibuprofen can worsen bleeding with hemotoxins).

## SPIDER BITES

Most spider bites are harmless or cause only local irritation. Two North American species are medically significant:

### Black Widow

Shiny black spider with a red hourglass marking on the abdomen. Found in woodpiles, outhouses, sheds, under rocks.

- The bite itself may feel like a pinprick or go unnoticed
- Within 30–60 minutes: severe muscle pain and cramping, starting near the bite and spreading. Abdominal rigidity (can mimic appendicitis), chest tightness, back spasms
- Sweating, nausea, headache, elevated blood pressure
- Rarely fatal in healthy adults. Dangerous for children, elderly, and people with heart conditions.

**Treatment:**
1. Clean the bite. Ice for pain.
2. Pain management: this is the main treatment. Ibuprofen and acetaminophen. Muscle relaxants if available (diazepam 5–10 mg, cyclobenzaprine 10 mg).
3. Warm baths or warm compresses help with muscle spasms.
4. Monitor breathing and blood pressure for 24 hours.
5. Most symptoms resolve in 24–72 hours without antivenom.

### Brown Recluse

Brown spider with a violin-shaped marking on its back. Found in closets, attics, storage boxes, under furniture.

- Bite is initially painless or mildly stinging
- Over 2–8 hours: the bite area develops a red, white, and blue bulls-eye pattern (red outer ring, white middle ring, blue-grey center)
- Over days: the center may become necrotic — a dark, sunken ulcer that can spread to several inches across
- Systemic symptoms (rare but serious): fever, rash, nausea, joint pain, and in severe cases hemolytic anemia (venom destroys red blood cells — dark urine is a sign)

**Treatment:**
1. Clean the wound. Ice intermittently for the first 24 hours.
2. Elevate the bitten limb.
3. Do NOT cut out the wound, apply heat, or apply suction.
4. Ibuprofen 600 mg every 8 hours for pain and inflammation.
5. If the wound becomes an open ulcer, treat it as a wound: clean daily, apply honey or antibiotic ointment, cover with non-stick dressing. These ulcers heal slowly — weeks to months.
6. If dark urine appears (hemolysis), push fluids aggressively. This is a sign of systemic envenomation and can cause kidney failure.
7. Dapsone is the specific medical treatment for brown recluse necrosis but is rarely available outside a pharmacy.

## SCORPION STINGS

Most scorpion stings cause intense local pain and swelling but aren't dangerous. The exception in North America is the Arizona bark scorpion — small, yellowish-brown, found in the southwestern US and Mexico.

**Dangerous sting signs (bark scorpion or tropical species):**
- Intense pain at the sting site that radiates
- Numbness and tingling spreading from the sting
- Involuntary muscle twitching, restlessness
- Roving eye movements (a distinctive sign in children)
- Difficulty swallowing, excess saliva
- In severe cases: respiratory distress, muscle spasms, seizures

**Treatment:**
1. Clean the sting site. Ice for pain.
2. Pain management: ibuprofen, acetaminophen.
3. For dangerous species: keep the person calm and still. Watch breathing closely — respiratory failure is the primary risk.
4. Children are at much higher risk than adults from the same sting. Monitor children very closely for 4–6 hours.
5. Antihistamines (diphenhydramine/Benadryl 25–50 mg) help with itching and mild swelling but won't treat true envenomation.

## TICK BITES

Ticks don't cause immediate emergencies, but they transmit diseases that can be serious or fatal if untreated.

### Tick Removal

1. Use fine-tipped tweezers. Grasp the tick as close to the skin surface as possible.
2. Pull upward with steady, even pressure. Don't twist, jerk, or squeeze the body — this can push gut contents (and pathogens) into the bite.
3. If the mouthparts break off and remain in the skin, try to remove them with tweezers. If you can't, leave them — the body will push them out eventually.
4. Clean the bite with alcohol or soap and water.
5. Do NOT use petroleum jelly, nail polish, heat, or a blown-out match to "encourage" the tick to back out. These old methods cause the tick to regurgitate into the wound, increasing disease transmission.

### Tick-Borne Disease — What to Watch For

Monitor for 30 days after a tick bite:

- **Lyme disease:** Bull's-eye rash (expanding red ring with central clearing) appearing 3–30 days after the bite. Fever, headache, fatigue, joint pain. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 14–21 days.
- **Rocky Mountain spotted fever:** Fever, headache, rash starting on wrists and ankles spreading inward, appearing 2–14 days after bite. Can be fatal without treatment. Treatment: doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 7 days minimum.
- **Ehrlichiosis/Anaplasmosis:** Fever, headache, muscle aches, sometimes confusion. Treatment: doxycycline.

> **NOTE:** If you develop a fever within 30 days of removing a tick and you have doxycycline, start it. All the major tick-borne diseases in North America respond to doxycycline, and early treatment prevents serious complications. Don't wait for a rash — some of these diseases don't produce one.

## BEE AND WASP STINGS

Single stings are painful but harmless unless the person is allergic (see allergic-reactions.md for anaphylaxis treatment).

**For normal stings:**
1. Remove the stinger if present. Scrape it off sideways with a flat edge (credit card, knife blade) — don't squeeze it with tweezers, which pushes more venom in. Honeybees leave stingers; wasps, hornets, and yellow jackets don't.
2. Clean with soap and water.
3. Ice for pain and swelling — 10 minutes on, 10 off.
4. Antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25–50 mg) for itching and local swelling.
5. Watch for allergic reaction signs for 30 minutes: hives beyond the sting site, facial swelling, difficulty breathing, dizziness.

**Mass stinging events (30+ stings):**
Even without an allergy, 30–50+ stings can cause a toxic venom reaction that mimics anaphylaxis. Treat with antihistamines, fluids, and monitoring. Watch for dark urine (venom hemolysis) and start pushing fluids.
