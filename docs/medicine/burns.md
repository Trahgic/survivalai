Burns kill in two ways: infection and fluid loss. A bad burn is an open door for bacteria, and your body dumps plasma through damaged skin faster than you'd expect. Most burns you'll deal with are minor and treatable. The ones that aren't — you need to recognize them fast and understand your limits.

## How Burns Are Classified

Burns are graded by depth, not by how much they hurt. In fact, the worst burns often hurt *less* because the nerves are destroyed.

**First degree (superficial):** Only the outer skin layer is damaged. Red, dry, painful, no blisters. Think bad sunburn. Heals in 3–7 days without scarring.

**Second degree (partial thickness):** Goes into the second skin layer. Wet-looking, blistered, very painful, pink or red underneath. Heals in 2–3 weeks if kept clean. Deep second-degree burns can take longer and may scar.

**Third degree (full thickness):** Destroys all skin layers. White, brown, or charred. Leathery texture. Little or no pain at the burn site because nerves are gone — but the edges hurt like hell. Will not heal on its own without skin grafting. High infection risk.

**Fourth degree:** Burns into muscle, tendon, or bone. Charred, black. These are catastrophic injuries. Without a hospital, survival depends on the size and location.

## Estimating Burn Size

You need to know how much of the body is burned to decide how aggressive your treatment needs to be. Use the **Rule of Nines** for adults:

- Head and neck: 9%
- Each arm: 9%
- Chest (front): 9%
- Abdomen (front): 9%
- Upper back: 9%
- Lower back: 9%
- Each upper leg (front and back): 9%
- Each lower leg (front and back): 9%
- Groin: 1%

For smaller or irregular burns, the person's palm (fingers together) equals roughly 1% of their body surface.

For children under 10, the head is proportionally larger — count it as 18% and subtract from the legs.

> **CRITICAL:** Any second-degree burn covering more than 10% of an adult's body (or 5% of a child's) is a life-threatening emergency. Fluid loss alone can kill within hours. If you have IV supplies and know how to use them, start fluids immediately. If not, push oral rehydration hard — small sips constantly.

## Thermal Burns — Treatment

This covers burns from fire, hot liquids, steam, and hot surfaces.

### Immediate Response

1. Remove the person from the heat source. If clothing is on fire: stop, drop, roll. If clothing is stuck to the burn, leave it — cutting around it is fine, but don't pull fabric off burned skin.
2. Cool the burn with clean, lukewarm water (not ice cold) for 10–20 minutes. Pour it over the burn or soak the area. Do this within the first 30 minutes for the best effect.
3. Remove rings, watches, belts, and tight clothing near the burn before swelling starts. Once it swells, you won't get them off without cutting.
4. Do not apply ice directly. It causes frostbite on damaged skin and worsens tissue death.
5. Do not apply butter, oil, toothpaste, egg whites, or any folk remedy. These trap heat and breed bacteria.

> **WARNING:** Never cool large burns (over 20% body surface) with water for extended periods. You'll cause hypothermia. Cool the burn, cover it, then focus on keeping the person warm overall.

### First-Degree Burns

1. Cool with water as above.
2. Apply pure aloe vera gel if available, or a thin layer of honey. Both reduce pain and have mild antibacterial properties.
3. Take ibuprofen (400–600 mg for adults) for pain and swelling. Acetaminophen works for pain but won't reduce inflammation.
4. No bandage needed unless clothing rubs the area. If you cover it, use a non-stick dressing.
5. Heals on its own. Watch for signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, pus) but these are rare with first-degree burns.

### Second-Degree Burns

1. Cool with water for 10–20 minutes.
2. Do not pop blisters. They're sterile fluid protecting the wound underneath. If a blister breaks on its own, leave the loose skin as a natural bandage — don't peel it off.
3. If a blister is larger than 2 inches across and tense (looks like it's about to burst), you can drain it with a sterilized needle at the base. Poke two small holes at the lowest point, press gently to drain, then leave the skin intact.
4. Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment (bacitracin or silver sulfadiazine if you have it) or raw honey as an alternative.
5. Cover with a non-stick dressing (Telfa pads, or plastic wrap in a pinch). Wrap loosely with gauze or a clean cloth. Don't wrap tight — burns swell.
6. Change dressings once daily. Wash the burn gently with clean water each time. Pat dry, reapply ointment, re-dress.
7. Pain management: ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 6–8 hours, or acetaminophen 500–1000 mg every 6 hours. Don't exceed 3200 mg ibuprofen or 3000 mg acetaminophen in 24 hours.
8. Watch for infection daily: increasing pain after day 2, spreading redness, red streaks, pus, fever, or foul smell all mean infection.

> **WARNING:** Second-degree burns on the face, hands, feet, groin, or over joints need extra attention. Burns over joints can contract and limit movement as they heal — gently stretch and move the joint several times daily to prevent this.

### Third-Degree Burns

You cannot fix third-degree burns in the field. You can keep the person alive.

1. Do not attempt to cool large third-degree burns with water — hypothermia is a bigger immediate risk.
2. Cover the burn with clean, dry, non-stick material. Plastic wrap (like kitchen cling film) works well — it's sterile off the roll, doesn't stick, and lets you see the wound. Wrap loosely in one layer.
3. Elevate burned limbs above the heart to reduce swelling.
4. Push fluids aggressively. An adult with significant burns needs 2–4 liters in the first 8 hours. Oral rehydration solution is ideal (1 liter water + 6 teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt). Small, frequent sips — gulping causes vomiting.
5. Monitor for shock: rapid pulse, pale skin, confusion, rapid breathing. Lay the person flat, elevate legs if no spinal injury suspected, keep warm.
6. Pain will be severe at the edges where second-degree burns border the third-degree area. Treat aggressively with whatever you have.
7. Do not remove dead skin or attempt debridement unless you have training. You'll cause bleeding and introduce infection.
8. These wounds will become infected. That's almost guaranteed without a sterile hospital environment. If you have oral antibiotics (ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily, or cephalexin 500 mg four times daily), start them at the first sign of infection — redness, swelling, fever, pus, or foul smell.

## Chemical Burns

Caused by acids, alkalis (bases), solvents, or industrial chemicals. Alkali burns (drain cleaner, oven cleaner, wet cement, ammonia) are generally worse than acid burns because they keep penetrating deeper.

1. Remove contaminated clothing immediately. Use gloves or a barrier if you can — don't become a second patient.
2. Flush with massive amounts of clean water for at least 20 minutes. This is the single most important step. Use a hose, bucket, shower — whatever moves the most water. Longer is better. 30–45 minutes for alkali burns.
3. Do not try to neutralize the chemical (no vinegar on alkali burns, no baking soda on acid burns). The neutralization reaction produces heat and makes things worse.
4. After flushing, treat the wound like a thermal burn of the same depth.
5. If the chemical got in the eyes: flush with clean water for at least 20 minutes. Hold the eyelids open. Pour water from the nose side outward so contaminated water doesn't flow into the other eye. This is extremely painful and the person will fight you. Do it anyway.

> **WARNING:** Dry lime powder and eleite calcium compounds should be brushed off DRY before flushing with water. Water activates these chemicals and makes the burn worse. Brush off as much as you can first, then flush.

## Electrical Burns

Electricity causes three types of injury: the entry and exit burns on the skin, internal tissue damage along the current's path, and cardiac arrhythmia (irregular heartbeat).

1. Do not touch the person until you're sure the power source is disconnected. Use a dry wooden stick, rope, or rubber-soled shoes to separate them from the source if needed.
2. Check for breathing and pulse. Electrical injury can stop the heart. Start CPR immediately if needed — see cpr-and-choking.md.
3. Look for two burns: the entry point (usually the hand) and the exit point (usually the foot or wherever the body was grounded). The exit wound is often worse.
4. The skin burns may look minor, but electricity travels through the body and cooks tissue along the way — muscles, nerves, blood vessels. Internal damage is almost always worse than what you see on the surface.
5. Treat visible burns like thermal burns.
6. Monitor urine color. Dark brown or red urine after electrical injury means muscle breakdown is dumping proteins into the kidneys (rhabdomyolysis). Push fluids hard — 2–3 liters over the next few hours. This can cause kidney failure.
7. Watch for compartment syndrome: a limb that becomes increasingly painful, tight, swollen, and numb in the hours after the injury. Skin may look shiny and taut. This is an emergency that requires surgical intervention to release pressure.

> **WARNING:** Lightning strike survivors often appear dead but are recoverable. Always attempt CPR on a lightning victim — they have better outcomes than you'd expect. Prioritize them over other injuries if multiple people are struck.

## Burn Infection — Recognition and Treatment

Without a hospital, burn infection is the primary killer. Every burn patient needs daily wound checks.

**Signs of local infection:**
- Increasing pain after the first 48 hours (should be decreasing, not increasing)
- Expanding redness beyond the burn edges
- Swelling that's getting worse, not better
- Pus — yellow, green, or brown drainage
- Foul smell
- Wound changing color — especially turning dark or developing black spots

**Signs of systemic infection (sepsis):**
- Fever above 101°F / 38.3°C or below 96.8°F / 36°C
- Heart rate above 90 beats per minute at rest
- Rapid breathing
- Confusion, disorientation
- The person just looks and acts sick in a way that's getting worse

**Antibiotic options if available:**
- Ciprofloxacin: 500 mg by mouth every 12 hours
- Cephalexin: 500 mg by mouth every 6 hours
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate: 875 mg by mouth every 12 hours
- Topical: silver sulfadiazine cream (standard burn antibiotic), mupirocin, or raw honey

Start antibiotics at the first sign of infection. Don't wait to see if it gets better on its own.

## Pain Management

Burns are among the most painful injuries. Undertreating pain isn't toughness — it causes shock, prevents sleep, and slows healing.

- **Mild (first degree, small second degree):** Ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 6–8 hours. Alternate with acetaminophen 500–1000 mg every 6 hours if needed.
- **Moderate (larger second degree):** Same as above, plus cool compresses. If you have tramadol (50–100 mg every 6 hours), add it.
- **Severe (large second degree, third degree):** You need opioids if you have them. Codeine, hydrocodone, or whatever you've got. Dressing changes on large burns without real pain medication are brutal — if you have any sedation options, use them before changes.
- **Improvised options:** Willow bark tea contains a compound related to aspirin. Steep a handful of inner bark shavings in hot water for 15 minutes. It's mild but better than nothing.

## When a Burn Is Beyond Field Treatment

Get to higher-level care if possible when you see any of these:

- Third-degree burns of any significant size
- Burns covering more than 15% of body surface (second degree or worse)
- Burns to the face, airway, hands, feet, or genitals
- Electrical burns (internal damage can't be assessed in the field)
- Chemical burns to the eyes
- Burns in children under 5 or adults over 60
- The person inhaled smoke or hot gases — watch for hoarseness, coughing, black soot in the nose or mouth, difficulty breathing. Airway burns swell and can close the airway hours after the initial injury
- Signs of sepsis
- Burns that circle entirely around a limb or the chest (circumferential burns) — swelling inside the burned skin can cut off blood flow or restrict breathing

> **CRITICAL:** If someone has burns around their face and neck, and you notice their voice getting hoarse or they're struggling to breathe — the airway is swelling shut. This can happen 2–12 hours after the burn. There is no field treatment for a closed airway from burn swelling. This is one of the most time-critical reasons to get to medical care immediately if any option exists.
