Shock isn't an emotional reaction — it's a medical condition where the body's organs aren't getting enough blood. It kills slowly and quietly. A person can be talking to you and dying of shock at the same time. If you treat the injury but miss the shock, they die anyway.

## What's Actually Happening

The circulatory system needs three things working: a pump (the heart), fluid (blood), and pipes (blood vessels). If any one of those fails badly enough, blood pressure drops, organs stop getting oxygen, and the body starts shutting down from the outside in. Fingers and toes go first. Then kidneys. Then the brain. Then the heart.

## Types of Shock

**Hypovolemic (most common in survival situations):** Not enough fluid in the system. Caused by bleeding (internal or external), severe burns, vomiting, diarrhea, or dehydration. Fix: replace fluid, stop the loss.

**Anaphylactic:** Massive allergic reaction causes blood vessels to dilate and airways to swell shut. Caused by bee stings, food allergies, medications, latex. Fix: epinephrine, then airway management. See allergic-reactions.md.

**Septic:** Overwhelming infection causes the blood vessels to dilate and leak. Usually develops over hours to days in untreated infections. Fever, rapid pulse, confusion. Fix: antibiotics and fluids. See infection-and-antibiotics.md.

**Cardiogenic:** The heart itself is failing — heart attack, severe arrhythmia, or chest trauma. Fix: this is beyond field treatment in most cases. Keep the person sitting up (not lying flat — fluid backs up into the lungs), keep them calm, and get to medical care.

**Neurogenic:** Spinal cord injury causes the nervous system to lose control of blood vessel tone. Blood vessels dilate, pressure drops. Unlike other shock types, the person may have a slow pulse instead of fast, and warm/dry skin instead of cold/clammy. Fix: lay flat, keep warm, fluids.

## Recognizing Shock Early

The early signs are subtle. By the time the obvious signs appear, the person is already in serious trouble. Look for the progression:

### Early (Compensated) — the body is still coping

- Anxiety, restlessness — the person just seems "off" or agitated
- Mildly increased heart rate (above 100 beats per minute at rest)
- Skin is normal or slightly pale
- Normal blood pressure (the body compensates hard at this stage)
- Slight thirst

### Progressive — the body is losing the fight

- Heart rate above 120 at rest
- Pale, cool, clammy skin — especially the hands and feet
- Noticeable thirst
- Nausea or vomiting
- Faster breathing
- Delayed capillary refill — press a fingernail white, release, takes more than 2 seconds to turn pink
- Decreased urine output (if you're tracking it)
- The person seems confused or disoriented

### Late (Decompensated) — organs are failing

- Heart rate very rapid (above 140) or becoming irregular
- Skin is grey, mottled, or bluish
- Blood pressure crashes (you can feel this — the radial pulse at the wrist disappears while the carotid pulse at the neck remains)
- Rapid, shallow breathing or gasping
- Severe confusion, loss of consciousness
- No urine output
- Body temperature drops

> **CRITICAL:** If you can feel a pulse at the wrist, systolic blood pressure is at least roughly 80 mmHg. If you can only feel it at the neck (carotid), it's around 60. If you can't feel a pulse anywhere, they're in cardiac arrest. This is the fastest blood pressure check you have without equipment.

## Treatment

The treatment depends on the type, but these basics apply to almost all shock:

### Position

1. Lay the person flat on their back.
2. Elevate the legs 8–12 inches (about a foot). This sends blood from the legs back to the core and brain. Use a pack, rolled blanket, log — whatever's available.
3. **Exceptions:** If chest injury or difficulty breathing — prop them up at 30–45 degrees. If head injury — keep flat, don't elevate legs. If spinal injury suspected — don't move them. If vomiting and no spinal injury — recovery position.

### Temperature

1. Hypothermia makes shock worse and shock makes hypothermia worse. It's a death spiral.
2. Insulate from the ground — a sleeping pad, blanket, pile of leaves, anything between them and cold ground.
3. Cover them. Sleeping bag, blankets, coats, space blanket — layer everything you've got.
4. Don't actively heat them (no hot water bottles directly on skin). Warm the air around them.

### Fluids

1. If the person is conscious and can swallow, start oral fluids immediately.
2. Oral rehydration solution is best: 1 liter clean water + 6 teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt. If you don't have that, any clean water is fine. Warm fluids are better than cold.
3. Small, frequent sips — not gulps. Gulping causes vomiting, which makes everything worse.
4. Target intake: if they can tolerate it, push for 200–300 mL (about a cup) every 15–20 minutes.
5. If they have abdominal injuries or will need surgery, don't give fluids by mouth — only wet their lips. Fluid in the stomach complicates surgery and can leak into the abdominal cavity through a perforated gut.
6. IV fluids are better than oral if you have supplies and training. Normal saline or lactated Ringer's, wide open for the first liter, then reassess.

> **WARNING:** For cardiogenic shock (heart failure), do NOT push fluids aggressively. The heart can't handle the extra volume. Give just enough to keep the lips moist. Sit the person up, don't lay them flat. Fluid will back up into the lungs and drown them.

### Stop the Cause

Shock is always caused by something else. Treat the cause:

- **Bleeding:** Apply direct pressure, tourniquet if needed. See wound-care.md.
- **Burns:** Cover, prevent heat loss, push fluids. See burns.md.
- **Dehydration:** Oral rehydration aggressively.
- **Allergic reaction:** Epinephrine. See allergic-reactions.md.
- **Infection:** Antibiotics. See infection-and-antibiotics.md.
- **Fracture with internal bleeding:** Splint, traction for femur. See fractures-and-splinting.md.

### What Not to Do

- Do not give food — the gut shuts down in shock and food will just sit there or come back up.
- Do not give alcohol — it dilates blood vessels and drops pressure further.
- Do not let the person sit up or walk unless it's cardiogenic shock or a chest injury.
- Do not remove impaled objects — they may be plugging a hole in a vessel.
- Do not apply heat sources directly to skin — they cause burns because circulation is poor and the body can't regulate temperature normally.

## Monitoring

Once you've started treatment, track changes over time. Improvement or deterioration tells you whether your treatment is working.

Check every 15 minutes:
- Pulse rate and strength (getting faster = getting worse)
- Skin color and temperature (warming up and pinking = improving)
- Mental status — are they more alert or less? Can they answer questions?
- Capillary refill at fingernails (under 2 seconds = improving)
- Urine output if you can track it (any urine = kidneys are still working)
- Breathing rate and effort

Write it down if you can. It's easy to miss gradual changes. Trends matter more than single readings.

## When It's Beyond Field Treatment

All shock is serious, and all shock patients need real medical care if available. But these situations are especially critical:

- Hypovolemic shock from internal bleeding you can't control (abdominal injuries, pelvic fractures, femur fractures)
- Cardiogenic shock (heart attack, severe chest trauma)
- Anaphylaxis that doesn't respond to epinephrine
- Septic shock (the person needs IV antibiotics and pressors — drugs that raise blood pressure)
- Any shock patient who's deteriorating despite your treatment
- Altered consciousness that's getting worse

Time is the enemy. Every minute in shock does cumulative organ damage. Even if the person stabilizes, prolonged shock causes kidney failure, liver damage, and multi-organ dysfunction that plays out over days.
