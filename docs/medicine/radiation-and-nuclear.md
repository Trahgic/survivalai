A nuclear event — whether a bomb, reactor meltdown, or dirty bomb — is survivable if you understand the basics. The immediate blast kills people nearby, but radiation and fallout are what threaten everyone else, and those threats follow predictable rules you can work with.

## Types of Nuclear/Radiological Events

**Nuclear detonation:** An actual nuclear weapon. Produces blast wave, thermal radiation (heat/light), initial nuclear radiation, and radioactive fallout. Everything within the fireball is gone. Beyond that, survival is a function of distance and shielding.

**Reactor accident (Chernobyl/Fukushima type):** Release of radioactive material over hours to days. No blast wave. The threat is contamination of air, water, soil, and food over a wide area.

**Dirty bomb (radiological dispersal device):** Conventional explosive wrapped with radioactive material. The bomb itself is the main killer — the radiation component is usually a long-term contamination problem more than an acute radiation threat.

## Immediate Response — First 15 Minutes

If you see a flash brighter than the sun or hear about a nuclear event:

1. **Get inside immediately.** You have roughly 15 minutes before fallout begins to arrive. The best shelter is a large concrete or brick building — the more mass between you and the outside, the better. Go to the center of the building, away from windows and exterior walls. Basement is ideal.
2. **If you're in a car:** Get to a building. A car provides almost no radiation protection.
3. **If you're outside and can't reach a building:** Any cover is better than none. A ditch, culvert, tunnel, or even lying flat behind a thick wall reduces exposure. Cover your mouth and nose with cloth.
4. **Stay inside for at least 24 hours.** Radiation levels from fallout drop dramatically — by about 80% in the first 24 hours and 99% within 48 hours. The temptation to flee immediately is strong but often deadly. Sheltering for 24 hours then moving is usually better than running through active fallout.

## Fallout

Fallout is radioactive dust and debris that rises into the atmosphere after a nuclear detonation and falls back to earth over hours to days. It looks like sand, ash, or grit. It settles on everything — roofs, ground, water, food, people.

**Protection is simple physics:** mass blocks radiation. The more dense material between you and fallout particles, the lower your dose.

**Protection Factor (PF) by shelter type:**
- Open air: PF 1 (no protection)
- Wooden house: PF 2–3 (reduces dose by 50–67%)
- Basement of wood house: PF 10 (90% reduction)
- Concrete/brick building, interior rooms: PF 10–20
- Basement of concrete building: PF 40–100
- 3+ feet of packed earth: PF 200+
- Purpose-built shelter with 3+ feet of concrete or earth: PF 1000+

### Improvised Fallout Shelter

If you have any warning time:

1. Go to the most interior room of the most solid building available. Basement is best.
2. Block all windows and openings with whatever dense material is available: dirt-filled bags, sandbags, bricks, books, water jugs. Water is a good radiation shield — even filling bathtubs and placing water containers between you and exterior walls helps.
3. Seal gaps around doors and windows with wet towels, duct tape, or plastic sheeting. You're trying to keep fallout dust from drifting in, not achieve a perfect seal.
4. If you're in a basement, pile earth, sandbags, or heavy objects on the floor above you for overhead protection.
5. Bring inside: water (as much as possible), food, a radio (for information), medications, sanitation supplies (bucket with lid and bags for a toilet), bedding.

### The 7-10 Rule

Radiation from fallout decays predictably. For every 7-fold increase in time after detonation, radiation intensity drops by a factor of 10.

- 1 hour after: 1000 units (hypothetical baseline)
- 7 hours after: 100 units
- 49 hours (~2 days): 10 units
- 343 hours (~2 weeks): 1 unit

This means that after 48 hours, it's roughly 100 times safer to go outside than it was 1 hour after the event. After 2 weeks, the acute fallout hazard is largely over, though residual contamination remains.

## Decontamination

If you were outside during fallout:

1. **Remove clothing as soon as you reach shelter.** Your outer clothing carries most of the contamination. Strip at the entrance, bag the clothes, and seal the bag. Place it as far from living areas as possible.
2. **Shower or wash with soap and water.** Scrub all exposed skin thoroughly. Shampoo hair — do not use conditioner (it binds radioactive particles to hair). If no shower, scrub with wet cloths. Pay attention to skin folds, ears, under nails, and around the mouth and nose.
3. **Blow your nose and wipe out your ears** with clean, damp cloths.
4. **If water is scarce:** dry brushing and removal of outer clothing still eliminates 80–90% of external contamination.
5. **Avoid using contaminated water for washing** — this just moves contamination around. Use stored clean water.

> **CRITICAL:** Decontamination is about removing particles from your body, not about radiation damage already received. Particles on your skin continue to irradiate you as long as they're in contact. Getting them off ASAP reduces your total dose significantly.

## Radiation Sickness (Acute Radiation Syndrome)

Radiation sickness occurs when the whole body receives a large dose of penetrating radiation over a short period. It's measured in Gray (Gy) or rad (100 rad = 1 Gy).

### Stages by Dose

**Below 1 Gy (100 rad):** Mild nausea possible. Most people show no symptoms. Full recovery expected. Blood counts may dip temporarily.

**1–2 Gy (100–200 rad):** Nausea and vomiting within hours. Fatigue. Reduced blood counts over the following weeks (increased infection risk). Most survive with supportive care.

**2–6 Gy (200–600 rad):** Severe nausea and vomiting within 1–2 hours. Diarrhea. The person may feel better for a period (latent phase, lasting days to weeks) before deteriorating as bone marrow fails. Without medical care, mortality ranges from 20% (2 Gy) to 95% (6 Gy). With supportive care (fluids, antibiotics, blood products), survival improves significantly.

**6–10 Gy (600–1000 rad):** Nausea and vomiting within minutes. Rapid deterioration. Bone marrow failure. GI tract destruction. Survival is unlikely without advanced medical intervention (bone marrow transplant).

**Above 10 Gy (1000 rad):** Fatal. Cardiovascular and neurological failure within hours to days.

### The Vomiting Rule of Thumb

How quickly someone vomits after exposure gives a rough estimate of dose:
- Vomiting after 4+ hours: mild dose, likely survivable
- Vomiting within 1–4 hours: moderate dose, serious but survivable with care
- Vomiting within 30 minutes: severe dose, poor prognosis
- Vomiting within 10 minutes: lethal dose almost certain

### Treatment

There is no cure for radiation sickness. Treatment is supportive — keeping the person alive while their body either recovers or doesn't.

1. **Hydration.** Vomiting and diarrhea cause rapid fluid loss. ORS (see dehydration-and-rehydration.md) or IV fluids.
2. **Anti-nausea medication.** Ondansetron 8 mg every 8 hours if available.
3. **Infection prevention.** As bone marrow fails (1–3 weeks after exposure), the immune system collapses. White blood cell count drops. Even minor infections become lethal. Isolate the patient if possible. Use antibiotics at the first sign of infection (fever in a radiation patient = start broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately — ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily plus amoxicillin 500 mg three times daily).
4. **Bleeding precautions.** Platelet counts drop, making bleeding easy and hard to stop. Avoid cuts and injuries. Apply pressure to any wound for extended periods.
5. **Nutrition.** The GI tract is damaged — small, bland, frequent meals. Avoid raw foods (infection risk with no immune system).
6. **Rest.** Complete rest conserves energy for recovery.

## Potassium Iodide (KI)

KI protects the thyroid gland from radioactive iodine (I-131), which is one of the most significant fallout components. The thyroid naturally absorbs iodine — if you saturate it with non-radioactive iodine first, it won't absorb the radioactive kind.

**KI only protects the thyroid.** It does nothing for the rest of the body. It is not a general radiation treatment.

**Dosing:**
- Adults (18+): 130 mg once daily
- Children (3–18): 65 mg once daily
- Infants (1 month–3 years): 32 mg once daily
- Newborns (under 1 month): 16 mg once daily

**When to take:** As soon as possible after learning of a release of radioactive iodine. Ideally before exposure but still effective up to 3–4 hours after. Continue daily for as long as the threat of radioactive iodine exposure continues (typically 10–14 days after a reactor accident).

**If no KI is available:** Strong betadine (povidone-iodine) solution painted on the skin of the abdomen or forearm (8 mL for adults) provides some thyroid iodine saturation through skin absorption. This is a last resort, not a first choice.

## Water and Food Safety After Fallout

**Water:**
- Sealed, stored water is safe.
- Running water from taps connected to a covered reservoir is usually safe — the volume dilutes contamination.
- Open water sources (streams, ponds, rain barrels without covers) are contaminated. The water itself can be filtered and treated, but radioactive particles need to be removed first. Run through multiple layers of fabric or filter to remove particulates, then boil or treat normally.
- Well water from deep wells (50+ feet) is usually safe since soil filters out particles.

**Food:**
- Any food that was sealed (canned, packaged) before the event is safe.
- Food in a closed building is usually safe — wash containers before opening.
- Outdoor crops and gardens are contaminated. Root vegetables are safest after thorough washing and peeling. Leafy greens absorb the most contamination — discard them.
- Animal products: animals that ate contaminated feed or drank contaminated water will have radioactive material in their milk and meat. Wait 2+ weeks and feed them clean food/water before consuming their products if possible.
- Don't eat or drink anything you find outdoors for at least 48 hours after fallout stops.

## Long-Term Contamination

After the acute fallout period (2 weeks), the remaining risk is from residual contamination in the environment.

- Remove and dispose of the top 2–4 inches of soil around your shelter — this is where most fallout particles settle.
- Wash down exterior surfaces with water from a clean source if possible.
- Monitor food and water sources. Without a Geiger counter, the best approach is conservative: prefer stored/sealed food, well water, and animal products from animals fed clean feed.
- Avoid dust. Wear a mask or cloth over the nose and mouth when working in potentially contaminated outdoor areas. Inhaled particles are far more dangerous than external exposure.
- Track health: unexplained bruising, recurring infections, fatigue, hair loss, bleeding gums — these can indicate ongoing radiation exposure or delayed effects.
