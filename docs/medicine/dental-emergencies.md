Dental problems aren't usually lethal on their own, but an untreated tooth abscess can spread to the jaw, throat, or bloodstream and kill you. Beyond that, severe dental pain is completely debilitating — you can't eat, sleep, think, or function. In a survival situation, a bad tooth can take you out of commission as effectively as a broken leg.

## Toothache — Assessment

The cause matters because the treatment is different.

**Cavity (dental caries):** Pain when eating sweet, hot, or cold foods. Usually sharp, brief, and located to one tooth. The tooth may have a visible dark spot or hole.

**Cracked tooth:** Sharp pain when biting or releasing a bite. Sensitivity to hot and cold. May not be visible.

**Pulpitis (inflamed nerve):** Throbbing pain that comes on spontaneously, worse at night, often hard to pin to one tooth. Hot makes it worse, cold sometimes relieves it (reversible pulpitis) or nothing helps (irreversible pulpitis).

**Abscess:** Constant, throbbing pain. The tooth hurts to tap on. Swelling of the gum, face, or jaw. Possible fever. A pimple-like bump on the gum near the tooth root means the abscess is draining — that's actually a safer situation than a closed abscess building pressure.

**Gum disease (periodontal):** Dull ache, bleeding gums, teeth feel loose. Usually more gradual onset.

## Pain Management

Start here regardless of the cause:

1. **Ibuprofen 400–600 mg every 6–8 hours.** Best drug for dental pain — it's anti-inflammatory and works directly on the swelling that causes most tooth pain. Alternate with acetaminophen 500–1000 mg for continuous coverage.
2. **Clove oil (eugenol)** is the single best field dental remedy. Soak a small cotton ball or piece of cloth in clove oil and pack it against or into the painful tooth. It numbs the area and has antibacterial properties. If you don't have clove oil, whole cloves crushed and packed against the tooth also work.
3. **Salt water rinse.** Dissolve 1 teaspoon of salt in 8 ounces of warm water. Swish and hold over the painful area for 30 seconds. Repeat several times a day. Draws fluid out of swollen tissue and creates a hostile environment for bacteria.
4. **Cold compress** on the outside of the cheek — 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off. Reduces swelling and numbs.
5. **Elevate the head** when lying down. Lying flat increases blood pressure to the head and worsens throbbing dental pain. Sleep propped up.
6. **Avoid hot food and drinks** — heat increases inflammation and pain.

## Temporary Filling

If a tooth has a hole from a lost filling or cavity and it's causing pain from food getting packed in or air hitting the exposed nerve:

1. Clean out the hole as best you can. Rinse with salt water, use a toothpick to remove debris.
2. **Commercial temporary filling material** (Dentemp, Cavit) is available at pharmacies and is worth keeping in a medical kit. Pack it into the hole, bite down to shape it, and it sets in minutes. Lasts days to weeks.
3. **Improvised options:**
   - Softened candle wax or beeswax packed into the hole
   - Sugarless chewing gum (sugar-containing gum feeds bacteria)
   - A small piece of cotton soaked in clove oil wedged into the cavity
   - Zinc oxide powder mixed with a drop of clove oil to make a paste — this is actually close to what dentists use for temporary fillings
4. Any temporary filling is just buying time. It's not fixing the problem — it's keeping debris out and protecting the nerve.

## Dental Abscess — Drainage

A dental abscess that's causing facial swelling and fever needs treatment. Antibiotics help, but draining the pus is essential.

### If the Abscess Is Pointing (Visible Swelling on the Gum)

1. Rinse mouth with salt water.
2. Sterilize a sharp, pointed instrument — needle, safety pin, or the tip of a knife — with flame or alcohol.
3. Pierce the most swollen, prominent point of the abscess. Push through the surface and you'll feel it give way as you enter the pus pocket.
4. Press gently around the abscess to express pus. It will taste horrible — have the person spit and rinse repeatedly.
5. Rinse with warm salt water multiple times.
6. The relief is often dramatic and immediate as pressure releases.
7. Have the person rinse with salt water every 2–3 hours for the next few days to keep the drainage opening clear.

### Antibiotics for Dental Infection

Start antibiotics if there's facial swelling, fever, or the infection is spreading.

- **First choice:** Amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours for 7–10 days
- **Penicillin allergy:** Clindamycin 300 mg every 6 hours, or metronidazole 500 mg every 8 hours
- **Severe infection with facial swelling:** Amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours PLUS metronidazole 500 mg every 8 hours

> **CRITICAL:** A dental abscess that causes swelling under the jaw, in the floor of the mouth, or in the neck is an emergency. Ludwig's angina is a spreading infection of the floor of the mouth that can close the airway. If the person has trouble swallowing, their voice sounds muffled ("hot potato voice"), or swelling is pushing the tongue upward, their airway can close within hours. Maximum dose antibiotics and prepare for potential airway emergency.

## Tooth Extraction — Last Resort

Pulling a tooth is a serious decision with no undo button. Only consider it when:

- Pain is unbearable and uncontrolled with medication
- Severe infection isn't responding to antibiotics and drainage
- The tooth is so broken or decayed that nothing else can be done
- No professional dental care is available or expected

### Before You Start

- Give maximum pain medication 30–45 minutes before. Ibuprofen 600 mg + acetaminophen 1000 mg.
- If you have any injectable local anesthetic (lidocaine with epinephrine), inject about 1.5 mL on each side of the tooth, between the gum and the cheek, near the root tip. Wait 5 minutes for numbness.
- If you have no anesthetic, this is going to be extremely painful. Have the person bite on a clove-oil-soaked cotton for 10 minutes beforehand for what numbness it provides. Have someone hold their head steady.
- Prepare: have clean gauze ready for bleeding, salt water for rinsing, and a clean surface to work on.

### Tools

Ideal: dental extraction forceps (shaped to grip specific teeth).

Improvised: a strong pair of pliers with narrow jaws (cleaned and sterilized). Vise-grips work if the jaws are small enough. For front teeth, a firm side-to-side rocking motion with a flat-blade tool (like a small screwdriver or butter knife) can work as an elevator to loosen first.

### Procedure

1. Have the person sit upright with their head supported and mouth open. Good lighting is essential — you need to see what you're doing.
2. Separate the gum from the tooth as much as possible. Use a clean, thin blade or the edge of a spoon to push the gum tissue down and away from the tooth on all sides.
3. Grip the tooth as far down (toward the root) as you can get. Grip the root, not the crown — the crown can crack and break off, leaving the root behind.
4. Do NOT pull straight out. Rock the tooth — push toward the tongue, then toward the cheek, slowly increasing the arc. Back and forth. Each rock loosens the tooth in the socket.
5. For multi-rooted teeth (molars), this takes more time and force. Be patient. Rushing breaks the tooth.
6. Once loosened, apply gentle outward pressure while continuing to rock. The tooth should slide out.
7. If the crown breaks off leaving roots behind: this is a complication. If the roots are visible and you can grip them, try to remove them. If they're deep in the socket, leave them — attempting to dig out root tips without proper tools causes more damage. Pack the socket and start antibiotics.

### Aftercare

1. Have the person bite firmly on folded clean gauze over the socket for 30–60 minutes without stopping. Pressure stops the bleeding.
2. Do not rinse, spit forcefully, drink through a straw, or smoke for 24 hours. All of these can dislodge the blood clot that forms in the socket.
3. After 24 hours, begin gentle salt water rinses (warm, not hot) every 3–4 hours.
4. Soft foods only for 3–5 days. Nothing sharp or crunchy that could poke the socket.
5. Pain will be significant for 2–3 days. Ibuprofen 600 mg every 8 hours.
6. Watch for "dry socket" — the blood clot falls out of the socket and the bone is exposed. Usually hits 2–4 days after extraction. The pain is extreme and distinctive — it radiates to the ear on that side. Treatment: pack the socket with a small piece of gauze soaked in clove oil. This will need to be replaced daily until the socket fills in (5–7 days).
7. Signs of post-extraction infection: increasing pain after day 3, fever, swelling, pus from the socket. Start antibiotics.

## Knocked-Out Tooth (Avulsion)

If a permanent tooth is knocked out cleanly:

1. Pick it up by the crown (white part), never the root.
2. If dirty, rinse gently with clean water. Do not scrub, do not use soap.
3. If possible, push it back into the socket immediately. Have the person bite on gauze to hold it in place. The sooner it goes back in, the better the chance it survives. Under 30 minutes is ideal.
4. If you can't replant immediately, store the tooth in milk, saline solution, or between the cheek and gum (in saliva). Do not let it dry out. Do not store in plain water.
5. A reimplanted tooth may need to be splinted to adjacent teeth — use thin wire or fishing line tied around adjacent teeth to hold it in place for 2 weeks.

> **NOTE:** Baby teeth should NOT be reimplanted — it can damage the developing permanent tooth underneath.

## Broken Jaw

A broken jaw is identified by: pain and swelling at the jaw, teeth that don't line up when biting, numbness of the lower lip, inability to open or close the mouth normally.

1. Immobilize the jaw. Wrap a bandage under the chin and over the top of the head to hold the jaw closed. Tie it on top of the head, not too tight — the person needs to breathe and be able to vomit without aspirating.
2. Liquid diet only until healed — 4–6 weeks.
3. Antibiotics if any teeth in the fracture line are broken or the fracture communicates with the mouth (which it usually does). Amoxicillin 500 mg every 8 hours.
4. Pain management: maximum ibuprofen and acetaminophen. Crush tablets or use liquid forms since the jaw shouldn't move.
