A leaking roof makes every other part of your shelter useless. Water destroys insulation, breeds mold, soaks bedding, and drops morale. The roof doesn't need to be beautiful — it needs to shed water completely for as long as you're using the structure.

## Roof Pitch — The Foundation Rule

Steeper is better for shedding water. As a general rule:

- **45 degrees or steeper** for thatch, leaf, and natural material roofs. Natural materials absorb water — steep pitch ensures it runs off before soaking through.
- **25–45 degrees** for sheet materials (metal, plastic, tarps). These shed water well even at lower angles.
- **Minimum 15 degrees** for any roof. Below this, water pools and finds its way through every seam and gap.

A simple rule: if you can stand in the rain and watch water run off your roof without pooling or hesitating, the pitch is adequate.

## Thatching

The most widely used roofing material in human history. Thick bundles of dried grass, reeds, rushes, or palm leaves layered to shed water.

### Materials

**Best:** Long-stemmed grass (3+ feet), reeds (cattail, phragmites), rushes, palm fronds. The longer the material, the better — it creates fewer overlap seams.

**Acceptable:** Shorter grass or leaves bundled tightly. Works but requires more overlap and a steeper pitch.

### Process

1. Gather and dry the thatching material. Green material rots quickly — dry it for several days in the sun first if possible.
2. Bundle the material into sheaves about 4–6 inches thick, tied with cord at one end.
3. Starting at the bottom edge of the roof, lay the first row of bundles with the cut ends pointing upward and the natural tips hanging down past the roof edge by 6–12 inches (this creates the drip edge).
4. Tie or wire each bundle to the roof framework (horizontal battens spaced 8–12 inches apart across the rafters).
5. Lay the next row overlapping the first by at least 2/3 of the bundle length. This overlap is critical — water runs down the outer layer and drips off before reaching the inner layer.
6. Continue up the roof, each row overlapping the previous by 2/3.
7. At the ridge (top peak), fold bundles over from both sides and tie them together, or cap with a separate ridge bundle.

### Thickness

A properly thatched roof should be 8–12 inches thick when compressed. Thin thatch leaks. The thicker the layer, the better it sheds water and the longer it lasts.

### Lifespan

Well-made thatch lasts 3–7 years in temperate climates, less in wet tropical environments. The ridge (most exposed) deteriorates first — plan to repair or replace it every 1–2 years.

## Bark Roofing

Large sheets of bark — particularly birch, elm, cedar, or poplar — make excellent waterproof roofing. Bark was a primary roofing material for indigenous peoples across North America.

### Harvesting

1. Select a straight tree with thick bark (6+ inch diameter trunk). Dead standing trees often release bark more easily than living trees.
2. Score two horizontal rings around the trunk about 3–4 feet apart.
3. Score a vertical line connecting them.
4. Pry the bark off in a sheet using a flat stick or the back of an axe. Work carefully to keep the sheet intact.
5. Flatten fresh bark sheets under weight (rocks, logs) while drying — otherwise they curl.

### Installation

1. Lay bark sheets on the roof framework like shingles — bottom row first, overlapping upward.
2. Overlap each sheet by at least 4–6 inches on the sides and 8–12 inches top-to-bottom.
3. Weight the bark with poles laid horizontally across it, or pin it with wooden pegs through drilled holes.
4. At the ridge, fold bark over from both sides or cap with a V-shaped bark trough.

Birch bark is the best — it's naturally waterproof, flexible, and lasts years. Cedar bark strips woven or layered also work well.

## Salvaged Corrugated Metal

In a post-disaster scenario, corrugated metal roofing is often available from damaged buildings. It's the ideal salvage material for roofing — lightweight, completely waterproof, durable, and easy to install.

### Installation

1. Lay sheets on rafters spaced 24 inches apart. Metal can span wider gaps than most natural materials.
2. Overlap sheets by at least one corrugation width on the sides and 6 inches at the ends.
3. Fasten with nails, screws, or wire tied through holes drilled near the edges.
4. Nail through the crowns (high points) of the corrugations, not the valleys — water runs in the valleys, and nail holes there leak.
5. Seal the ridge with bent metal, a wooden cap, or folded material.

### Problems

- **Noise:** Rain on metal is extremely loud. A layer of thatch, leaves, or cloth under the metal reduces noise dramatically.
- **Condensation:** Cold metal surfaces cause interior condensation. Insulation under the metal (leaves, straw, plastic sheeting) prevents dripping.
- **Heat:** Metal roofs get very hot in direct sun. Insulation or an air gap between the metal and the living space is essential in hot climates.
- **Wind:** Light metal sheets catch wind like sails. Fasten securely, especially at edges and corners. Weight with rocks or tie down with wire/rope over the top.

## Plastic Sheeting and Tarps

The fastest roofing solution if you have the material.

1. Drape over a ridgepole or framework with at least a 30-degree pitch.
2. Secure edges with rocks, stakes, or by burying the edges in trenches.
3. Ensure the sheet is taut — sagging creates pools where water collects and eventually breaks through or collapses the structure.
4. UV degradation is the main enemy of plastic. Standard construction poly (6 mil) lasts 6–12 months in direct sun. Reinforced tarps last longer. If possible, cover the plastic with a layer of natural material (branches, leaves) to protect it from sun.

## Split Wood Shingles

Hand-split wooden shingles were the standard roofing in North America before commercial materials.

1. Select straight-grained, rot-resistant wood: cedar, cypress, oak, chestnut.
2. Cut rounds (cross-sections of the log) 18–24 inches long.
3. Split the rounds into thin shakes using a froe (a wedge-shaped splitting tool) and mallet. Each shake should be ½ to ¾ inch thick with a clean grain.
4. Install like commercial shingles: start at the bottom, overlap each row by at least half the shingle length.
5. Offset the seams between rows so water can't channel straight through.
6. Use two layers (double coverage) for better waterproofing.

Labor-intensive but extremely durable — split cedar shingles last 20–30 years.

## Sod Roofing

Traditional in Scandinavia and the Great Plains. A layer of living grass/turf on top of the roof structure. Excellent insulation and waterproofing, extremely heavy.

1. Build a very strong roof structure — sod roofs weigh 80–100 lbs per square foot when wet.
2. Lay a waterproof layer on the roof boards (birch bark traditionally, or plastic sheeting).
3. Cut sod (grass and root mat) into blocks about 4 inches thick.
4. Lay sod blocks on the waterproof layer, grass side up, in two overlapping layers.
5. The grass grows and roots knit together, creating a waterproof, insulated, living roof.

**Major consideration:** The roof structure must handle the weight. Standard pole rafters may not be sufficient — use heavy beams or closely spaced rafters with thick decking.

## Maintenance

All roofs need ongoing attention:

- Inspect after every storm. Repair leaks immediately — small leaks become big problems fast.
- Thatch: re-pack loose areas, add material to thin spots, replace the ridge annually.
- Bark: re-weight shifted sheets, replace curled or rotted pieces.
- Metal: re-secure loose fasteners, patch holes with additional metal and sealant.
- Any roof: keep drainage clear. Water that backs up under the roof edge destroys the wall below.
