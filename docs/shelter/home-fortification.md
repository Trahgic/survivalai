Fortifying a home or shelter is about buying time — making it harder and slower for an intruder to get in, giving you time to respond or escape. No residential structure is impenetrable. The goal is to make yours harder than the alternatives so threats move on, and to create layers that slow anyone who doesn't.

## Principles

1. **Layers.** Multiple barriers force an attacker through several obstacles, each costing time and making noise. Perimeter → exterior walls → interior barriers → safe room.
2. **Noise.** Detection matters more than strength. A barrier that breaks but makes noise while breaking is more valuable than a silent one that holds slightly longer.
3. **Visibility.** You need to see threats approaching without exposing yourself. Observation without exposure.
4. **Escape routes.** Never fortify yourself into a box with no exit. Every fortified position needs at least one planned escape route.
5. **Proportional response.** Over-fortification in a community setting breeds fear and conflict. Fortify appropriately to the actual threat level.

## Doors — The Primary Entry Point

Most forced entries come through doors. A door is only as strong as its weakest component: the frame, the hinges, or the lock.

### Reinforcing Existing Doors

1. **Frame reinforcement:** Most door frames are held by short screws into soft wood. Replace all hinge screws and strike plate screws with 3-inch screws that reach through the frame into the structural studs behind it. This single upgrade stops most kick-ins.
2. **Strike plate upgrade:** Replace the standard strike plate (the metal piece the door bolt goes into) with a heavy-duty reinforced plate that spans 12+ inches vertically. Secure with 3-inch screws.
3. **Deadbolt:** A doorknob lock alone is nearly useless. Add a deadbolt with at least a 1-inch throw. Double-cylinder deadbolts (keyed on both sides) prevent someone from breaking glass and reaching through to unlock.
4. **Bar across the door:** A horizontal bar or plank in brackets across the inside of the door is the simplest and most effective reinforcement. U-shaped brackets mounted on each side of the frame, with a 2×4 or steel bar dropped in. Impossible to force from outside.
5. **Door material:** Solid wood or metal doors resist force. Hollow-core interior doors are essentially cardboard — they stop nothing. If your exterior door is hollow-core, replace it with the heaviest solid door available or barricade behind it.

### Improvised Barricade

If you're barricading quickly:
1. Wedge a chair under the doorknob (only works on inward-opening doors).
2. Stack heavy furniture against the door.
3. Drive a wedge (door stop, folded cardboard, wood shim) under the bottom of the door from the inside.
4. Nail or screw boards across the door and frame from inside.

## Windows

Windows are the weakest part of any structure. Glass breaks easily and quietly with the right tool.

### Boarding Up

1. **Plywood:** ¾-inch plywood over the entire window frame, secured with lag bolts or long screws into the structural framing (not just the window trim). Cut the plywood 4 inches wider than the window on all sides to catch the framing.
2. **Leave observation gaps.** Don't blind yourself. Cut a narrow horizontal slot (2 inches tall) in the plywood at eye height — wide enough to see through, narrow enough to provide protection.
3. **Multiple layers:** For serious threats, two layers of ¾-inch plywood with the grain running perpendicular (cross-lamination) provides significantly more resistance than a single layer.

### Without Plywood

- Furniture placed in front of windows (bookshelves, dressers) stops casual entry and blocks visibility from outside.
- Heavy blankets or curtains block light discipline at night (your lights make you visible and give away your presence).
- Mattresses propped against windows stop visibility and provide some ballistic resistance.
- Cross-nailed boards from demolition or pallets provide improvised boarding.

### Security Film (Pre-Disaster Upgrade)

Security window film applied to glass holds shattered glass in place — the window cracks but doesn't fall away, forcing repeated strikes to make an entry hole. Significantly slows entry and makes noise. This is a pre-disaster preparation, not a field improvisation.

## Perimeter

### Awareness and Detection

- **Clear sight lines.** Cut or move vegetation that provides cover for someone approaching. You want open ground around your structure — ideally 30–50 feet where you can see movement.
- **Noise-makers on approaches.** String cans, bottles, or bells on wire or fishing line at ankle height across likely approach routes. Simple and effective night detection.
- **Gravel paths.** Gravel around the building is nearly impossible to walk on silently.
- **Dogs.** A dog is the most effective early warning system available. They detect approach before any electronic system and don't need batteries.

### Physical Barriers

- **Fencing.** Even a basic fence forces an intruder to climb, creating noise and delay. Chain-link is hard to cut quietly. Thorny hedge (roses, hawthorn, barberry) is a living barrier.
- **Vehicle barriers.** Disabled vehicles, concrete blocks, or earth-filled barriers across vehicle approaches prevent ram attacks.
- **Ditches and berms.** An earth berm or ditch around a property slows approach and provides cover for defenders. Even 3 feet of earth stops most rifle rounds.

## Safe Room

A fortified interior room where occupants retreat during a breach. The last layer of defense.

### Selection

- **Interior room with no exterior windows** (bathroom, closet, interior bedroom).
- **Solid walls.** Brick or concrete is ideal. Standard residential drywall stops nothing — if your safe room has drywall, reinforce it with plywood.
- **One strong entry point.** A solid-core or metal door with deadbolt and bar.
- **Communication.** Have a way to call for help or communicate with the outside (phone, radio, signal device).

### Stocking

Keep in the safe room:
- Communication device
- First aid kit
- Water (at least a gallon)
- Light source (flashlight)
- Tools (for escaping if needed — pry bar, hammer for breaking through a wall as an escape route)
- Self-defense items

### Escape

The safe room should have an escape route — a window leading outside (ground floor), a tunnel, or a pre-planned breach point through a wall into an adjacent room or exterior. A safe room with no exit is a trap.

## Light Discipline

At night, light from your windows tells everyone within visual range exactly where you are, how many rooms you're using, and where you are inside the structure.

- Cover all windows before turning on any light.
- Use blackout curtains, heavy blankets, or cardboard taped over window frames.
- Use the minimum light necessary — candles and dim flashlights. Avoid bright white light visible at a distance.
- At night, position a lookout who has been in darkness for 20+ minutes (dark-adapted eyes see far better than transitioning from lit interior).

## Defensive Positioning

If your shelter is threatened:

- **Don't go outside.** The structure is your advantage — it's concealment and cover. Outside, you're exposed.
- **Upstairs advantage.** Higher ground gives visibility and forces an attacker to climb while exposed.
- **Identify hard cover.** Know which walls, furniture, and positions in your home can actually stop bullets (solid wood, brick, concrete, stone, bookshelves full of books, refrigerator, engine block). Drywall, car doors, and interior walls do not stop rifle rounds.
- **Know your exits.** Always have at least two planned escape routes.
- **Communication plan.** Everyone in the structure should know the plan — where to go, what to do, when to evacuate.
