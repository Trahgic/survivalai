Where you build matters as much as what you build. The wrong location turns a good shelter into a death trap — flooded by rain, exposed to wind, contaminated by runoff, or crushed by falling trees. Take 20 minutes to assess a site before spending hours building on it.

## The Five W's of Site Selection

### Water

- **Close enough to access** without a long walk (100–300 yards is ideal).
- **Far enough to avoid flooding.** Check for high-water marks on trees and rocks along rivers and streams. Camp above the highest mark you can see. Flash floods in canyon and valley bottoms kill campers regularly.
- **Above the water source, not below** — you don't want your waste running into your drinking water.
- **Not in a dry wash or riverbed.** Even if it hasn't rained where you are, upstream rain can send a wall of water down a dry channel with no warning.

### Wind

- **Identify prevailing wind direction** by observing tree lean, cloud movement, or hanging a strip of cloth.
- **Face the shelter opening away from prevailing wind.** Or position the shelter behind a natural windbreak — a rock face, dense tree line, or hill.
- **Ridgetops and passes** are the windiest locations. Avoid them for camp.
- **In hot climates,** some airflow is desirable to reduce insect pressure and cool the shelter. Don't seal yourself into a completely windless spot if heat is the enemy.

### Weather

- **Check the sky and terrain for weather patterns.** Darkening clouds, dropping temperature, and shifting wind may mean a storm is approaching.
- **Avoid terrain that becomes dangerous in rain:** valley bottoms (flooding), clay hillsides (landslides), areas below loose rock (rockfall increases when rock gets wet).
- **In lightning-prone areas,** avoid isolated tall trees, ridgetops, and open fields. The safest position in a lightning storm is in a low area among shorter trees or dense forest.

### Wood (Fuel and Materials)

- **Dead, dry wood for fire** should be abundant nearby. Having to walk 500 yards for every armload of firewood wastes enormous energy.
- **Building materials** — poles, boughs, bark, leaves — should be within easy reach.
- **Don't strip live trees near your camp** more than necessary. You may be here for a while, and denuding the immediate area removes your windbreak and shade.

### Widowmakers and Hazards

- **Look up.** Dead standing trees (snags) and dead branches overhead are called widowmakers for good reason. Wind brings them down without warning. Never camp under dead trees or large dead limbs.
- **Look down.** Ant hills, wasp nests, and animal burrows at your site mean unwanted neighbors. Check before building.
- **Look around.** Steep slopes above you mean rockfall or avalanche risk. Undercut riverbanks mean erosion and collapse. Game trails through your site mean animals will walk through at night.

## Terrain Types — Where to Camp

### Forest

**Best spots:** Slightly elevated ground with tree canopy for rain protection, near a water source, with deadfall for fuel.

**Avoid:** Under lone tall trees (lightning), in dense undergrowth (limited airflow, insect habitat, difficult building), on steep slopes.

### Mountains

**Best spots:** Lee side (downwind side) of ridges, partway up a slope (not the top, not the bottom). Valley floors collect cold air at night — temperatures can be 10–20°F colder than slopes above.

**Avoid:** Ridgetops (wind/lightning), valley bottoms (cold pooling, flooding), below cliff faces (rockfall), avalanche chutes (recognizable by stripped vegetation and debris fans).

### Desert

**Best spots:** Near shade (rock overhangs, canyon walls — but not in narrow slot canyons, which flash-flood). Near water sources. On elevated ground with airflow.

**Avoid:** Direct sun exposure (obvious). Low spots that may flood (desert flash floods are sudden and violent). Dry washes. Areas where poisonous snakes and scorpions congregate (rocky areas with crevices near water).

### Coastal

**Best spots:** Above the high-tide line (look for the debris line marking previous high water). Behind dunes for wind protection. Near fresh water sources.

**Avoid:** Below high-tide line (obvious but people misjudge it). In areas subject to storm surge. On eroding bluffs or undercut banks.

### Snow/Arctic

**Best spots:** Sheltered positions with packed snow (for snow shelter construction). Protected from wind by terrain features. On slopes (cold air drains to valley floors).

**Avoid:** Avalanche zones, wind-exposed ridges, ice over water (can break through), under snow-loaded trees (snow shedding from canopy).

## Long-Term Camp Layout

If you're establishing a base camp for days or weeks, plan the layout:

1. **Sleeping area:** Uphill and upwind from everything else. The quietest, most protected spot.
2. **Cooking/fire area:** Downwind from sleeping area (smoke drifts away). Near fuel supply. Clear of overhead branches (fire sparks rise).
3. **Latrine:** Downhill and at least 100 feet (30 meters) from water sources, 200 feet from sleeping and cooking areas, and downwind. See sanitation-and-latrines.md.
4. **Water collection/treatment:** Between the camp and the water source. Keep a clear path — you'll walk this many times daily.
5. **Storage:** Food storage away from sleeping area (bears and other animals follow the smell). Hang food bags from a branch 10+ feet high and 4+ feet from the trunk if bears are possible.
6. **Drainage:** Dig a small trench around the uphill side of the sleeping area to divert rainwater runoff. Don't build in a depression — it becomes a puddle.

## Reading the Landscape

**Natural indicators of good sites:**
- Flat ground with slight drainage slope (not pooling water, not steep)
- Old fire rings or cleared ground (someone chose this spot before — they may have known something)
- Green, healthy vegetation (healthy soil, good drainage)
- Evidence of wildlife but not active dens or nesting sites

**Natural indicators of bad sites:**
- Waterlogged ground (soggy, standing water, thick moss on ground)
- Dead, leaning, or downed trees (unstable ground, disease, or wind damage)
- Erosion channels and exposed roots (flooding area)
- Insect swarms (standing water nearby)
- Animal scat and tracks concentrated (active trail — they'll come through at night)
- Discolored or bare soil (possible contamination, mineral deposits, or repeatedly flooded ground)

## The 20-Minute Rule

Before building, spend 20 minutes at the site:

1. Walk the area. Look for hazards above, below, and around.
2. Check drainage — pour a bottle of water and see where it goes. Water at your shelter goes where the ground tells it.
3. Sit for 5 minutes and observe. Feel the wind. Listen for water. Watch for insects. What seemed like a perfect spot standing up may feel different when you're at ground level.
4. Consider worst case — if it rains hard tonight, will this spot flood? If the wind shifts, am I exposed?
5. If the site fails any of these checks and a better option exists within reasonable distance, move. Twenty minutes of scouting saves hours of rebuilding.
