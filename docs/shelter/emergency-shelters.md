Exposure kills faster than dehydration or starvation. In cold, wet, or windy conditions, an unprotected person can die of hypothermia in hours. Building shelter is often your first survival priority — before water, before food, before signaling. A shelter doesn't need to be pretty. It needs to block wind, shed rain, and retain heat.

## Priorities in Order

1. **Wind protection.** Wind strips body heat through convection. Even a simple windbreak cuts heat loss dramatically.
2. **Insulation from the ground.** The ground steals heat through conduction 50x faster than air. A pile of leaves, pine boughs, or grass between you and the ground is more important than a roof.
3. **Rain/snow protection.** Wet clothing and skin accelerate heat loss. A waterproof layer above keeps you dry.
4. **Heat retention.** A small shelter retains body heat better than a large one. Build just big enough to lie in.

## Site Selection Quick Check

Before building, look up, look down, look around:

- **Up:** Dead branches overhead ("widowmakers") that could fall. Avoid building under dead trees or large limbs.
- **Down:** Is the ground dry? Avoid low spots that collect water. Is there natural insulation available (leaves, pine needles)?
- **Around:** Wind direction — face the shelter opening away from prevailing wind. Nearby materials for construction. Proximity to water (close enough to access, not so close you're in a flood zone).
- Avoid hilltops (wind exposure), valley bottoms (cold air sinks and pools), and dry riverbeds (flash flood risk).

## Debris Hut

The best emergency shelter you can build with no tools and no materials except what's on the forest floor. Properly built, a debris hut keeps you warm in below-freezing temperatures with no fire.

### Construction

1. **Ridgepole:** Find a sturdy pole 9–12 feet long. Prop one end on a stump, rock, or forked tree about 3 feet off the ground. The other end rests on the ground. This is the spine of your shelter. It should be at roughly a 30-degree angle.

2. **Ribbing:** Lean sticks along both sides of the ridgepole at 45-degree angles, spaced 6–8 inches apart. These form the skeleton. The shelter should be just wide enough for your body — shoulders plus a few inches on each side. Narrower = warmer.

3. **Lattice:** Lay smaller sticks horizontally across the ribs to create a grid. This prevents the insulation layer from falling through.

4. **Insulation (the critical step):** Pile dry leaves, pine needles, grass, ferns, or any dead vegetation on top of the lattice. You need at least 2–3 feet thick — yes, feet. Think "pile of leaves that a car could park on." Thinner insulation and you'll be cold. This is where most people under-build. More is always better.

5. **Ground insulation:** Fill the interior with 6+ inches of dry leaves or pine boughs. This is your mattress. The ground will suck heat from your body all night if you lie directly on it.

6. **Door:** Stuff the entrance with a loose pile of leaves or a stuffed garbage bag. You want to seal the opening to trap body heat inside.

7. **Optional:** Pile leaves on the ground outside the entrance to stand on (keeps your feet out of mud when entering/exiting).

### Key Points

- The shelter should be just big enough for your body. Dead air space that your body has to heat is wasted energy. If two people can fit side by side, it's too wide for one person.
- Pack the leaf insulation tight enough that rain runs off instead of soaking through. Test by pouring a bottle of water on the outside — it should run off, not through.
- A well-built debris hut takes 2–3 hours. Start building with at least 3 hours of daylight remaining.
- This shelter works by trapping your body heat in a small insulated space. No fire needed — and you shouldn't try to have a fire inside.

## Lean-To

The simplest structural shelter. Fast to build, good for mild conditions, but poor heat retention compared to an enclosed shelter.

### Construction

1. Find or create a horizontal beam 8–10 feet long, supported 4–5 feet off the ground between two trees, in forked branches, or propped on rock faces.
2. Lean long poles against the beam at 45–60 degrees, forming a sloped wall.
3. Cover with whatever waterproofing is available: tarp, poncho, bark slabs, layered green boughs (start from the bottom and overlap upward like shingles), or thick pile of leaves.
4. Build a fire in front of the open side. Position a reflector wall behind the fire (a stack of green logs or a rock face) to bounce heat back toward you.

**Limitations:** Open on one side, so wind and rain from that direction hit you. Cold night air flows through. Best when you have a fire and the opening faces away from wind.

**Improvement:** Build a second lean-to facing the first, creating an A-frame with a fire between them. Or close the sides with brush and debris to create a more enclosed space.

## Tarp Shelter Configurations

If you have a tarp, poncho, or plastic sheeting, your options expand significantly.

### A-Frame

1. String a line between two trees at chest height.
2. Drape the tarp over the line, staking or weighing the edges to the ground.
3. Creates a peaked roof that sheds rain well. Open at both ends unless you close them with debris or additional material.

### Lean-To Tarp

1. Tie two corners of the tarp to trees at about 4 feet height.
2. Stake the opposite edge to the ground.
3. Fast, simple, provides rain and wind protection from one direction.

### C-Fly (Burrito Wrap)

For solo sleeping in rain:
1. Lay the tarp flat. Lie in the center.
2. Pull one side over you, tucking it under your body on the far side.
3. Pull the other side over on top.
4. You're wrapped like a burrito with waterproof material above and below. Not comfortable, but dry.

### Diamond Fly

1. Stake one corner to the ground (foot end).
2. Raise the opposite corner as the peak (tie to a tree or prop with a stick).
3. Stake the two side corners to the ground.
4. Creates a low, spacious shelter with good rain runoff.

## Snow Cave

In deep snow (at least 4–5 feet), a snow cave provides excellent insulation. Snow is a superb insulator — interior temperature stabilizes around 32°F (0°C) regardless of outside temperature.

### Construction

1. Find a deep snowbank or drift, ideally on a slope. The more consolidated (packed, windblown) the snow, the better it holds its shape. Fresh powder collapses.
2. Dig an entrance tunnel at the base, angling slightly upward. The entrance should be lower than the sleeping platform — cold air sinks, so the sleeping area stays warmer.
3. Hollow out a sleeping chamber big enough for your body (or your group). The ceiling should be domed — a dome distributes weight and resists collapse.
4. Carve the sleeping platform at least 1–2 feet above the entrance tunnel floor. This critical design element keeps you in the warm air layer (heat rises).
5. Smooth the ceiling so drips run down the walls instead of dripping on you.
6. Poke a ventilation hole (about 2 inches diameter) through the ceiling with a stick. **This is non-negotiable** — without ventilation, carbon dioxide builds up and suffocates you. If you're using a candle or stove inside, you need 2 ventilation holes.
7. Mark the entrance from outside (stick a pole, backpack, or bright cloth nearby) so you can find it if snow falls and covers the entrance.

> **CRITICAL:** Check your ventilation hole periodically. Blowing snow can seal it. People have died from CO2 buildup in snow caves with blocked ventilation holes. If you feel drowsy or develop a headache, ventilate immediately.

### Limitations

- Takes 2–4 hours to dig with a shovel, longer without tools.
- You get wet from digging. Have dry clothing to change into.
- Risk of collapse if snow is unconsolidated. Test by packing the snow — if it doesn't hold a fist-print, it's too loose.
- Melting from body heat can cause dripping. Smooth ceilings and avoid touching the walls.

## Quinzhee

A quinzhee is a snow shelter built by piling snow into a mound, letting it consolidate, then hollowing it out. Works even with loose, powdery snow that won't support a snow cave.

1. Pile snow into a mound 6–8 feet tall and 8–10 feet in diameter. Pack it loosely.
2. Insert sticks 12–18 inches long throughout the mound at various angles. These serve as thickness guides when you hollow it out from inside — when you hit a stick, stop digging in that direction.
3. Let the mound sit for 2–3 hours (longer in very cold conditions). The snow crystals bond together through sintering — the mound becomes structurally sound.
4. Dig an entrance tunnel and hollow out the interior, maintaining 12–18 inch wall thickness (guided by the sticks).
5. Follow the same principles as a snow cave: elevated sleeping platform, domed ceiling, ventilation hole.

## Vehicle Shelter

If you're stranded with a vehicle:

- Stay with the vehicle — it's visible to rescuers, provides shelter from wind, and has materials you can use.
- Run the engine for heat in 15-minute intervals, then turn it off to conserve fuel. Crack a downwind window for ventilation — carbon monoxide from the exhaust is lethal in an enclosed space.
- Make sure the exhaust pipe isn't blocked by snow. Check periodically.
- Insulate the floor and windows. Use floor mats, seat covers, newspapers, clothing — anything between you and the cold metal/glass.
- Signal: turn on hazard lights periodically, tie bright cloth to the antenna.

## Natural Shelters

Before building from scratch, look for existing shelter:

- Rock overhangs and shallow caves (check for animal inhabitants before moving in)
- Dense evergreen trees — the canopy blocks rain and wind, the base provides a dry spot
- Fallen trees with root balls — the upturned root mass creates a natural wall
- Hollow logs (large enough to crawl into)
- Abandoned structures — barns, sheds, even ruins provide walls and partial roofing

## Heat Retention Tips for Any Shelter

- **Smaller is warmer.** Only build as big as you need.
- **Insulate from the ground.** This is the biggest heat loss you can control. Pile it thick — 6 inches minimum.
- **Block airflow.** Seal gaps with leaves, grass, moss, mud, or snow.
- **Body heat is your furnace.** A small sealed shelter with one person inside warms to 20–30°F above outside temperature within an hour.
- **Buddy heat.** Two people in a shelter share body heat — significantly warmer than solo.
- **Hot rocks.** Heat rocks by the fire, wrap in cloth, bring into the shelter as warmers. They radiate heat for hours. Don't use wet rocks (explosion risk) and don't put them directly against skin or flammable material.
- **Candle.** A single candle in a small enclosed shelter can raise the temperature noticeably. Provides light too. Ensure ventilation.
