Poor sanitation kills more people than violence in every disaster and collapse scenario in recorded history. Cholera, typhoid, dysentery, and hepatitis spread through the fecal-oral route — feces contaminating water or food. A proper latrine and waste management system isn't a comfort item. It's critical infrastructure that prevents epidemic disease.

## Emergency Latrine — First 24 Hours

If you've just arrived at a site and need something immediately:

1. Pick a spot at least 200 feet (60 meters) from any water source and downhill from camp.
2. Dig a cat hole: 6–8 inches deep, 4–6 inches wide. Do your business. Cover completely with the excavated soil.
3. Mark the spot so nobody steps in it or digs there.
4. Each person, each time — new hole, fully buried.

This is temporary. Within 24–48 hours, build a proper latrine.

## Pit Latrine

The simplest permanent latrine. A hole in the ground with a seat or squatting platform.

### Construction

1. **Location:** Minimum 100 feet (30 meters) from any water source. Downhill and downwind from camp. At least 50 feet from cooking and sleeping areas.
2. **Dig the pit:** 3–4 feet wide, 5–8 feet deep. Deeper lasts longer. Round or rectangular — whatever's easier to dig. In sandy or loose soil, line the upper portion with stones, logs, or boards to prevent collapse.
3. **Build a platform:** Lay logs or boards across the pit, leaving a hole in the center (about 10 inches in diameter for a squat hole, or frame a seat). The platform must support body weight securely.
4. **Seat (optional but appreciated):** Build a box with an opening, like a wooden toilet seat. Or use a 5-gallon bucket with the bottom cut out, set over the pit hole.
5. **Shelter:** Build a basic enclosure for privacy — a tarp wrapped around poles, a wattle screen, or a small structure. Include a roof — rain washing into the pit accelerates filling and creates splashing.
6. **Cover:** Keep a plywood board, piece of metal, or thick cloth cover over the pit hole when not in use. This prevents flies from accessing the waste — fly contact is the primary disease transmission route.
7. **Handwashing station:** Set up a handwash point with soap and water right at the latrine exit. Make it unavoidable — everyone who uses the latrine washes their hands. No exceptions.

### Use and Maintenance

1. After each use, throw in a handful of dirt, ash, or lime. This covers the waste, reduces smell, controls flies, and aids decomposition.
2. Ash is particularly good — it raises pH, kills bacteria, and controls insects.
3. Do not throw trash, food waste, or non-biodegradable items into the latrine.
4. When the pit fills to within 18 inches of the surface, cover it with at least 12 inches of compacted soil. Dig a new pit. Mark the old one — you don't want to dig there again or build on it for at least a year.

### Lifespan

A 5-foot-deep pit serves a family of 4 for roughly 6–12 months depending on use and volume of cover material added.

## Composting Toilet

A composting toilet breaks human waste down into usable compost through aerobic decomposition. Takes longer to set up but produces a useful end product and doesn't require digging.

### Simple Twin-Vault Design

1. Build two chambers side by side — wooden boxes, cinder block enclosures, or 55-gallon drums.
2. Only use one chamber at a time. After each use, add a generous scoop of carbon-rich cover material: sawdust, dry leaves, straw, wood ash, or dry grass. This is essential — it provides carbon for composting, covers waste, controls odor, and absorbs moisture.
3. When one chamber is full, seal it and switch to the second. The full chamber composts for 6–12 months while you fill the other.
4. After 6–12 months, the composted material is safe to use on non-food gardens (or food gardens if you're comfortable — properly composted human waste at high temperature is pathogen-free, but this requires reaching 131°F/55°C which passive systems may not achieve).

### Key to Success

- **Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.** Human waste is high-nitrogen. Cover material adds carbon. The ideal ratio is about 25–30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen. In practice: add a generous handful of sawdust or leaves after every use. If it smells bad, you're not adding enough cover.
- **Moisture.** The pile should be damp like a wrung-out sponge, not wet and not dry. Too wet = anaerobic (stinks). Too dry = composting stalls.
- **Urine diversion (optional but helpful):** Separate urine from feces. Urine is basically sterile and can be diluted 10:1 with water and used directly as fertilizer. Separating it keeps the compost pile drier and reduces smell.

## Greywater Management

Greywater is wastewater from washing — dishes, laundry, bathing. It contains soap, food particles, and bacteria but not fecal matter. It still needs management — dumped carelessly, it creates mud, attracts insects, and can contaminate water sources.

### Simple Greywater Systems

**Soak pit (the easiest):**
1. Dig a hole 3 feet deep, 2–3 feet across.
2. Fill with rocks and gravel.
3. Pour greywater into the pit. It percolates through the gravel and into the soil. The gravel prevents surface pooling and the soil filters the water.
4. Place the soak pit at least 50 feet from any water source.

**Greywater garden:**
Pour greywater directly onto garden beds (not on edible plant leaves — onto the soil around root zones). The soil filters the water and the nutrients feed the plants. Use biodegradable soap only — chemical-heavy detergents harm plants and soil.

## Garbage and Waste Disposal

- **Burn what you can.** Paper, cardboard, food scraps (once dried), and wood waste should be burned. Fire reduces volume and eliminates organic material that attracts pests.
- **Bury what you can't burn.** Dig a garbage pit away from camp and water sources. Cover each deposit with soil.
- **Separate recyclable materials** if you're in a long-term scenario. Metal, glass, and durable plastics have reuse value.
- **Food waste** should never sit exposed. It attracts rodents, flies, and larger animals. Burn it, bury it, or compost it.

## Hygiene Stations

Set up dedicated stations that make hygiene convenient:

**Handwashing station (tippy-tap):**
1. Hang a water jug (with a small hole or spigot) from a stick or frame.
2. Attach a foot pedal (stick with rope) that tilts the jug when stepped on — hands-free operation.
3. Place a bar of soap in a mesh bag hung next to it, or a soap dish.
4. Position one at the latrine and one at the cooking area.

**Dish washing:**
Three basins: wash (hot soapy water), rinse (clean water), sanitize (water with a few drops of bleach). Air dry on a clean rack.

**Bathing area:**
A screened enclosure with drainage (greywater soak pit). A solar shower bag (black container hung in sun, gravity-feeds warm water) provides hot water. Even a bucket and cloth in a private spot is better than nothing — regular bathing prevents skin infections and parasites.

## Managing Medical Waste

Blood-soaked bandages, used wound dressings, and contaminated materials need special handling:

1. Burn whenever possible. Medical waste should go into a hot fire — not smoldering coals.
2. If burning isn't possible, bury at least 18 inches deep in a dedicated pit away from the latrine and water sources.
3. Use gloves or barriers when handling.
4. Sharps (needles, scalpel blades): collect in a puncture-proof container (hard plastic bottle, metal can). Burn or bury when full.

## When Things Go Wrong

If people in your group develop diarrheal illness:

1. Immediately assess the latrine situation — is it contaminated, too close to water, or being bypassed?
2. Chlorinate the drinking water supply.
3. Enforce strict handwashing.
4. Isolate sick individuals if possible — they're shedding massive amounts of bacteria.
5. See hygiene-and-disease-prevention.md for epidemic response.
