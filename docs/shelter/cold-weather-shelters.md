Cold weather changes everything about shelter building. Insulation requirements increase dramatically, heat management becomes the central design problem, and mistakes in ventilation can be lethal. A cold weather shelter needs to do three things: block wind, trap heat, and vent carbon dioxide and smoke.

## Snow Shelters

See emergency-shelters.md for snow cave and quinzhee construction details. This section covers additional cold-weather-specific shelters and heated designs.

## Igloo

An igloo is the most effective cold-weather shelter possible. Properly built, the interior stays 40–60°F (20–33°C) above outside temperature with body heat alone. With a small heat source, it's remarkably comfortable.

### Construction

1. **Snow quality.** You need wind-packed or consolidated snow that holds its shape when cut. Test by stepping on the snow — if your foot sinks more than a few inches, it's too loose. Look for wind-packed drifts or let loose snow consolidate for 24+ hours after piling.

2. **Cut blocks.** Use a saw, machete, large knife, or flat stick to cut blocks about 36 inches long, 18 inches wide, and 6 inches thick. Cut from a quarry area near the build site — the hole becomes your entrance tunnel or sunken floor.

3. **Lay the first course.** Arrange blocks in a circle 8–10 feet in diameter for a 2-person igloo. Tilt the blocks slightly inward (about 10 degrees). Each block leans against the one before it.

4. **Spiral upward.** Cut a ramp into the top of the first course so the second course spirals continuously rather than stacking in flat layers. This is the key technique — the spiral allows each block to lean on the previous one for support. Without the spiral, the dome won't close.

5. **Each course tilts more inward.** The lean increases as you go up, forming the dome. Trim the inner edge of each block so it contacts the block below along its full width — this is where the structural strength comes from.

6. **Cap stone.** The final block at the top is shaped to fit the remaining hole. Cut it slightly oversized, place it on top, then trim from inside.

7. **Chink the gaps.** Fill all gaps between blocks with loose snow packed from the outside. Then smooth the interior surface — bumps cause dripping when body heat warms the inside.

8. **Entrance tunnel.** Dig or cut an entrance below the floor level of the igloo. Cold air sinks — if the entrance is lower than the living floor, warm air stays inside. The tunnel should have at least one right-angle turn to block wind from blowing directly in.

9. **Sleeping platform.** Carve a raised platform (12–18 inches above the floor) for sleeping. Cold air pools at the lowest point — sleeping elevated keeps you in the warm layer.

10. **Ventilation hole.** Poke at least one 2-inch hole through the wall near the top. Check it regularly — it can get sealed by drifting snow or internal glazing from moisture.

### How Long Does It Take?

An experienced builder can construct a 2-person igloo in 1.5–2 hours. First-timers should expect 3–5 hours. Build in daylight with at least 4 hours remaining.

## Heated Shelters — Fire Inside

Any enclosed shelter with a fire inside requires careful ventilation design. Carbon monoxide is odorless and kills without warning. Smoke inhalation causes long-term lung damage. The fire must have a dedicated air supply and exhaust path.

### Heated Lean-To

The simplest heated shelter — not fully enclosed, so ventilation is automatic.

1. Build a standard lean-to (see emergency-shelters.md).
2. Build a reflector wall behind the fire — a stack of green logs, stones, or earth piled opposite the lean-to opening.
3. The fire goes between the lean-to and the reflector. The reflector bounces radiant heat toward you while the lean-to traps some of it.
4. This is open enough that CO buildup isn't a concern, but you get significant radiant warming.

### Log Cabin with Fireplace

For a permanent structure:

1. Build the fireplace against an exterior wall or in a corner. Use stone, brick, or thick clay/cob construction. The firebox opening should face the interior.
2. Build a chimney above the fireplace — minimum 3 feet above the roof peak to create good draft. The chimney can be stone, clay pipe, stacked metal cans, or even a log chimney lined with thick clay (yes, a clay-lined wooden chimney works — the clay protects the wood from flame, though it needs regular inspection).
3. A damper (adjustable plate in the chimney) controls draft. Open for fire, partially close to retain heat after the fire dies down.
4. The hearth (floor in front of the fireplace) should be stone or clay — a non-combustible surface extending at least 18 inches in front of the firebox.

### Heated Sleeping Platform (Kang/Ondol)

A traditional Asian heated sleeping platform where hot smoke from a fire passes through channels under a raised platform before exiting through a chimney. The stone or earthen platform absorbs heat and radiates it upward all night.

1. Build a fire box at one end, below the platform level.
2. Construct channels (from stone or brick) running under the platform from the fire box to a chimney at the opposite end.
3. The platform surface (stone slabs, thick clay) sits on top of the channels.
4. Light a fire in the fire box. Hot gases travel through the channels, heating the platform, and exit through the far chimney.
5. The thermal mass of the platform retains heat for 8–12 hours after the fire dies.

This is one of the most efficient heating systems ever developed. It uses a fraction of the fuel of an open fire and delivers sustained, even warmth through the night.

### Improvised Stove

A metal container (55-gallon drum, large can, old oven) converted to a wood stove:

1. Cut a door opening in one end for loading fuel.
2. Cut a hole in the top or rear for the stovepipe (chimney).
3. Add legs to raise it off the floor (prevents scorching and allows airflow underneath for draft).
4. Run the stovepipe up and out through the roof. The pipe must pass through the roof with a safe clearance — wrap the pipe-to-roof junction with non-combustible material (metal flashing, clay, stone) and maintain at least 6 inches between the hot pipe and any wood or fabric.
5. A stovepipe that runs horizontally for a few feet inside the shelter before exiting radiates additional heat into the room.

> **WARNING:** Any fire inside an enclosed shelter requires ventilation. Even with a chimney, ensure a low-level fresh air intake (a small opening near the floor on the wall opposite the chimney). If anyone experiences headache, dizziness, or drowsiness, ventilate immediately — these are signs of carbon monoxide buildup.

## Cold Weather Sleep System

Your shelter is only half the equation. Your sleeping arrangement within the shelter determines whether you sleep or shiver.

### Ground Insulation

This cannot be overemphasized. The ground steals more heat than the air in cold conditions.

- Minimum 6 inches of compressible insulation — pine boughs, dry grass, dry leaves
- Layer boughs with tips up and butt ends toward the ground — creates air pockets
- A raised platform (logs, boards, packed snow) gets you completely off the ground
- Even a single layer of cardboard is better than nothing between you and frozen ground

### Sleeping Warm

1. **Remove wet clothes** before getting into your sleeping system. Sleep in dry base layers. Moisture destroys insulation value.
2. **Wear a hat.** You lose disproportionate heat from your head.
3. **Put tomorrow's clothes in the sleeping bag with you.** They'll be warm when you put them on instead of frozen.
4. **Hot water bottle.** Fill a water bottle with hot water, cap it, put it in the sleeping bag at your core (stomach/groin area). Radiates heat for hours. Also prevents your water from freezing overnight.
5. **Eat before bed.** Digestion generates body heat. A fatty, calorie-dense meal before sleep gives your body fuel to burn through the night.
6. **Don't hold your urine.** Your body spends energy keeping the urine warm. Get up and go. Keep a pee bottle inside the shelter if going outside is impractical.
7. **Exercise briefly** before getting into your bag — jumping jacks, pushups, anything that gets your heart rate up and warms your core. Get in the bag while warm.

## Emergency Cold Weather Priorities

If you're caught in cold without shelter:

1. Get out of wind immediately — even behind a rock or in a depression.
2. Insulate from the ground — sit on your pack, pile branches, anything between you and the earth.
3. Cover your head.
4. Huddle with others — shared body heat.
5. Keep moving if you can't shelter — activity generates heat. Stop only when you have adequate shelter to trap your body heat.
6. Build a fire if you can. Any fire. A fire and a reflector can keep you alive in extreme cold even without a shelter. See fire-starting-methods.md and fire-starting-wet-conditions.md.
