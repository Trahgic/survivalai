Emergency shelters keep you alive for days. Long-term structures keep you comfortable for months and years. When you've decided to stay in a location, investing the time to build a proper structure pays off enormously in quality of life, health, and efficiency. These methods use materials available in most environments with hand tools only.

## Choosing a Method

Pick based on what's available:

- **Trees/forest:** Log cabin or timber frame
- **Clay/earth soil:** Cob, rammed earth, or earth-bag
- **Rocks/stone:** Stone wall construction
- **Mixed environment:** Wattle and daub, or hybrid methods
- **Salvage available:** Repurpose existing materials — pallets, corrugated metal, lumber from demolished structures

## Log Cabin — Basics

The classic homesteader structure. Requires trees, an axe (or saw), and patience. A small cabin (10×12 feet) takes 2–4 people about 2–4 weeks.

### Foundation

1. Clear and level the site. A slight slope for drainage away from the structure is fine.
2. Lay foundation logs (sill logs) on flat rocks, concrete blocks, or leveled stumps. Keep the wood off the ground — contact with soil causes rot. Raise the sill at least 6–12 inches off the ground.
3. Alternatively, build stone pier foundations at each corner and midpoints. Dry-stacked flat stones or concrete block pillars.

### Walls

1. Select straight logs 6–10 inches in diameter. Uniform size makes stacking easier. Remove bark (bark holds moisture and harbors insects).
2. Notch the logs at the corners. Common notch types:
   - **Saddle notch:** Cut a rounded cup on the underside of each log that sits over the log below. Simple, effective, traditional.
   - **Square notch:** Cut a flat notch halfway through each log. Interlocking flat surfaces. Easier to cut with a saw but takes more time.
3. Stack logs alternating directions at each corner, building walls up evenly.
4. Fill gaps between logs (chinking) with moss, mud, clay, or a mixture of clay, sand, and grass/straw. Let it dry, reapply as it cracks. Modern chinking uses flexible caulk, but mud works fine.
5. Stagger seams — don't stack the gaps between logs directly above each other, or water channels straight through the wall.

### Roof

The simplest approach: extend two gable-end walls upward in a triangular shape, lay a ridge beam across the peaks, then lay rafters from the ridge to the wall tops. Cover with whatever roofing you have — see roofing-improvised.md.

### Door and Windows

Cut openings after the walls are up. Brace the cut logs on both sides of the opening with vertical framing boards spiked into the log ends to prevent shifting. Hang a door from leather hinges, rope hinges, or salvaged hardware.

Windows: cut an opening, frame it, cover with clear plastic, glass (if available), or leave open with a shutter for closing at night and in storms.

## Earth-Bag Construction

Polypropylene bags (sandbags, grain bags, feed bags) filled with earth and stacked like bricks. Surprisingly strong — earth-bag structures have withstood earthquakes. Requires minimal tools and uses the most abundant building material on the planet: dirt.

### Process

1. Fill bags with slightly damp, tamped soil (subsoil with clay content works best — topsoil has too much organic matter). Fill about 90% full.
2. Fold the bag top closed. Lay the bag flat and tamp it firm with a tamper (a flat piece of wood on a handle, or a flat rock).
3. Lay bags in courses like bricks. Offset each course (stagger the seams).
4. Lay two strands of barbed wire between each course. The barbs grip the bag fabric and lock courses together — this is the key engineering feature that makes earth-bag walls structural.
5. Continue stacking. For curved walls (dome shapes), each course is slightly inset from the one below — this creates a natural dome or arch without formwork.
6. Plaster the exterior with a cement-sand mix, lime plaster, or earth plaster (clay-sand-straw) to protect the bags from UV degradation (polypropylene breaks down in sunlight within a year if unprotected).

### Advantages

- Nearly indestructible walls. Bulletproof, fireproof, earthquake-resistant.
- Materials cost nearly zero if you're filling with site soil.
- No special skills needed — if you can fill a bag and stack it, you can build.
- Thermal mass — thick earth walls absorb heat during the day and release it at night, moderating interior temperature.

## Cob Construction

Cob is a mixture of clay, sand, straw, and water sculpted into walls without forms or molds. It's essentially free-form earthen building — you're making walls out of glorified mud, and the result is a structure that can last centuries.

### Mixing

1. Test your soil: make a test ball from the subsoil. If it cracks when it dries, add more sand. If it crumbles, add more clay. The ideal mix is roughly 70% sand, 30% clay.
2. Mix soil with water on a tarp — stomp it with your feet (traditional method). Add straw as you mix — the straw provides tensile strength (like rebar in concrete).
3. The mix should be stiff enough to hold its shape when formed into a ball, but wet enough to stick to itself.

### Building

1. Lay a stone or gravel foundation — cob must not sit on bare ground (it wicks moisture and erodes).
2. Form the cob mix into loaf-sized lumps ("cobs") and stack them on the foundation.
3. Build walls 12–24 inches thick. Press each new layer into the one below, blending the seams. Poke holes through with a stick to create a mechanical bond between layers.
4. Build 12–18 inches per day maximum — let each section firm up before adding more, or the weight causes slumping.
5. Trim and shape the walls with a machete or flat tool as they firm.
6. Embed window and door frames as you build around them.

### Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:** Free material, sculpt any shape, excellent thermal mass, fireproof, can last 500+ years with a good roof.

**Limitations:** Labor-intensive (a small structure takes weeks). Doesn't work in very wet climates without significant roof overhang. Weak in earthquakes unless reinforced. Must be protected from rising damp (foundation) and rain erosion (plaster and roof overhang).

## Stone Construction

Where rocks are abundant, dry-stack stone walls are among the most durable structures possible.

### Principles

1. Sort stones by size. Use the largest, flattest stones for the base.
2. Build two parallel faces (inner and outer wall) with the gap between filled with smaller rubble. This creates a thick, stable wall.
3. **Lay stones flat, not standing.** Wide face down. Each stone should rest stable without rocking.
4. **Break the joints.** Every stone should bridge the gap between the two stones below it. Continuous vertical seams create weak lines.
5. Place long "bond stones" that extend from the inner face to the outer face periodically. These tie the two wall faces together.
6. Mortar (lime, clay, or cement) strengthens the wall enormously but isn't strictly necessary for low structures.
7. Corners: interlock stones from alternating walls, like log cabin corners.

### Without Mortar

Dry-stack stone walls (no mortar) have been used for thousands of years. They flex in earthquakes instead of cracking, drain water instead of trapping it, and can be disassembled and rebuilt. Skill is in selecting and placing stones so they lock together under gravity. Batter (leaning) the wall inward slightly — 1 inch per foot of height — for stability.

## Wattle and Daub

A framework of woven sticks (wattle) plastered with a mud mixture (daub). Fast, uses minimal materials, and is effective for walls in mild to warm climates.

### Wattle

1. Drive vertical stakes into the ground or into a sill beam, spaced 6–12 inches apart.
2. Weave flexible branches (willow, hazel, any green bendy wood) horizontally through the stakes, alternating in front of and behind each stake. Like weaving a basket.
3. Pack the weaving tight — the denser the wattle, the better the daub adheres.

### Daub

1. Mix clay-heavy soil with water, chopped straw, and sometimes animal dung (adds binding fiber and reduces cracking).
2. Force the daub into the wattle from both sides simultaneously, pressing through the gaps to create a solid wall.
3. Build up in layers, letting each layer partially dry before adding the next.
4. Final thickness: 3–6 inches. Smooth the exterior surface.
5. Whitewash with lime for weather protection and aesthetics.

## General Construction Principles

- **Overhang the roof.** The number one killer of natural buildings is water at the base. A roof overhang of 12–24 inches throws rainwater away from the walls.
- **Raise the foundation.** Get the walls off the ground with a stone, gravel, or concrete foundation. Rising damp destroys earth buildings.
- **Ventilation.** Include openings for airflow. Stagnant air in a sealed building promotes mold, condensation, and respiratory problems.
- **Plan for fire.** Include a safe location for a heating fire or stove with a chimney or smoke hole. See cold-weather-shelters.md for heated shelter designs.
- **Start small.** A 10×12 foot single-room structure is manageable for 1–2 people to build in weeks. Adding rooms later is easier than building big from the start.
