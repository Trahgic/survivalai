Fish are one of the most reliable food sources in a survival setting. They're predictable (they live in water — you know where they are), catchable with improvised gear, calorie-dense, and available year-round in most environments. You don't need a rod and reel. Humans caught fish for thousands of years with sticks, string, bones, and rocks.

## Handline Fishing

A hook, a line, and something for bait. That's all you need.

### Improvised Hooks

**Wire:** Bend a small piece of wire (paper clip, safety pin, picture hanging wire) into a J-shape. File or grind a point.

**Thorns:** Acacia, hawthorn, honey locust, and barberry thorns are naturally curved and sharp. Tie them to the line at the base.

**Bone or antler:** Carve a small hook shape from a bone splinter. Grind the point sharp on a rock.

**Gorge hook:** The oldest hook design. A short piece of bone, wood, or thorn sharpened at both ends. Tied to the line at the center. Bait hides it. The fish swallows it, you pull, and it turns sideways in the throat. Effective for larger fish.

**Soda can tab:** Bend it to create a hook shape. Surprisingly effective and ready-made.

**Safety pin:** Open it, bend the sharp end outward at a hook angle. Ready to fish in seconds.

### Line

Anything strong and thin. Fishing line is ideal but:
- Dental floss (unwaxed is less visible to fish)
- Thread or string doubled/tripled for strength
- Paracord inner strands (each white strand is strong enough for panfish)
- Unraveled fabric threads braided together
- Natural cordage: nettle fiber, inner bark strips
- Shoelaces

### Bait

**Live bait is almost always better than artificial.**
- Worms — dig in moist soil, under logs and rocks, in compost
- Grubs and larvae — found under bark, in rotting wood
- Insects — grasshoppers, crickets, beetles, flies
- Small frogs or tadpoles
- Minnows or small fish (catch with a cloth or shirt used as a seine)
- Crawfish — found under rocks in streams
- Meat scraps, fish guts from previous catch
- Bread dough rolled into balls (if you have bread)

**Improvised lures:**
- A piece of bright cloth or foil on a hook
- A small spinner made from a soda can cut into an oval and bent slightly
- Feathers tied to a hook (improvised fly)

### Technique

1. Tie the hook to the line. Use a clinch knot: thread through the hook eye, wrap the line around itself 5 times, thread the end back through the first loop, pull tight.
2. Add weight 6–12 inches above the hook. A small rock tied on, a split shot, or a nut with a hole works.
3. Add a float 2–4 feet above the hook if you want to fish at a specific depth. A small piece of wood, cork, or sealed plastic bottle cap.
4. Cast or lower the baited hook into the water. Fish near structure: fallen trees, undercut banks, rocks, weed beds, bridge pilings. Fish hang out where they have cover and food flows to them.
5. Best fishing times: early morning (dawn to 2 hours after), late evening (2 hours before dusk), and overcast days. Midday sun drives fish deep.
6. Let the fish take the bait. When you feel a pull or see the float go under, wait 1–2 seconds for the fish to turn the bait in its mouth, then set the hook with a firm pull (not a yank).

## Trotline

A trotline is a main line stretched across a body of water with multiple hooks hanging from short branch lines (dropper lines). It's a force multiplier — one trotline with 10 hooks is 10x more productive than one handline.

1. Stretch a strong line across a river, stream, or between two points along a lake shore. Tie each end to something solid (tree, stake, rock).
2. Tie dropper lines every 3–5 feet along the main line. Each dropper is 12–24 inches long with a hook at the end.
3. Bait every hook.
4. Set the main line so the hooks hang at various depths — some near surface, some mid-water, some near bottom. Different fish species feed at different levels.
5. Check every few hours. Remove fish, re-bait empty hooks.
6. A trotline works while you sleep, build shelter, or do other tasks.

## Fish Traps

### Funnel Trap (Minnow Trap)

Works on the principle that fish swim in through a funnel but can't find the small opening to get back out.

1. Use a plastic bottle: cut the top third off, invert it into the body (funnel pointing inward), and secure with wire or string. Poke small holes for water flow. Add bait inside.
2. Larger version: build a basket from sticks, wire, or woven branches. Make a cone-shaped entrance that narrows to a small opening. Fish enter the wide end of the cone and can't navigate back through the narrow exit.
3. Place in a stream, river, or lake near shore. Weight it down so it doesn't float away.
4. Bait with bread, fish guts, crushed snails, or anything smelly.

### Weir (Fish Dam)

A weir channels fish into a confined area where they're easy to catch.

1. Build a V-shaped wall of rocks across a stream, with the point of the V facing downstream.
2. At the point of the V, leave a narrow opening that leads into a small holding pen (a circle of rocks).
3. Fish swimming upstream enter the wide mouth of the V and are funneled toward the narrow gap. Once in the holding pen, they can't find their way back out.
4. Harvest from the pen by hand, net, or spear.
5. In tidal areas, build the weir at mid-tide level. Fish swim in at high tide and are trapped as the water recedes.

## Gill Net

A gill net is a wall of fine mesh hung vertically in the water. Fish swim into it, get their gills caught in the mesh, and can't back out. Gill nets are the most efficient fishing method by far — but they're hard to improvise from scratch.

**Mesh size determines what you catch:** Small mesh (1–2 inch stretch) catches panfish. Medium mesh (3–4 inch) catches bass-sized fish. Large mesh (5+ inch) catches big fish.

**Improvised net material:** Paracord inner strands, fine wire, dental floss, unraveled netting from produce bags, or any thin cord tied in a square mesh pattern. Making a full gill net from scratch is hours of tedious work, but even a small section (4–6 feet long, 2–3 feet deep) catches fish.

**Setting:** Stretch the net across a narrow point in a stream, a channel between islands, or along a productive shoreline. The top edge needs floats (wood pieces, sealed bottles). The bottom edge needs weights (rocks tied on).

## Spearfishing

Effective in shallow, clear water. Takes practice because light refraction makes fish appear to be in a different position than they actually are — aim below where you see the fish.

**Improvised spear:**
1. Cut a straight pole 6–8 feet long.
2. Split the end into 4 prongs by making two perpendicular cuts about 6 inches deep.
3. Wedge small sticks into the splits to spread the prongs apart.
4. Sharpen each prong.
5. The multi-prong design compensates for aiming errors.

**Technique:** Wade slowly. Wait for fish to settle. Strike fast, straight down, pinning the fish against the bottom. Don't throw — thrust.

## Hand Fishing (Noodling)

Reaching into underwater holes and pulling catfish out by hand. Catfish hide in submerged logs, rock crevices, and bank holes. Feel around the hole, and when you feel the fish, jam your hand into its mouth and grip. The fish will clamp down — catfish have small, sandpaper-like teeth that won't cause serious injury.

> **WARNING:** Before reaching into a hole in unfamiliar water, consider what else might be in there — snapping turtles, water snakes, muskrats, or beaver. In the southern US, water moccasins and alligator snapping turtles are real risks.

## Night Fishing with Light

Fish are attracted to light at night. A torch, lantern, or flashlight held over the water draws insects, which draw baitfish, which draw larger fish.

1. Position a light source over the water from a bank, dock, or boat.
2. Fish will congregate in the lit area within 15–30 minutes.
3. Catch them by handline, net, or spear.
4. Fire on the bank reflected onto the water also works.

## Cleaning Fish

1. Kill the fish with a sharp blow to the head.
2. Scale: hold the fish by the tail, scrape from tail to head with a knife blade held at an angle. Scale before gutting — it's easier.
3. Gut: insert the knife tip into the vent (anal opening) and cut forward to the gills. Remove the entrails. Save the guts for bait.
4. Remove the gills — they spoil first and give the fish a bitter taste.
5. Rinse in clean water.
6. Cook immediately or preserve (smoke, salt, dry). Fish spoils faster than any other meat.

**Eating raw fish:** Freshwater fish carry parasites (tapeworms, flukes) that infect humans. Always cook freshwater fish. Saltwater fish are lower risk for parasites but can still carry them. If you must eat fish raw (no fire), freeze it first if possible (kills parasites) or accept the risk.
