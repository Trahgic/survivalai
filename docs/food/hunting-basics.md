In a survival situation, small game is your primary protein source — not deer. Rabbits, squirrels, birds, and rodents are more abundant, easier to catch with improvised tools, and less dangerous to pursue. Trapping is more energy-efficient than active hunting because traps work while you sleep. Set multiple traps and check them daily.

## Trapping vs. Hunting

**Trapping advantages:** Works 24/7, requires no stalking skill, covers more area (set 10 traps = 10 chances), low energy expenditure, low risk.

**Hunting advantages:** Faster results if you spot game, can target larger animals, works when you can't set traps (traveling through an area).

For sustained survival, trap. For opportunistic feeding, hunt. Ideally, do both.

## Simple Snares

A snare is a loop of wire, cordage, or string that tightens around an animal as it passes through. Snares are the most productive improvised traps.

### Basic Fixed Snare

1. Make a loop from snare wire (brass or steel wire, 20–24 gauge, is ideal), paracord, fishing line, or any strong thin cordage. The loop should be about fist-sized for rabbit, smaller for squirrel.
2. Create a small loop at one end (about ½ inch), thread the other end through it to form a noose.
3. Attach the free end securely to a stake, tree, or heavy rock.
4. Set the snare on a well-worn game trail, at the mouth of a burrow, or in a gap in brush. The bottom of the loop should be 2–4 inches off the ground for rabbits (roughly 3 finger-widths).
5. Use sticks or brush to create a funnel on both sides of the snare, guiding the animal through the loop. Animals follow the path of least resistance.
6. The animal walks through, the loop tightens around its neck, and its forward momentum cinches it tight.

### Spring Snare (Twitch-Up)

More effective than a fixed snare because it lifts the animal off the ground (preventing it from chewing through the cord and keeping it away from predators).

1. Find a young sapling or flexible branch near the trail.
2. Bend it down and attach a trigger mechanism — a notch-and-peg system where the tension of the bent sapling is held by two interlocking pieces of wood.
3. Tie the snare loop to the trigger via a line to the bent sapling.
4. When the animal hits the snare, the trigger releases, the sapling springs up, and the animal is lifted and strangled.

**Trigger styles:**
- **Peg and notch:** Drive a peg into the ground. Tie the snare line to a horizontal toggle stick that hooks under the peg. Tension from the sapling keeps the toggle engaged. The snare loop connects to the toggle — when pulled, the toggle unhooks.
- **Figure-4 trigger:** Three sticks notched to interlock in an unstable arrangement. Pressure from the snare loop or a bait platform releases the mechanism.

### Where to Set Snares

Placement is everything. A snare in the right spot catches animals passively.

- **Game trails:** Look for worn paths through grass, compressed leaf litter, tracks, droppings. Set snares in narrow points along the trail.
- **Gaps in natural barriers:** Animals squeeze through gaps in fences, brush piles, fallen logs. Put the snare in the gap.
- **Water crossings:** Animals use consistent crossing points at streams. Set snares on the approaches.
- **Burrow entrances:** Rabbit warrens, groundhog holes. Set the snare right at the opening.
- **Between feeding and bedding areas:** Game trails connecting these areas get regular traffic.

Set as many snares as you can build — 10 or more. Check them at least once daily, ideally morning and evening. An animal left in a snare too long gets taken by predators, and unchecked traps cause unnecessary suffering.

## Deadfall Traps

A heavy weight (rock, log) propped up with a trigger mechanism. When an animal takes the bait or bumps the trigger, the weight falls and crushes it. Effective for small to medium game.

### Simple Figure-4 Deadfall

1. Find a flat rock weighing at least 5x the target animal's weight. For a squirrel, 5+ lbs. For a rabbit, 15+ lbs.
2. Carve three sticks:
   - **Upright:** Flat notch near the top.
   - **Diagonal (trigger):** Notch at the top to interlock with the upright, pointed at the bottom.
   - **Horizontal (bait bar):** Notch at one end to interlock with the upright, bait attached to the other end. This bar extends under the rock.
3. Assemble so the three sticks form a figure-4 shape, supporting the heavy rock in a propped-up position.
4. When the animal tugs or bumps the bait bar, the figure-4 collapses and the rock falls.
5. Place bait appropriate to the target: nuts and seeds for squirrels, vegetation for rabbits, meat scraps for larger predators.

## Hunting Small Game

### With Improvised Weapons

**Throwing stick / rabbit stick:** A sturdy stick 2–3 feet long, slightly curved. Throw sidearm at ground-dwelling game from 15–30 feet. Aim low — you want it skipping across the ground to sweep the animal's legs. Practice before you need it.

**Slingshot:** A forked branch with elastic material (surgical tubing, rubber strips, bungee cord). Effective to 30+ feet with practice. Use smooth round stones about ½ inch diameter.

**Spear:** A straight shaft 6–8 feet long, sharpened at one end or with a knife lashed to it. Better for fish and frogs than for fast-moving game. Can be thrown or used to pin.

**Rocks:** Seriously. A fist-sized rock thrown accurately kills small game. Ancient humans did this for millions of years. Collect a pile and throw at roosting birds, sunning lizards, or stationary game.

### Stalking Principles

- Move slowly. Stop frequently. Animals detect movement faster than shape.
- Stay downwind. Approach from the direction the wind is blowing toward you. Animals smell you before they see you.
- Stay low. Your upright silhouette is the most unnatural shape in the woods.
- Move when the animal's head is down (feeding, drinking). Freeze when it looks up.
- Dawn and dusk are peak activity times for most game.

## Field Dressing (Gutting)

Once you've killed an animal, field dress it immediately. Internal organs decompose fast and taint the meat.

### Small Game (Rabbit, Squirrel)

1. Lay the animal on its back. Pinch the skin at the center of the belly and make a small cut — just through the skin, not into the abdominal cavity.
2. Insert two fingers under the skin to lift it away from the organs, then cut from the pelvis to the ribcage. Keep the blade edge up to avoid puncturing the guts.
3. Reach in and pull the intestines, stomach, liver, and lungs out as one mass. They come out easily with a gentle pull. Save the heart and liver — they're excellent eating.
4. Cut around the anus to free the lower intestines.
5. Wipe the cavity clean with grass or a cloth. Don't wash with water unless you have plenty — moisture promotes bacterial growth.
6. If not cooking immediately, hang in a cool, shaded spot with the cavity open for airflow.

### Skinning Rabbit

1. After gutting, cut through the skin around the middle of the body (belt cut).
2. Get your fingers under the skin and pull — one hand pulls the skin toward the head, the other pulls toward the tail. Rabbit skin peels off like removing a sweater.
3. Cut off the head, feet, and tail.
4. Rinse and cook.

### Large Game (Deer)

Field dressing a deer follows the same principles, just bigger. See butchering-and-processing.md for the full guide. Key points:

1. Dress immediately — within 30 minutes in warm weather.
2. Roll the animal on its back, propped against a slope if possible.
3. Cut from the pelvis to the sternum, being very careful not to puncture the stomach or intestines (the green bile and stomach contents taint the meat with a foul taste that can't be removed).
4. Split the pelvis bone (a saw or hatchet helps) to fully access the lower digestive tract.
5. Cut the diaphragm (the membrane separating the chest from the abdomen) along the rib walls.
6. Reach up into the chest and cut the windpipe and esophagus as high as you can reach.
7. Roll the animal on its side and let the entire gut pile slide out.
8. Prop the cavity open with sticks for cooling.
9. In warm weather, get the hide off and the meat into shade as fast as possible. Meat with the hide on retains heat and spoils rapidly.

## Cooking Game

- **Always cook wild game thoroughly.** Wild animals carry parasites (trichinosis in wild boar and bear, tularemia in rabbits, tapeworms in many species) that are killed by cooking to internal temperature of 165°F (74°C).
- **Rabbit starvation:** Rabbit meat is extremely lean — almost no fat. If rabbit is your primary food source, you'll develop protein poisoning (rabbit starvation): diarrhea, headache, fatigue, low blood pressure. You MUST supplement with fat from other sources. Eat the organ meats (higher fat), render fat from any available source, or supplement with nuts, seeds, or oily plants.
- **Boiling is the most efficient cooking method.** It captures all the nutrients in the broth, which you drink. Roasting and grilling lose fat and juices to the fire.
- **Save the bones.** Boil them for bone broth — crack long bones first to release marrow. Bone broth provides minerals (calcium, phosphorus), gelatin, and fat.

## Game Laws and Ethics

In a true survival emergency, game laws don't apply — survival takes priority. But in a scenario where society is still partially functioning, understand that poaching carries serious penalties. Trapping without a license and hunting out of season are crimes in normal times.

In any scenario, don't waste. Use as much of the animal as possible — meat, organs, bones, hide, sinew. Waste in a survival situation is both unethical and strategically stupid.
