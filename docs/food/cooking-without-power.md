No electricity doesn't mean no cooking. Humans have cooked food over fire for 400,000 years. You just need heat, fuel, and a way to control both. The method you choose depends on what fuel is available, how much you're cooking, and whether smoke is a concern.

## Open Fire Cooking

The most basic method. Works, but wastes a lot of fuel and heat.

### Fire Layouts for Cooking

**Log cabin fire:** Stacked logs in alternating layers. Burns evenly, creates a flat cooking surface on top, and produces good coals quickly.

**Keyhole fire:** A fire circle connected to a narrow channel (the keyhole shape). Burn the fire in the circle, rake coals into the channel for cooking. The narrow channel concentrates heat under your pot.

**Two-log fire:** Place two thick logs parallel, 4–6 inches apart. Build the fire between them. Pots and pans sit on top of the logs directly over the flames. As the logs burn, you push them closer together.

**Platform fire:** Build a fire on top of a platform of green logs if the ground is wet, snowy, or you need to elevate the heat source.

### Cooking Over Coals vs. Flames

Coals are better than flames for cooking. Flames are uneven, hard to control, and blacken food. Let the fire burn down to a bed of hot coals, then cook over those.

Coals provide steady, consistent heat and last longer than flames. Rake coals to create heat zones — a thick bed for high heat, a thin layer for low heat, and an area with no coals for warming or resting food.

### Improvised Cooking Surfaces

- **Flat rock:** Heat a flat, dry rock beside or over the fire. Cook directly on the surface — eggs, flatbread, thin meat. Make sure the rock is dry — wet rocks (especially river rocks) can explode from steam pressure when heated.
- **Green wood grill:** Lay green (live) sticks across two supports over the coals. They'll char slowly but won't burn through as quickly as dry wood.
- **Metal salvage:** Car hoods, oven racks, metal shelving — any flat metal works as a griddle or grill surface.

## Rocket Stove

A rocket stove is the most fuel-efficient cooking system you can build from scrap. It uses about 1/4 the wood of an open fire and produces almost no smoke once it's running.

### How It Works

An L-shaped combustion chamber: fuel goes in horizontally, burns at the elbow, and hot gases shoot straight up through an insulated chimney to the cooking surface. The insulated chimney creates a powerful draft that pulls air through the fuel chamber, achieving near-complete combustion.

### Construction

**Cinder block version (easiest):**
1. Stack 4 cinder blocks in an L-shape — two on the bottom forming the horizontal fuel feed, two on top forming the vertical chimney.
2. Leave the top open for the pot. The pot should sit on top of the chimney with a small gap around the edges for exhaust.
3. Pack dirt, clay, or ash around the outside for insulation.

**Tin can version:**
1. Use a large (#10) can for the chimney and a smaller can for the fuel feed.
2. Cut a hole in the side of the large can near the bottom, sized to fit the smaller can.
3. Insert the smaller can as the horizontal fuel feed.
4. Fill the space between the large can and an even larger outer container with insulation — ash, dirt, sand, perlite, or vermiculite.
5. Feed small sticks into the horizontal tube. The fire burns at the junction and hot gases rise up the chimney.

**Key principles:**
- Insulate the chimney. An uninsulated chimney loses heat to the walls instead of sending it up. Insulation keeps the gases hot, which maintains the draft.
- Size the fuel opening for small sticks (thumb-diameter). Rocket stoves are designed for small fuel — not logs. Break or cut wood into small pieces.
- Once established, a rocket stove should produce almost no visible smoke. If it's smoking, the combustion is incomplete — ensure adequate airflow through the fuel inlet.

## Dakota Fire Hole

A below-ground fire system that's wind-resistant, produces minimal smoke and light signature, and uses fuel efficiently. Originally used by Plains indigenous peoples.

1. Dig a primary hole about 1 foot in diameter and 1 foot deep. This is the fire chamber.
2. Dig a second, smaller hole (6 inches diameter) about 1 foot away, connected to the first hole by a tunnel at the bottom. This is the air supply.
3. The air hole should be on the upwind side — wind blows down the air hole, through the tunnel, and feeds the fire from below.
4. Build the fire in the main hole. Place the cooking pot across the top of the fire hole.
5. The underground fire is hidden from view (reduces light signature at night), protected from wind, and the tunnel provides excellent draft for hot, efficient combustion.

**Limitation:** Doesn't work well in wet ground, very rocky soil, or areas with a high water table.

## Solar Cooker

No fuel required — just sunlight. A solar cooker concentrates sunlight to generate enough heat for cooking (250–400°F / 120–200°C depending on design).

### Box Cooker

1. Find or build a box — cardboard, wood, or any rigid material. About 2 feet square.
2. Line the inside with reflective material — aluminum foil, space blanket, or even the shiny side of chip bags.
3. Paint or coat the inside bottom of the box flat black (or place a dark-colored pot inside).
4. Cover the top with clear glass or plastic (the greenhouse effect traps heat inside).
5. Angle reflective flaps (made from foil-covered cardboard) around the opening to direct more sunlight into the box.
6. Place food in a dark, covered pot inside the box.
7. Aim the opening toward the sun. Adjust every 30–60 minutes to track the sun.
8. Cooking temperature: 250–350°F. Cooking time: 2–4 hours for most meals. It's slow but works.

### Parabolic Cooker

More powerful than a box cooker but harder to build.

1. Shape a concave reflective surface — a satellite dish lined with foil, a bent sheet of reflective metal, or mirrored material formed into a parabolic curve.
2. Position the pot at the focal point of the parabola (where sunlight concentrates). For a 3-foot parabolic dish, the focal point is roughly 12–18 inches in front of the center.
3. Can reach temperatures above 500°F — hot enough to fry.
4. Requires adjustment every 15–20 minutes to track the sun.

> **WARNING:** Parabolic solar cookers concentrate enough energy to cause severe burns and eye damage. Never look at the focal point. Keep children and pets away.

## Earth Oven (Ground Oven / Hangi / Umu)

Cook large quantities of food underground using hot rocks. Used traditionally across Polynesia, New Zealand, and the Americas.

1. Dig a pit 2–3 feet deep and wide enough for your food.
2. Build a large fire in the pit. Place river-smooth rocks (fist-sized to head-sized) in the fire. Heat for 1–2 hours until rocks are glowing.
3. Remove remaining wood and embers, leaving the hot rocks.
4. Lay a bed of green leaves (banana leaves, corn husks, large non-toxic leaves) over the rocks.
5. Place food on the leaves. Whole root vegetables, wrapped meat, corn, squash — anything that benefits from slow, moist cooking.
6. Cover with more green leaves, then a wet cloth or burlap, then a layer of dirt to seal in the heat and steam.
7. Cook for 3–8 hours depending on the amount. Don't open to check — you lose all the heat. Estimate and trust the process.
8. Dig up and serve.

> **WARNING:** Do NOT use river rocks that may contain trapped moisture — they can explode violently when heated. Use rocks from dry, elevated areas. Limestone and sandstone are safer than river-tumbled rocks. If a rock hisses or steams when first heated, remove it before it explodes.

## Reflector Oven

A wall of reflective material positioned beside a fire to radiate heat onto food from the side — like a mini broiler.

1. Build a flat reflective surface — a sheet of aluminum, foil-covered board, or flat rock.
2. Prop it at an angle beside the fire, facing the cooking area.
3. Reflected heat cooks food from the fire side while the reflector cooks it from the opposite side — more even cooking than direct fire.
4. Good for roasting meat, baking flatbread, and drying food.

## Cooking Vessels

If you have no pots or pans:

- **Direct on coals:** Wrap food in large, non-toxic green leaves (banana, grape, plantain, corn husk) and place directly on coals. Steam-cooks inside the leaf wrapper.
- **Hot rock boiling:** Heat rocks in a fire, then use sticks or tongs to drop them into a container of water (a hollowed log, animal skin bag, bark container, or even a hole lined with an animal hide). The rocks boil the water. Replace cooled rocks with fresh hot ones.
- **Green bamboo:** Cut a section of bamboo with one intact node at the bottom (to hold water). Fill with water and food. Lean over the fire or set beside it. The bamboo chars on the outside but holds water inside long enough to cook the contents.
- **Clay pot:** Form a pot from clay, let it dry, then fire it in your cooking fire. Primitive but functional.
- **Tin cans:** Any can becomes a pot. Use a stick through two holes near the rim as a handle.
