Long-term food storage is insurance against disruption. Whether it's a natural disaster, economic collapse, or extended grid-down scenario, having months or years of food stored properly means you eat while everyone else scrambles. The key is storing the right foods, in the right containers, in the right conditions.

## Calorie Planning

Before you buy anything, figure out how much you actually need.

**Minimum survival:** 1,200 calories per day per person. You'll lose weight and energy, but you'll live.

**Functional baseline:** 2,000 calories per day per person. Enough for light activity.

**Active/working:** 2,500–3,500 calories per day. If you're chopping wood, hauling water, and building shelter, you burn more.

**Per-person annual baseline (2,000 cal/day):** 730,000 calories per year.

For a family of 4 for 1 year at 2,000 calories: you need roughly 2.9 million calories stored.

## The Staples — What to Store in Bulk

These foods provide the calorie foundation. They're cheap, compact, and last decades when stored properly.

### White Rice

- **Shelf life:** 25–30 years in sealed, oxygen-free containers
- **Calories:** ~1,650 per pound
- **Notes:** White rice stores far longer than brown rice (brown rice has oils that go rancid within 6–12 months). White rice is the single best long-term calorie-per-dollar investment.
- **Storage:** Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers inside 5-gallon buckets. Or sealed #10 cans.

### Hard Red Wheat (Wheat Berries)

- **Shelf life:** 30+ years properly stored
- **Calories:** ~1,500 per pound
- **Notes:** Must be ground into flour for most uses (need a grain mill). Whole wheat berries last far longer than pre-ground flour (flour has 1–2 year shelf life). Also can be sprouted for fresh greens.
- **Storage:** Same as rice — mylar bags with oxygen absorbers in buckets.

### Dried Beans (Pinto, Black, Navy, Lentils)

- **Shelf life:** 25–30 years for calorie value; flavor and texture degrade after 8–10 years (beans get harder to cook — they'll still provide nutrition but take longer to soften)
- **Calories:** ~1,500 per pound
- **Notes:** Combined with rice, beans provide complete protein (all essential amino acids). Rice + beans is the foundation of food storage for a reason.
- **Storage:** Mylar with oxygen absorbers. Lentils cook faster than other beans — good for fuel conservation.

### Rolled Oats

- **Shelf life:** 25–30 years in oxygen-free storage
- **Calories:** ~1,700 per pound
- **Notes:** Versatile — eat as porridge, grind into flour, add to bread dough.
- **Storage:** Mylar with oxygen absorbers.

### Sugar

- **Shelf life:** Indefinitely (sugar doesn't spoil)
- **Calories:** ~1,725 per pound
- **Notes:** Provides quick energy, preserves other foods (jam making, fruit preservation), improves palatability of bland storage foods. Hardens into a brick in dry conditions — break it up, it's still fine.
- **Storage:** Original bag inside a sealed bucket is fine. Doesn't need oxygen absorbers.

### Salt

- **Shelf life:** Indefinitely
- **Notes:** Not a calorie source but essential for survival (electrolyte balance), food preservation (curing, brining), and flavor. Store more than you think — you'll use it for preserving meat and vegetables, not just seasoning.
- **Storage:** Keep dry. Any sealed container works.

### Cooking Oil

- **Shelf life:** 1–2 years unopened (shorter than dry goods — this is the weak link in most food storage plans)
- **Calories:** ~4,000 per pound (most calorie-dense food there is)
- **Notes:** Coconut oil lasts longer than vegetable oil (3+ years sealed). Olive oil lasts about 2 years. Shortening (hydrogenated vegetable oil) lasts 3–5 years. You need fats — a diet of only grains and beans without fat leads to malnutrition.
- **Storage:** Cool, dark. Rotate regularly. This is the item most likely to go bad in your stockpile.

### Powdered Milk

- **Shelf life:** 20–25 years in sealed, oxygen-free storage (nonfat powdered milk)
- **Calories:** ~1,600 per pound
- **Notes:** Important calcium and protein source. Whole milk powder has a shorter shelf life (2–5 years) due to fat content.

## Packaging for Long-Term Storage

### Mylar Bags + Oxygen Absorbers + Buckets

This is the gold standard for home food storage.

1. **5-gallon food-grade HDPE buckets** with gamma seal lids (spin-on, reusable) or standard snap-on lids. Food-grade is important — non-food-grade buckets can leach chemicals.
2. **Mylar bags (5 mil thickness or thicker):** Line the inside of the bucket with a mylar bag. Mylar blocks light and oxygen far better than plastic alone.
3. **Oxygen absorbers:** Drop in the appropriate size (2,000 cc for a 5-gallon bag of grain/beans). These chemically absorb oxygen inside the sealed bag, creating a near-vacuum that prevents oxidation and kills insects (any eggs or larvae in the food die without oxygen).
4. **Seal the mylar bag** with a hot iron, flat iron, or heat sealer. Press firmly across the entire opening. Check for wrinkles that could let air in.
5. **Snap the bucket lid on.** The bucket provides physical protection for the mylar bag.

### #10 Cans

The commercial standard for long-term food. Requires a can sealer (expensive for home use but some LDS and community canneries offer the service).
- Steel can with enamel lining
- Oxygen absorbers inside
- Shelf life: 25–30 years for dry goods

### Vacuum Sealing

Good for 3–5 year storage. Not as long as mylar + oxygen absorbers because vacuum bags aren't completely gas-impermeable — small amounts of oxygen permeate over time.

## Storage Conditions

The enemies of stored food: heat, moisture, light, oxygen, and pests.

**Temperature:** For every 10°F (5.5°C) decrease in storage temperature, shelf life roughly doubles. Stored at 60°F, food lasts about twice as long as at 70°F. A cool basement is the ideal location. Garages (temperature swings) and attics (heat) are poor choices.

**Moisture:** Keep relative humidity below 15% for stored dry goods. The mylar bag handles this, but the storage area itself should be dry. Don't store on concrete floors — place on wooden pallets or shelving.

**Light:** Breaks down nutrients and accelerates fat rancidity. Store in dark containers in dark locations.

**Pests:** Mice and rats chew through mylar bags easily. The bucket provides the rodent barrier. Metal containers are best if rodents are a concern.

## Rotation System

**FIFO: First In, First Out.** Label everything with the date stored. Use the oldest food first.

For short-term rotation (1–5 year items like canned goods, oil, peanut butter): build into your regular diet. Buy what you eat, eat what you store. When you use a can of beans from storage, replace it with a new one at the back of the shelf.

For long-term items (25+ year shelf life): check annually. Look for damaged containers, pest intrusion, or moisture. No rotation needed if properly packaged — just verify integrity.

## What About Freeze-Dried Food?

Commercial freeze-dried meals (Mountain House, Augason Farms, etc.) are convenient and have 25–30 year shelf life. They're also expensive — roughly $3–10 per meal vs. pennies per meal for bulk staples.

**Use freeze-dried as a supplement to bulk staples, not as the foundation.** A few cases of freeze-dried meals provide variety, convenience, and morale. But a year's supply of freeze-dried food costs $3,000–5,000+ per person, while a year of bulk staples costs $300–500.

## Common Mistakes

- **Storing only rice and beans.** You'll survive but you'll be miserable and malnourished. Add fats, sugar, salt, and variety.
- **No rotation.** Oil and canned goods expire. Rotate them.
- **Storing in the garage.** Temperature swings destroy shelf life.
- **Forgetting water.** Food storage without water storage is useless. See water-storage.md.
- **Not storing what you eat.** If you've never cooked dried beans, a crisis isn't the time to learn. Store foods you know how to prepare.
- **No way to cook.** Stored grain without a way to grind it and cook it is just seeds. Include a manual grain mill and cooking supplies.
