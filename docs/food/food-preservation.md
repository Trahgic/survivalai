Without refrigeration, fresh food lasts days at best. Preserved food lasts months to years. Every civilization in history figured this out, and the methods haven't changed much — you're removing moisture, killing bacteria, or creating an environment where they can't grow. The more methods you know, the less food you waste.

## Drying / Dehydration

The oldest preservation method. Bacteria, mold, and yeast need moisture to grow. Remove enough moisture (below 10–15% water content) and food becomes shelf-stable for months to years.

### Sun Drying

Works best in hot, dry climates with low humidity. Not practical in humid environments.

1. Cut food into thin, uniform slices — ¼ inch or thinner. Thinner dries faster and more evenly.
2. Lay slices in a single layer on a clean rack, screen, or frame that allows air circulation on all sides. Don't lay food directly on the ground.
3. Cover with a thin cloth (cheesecloth, mesh, window screen) to keep flies and insects off while allowing airflow.
4. Place in direct sunlight with good air movement. Elevate the rack if possible.
5. Turn pieces once or twice daily.
6. Drying takes 2–4 days depending on thickness, humidity, and temperature.
7. Food is done when it's leathery (fruit) or brittle (vegetables, meat). It should snap, not bend. If it bends, it still has moisture.

### Smoke Drying

Smoke contains chemicals (phenols, formaldehyde, acetic acid) that inhibit bacteria and add flavor. Combined with heat and airflow, smoking is one of the most reliable field preservation methods.

**Cold smoking (60–90°F / 15–32°C):** Flavors and partially preserves. Used for fish, sausage, cheese. Takes 1–14 days depending on the product. Cold-smoked food still needs cooking or another preservation step.

**Hot smoking (126–275°F / 52–135°C):** Cooks AND preserves. Used for meat, fish, poultry. Takes 4–12 hours.

**Building a field smoker:**
1. Dig a fire pit about 1 foot deep and 1 foot across.
2. Dig a trench or lay a pipe from the fire pit to your smoking chamber — 4–8 feet long. This separates the heat source from the food, cooling the smoke.
3. The smoking chamber can be a barrel, wooden box, tarp teepee, or even a hole in the ground with a grate over it.
4. Hang or lay food on racks inside the chamber.
5. Burn hardwood — hickory, oak, apple, cherry, maple, mesquite. Never use pine, spruce, or other softwoods (resinous, bitter, potentially toxic). Never use pressure-treated wood.
6. Maintain thin, wispy smoke — not billowing clouds. Thick white smoke means insufficient oxygen and deposits creosote (bitter, potentially carcinogenic) on the food.
7. For hot smoking, maintain chamber temperature of 150–200°F. Use a thermometer if possible.

### Jerky

The quintessential survival food. Properly made jerky lasts 1–2 months without refrigeration, longer if vacuum-sealed.

1. Use lean meat — fat goes rancid and spoils jerky. Trim all visible fat. Deer, elk, beef, goat, and rabbit all work well.
2. Cut with the grain into strips ¼ inch thick, 1–2 inches wide, any length.
3. Optional: marinate in a salt-heavy brine (¼ cup salt per quart of water, with whatever seasoning you have) for 4–12 hours. Salt draws out moisture and inhibits bacteria.
4. Dry using any method: smoke, sun, or low oven/fire heat (150–170°F / 65–77°C).
5. Done when the strip bends and cracks but doesn't break in half. It should be dry through with no moist spots in the center.
6. Store in a dry, sealed container. Check periodically for mold — if you see it, the jerky wasn't dry enough.

## Salting and Curing

Salt kills bacteria through osmosis — it draws water out of both the food and any bacteria on it. Salt concentration above 10% inhibits most bacteria. Above 20% inhibits almost everything.

### Dry Salting (Meat and Fish)

1. Use non-iodized salt if available (iodized salt works but can cause off flavors in large quantities).
2. Cut meat or fish into pieces no thicker than 2 inches.
3. Coat every surface generously with salt — ¼ to ½ inch layer. You can't use too much.
4. Layer in a container: salt, meat, salt, meat, salt. End with salt on top.
5. Place a weight on top to press out moisture.
6. After 24 hours, pour off the liquid (brine) that has accumulated. Re-salt any spots that look bare.
7. Continue for 5–14 days depending on thickness. Thicker pieces need longer.
8. Rinse excess salt off the surface before eating. Soak in fresh water for a few hours if it's too salty.
9. Hang in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area to dry further. Salt-cured meat keeps for months.

### Brining (Wet Curing)

1. Make a brine strong enough to float a raw egg — about 1 cup of salt per gallon of water.
2. Submerge meat completely. Weigh it down so nothing floats above the surface.
3. Store in a cool place (below 40°F / 4°C is ideal, but any cool spot works for shorter cures).
4. Small pieces (1 inch): 3–5 days. Large pieces (3+ inches): 10–14 days.
5. Rinse before eating.

## Fermentation

Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria (lactobacillus) to convert sugars to lactic acid, creating an acidic environment that prevents spoilage. Fermented foods are often more nutritious than the original — fermentation creates B vitamins and makes minerals more bioavailable.

### Sauerkraut (Fermented Cabbage)

1. Shred cabbage finely. Remove the core.
2. Weigh the cabbage. Add 2% salt by weight (about 1 tablespoon per pound of cabbage).
3. Massage the salt into the cabbage for 5–10 minutes until it releases liquid and becomes limp.
4. Pack tightly into a clean jar or crock, pressing down so the liquid rises above the cabbage.
5. All cabbage must stay submerged below the brine. Use a weight — a smaller jar filled with water, a rock, or a ziplock bag filled with brine.
6. Cover loosely (gas must escape — don't seal airtight or the container will explode).
7. Keep at room temperature (60–75°F / 15–24°C). Fermentation starts within 1–3 days (you'll see bubbles).
8. Taste after 1 week. Ferment for 2–6 weeks to your preference. Longer = more sour.
9. Once fermented to your liking, seal and store in a cool place. Properly fermented sauerkraut keeps for 6+ months.

This same process works for any vegetable: carrots, radishes, green beans, peppers, cucumbers (to make pickles), beets, garlic, onions.

### Fermented Fish (Fish Sauce / Preserved Fish)

1. Layer fresh fish or fish scraps with salt — 30% salt by weight (roughly 1 part salt to 3 parts fish).
2. Pack into a sealed container.
3. Store in a warm place for 3–12 months. Yes, months.
4. The result is a liquid fish sauce and a paste. Both are protein-rich, shelf-stable, and used as seasoning. Southeast Asian fish sauce (nam pla, nuoc mam) is made exactly this way.

## Canning

Canning preserves food by sealing it in an airtight container after heating to kill all bacteria. Done correctly, canned food lasts 2–5 years or longer.

### Water Bath Canning (High-Acid Foods Only)

Safe for: fruits, jams, jellies, pickled vegetables, tomatoes (with added acid), fermented foods.

1. Sterilize jars by boiling for 10 minutes. Keep lids hot but don't boil (rubber seals degrade).
2. Fill hot jars with hot food, leaving ½ inch headspace at the top.
3. Remove air bubbles by running a knife around the inside edge.
4. Wipe jar rims clean. Place lids and screw bands on fingertip tight.
5. Submerge jars in boiling water — water should cover lids by 1–2 inches.
6. Boil for the required time (typically 10–25 minutes depending on food and jar size).
7. Remove jars and let cool undisturbed for 12–24 hours. The lids should pop down (concave) as they cool, indicating a vacuum seal.
8. Test the seal by pressing the center of the lid. If it doesn't flex, it's sealed. If it clicks up and down, it didn't seal — refrigerate and eat within a week, or reprocess.

> **CRITICAL:** Low-acid foods (meat, poultry, fish, most vegetables, soups, stews) MUST be pressure-canned. Water bath canning does NOT reach high enough temperatures to kill Clostridium botulinum spores. Botulism toxin is odorless and tasteless, and it's one of the most potent poisons known. If you water-bath-can low-acid food, you are creating a potential death jar. Use a pressure canner (15 PSI, 240°F / 116°C, for the required time) or use a different preservation method.

## Root Cellaring

Not processing food — just storing it in conditions that slow spoilage. A root cellar maintains cool (32–40°F / 0–4°C), humid (80–95%) conditions year-round.

**Foods that store well in a root cellar:**
- Root vegetables: potatoes (dark, 38–40°F), carrots, turnips, beets, parsnips — packed in damp sand, they last 4–6 months.
- Onions and garlic: cool and dry (50–60% humidity), not in sand. Hang in mesh bags with good airflow. Last 6–8 months.
- Winter squash and pumpkins: cure in warmth (80°F) for 2 weeks first, then store at 50–55°F. Last 3–6 months.
- Apples: keep away from potatoes and onions (ethylene gas causes sprouting). Last 2–5 months depending on variety.
- Cabbage: hang upside down by the root, or wrap in newspaper. Lasts 3–4 months.

**Improvised root cellar:**
- Dig a hole 4+ feet deep (below the frost line). Line with straw or leaves for insulation.
- Use a buried trash can or barrel — insulate around it with straw.
- A north-facing hillside dugout works well. The earth insulates naturally.
- Always include ventilation — a small pipe from the cellar to the surface. Without air exchange, decomposing food depletes oxygen and produces carbon dioxide.

## Oil/Fat Preservation (Confit)

Fully cooked meat submerged in rendered fat is sealed from air and bacteria. Duck confit, pork rillettes, and potted meats all use this principle.

1. Cook meat thoroughly — slow braised or simmered until falling apart.
2. Pack into clean jars.
3. Pour melted rendered fat (lard, tallow, duck fat, butter) over the meat until completely covered with no air gaps.
4. Let the fat solidify. It forms an airtight seal.
5. Store in a cool place. Keeps 2–6 months depending on temperature.
6. Once opened, eat within a week.
