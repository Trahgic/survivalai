Wild edible plants are everywhere in temperate regions — most people walk past food daily without knowing it. But plant identification mistakes can be fatal. Learn a few plants well rather than many poorly. Start with the ones that are abundant, easy to identify, and have no dangerous lookalikes.

> **CRITICAL:** Never eat a plant you haven't positively identified. "Pretty sure" gets people killed. If you have any doubt, use the Universal Edibility Test (see poisonous-plants.md) or skip it. There are enough safe, easy-to-identify plants that you never need to gamble.

## High-Value Edibles — Learn These First

### Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

Found everywhere — lawns, fields, roadsides, cracks in pavement. One of the most nutritious wild plants available.

**Identification:** Jagged, deeply lobed leaves growing in a rosette from a central point at ground level. Single yellow flower head on a hollow stem. White milky sap when stem is broken. Fluffy seed head (the "wish flower" kids blow on).

**Edible parts:** Everything. Young leaves (raw in salad or cooked as greens — older leaves are bitter but still edible). Flowers (eat raw, batter and fry, or make into tea). Roots (roast and grind as a coffee substitute, or boil as a root vegetable).

**Season:** Spring through fall. Best eating in early spring before flowering (less bitter).

**Lookalikes:** Cat's ear and chicory look similar but are also edible, so misidentification isn't dangerous.

### Cattail (Typha latifolia and T. angustifolia)

Found in marshes, pond edges, ditches, and wet areas. Called the "supermarket of the swamp" because almost every part is edible year-round.

**Identification:** Tall (3–10 feet), grass-like leaves growing from the base. The distinctive brown, cigar-shaped seed head on a stiff stalk is unmistakable.

**Edible parts:**
- **Spring shoots:** Pull young shoots from the base. Peel outer leaves to reach the tender white core. Eat raw or cooked. Tastes like mild cucumber.
- **Pollen (early summer):** Shake the yellow pollen from the top of the flower spike into a bag. Mix with flour for baking. High in protein.
- **Green flower heads (early summer):** Boil and eat like corn on the cob when they're still green and immature.
- **Roots (year-round):** Pull or dig the rhizomes. Peel, crush, and soak in water — the starch settles to the bottom. Pour off the water and dry the starch for flour. Or roast the roots directly.

**Lookalikes:** Iris leaves look somewhat similar but don't grow the seed head. Iris roots are toxic. The distinctive brown seed head makes cattail almost impossible to misidentify once you know it.

### Plantain (Plantago major and P. lanceolata)

Not the banana-like fruit — this is a common lawn and trailside weed.

**Identification:** P. major has broad, oval leaves with prominent parallel veins, growing in a rosette at ground level. P. lanceolata has narrow, lance-shaped leaves. Both produce a thin flower spike on a leafless stalk.

**Edible parts:** Young leaves raw or cooked. Older leaves are tough — cook them. Seeds can be ground into flour. Leaves also have medicinal use as a poultice for insect stings and minor wounds.

**Season:** Spring through fall.

### Clover (Trifolium species)

Red and white clover grow everywhere — lawns, fields, roadsides.

**Identification:** Three round leaflets on a stem. White or pink/red round flower heads. Everyone has seen clover.

**Edible parts:** Flower heads (raw or dried for tea — very nutritious). Young leaves (raw or cooked). Seeds can be sprouted. Dried flower heads can be ground into flour.

**Note:** Eat in moderation — large quantities raw can cause bloating. Cooking or drying reduces this.

### Lamb's Quarters (Chenopodium album)

One of the most nutritious wild greens. More iron, protein, and vitamin C than spinach.

**Identification:** Upright plant, 1–5 feet tall. Diamond-shaped to triangular leaves with slightly toothed edges. Young leaves have a whitish, powdery coating (like flour dust) on the underside. Small green flowers clustered at the top.

**Edible parts:** Leaves (cooked like spinach — eat raw in small amounts). Seeds (tiny, black — can be ground into flour). The entire young plant is edible.

**Season:** Spring through fall. Best when young.

### Acorns (Quercus species — all oaks)

Acorns from any oak species are edible after processing. They're high in calories (especially fat and carbohydrates) and were a staple food for many indigenous cultures.

**Processing (required — raw acorns contain bitter tannins):**
1. Shell the acorns. Discard any with holes (weevil larvae) or mold.
2. Crush or grind the nutmeats.
3. Leach the tannins: soak ground acorn meal in water, changing the water every few hours until the bitterness is gone (cold water leaching takes 1–3 days, hot water leaching takes hours but may affect texture).
4. Dry the leached meal for flour, or use wet as a porridge.
5. White oak group (rounded leaf lobes) has less tannin and needs less processing than red oak group (pointed leaf lobes).

### Wild Garlic / Ramps (Allium species)

**Identification:** Smells like garlic or onion when leaves are crushed. This is the key identifier — if it doesn't smell like onion/garlic, it's NOT a wild allium and may be toxic. Broad, smooth leaves, often with a reddish stem. Grows in colonies in moist woods.

**Edible parts:** Everything — leaves, bulbs, flowers. Use like domestic garlic/onion. 

> **WARNING:** Death camas and lily of the valley look superficially similar to wild garlic but are highly toxic. THE SMELL TEST IS ESSENTIAL. If crushed leaves don't smell strongly of garlic or onion, do not eat the plant.

## Nuts and Seeds

**Black walnut:** Hard to crack but extremely nutritious. Distinctive round green husks that stain everything black. Harvest in fall.

**Hickory nuts:** Sweet, edible raw. Shagbark hickory is easiest to identify (peeling bark). Crack and pick nutmeats.

**Pine nuts:** Seeds from pinecones of certain species (pinyon pine is the classic). Harvest cones in fall, dry them, shake out the seeds.

**Sunflower seeds:** Wild sunflowers produce smaller seeds than domesticated varieties but they're abundant and nutritious.

## Berries — The Safe Ones

**Blackberries/Raspberries (Rubus species):** Compound berries on thorny canes. Everyone knows what these look like. No dangerous lookalikes.

**Blueberries/Huckleberries (Vaccinium species):** Small round blue-black berries. No dangerous lookalikes among the blue berries — if it looks like a blueberry and grows on a bush, it's probably safe.

**Elderberries (Sambucus nigra):** Clusters of small dark purple/black berries on large bushes/small trees. Cook before eating — raw elderberries cause nausea. Flowers are also edible.

**Wild strawberries (Fragaria virginiana):** Tiny versions of domestic strawberries. No dangerous lookalikes.

**Wild grapes (Vitis species):** Grow on vines. Seeds inside. Eat fresh, dry as raisins, or make into juice.

> **WARNING:** As a general rule in North America: aggregate berries (like blackberry/raspberry structure) are all safe. White and yellow berries should be avoided — about 90% are toxic. Red berries are a coin flip. Blue and black berries are mostly safe but exceptions exist. Never rely on color alone — identify the specific species.

## Roots and Tubers

**Burdock (Arctium species):** First-year roots are edible — peel, slice, boil or roast. Taste like artichoke hearts. Identified by huge leaves and the hooked burrs that stick to clothing.

**Jerusalem artichoke / Sunchoke (Helianthus tuberosus):** Knobby tubers growing under a tall, sunflower-like plant. Eat raw or cooked. Found in disturbed soil, roadsides, field edges.

**Chicory (Cichorium intybus):** Tap root is roasted and ground as a coffee substitute. Blue flowers along roadsides are distinctive. Young leaves are edible as greens (bitter).

## Greens

**Chickweed (Stellaria media):** Small, low-growing plant with tiny white star-shaped flowers. Entire plant is edible raw. Mild flavor. Found in gardens, fields, and disturbed soil.

**Sorrel (Rumex species):** Arrow-shaped leaves with a sour, lemony taste. Eat raw or cooked. Contains oxalic acid — don't eat in huge quantities if you have kidney problems.

**Violet (Viola species):** Heart-shaped leaves and purple/blue/white flowers. Both leaves and flowers are edible raw. High in vitamin C.

**Watercress (Nasturtium officinale):** Grows in running water — streams, springs. Peppery flavor. Eat raw. Wash thoroughly — water-borne parasites can cling to leaves if the water source is contaminated with animal waste.

## What to Avoid

See poisonous-plants.md for the full list, but the most common deadly plants in temperate North America:

- Water hemlock (Cicuta) — most poisonous plant in North America. Looks like wild carrot/parsley. Found in wet areas. One bite of the root can kill.
- Poison hemlock (Conium maculatum) — purple-spotted smooth stems, carrot-like leaves, musty smell. The plant that killed Socrates.
- Death camas (Zigadenus) — grass-like leaves, white flowers, looks like wild onion but NO onion smell.
- Nightshade berries (Solanum species) — small, round, shiny berries in clusters. Green, black, or red depending on species and ripeness.
- Pokeweed (Phytolacca) — young shoots are edible if boiled in multiple changes of water. Roots, mature plants, and berries are toxic. Not recommended unless experienced.
