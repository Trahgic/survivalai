Without refrigeration, the rules for food safety change dramatically. Bacteria double every 20 minutes in the "danger zone" (40–140°F / 4–60°C). Food that's safe at breakfast can be dangerous by lunch. Understanding what spoils fast, what keeps, and how to detect spoilage prevents the food poisoning that can incapacitate or kill you in a survival situation.

## The Danger Zone

Bacteria that cause food poisoning grow fastest between 40°F and 140°F (4–60°C). At room temperature (roughly 70°F / 21°C), bacteria double every 20 minutes. After 2 hours in the danger zone, bacterial counts reach problematic levels. After 4 hours, food should be considered unsafe.

**The 2-hour / 1-hour rule:**
- Below 90°F (32°C): discard food left out more than 2 hours
- Above 90°F (32°C): discard food left out more than 1 hour

This applies to cooked food, cut fruit, dairy, eggs, meat, and anything that bacteria thrive on. It does NOT apply to foods that are inherently shelf-stable (see below).

## What Spoils Fast (Eat Immediately or Preserve)

**Meat and fish:** Raw meat starts to go bad within hours at room temperature. Cooked meat lasts slightly longer (bacteria were killed by cooking, recontamination takes time) but still no more than 4–6 hours without refrigeration in warm weather. Cook only what you'll eat in one sitting unless you're preserving the rest.

**Dairy:** Milk, cheese (soft), yogurt. In the absence of refrigeration, consume immediately after obtaining. Hard cheeses (cheddar, parmesan) last longer — weeks to months if kept cool and dry. Wax-coated cheeses last even longer.

**Eggs:** Unwashed eggs with the natural bloom (protective coating) intact last 2–4 weeks without refrigeration. Washed eggs (typical US supermarket eggs) spoil faster — use within a few days without refrigeration. Store in a cool, dark place. The float test works: place in water — if it sinks and lies flat, it's fresh. If it stands on end, it's older but edible. If it floats, discard it.

**Cooked rice and grains:** Bacillus cereus spores survive cooking and germinate quickly at room temperature. Cooked rice left out for more than 2 hours is a common food poisoning source. Cook only what you'll eat, or dry/dehydrate leftovers immediately.

**Cut fruits and vegetables:** Once the skin is broken, bacteria have access. Eat immediately or within a few hours.

## What Keeps Without Refrigeration

**Whole, intact fruits and vegetables:** Apples, oranges, potatoes, onions, garlic, squash, carrots — these have natural protective skins and keep for days to months depending on conditions. Store in a cool, dark, well-ventilated area.

**Dried foods:** Jerky, dried fruit, dehydrated vegetables, dried beans, rice, flour, pasta. If moisture content is below 10–15%, bacteria can't grow. Keeps for months to years in sealed containers.

**Canned goods:** Shelf-stable for 2–5+ years. Commercial canning kills all bacteria including spores. Once opened, treat as perishable.

**Honey:** Never spoils. Literally. Edible honey has been found in Egyptian tombs. It may crystallize — warm gently to re-liquify.

**Salt-cured and smoked foods:** Properly cured meats, fish, and other products. See food-preservation.md.

**Fermented foods:** Sauerkraut, kimchi, pickles in brine. The acidic environment inhibits harmful bacteria. Keep submerged in brine.

**Nuts in the shell:** Last months. Shelled nuts go rancid faster (exposure to air oxidizes the oils) — keep sealed.

**Hard crackers, pilot bread, hardtack:** Low moisture, long shelf life. Hardtack has been military trail food for centuries because it essentially doesn't spoil.

**Peanut butter:** Sealed jar lasts months. High oil content eventually goes rancid, but it's slow.

**White rice:** Properly stored, white rice lasts 25–30 years. Brown rice has oils that go rancid — use within 6 months.

## Detecting Spoilage

**Trust your senses — they evolved for this.**

**Smell:** The most reliable sense for detecting spoilage. Sour, ammonia, sulfur (rotten eggs), or generally "off" smells mean bacterial decomposition. If it smells wrong, it IS wrong. Discard it.

**Visual:** Mold (fuzzy patches — white, green, black, or blue), slime on surfaces, color changes (grey meat, green discoloration), bubbling or swelling in containers (gas from bacterial fermentation).

**Touch:** Slimy or sticky surface on meat or vegetables. Mushy texture where there should be firmness.

**Taste:** Only if smell and visual pass. A sour or "off" taste means stop eating. Spit it out. BUT — some bacterial toxins are tasteless and odorless (botulism toxin, for example). Taste alone is not a guarantee of safety.

> **WARNING:** Botulism toxin is odorless, colorless, and tasteless. It's produced by Clostridium botulinum in low-oxygen, low-acid environments — typically improperly canned food. Signs of botulism in canned food: bulging or swollen lids, hissing when opened, foamy or murky liquid, food that smells slightly off. When in doubt about home-canned low-acid food, boil it for 10 minutes before eating. Boiling destroys botulism toxin (but not the spores).

## Extending Food Life Without a Fridge

### Cool Storage

Even without electric refrigeration, you can keep food cooler than ambient temperature:

- **Evaporative cooler (zeer pot):** Place a small clay pot inside a larger one. Fill the gap with wet sand. Cover with a wet cloth. As water evaporates from the sand and cloth, it cools the inner pot by 15–25°F below ambient. Works best in dry climates. Re-wet the sand twice daily.
- **Root cellar or underground storage:** Ground temperature a few feet down stays 50–60°F year-round in most climates. See food-preservation.md for root cellar construction.
- **Spring or stream cooling:** Seal food in waterproof containers and submerge in cold running water. Stream water is typically 50–60°F.
- **Shade and airflow:** At minimum, store food in the shadiest, most ventilated area available. Direct sun dramatically accelerates spoilage.
- **Nighttime cooling:** In dry climates with large day-night temperature swings, expose food containers to night air (covering to prevent dew and insects), then insulate during the day.

### Other Methods

- **Cook thoroughly and eat immediately.** The safest strategy. Don't store cooked food — cook only what you'll consume.
- **Re-cook leftovers:** If you must save cooked food, bring it back to a full boil before eating again. This kills bacteria that grew during storage (but doesn't destroy toxins already produced by some bacteria, like Staphylococcus aureus).
- **Keep it covered.** Flies carry bacteria directly from feces to food. Cover all food with cloth, mesh, or lids.
- **Separate raw and cooked.** Cross-contamination from raw meat to cooked food or ready-to-eat items is a major food poisoning source. Use different utensils, cutting surfaces, and containers.
- **Acid preservation:** Marinating or storing meat in vinegar or citrus juice inhibits bacterial growth. Ceviche — raw fish "cooked" in citrus acid — is a traditional preservation method.

## Shelf Life Without Refrigeration (Approximate)

| Food | Shelf Life (room temp) |
|------|----------------------|
| Raw meat/fish | 1–2 hours (warm), 4–6 hours (cool) |
| Cooked meat | 2–4 hours (warm), 6–12 hours (cool) |
| Eggs (unwashed, bloom intact) | 2–4 weeks |
| Hard cheese (wax-coated) | 2–6 months |
| Bread | 3–5 days (mold risk increases with humidity) |
| Fresh fruits/vegetables (whole) | Days to weeks depending on type |
| Dried meat (jerky) | 1–2 months |
| Dried fruits and vegetables | 6–12 months |
| White rice, dried beans | Years |
| Canned goods | 2–5+ years |
| Honey | Indefinitely |
| Salt, sugar | Indefinitely |
| Peanut butter (sealed) | 6–12 months |
