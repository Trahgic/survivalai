Killing an animal is just the first step. Proper butchering determines whether you get days of clean meat or a spoiled carcass full of bacteria. Speed matters — in warm weather, a deer can start spoiling within an hour if not field dressed. This covers the practical steps from field to food for the animals you're most likely to encounter.

## General Principles

1. **Field dress (gut) immediately after the kill.** Intestinal bacteria start migrating into the meat within minutes of death, especially in warm weather. The stomach and intestines are the primary contamination source.
2. **Cool the meat as fast as possible.** Open the body cavity to air, skin the animal in warm weather, hang in shade with airflow. Meat temperature needs to drop below 40°F (4°C) within a few hours ideally.
3. **Keep it clean.** Dirt, hair, leaves, and fecal matter introduce bacteria. Keep the exposed meat off the ground. Wipe don't wash — water promotes bacterial growth.
4. **Use everything.** Organ meats are more nutritious than muscle. Bones make broth. Hides make leather. Fat renders into cooking oil. In a survival situation, waste is a luxury you can't afford.
5. **Sharp knife.** A sharp knife makes clean cuts, wastes less meat, and is actually safer (less force = less slipping). Sharpen before you start. See sharpening.md.

## Deer (and Elk, Goat, Sheep)

### Field Dressing

1. Position the deer on its back, propped on a slope if possible with the head uphill.
2. Cut around the anus, freeing the lower intestine. Tie it off with string to prevent leakage.
3. For bucks, cut around the genitals and remove them.
4. Starting at the pelvis, make a shallow cut through the skin and abdominal wall up to the sternum (breastbone). Use two fingers under the blade to lift the skin away from the organs — you do NOT want to puncture the stomach or intestines. If you do, the green/brown contents will taint the meat with a foul taste.
5. Open the pelvis. In a young animal, you can spread it by hand. In an older animal, use a saw, hatchet, or heavy knife to split the pelvic bone.
6. Cut the diaphragm (the membrane separating the chest from the abdomen) along the rib walls on both sides.
7. Reach into the chest cavity and cut the windpipe and esophagus as high as you can reach. Cut with the blade facing away from you.
8. Roll the deer on its side. The entire gut pile — stomach, intestines, organs — should slide out with some pulling. Reach back and cut any remaining attachments.
9. **Save the heart and liver.** They're the most nutritious parts of the animal. The liver should be dark red-brown, smooth, and firm. If it has white spots, cysts, or looks diseased, discard it (parasites).
10. Prop the body cavity open with a stick to allow airflow and cooling.
11. Drain any blood from the cavity.

### Skinning

1. Hang the deer by the hind legs from a tree, gambrel, or improvised hanging bar. Cut a slit in each hind leg between the tendon and bone at the hock — pass a stick, rope, or hook through.
2. Cut the skin around each hind leg just below the hanging point.
3. Cut down the inside of each hind leg to the original belly incision.
4. Start peeling the hide downward. Use your fist between the hide and meat, pulling and pushing. Use the knife only where connective tissue holds tight — the less you cut, the cleaner the result.
5. Work the hide down over the shoulders and front legs. Cut around the front legs.
6. Pull the hide over the neck and cut at the head.
7. Trim any hair or debris from the exposed meat.

### Breaking Down the Carcass

With the skinned deer hanging:

1. **Remove the front legs.** There's no ball-and-socket joint connecting them to the body — the front legs are attached only by muscle. Cut between the leg and the ribcage, pulling the leg outward. The shoulder comes off cleanly. Each front leg gives you the shoulder roast and front shanks.

2. **Remove the backstraps (loins).** These run along both sides of the spine from the shoulder to the hip. They're the best meat on the animal. Cut along the spine on each side, following the bone, then cut along the tops of the ribs. Peel off two long strips of meat.

3. **Remove the tenderloins.** These are inside the body cavity, along the spine near the pelvis. Two small, tender cylinders. Often eaten first because they're small and deteriorate quickly.

4. **Remove the hind legs.** Cut through the ball-and-socket joint at the hip. Each hind leg gives you the rump roast, round steaks, and rear shanks. The hind legs have the most meat.

5. **Ribs and neck.** Trim any remaining meat from the ribcage. Ribs can be separated with a saw or heavy knife. The neck has good meat for stewing or grinding.

6. **Trim and sort.** Separate lean muscle from sinew and connective tissue. Pieces too small or tough for steaks go into the "stew and grinding" pile.

## Rabbit

The easiest large-ish animal to process. Takes about 5 minutes once you've done it a few times.

1. **Skinning:** Cut the skin around the midsection (belt cut). Insert fingers under the skin and pull — one hand toward the head, one toward the tail. Rabbit skin peels off smoothly. Cut off the head, feet, and tail.
2. **Gutting:** Small belly incision from pelvis to ribcage. Scoop everything out in one pull. Save the liver and heart.
3. **Sections:** For cooking, section into 5 pieces: two hind legs (cut at the hip), two front legs (pull away from the body and cut), and the saddle/back (the torso).
4. **Rabbit is extremely lean.** See the note about rabbit starvation in hunting-basics.md. You need supplemental fat.

## Poultry (Chicken, Duck, Goose, Turkey, Game Birds)

### Killing

For domestic birds: hold the bird by the legs, lay the neck across a chopping block, and remove the head with one firm stroke. Or hold the bird by the head and body and twist the neck sharply (cervical dislocation). The bird will thrash — this is nerve reflex, not consciousness.

### Plucking

1. Dip the bird in hot water (145–160°F / 63–71°C — NOT boiling) for 30–60 seconds. This loosens the feathers.
2. Pull feathers out in the direction they grow, starting with the large wing and tail feathers.
3. Small pin feathers can be singed off with a flame after plucking.

### Gutting

1. Cut off the feet at the ankle joint.
2. Cut around the vent (rear opening).
3. Make a horizontal cut between the vent and the breastbone, large enough to reach inside.
4. Reach in and pull the viscera out in one mass. The gizzard, heart, and liver are edible — save them.
5. Reach up into the neck cavity and remove the crop (a food-storage organ) and windpipe.
6. Rinse the cavity if you have water.
7. Cook whole, or section into pieces: breasts, thighs, drumsticks, wings, back.

### Without Plucking

If you're in a hurry, skip plucking and skin the bird instead. You lose the skin (and its fat and calories) but save 20 minutes of work. Cut through the skin, peel it off with the feathers attached.

## Fish

See fishing-improvised.md for the cleaning process. Key additional points:

- **Gut immediately.** Fish spoil faster than any other meat.
- **Small fish (panfish):** Can be cooked whole — head, bones, and all. The bones soften during cooking and are safe to eat (good calcium source).
- **Filleting:** For larger fish, cut behind the gill plate down to the spine, turn the blade, and run it along the spine to the tail. Flip and repeat. You get two boneless fillets.
- **Skin or no skin:** Leaving the skin on helps hold the fillet together during cooking. Some fish have tough or bad-tasting skin — remove it by laying the fillet skin-down and sliding the knife between skin and flesh.
- **Fish heads:** Boil for stock. Lots of collagen and flavor. The cheeks are prime eating on large fish.

## Organ Meats — Don't Waste These

Organ meats are significantly more nutritious than muscle meat. In a survival context, they're too valuable to discard.

**Liver:** Highest concentration of iron, vitamin A, and B vitamins of any food. Slice thin, cook quickly (overcooked liver is tough and bitter). Or dice and add to stews. Examine before eating — healthy liver is smooth, dark red-brown. Liver with white spots, cysts, hard nodules, or unusual coloration may indicate parasites or disease — discard it.

**Heart:** Lean, dense muscle. Excellent sliced thin and grilled, or cubed in stew. Heart tastes like mild, lean steak. Trim the fat cap and blood vessels from the top, slice open, rinse any clotted blood.

**Kidneys:** Slice in half, remove the white fatty core and any membrane. Soak in salt water for 30 minutes (reduces the strong flavor). Cook in stews or fry. Strong taste — best mixed with other meat.

**Tongue:** Boil for 2–3 hours until tender. Peel the thick outer skin (it comes off easily after cooking). Slice and eat — excellent tender meat.

**Brain:** High in fat and DHA. Scramble like eggs, or fry. Also used in brain tanning of hides (see tanning-and-leather.md).

**Bone marrow:** Split long bones (femur, humerus) and roast. Scoop out the cooked marrow — it's essentially pure fat and one of the most calorie-dense foods available from an animal.

## Fat Rendering

Animal fat is too valuable to waste in a survival situation. Render it into a clean cooking fat.

1. Cut fat into small pieces or grind it. Smaller pieces render faster.
2. Place in a pot over low heat. Add a splash of water to prevent scorching at the start.
3. Heat slowly. The fat melts out as liquid (tallow from beef/deer, lard from pork, schmaltz from poultry).
4. As the fat melts, small pieces of connective tissue float and eventually crisp (cracklings — they're edible and tasty).
5. When the cracklings are golden and floating, the rendering is done.
6. Strain through cloth into a clean container. The liquid cools into solid white fat.
7. Rendered fat stores for months in a cool place in a sealed container. It's your cooking oil, lamp fuel, soap ingredient, and leather preservative.

## Preserving the Meat

In a survival situation without refrigeration, you must preserve any meat you can't eat within a few hours:

- **Smoking:** Hot smoke for 4–12 hours. See food-preservation.md.
- **Drying/Jerky:** Cut thin, salt, dry. See food-preservation.md.
- **Salting:** Pack in salt. See food-preservation.md.
- **Cooking and eating immediately:** The simplest preservation is to cook and consume it all. In a group, a deer can be consumed in 2–3 meals if the group is large enough.
- **Cool storage:** Hang in shade, cool, breezy location. In cool weather (below 40°F / 4°C), a hung carcass keeps for days.
