Insects are the most abundant animal food source on the planet. They're high in protein (40–75% by dry weight), fat, and minerals. Over 2 billion people eat insects regularly. Your squeamishness is a cultural artifact, not a survival strategy. In a food-scarce situation, insects are the fastest, easiest, and most reliable animal protein available.

## Safe Insects — General Rules

**Eat these:**
- Crickets and grasshoppers
- Ants (most species)
- Beetle larvae (grubs) — found in rotting wood
- Termites
- Moth and butterfly larvae (caterpillars — with exceptions below)
- Bee and wasp larvae (found in the nest)
- Earthworms (technically not insects, but collected and eaten the same way)
- Cicadas
- Dragonflies and dragonfly nymphs
- Aquatic insect larvae (mayfly, caddisfly, stonefly nymphs — found under rocks in streams)

**Avoid these:**
- Brightly colored insects (reds, oranges, yellows) — bright colors often signal toxicity
- Hairy or fuzzy caterpillars — many have irritating or venomous hairs
- Insects that stink when handled — the smell is usually a chemical defense (stink bugs, blister beetles)
- Disease-carrying insects — flies, mosquitoes, ticks (obvious, but worth stating)
- Venomous insects — scorpions (edible if the stinger is removed), spiders (low meat yield, not worth the risk)
- Slugs and snails — technically edible but carry rat lungworm parasite. Only eat if thoroughly cooked (boil for 5+ minutes).

> **WARNING:** Always cook insects before eating. They can carry parasites, bacteria, and pesticide residues. Roasting, boiling, or frying kills parasites and improves flavor. Raw insect consumption is a last resort.

## Collection Methods

### Crickets and Grasshoppers

Most productive catch times are early morning when they're cold and sluggish.

1. Walk through tall grass slowly, watching for movement.
2. Cup your hand over them against the ground.
3. In the early morning, shake grass and bushes over a cloth or tarp — cold insects fall off.
4. Set a pitfall trap: bury a container (can, jar, plastic bottle with the top cut off) flush with the ground surface. Insects walk over the edge and fall in. Add a small amount of water in the bottom to prevent escape.
5. At night, set up a light source near a container of water. Insects are attracted to the light and fall into the water.

### Grubs (Beetle Larvae)

Found in rotting logs, under bark of dead trees, in the soil near decaying roots.

1. Break apart dead, decaying logs and look for white, C-shaped larvae. They range from fingernail-sized to thumb-sized.
2. Look under loose bark — bark beetles and their larvae leave visible tunnels.
3. Dig in soil near the base of dead trees.
4. Grubs are some of the highest-calorie insects — high in fat.

### Ants

1. Follow ant trails to the nest.
2. Place a stick into the nest entrance. Ants will swarm it. Pull the stick out and tap ants into a container of water (drowns them quickly).
3. Ant larvae and pupae (found inside the nest — white, rice-grain-shaped) are the real prize. More protein, milder flavor, better texture than adult ants.
4. Avoid fire ants and bullet ants — their stings make collection painful and dangerous.

### Termites

1. Break open a termite mound. Use a stick to extract termites.
2. Place a dampened piece of cloth or cardboard near a mound in the evening. Termites will colonize it overnight. Collect in the morning.
3. During swarming season (after rain in tropical areas), winged termites emerge in massive numbers and can be collected by the handful.

### Earthworms

1. Dig in moist soil, compost, or under rotting vegetation.
2. After rain, worms surface and can be collected from the ground.
3. Purge before eating: place worms in a container with damp grass for 24 hours. They'll empty their digestive tracts. This improves flavor significantly.

## Preparation

### Roasting

The easiest and most common method. Produces a crunchy, nutty result.

1. Spread insects on a flat rock, metal sheet, or in a dry pan near the fire.
2. Roast until they dry out and become crunchy — 5–10 minutes depending on size and heat.
3. Stir occasionally for even roasting.
4. Remove legs and wings from grasshoppers and crickets before or after roasting — the legs have small hooks that can irritate the throat.

### Boiling

1. Drop insects into boiling water for 5 minutes.
2. Remove and eat, or dry for storage.
3. Save the broth — it contains dissolved nutrients. Drink it as a protein-rich soup base.

### Drying for Storage

1. Roast or boil insects first.
2. Spread on a rack in the sun or near a fire until completely dry and brittle.
3. Dried insects keep for weeks to months in a sealed container.
4. Can be ground into powder and added to other foods — mixed into flour, stirred into soups, or eaten straight. This is the easiest way to overcome squeamishness.

## Nutritional Value

| Insect (per 100g dry weight) | Protein | Fat | Calories |
|------------------------------|---------|-----|----------|
| Crickets | 65g | 15g | ~400 |
| Grasshoppers | 60g | 10g | ~350 |
| Mealworm larvae | 50g | 30g | ~450 |
| Beetle grubs | 35g | 40g | ~500 |
| Termites | 35g | 45g | ~550 |
| Ants | 45g | 10g | ~300 |
| Earthworms | 60g | 10g | ~350 |

For comparison, beef jerky is about 50g protein and 350 calories per 100g. Insects are nutritionally competitive with conventional meat.

## Allergies

People allergic to shellfish (shrimp, crab, lobster) may also be allergic to insects — they share the protein tropomyosin. If someone has a known shellfish allergy, start with a very small amount of insect protein and watch for reactions.

## Overcoming the Mental Barrier

If you can't bring yourself to eat whole insects:

1. **Grind them into powder.** Mixed into other food, you won't taste or see them.
2. **Start with larvae/grubs.** They don't look like "bugs" and taste relatively neutral.
3. **Deep fry if you have oil.** Everything tastes better fried.
4. **Mix into strong-flavored food.** Stews, soups, sauces — the insect flavor disappears.
5. **Remind yourself of the facts.** Insects are eaten by billions of people. They're more sustainable than livestock. Your disgust is learned, not instinctive. And it's definitely not worth starving over.
