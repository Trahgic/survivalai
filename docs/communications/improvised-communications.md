When radios aren't available, you fall back on methods that predate electronics. Humans communicated across distances for thousands of years using light, sound, messengers, and written messages. These methods are slower but require zero technology and zero power.

## Visual Signals

### Signal Mirror

See signaling-for-rescue.md for detailed mirror technique. For communication (not just rescue):

Establish a pre-arranged code with your group. Simple binary system: one flash = yes/acknowledged. Two flashes = no/negative. Three flashes = come to my position. Continuous flashing = emergency.

Range: up to 50 miles on a clear day with a proper mirror. Even an improvised reflector (CD, foil, polished metal) works at 5–10 miles.

### Flag Signals (Semaphore — Simplified)

Full semaphore alphabet uses two flags in specific arm positions for each letter. For field use, simplify to a few critical signals:

- **Both flags raised overhead:** Attention / Message follows
- **One flag waved side to side:** Acknowledged / I see you
- **Both flags crossed overhead:** Emergency / Come immediately
- **One flag pointed in a direction:** Move that direction / Threat from that direction

Agree on meanings before separating. Use contrasting colors against the background — bright red or orange against green forest, white against dark sky.

### Smoke Signals

Beyond rescue signaling (see signaling-for-rescue.md), smoke can convey pre-arranged messages:

- One puff: attention / all clear
- Two puffs: come to my location
- Three puffs: danger / emergency
- Continuous smoke: position marker / I'm here

Create puffs by smothering a smoky fire with a wet blanket and removing it briefly. The blanket traps smoke, removing it releases a discrete puff. Timing and number convey the message.

## Sound Signals

### Whistle Codes

Establish a code with your group:

- One long blast: attention / rally
- Two short blasts: acknowledged / all clear
- Three short blasts: distress (universal)
- Repeated short blasts: warning / threat / come immediately
- One short + one long: message follows (followed by Morse or other code)

A whistle carries 1 mile in favorable conditions. Much farther than voice.

### Drum/Percussion

Beating on a hollow log, metal surface, or drum carries a rhythmic signal through forest that voice cannot penetrate. Establish rhythmic codes:

- Steady beat: all clear / check in
- Rapid beat: alert / come now
- Specific patterns: pre-arranged messages

African talking drums communicated complex messages across miles using tonal patterns. You don't need that sophistication — even simple coded rhythms work.

### Gunshots

Pre-arranged meaning. One shot = position marker. Two shots = all clear. Three shots = distress (universal). Rapid fire = contact / active threat.

Downside: gunshots carry far and attract anyone in the area, not just your group. They also consume irreplaceable ammunition. Use only when faster methods aren't available.

## Runner Systems

The oldest reliable long-distance communication: send a person with a message.

### Organizing Runners

1. **Relay stations:** Position people at intervals along a route. Each runner carries the message to the next station, where a fresh runner takes over. Like the Pony Express — the message travels faster than any single runner because no one has to cover the full distance.
2. **Message format:** Written messages are better than verbal. Verbal messages get garbled through relay. Write the message, fold and seal it, include the date/time and sender's name.
3. **Route planning:** Runners follow pre-established paths with known landmarks. Mark the route. Runners who get lost defeat the purpose.
4. **Security:** Runners are vulnerable. Vary routes if threat exists. Use pairs for dangerous routes. Encode sensitive messages.

### Message Content Protocol

Every message should include:
- Date and time written
- Sender (who wrote it)
- Recipient (who it's for)
- Priority (routine, urgent, emergency)
- The message (clear, concise — ambiguity kills in communication)
- Instructions (reply requested, acknowledge receipt, action required)

## Dead Drops and Message Caching

A dead drop is a pre-arranged location where messages are left for pickup by someone else. The sender and receiver don't need to be there at the same time.

### Setting Up Dead Drops

1. Choose locations known to your group but not obvious to outsiders. A specific tree hollow, a rock cairn with a gap, a specific fence post, a marked spot in a building.
2. Protect messages from weather — wrap in plastic, place in a sealed container.
3. Use a signal to indicate a message is waiting — a stone placed on top of a specific post, a stick arranged in a specific pattern, a chalk mark on a wall. Something natural-looking that only your group would recognize.
4. Retrieve messages promptly and remove the signal indicator.
5. Don't reuse the same dead drop indefinitely — if one is compromised, the entire system is compromised. Rotate locations periodically.

### Trail Markers

Leave directional and informational signs along travel routes for those who follow:

- **Stacked rocks (cairns):** Universal trail marker. Additional stones placed on specific sides indicate direction.
- **Broken branches:** A branch broken and left hanging points the direction of travel.
- **Scratches or marks on trees/rocks:** An arrow scratched into bark, a mark in a specific color.
- **Tied grass or strips of cloth:** On branches at eye height marking the route.

Establish a marking system with your group: what the marks look like, what they mean, and how to leave additional information (date, condition, warnings).

## Written Communication

### Code Systems

For sensitive information, use a simple code agreed upon in advance:

**Caesar cipher (simple letter shift):** Shift each letter forward or backward by an agreed number. Shift of 3: A→D, B→E, C→F, etc. "HELP" becomes "KHOS." Easy to use, easy to break with effort, but stops casual reading.

**Book cipher:** Both parties have the same book. Each word is encoded as page number, line number, word number. Nearly unbreakable without the key book. Requires both parties to have identical copies.

**One-time pad:** The theoretically unbreakable cipher. Both parties have identical lists of random numbers. Each number shifts the corresponding letter. Use each page of numbers only once, then destroy it. This is what real intelligence agencies use when security is paramount.

For most survival purposes, a simple code or even writing in an obscure shorthand is sufficient — you're protecting against casual interception, not professional cryptanalysis.

### Message Formats

**SALUTE report** (military standard for reporting information):
- **S**ize: How many people/vehicles/etc.
- **A**ctivity: What are they doing
- **L**ocation: Where are they
- **U**niform/equipment: What are they wearing/carrying
- **T**ime: When did you observe this
- **E**quipment: Notable equipment or vehicles

This format ensures reports are complete and standardized regardless of who writes them.

## Combining Methods

The most reliable communication plans use multiple methods:

1. **Primary:** Radio (HAM, GMRS, FRS, or Meshtastic)
2. **Alternate:** Runner with written message
3. **Contingency:** Dead drop system
4. **Emergency:** Visual/sound signals (smoke, mirror, whistle, gunshot)

If primary fails, move to alternate. If alternate fails, move to contingency. Emergency signals are for immediate, urgent situations.

This is called the PACE plan (Primary, Alternate, Contingency, Emergency). Establish it with your group before you need it. Practice each method.
