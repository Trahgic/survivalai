You don't need a HAM license to use two-way radio. Several radio services are available with no license (FRS, CB, MURS) or a simple no-exam license (GMRS). These have more limited range than HAM but are accessible to anyone, and the radios are cheap and widely available.

## FRS (Family Radio Service)

The "walkie-talkies" you buy at Walmart. No license required.

**Frequencies:** 462/467 MHz (UHF). 22 channels.

**Power:** 2 watts maximum (channels 1–7 and 15–22). 0.5 watts on channels 8–14.

**Range:** Realistic: ½–2 miles in developed areas, 1–5 miles in open terrain. Manufacturers claim "35 miles" — this is under perfect line-of-sight conditions that never exist in practice. Plan for 1–2 miles as your working range.

**Radios:** $20–60 per pair. Available everywhere — Motorola Talkabout, Midland, Cobra.

**Best for:** Short-range group communication. Perimeter security. Coordinating work parties within a camp or homestead. Communication between buildings or nearby positions.

**Limitations:** Short range. No repeater capability. Cannot legally modify or attach external antennas. Heavily used channels in populated areas — no privacy.

**Privacy codes (CTCSS/DCS):** The "privacy codes" on FRS radios do NOT make your communication private. They only filter what your radio plays through the speaker. Everyone on the channel can still hear you if they're not using a privacy code. These are squelch codes, not encryption.

## GMRS (General Mobile Radio Service)

The higher-power version of FRS. Requires a license ($35, no exam, covers your entire family for 10 years).

**Frequencies:** Same 462/467 MHz range as FRS. Shares channels 1–22 with FRS.

**Power:** Up to 50 watts (handheld typically 5 watts, mobile/base 25–50 watts).

**Range:** 5–25 miles depending on terrain, antenna, and power. GMRS repeaters exist and significantly extend range — up to 50+ miles through a well-placed repeater.

**Radios:** Handheld: $30–100. Mobile: $100–300. Compatible with FRS radios on shared channels.

**Best for:** Regional communication with more range than FRS. Small community networks. Vehicle-to-base communication. The best "next step up" from FRS for people who don't want to study for a HAM license.

**Key advantage over FRS:** External antennas are allowed. A GMRS radio with an antenna mounted at 30 feet on a pole dramatically outperforms a handheld with a rubber duck antenna.

## CB (Citizens Band)

The classic trucker radio. No license required.

**Frequencies:** 26.965–27.405 MHz (HF). 40 channels.

**Power:** 4 watts AM, 12 watts SSB (single sideband).

**Range:** 3–10 miles typical for AM. 15–30 miles for SSB. Occasionally "skip" conditions bounce signals hundreds or thousands of miles via ionospheric reflection — this is unpredictable and not reliable for planned communication.

**Radios:** $30–100 for a basic mobile unit. Handheld CB radios exist but perform poorly (antenna is too short for the wavelength).

**Channel 9:** Designated emergency channel. Monitored by some volunteer groups and occasionally by law enforcement.

**Channel 19:** Trucker highway channel. Useful for road information and making contact.

**Best for:** Vehicle-to-vehicle communication. Highway travel. Making contact with strangers in a disaster (CB is widely owned). Requires a proper antenna — a magnetic-mount mobile antenna on a vehicle roof is the minimum for usable performance.

**Limitations:** Crowded in populated areas. No repeater infrastructure. Long antenna required for the wavelength (4–9 feet ideal). Noisy band — atmospheric and electrical interference are common on HF.

## MURS (Multi-Use Radio Service)

The least-known license-free radio option. Often overlooked — worth considering.

**Frequencies:** 151.820, 151.880, 151.940, 154.570, 154.600 MHz (VHF). 5 channels.

**Power:** 2 watts.

**Range:** 2–8 miles typical. VHF propagation works slightly better than UHF (FRS/GMRS) in wooded and hilly terrain because VHF waves diffract better around obstacles.

**Radios:** $30–100. Less selection than FRS/GMRS but available from Dakota Alert, Motorola, and others.

**Best for:** Property monitoring (Dakota Alert makes MURS-based motion sensor systems that alert a base radio when sensors are triggered). Quiet channels — far less traffic than FRS or CB. Rural communication.

**Key advantage:** VHF propagation in terrain. External antennas allowed. Channels are almost never congested because most people don't know MURS exists.

## Channel Plans for Your Group

Establish a communication plan before you need it:

1. **Primary channel:** Your group's main frequency. Check in at set times (e.g., every hour on the hour, or at 0800 and 1800 daily).
2. **Alternate channel:** Backup if primary is busy or compromised. Agree on this in advance.
3. **Emergency channel:** For urgent traffic only. Keep it clear unless someone has an emergency.
4. **CTCSS tone:** Pick a privacy code for your group's radios. It won't encrypt your traffic, but it filters out other users' chatter on the same channel.

Write the channel plan down and give a copy to everyone in the group. People forget frequencies under stress.

## Radio Discipline

- **Brevity.** Say what you need to say in as few words as possible. Long transmissions tie up the channel and drain batteries.
- **Authentication.** Use agreed-upon callsigns or code words so you can verify who you're talking to. In a crisis, people may attempt to deceive via radio.
- **Don't transmit sensitive information.** Assume everything you say is being heard by someone else. Anyone with a scanner or a radio on your frequency hears everything. Plan accordingly.
- **Battery conservation.** Turn the radio off when not in use or monitoring. Lower the power setting when range allows. High power drains batteries much faster.
- **Scheduled check-ins** reduce the need for continuous monitoring. "We check in at the top of every hour on channel 3" means radios can be off the rest of the time.
