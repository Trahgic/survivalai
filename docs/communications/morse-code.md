Morse code is the most resilient communication method ever created. It works with any medium that can produce two states — light on/off, sound high/low, radio on/off, flag up/down. A Morse code signal cuts through noise that makes voice communication impossible. It requires less power, less bandwidth, and simpler equipment than any other radio mode.

## The Code

Each letter, number, and punctuation mark is represented by a combination of short signals (dits, written as ".") and long signals (dahs, written as "-"). A dah is three times the length of a dit.

### Critical Signals

**SOS** (international distress signal): ... --- ... (three dits, three dahs, three dits — no spaces between letters). Send continuously with a brief pause between repetitions.

**CQ** (calling any station): -.-. --.- Means "I'm looking for anyone to talk to."

**AR** (end of message): .-.-. Means "I'm done transmitting."

**SK** (end of contact): ...-.- Means "goodbye, signing off."

## Timing

The rhythm is what makes Morse readable. Without proper timing, it's just noise.

- **Dit:** 1 unit of time
- **Dah:** 3 units
- **Space between parts of the same letter:** 1 unit
- **Space between letters:** 3 units
- **Space between words:** 7 units

At 5 words per minute (beginner speed), one dit is about 240 milliseconds. At 13 WPM (useful working speed), one dit is about 92 milliseconds.

## Learning Morse Code

### The Koch Method (Most Effective)

1. Start by learning just two characters at full speed (13+ WPM).
2. Practice until you can copy them correctly 90% of the time.
3. Add one new character. Practice until 90% accuracy with all learned characters.
4. Repeat until all characters are learned.

This method builds pattern recognition at speed. Learning at slow speed and then trying to speed up later is much harder — your brain learns the wrong patterns.

### Practice Resources (Pre-Disaster)

- LCWO.net (Learn CW Online) — free Koch method trainer
- Morse code apps on smartphones
- Practice with a partner using a flashlight, buzzer, or radio

### Mnemonics for Key Letters

- E = . (shortest, most common letter)
- T = - (second most common)
- SOS = ... --- ... (everyone should memorize this one pattern minimum)
- The word "EISH" gives you ., .., ..., .... (1–4 dits)
- The word "TMO" gives you -, --, --- (1–3 dahs)

## Using Morse Code Without a Radio

### Light Signals

A flashlight, lantern, mirror, or any controllable light source can transmit Morse. Cover and uncover the light for dits and dahs. Visible for miles at night with a bright light.

### Sound Signals

A whistle, horn, drum, or tapping on metal. Short blast = dit. Long blast = dah. Range depends on the sound source — a whistle carries a mile, tapping carries hundreds of feet.

### Visual Flags/Arms

Raise and lower a flag or your arms. Quick up-down = dit. Sustained raise = dah. Usable at distances where you can see movement but can't hear voices.

### Writing/Scratching

Morse can be left as written messages — scratch dits and dahs into bark, scratch on a rock, write on paper. A message trail that doesn't require language — anyone who knows Morse in any country can read it.

## Morse on Radio (CW Mode)

CW (Continuous Wave) is Morse code transmitted over radio. It's the most efficient radio mode:

- Narrower bandwidth than voice — less interference, more stations fit in the band
- Readable at signal levels far below what voice requires
- Lower power needed — 5 watts of CW reaches farther than 50 watts of voice
- Simple equipment — a CW transmitter can be built from a handful of components

### Operating CW

1. Set your radio to CW mode (if it has one).
2. Use a key or paddle to send Morse. A straight key (simple lever) is the traditional and easiest to improvise. Push down = signal on. Release = signal off.
3. Listen on the same frequency for a response.
4. Standard CW procedure:
   - Call CQ: CQ CQ CQ DE [your callsign] [your callsign] K
   - Answer a CQ: [their callsign] DE [your callsign] K
   - Send your message.
   - End with SK (signing off) and your callsign.

## Emergency Morse Procedure

If you're in distress and have any Morse-capable device:

1. Send SOS repeatedly: ... --- ... (no pause between letters)
2. Follow with your location if known
3. Follow with nature of emergency
4. Pause 30 seconds. Listen for response.
5. Repeat.

SOS is universally recognized. Any listener — military, civilian, HAM operator, maritime — who hears SOS knows someone needs help, regardless of language.

## Building a Simple Morse Oscillator (Practice Buzzer)

1. A 9V battery, a small speaker or piezo buzzer, a pushbutton switch, and wire.
2. Wire in series: battery → switch → buzzer → back to battery.
3. Press the button for dits and dahs. The buzzer sounds.
4. Practice sending and have a partner practice receiving.

This same circuit, connected to a radio transmitter instead of a buzzer, becomes a working CW transmitter.
