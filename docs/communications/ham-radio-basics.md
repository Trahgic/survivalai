Amateur (HAM) radio is the most reliable long-distance communication available when everything else fails. Cell towers go down, internet goes down, power goes down — HAM radio works because it's point-to-point with no infrastructure between you and the other station. A basic handheld radio talks 5–30 miles. A proper HF station talks across continents.

## Why HAM Radio Matters for Preparedness

- **No infrastructure required.** Works without cell towers, internet, satellites, or grid power.
- **Range from miles to global** depending on equipment and frequency.
- **Established emergency networks.** ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service) and RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) activate during disasters.
- **Weather information.** NOAA weather radio frequencies are receivable on many HAM radios.
- **Versatile.** Voice, Morse code, digital modes, text messaging, even email over radio.

## Licensing (Normal Times)

In the US, HAM radio requires an FCC license. Three levels:

**Technician:** Entry level. Covers VHF/UHF frequencies (local communication, 5–50 miles). 35-question multiple-choice exam. No Morse code requirement. Enough for most emergency use.

**General:** Adds most HF frequencies (long-distance communication, 100–10,000+ miles). 35-question exam on top of Technician.

**Extra:** Full access to all HAM frequencies. 50-question exam.

In a true grid-down emergency, licensing is a legal formality. FCC rules explicitly allow unlicensed transmission when life or property is in immediate danger. But learning the material before an emergency matters — knowing how to operate effectively isn't something you pick up under stress.

Study resources: ARRL study guides, hamstudy.org (free practice exams), local ham club exam sessions.

## Radio Types

### Handheld (HT — Handy Talky)

VHF/UHF handheld radios. The entry point for most people.

- **Range:** 1–5 miles typical (line of sight), up to 30+ miles hitting a repeater.
- **Power:** 1–8 watts.
- **Cost:** $25–50 for a Baofeng UV-5R (capable, cheap, widely available), $200–400 for a Yaesu/Icom/Kenwood.
- **Bands:** 2-meter (144–148 MHz VHF) and 70-centimeter (420–450 MHz UHF).
- **Limitations:** Short range without a repeater. Battery-dependent. Small antenna limits performance.

### Mobile Radio (Vehicle-Mounted)

VHF/UHF radios designed for vehicle installation. More power, better antenna = more range.

- **Range:** 10–50 miles (more with a good antenna and elevation).
- **Power:** 25–75 watts.
- **Cost:** $150–400.
- Can be used as a base station with a 12V power supply.

### HF Base Station

The long-distance workhorse. HF radio waves bounce off the ionosphere, allowing communication over hundreds to thousands of miles.

- **Range:** 50–10,000+ miles depending on frequency, time of day, and atmospheric conditions.
- **Power:** 5–100 watts.
- **Cost:** $500–3,000+ for the radio. Antenna is separate.
- **Bands:** 160 meters through 10 meters (1.8–30 MHz). Each band has different propagation characteristics.
- **Requires:** A proper antenna (the most critical component for HF), a tuner, and a good ground.

## Key Frequencies

### HAM Bands for Emergency Use

- **2 meters (146.520 MHz):** National simplex calling frequency. If you're trying to reach anyone on VHF without knowing a repeater, call here. Monitor this frequency.
- **70 cm (446.000 MHz):** UHF calling frequency.
- **20 meters (14.300 MHz):** Primary HF emergency and traffic net frequency. Long-distance daytime communication.
- **40 meters (7.260 MHz):** Regional HF. Good for 100–1,000 mile communication, especially at night.
- **80 meters (3.860 MHz):** Regional/local HF. Best at night. 100–500 mile range.

### Non-HAM Emergency Frequencies

- **156.800 MHz (Channel 16):** Marine distress and calling. Monitored by Coast Guard.
- **121.500 MHz:** Aviation emergency. Monitored by aircraft and SAR.
- **162.400–162.550 MHz:** NOAA weather radio broadcasts. Receive-only — continuous weather updates and emergency alerts.
- **CB Channel 9 (27.065 MHz):** Citizens Band emergency channel. No license required.

## Basic Operation

### Simplex (Direct Radio-to-Radio)

Your radio transmits directly to another radio on the same frequency. Both stations must be within range of each other. Simple, requires no infrastructure.

1. Set your radio to the desired frequency.
2. Listen first. Make sure the frequency is clear.
3. Press the PTT (Push To Talk) button, wait a beat, then speak clearly: "This is [your callsign], calling any station." Or call a specific station.
4. Release PTT. Listen for a response.
5. Take turns — only one station can transmit at a time on a frequency. Say "over" when you're done talking and waiting for a reply. Say "out" when the conversation is finished.

### Repeaters

A repeater is a radio station on a hilltop or tower that receives your signal and rebroadcasts it at higher power and better elevation. Repeaters dramatically extend the range of handheld and mobile radios.

- Repeaters use two frequencies: one for input (your transmit frequency) and one for output (what you hear). This offset is typically +/- 600 kHz on 2 meters.
- Many repeaters require a CTCSS tone (a sub-audible tone your radio transmits to "unlock" the repeater). Without the correct tone, the repeater ignores your transmission. Check local repeater directories for tone information.
- Repeaters are typically powered by commercial power with battery backup. In a prolonged grid-down event, repeaters may go offline as batteries deplete — be prepared for simplex-only communication.

## Radio Etiquette

- **Listen before transmitting.** Don't step on (transmit over) someone else's conversation.
- **Identify yourself.** Use your callsign at the beginning and end of each contact, and every 10 minutes during a long conversation. This is a legal requirement but also practical — people need to know who they're talking to.
- **Keep transmissions brief.** Say what you need to say concisely. Long, rambling transmissions tie up the frequency.
- **Use plain language for emergencies.** Drop the jargon and phonetic alphabet if the person you're talking to isn't a ham operator. Clear, simple communication saves lives.
- **Emergency traffic has priority.** If someone calls "MAYDAY" or "EMERGENCY" on any frequency, all other traffic stops. Anyone hearing the call should assist or relay.
- **Don't transmit personal information** (addresses, security details, financial information) on the air. Radio is not private — anyone with a receiver can hear you.

## Power for Radios

- **Handheld:** Internal rechargeable battery. Lasts 8–24 hours depending on use. Charge from solar panel, car charger, or USB. Carry spare batteries.
- **Mobile/Base:** 12V DC. Runs directly from a car battery, solar battery bank, or any 12V source. Current draw: 1–2 amps receiving, 5–20 amps transmitting.
- **HF station:** 12V DC, 20–25 amps on transmit. Needs a substantial battery bank or generator.

A basic solar panel (50W) keeps a handheld radio charged indefinitely and can maintain a mobile radio with moderate use.

## Antenna Basics

The antenna is more important than the radio. A great antenna on a cheap radio outperforms an expensive radio with a bad antenna every time.

**For handhelds:** Replace the factory "rubber duck" antenna with an aftermarket antenna (Nagoya NA-771 is a common upgrade). The improvement is dramatic — often 2–3x range improvement for under $10.

**For base stations:** Get the antenna as high as possible. Height = range for VHF/UHF. A simple ground plane antenna (a vertical element with radials) at 30+ feet works well.

**For HF:** Antenna length is related to wavelength. A dipole antenna for 20 meters needs to be about 33 feet long. HF antennas are big — but they can be simple wire strung between trees.
