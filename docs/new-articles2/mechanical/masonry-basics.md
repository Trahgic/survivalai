Masonry is building with units (brick, stone, block) joined by mortar. It produces the most durable structures possible with low-tech methods — stone walls built by Romans are still standing 2,000 years later. The critical skill is mortar mixing.

## Mortar Recipes

**Lime mortar (traditional — no Portland cement needed):**
1. Burn limestone or seashells in a hot fire (above 1500°F) for several hours until they crumble to powder. This is quickite.
2. Slake the quicklime: slowly add water. It heats violently — stand back. The result is slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) putty.
3. Mix 1 part slaked lime putty to 3 parts clean sand. Add water to a thick, butter-like consistency.
4. This mortar sets slowly (weeks) by absorbing CO2 from the air. It stays slightly flexible, which is why ancient buildings survive earthquakes better than modern rigid concrete.

**Mud mortar (simplest):** Clay-rich soil mixed with sand and straw. Same recipe as cob or adobe. Works for non-load-bearing walls and fills between stones. Not weather-resistant without protection — plaster the exterior.

**Portland cement mortar (if cement is available):** 1 part Portland cement, 1 part lime, 6 parts sand. Water to butter consistency. Sets in 24 hours, full strength in 28 days.

## Laying Stone

1. Select stones with at least one flat face. Lay flat faces outward for a clean wall face.
2. Each stone should span across the joint below — never stack joints vertically (running bond pattern). This is the single most important rule in masonry.
3. Apply a bed of mortar 3/4 inch thick. Press the stone into the mortar and tap with a hammer handle until level.
4. Fill all gaps with mortar. Push mortar into joints with a pointing tool or stick.
5. Use larger stones at corners and for quoins (corner stones). These tie the two wall faces together.
6. Every 3-4 courses, lay a "through stone" — a stone long enough to span the full wall thickness. This ties the inner and outer faces together and prevents the wall from splitting.
7. Wall thickness minimum: 18 inches for a structural wall, 12 inches for a non-structural partition.

## Laying Brick

Standard brick: 8 × 3.75 × 2.25 inches. Bricks are faster and more uniform than stone.

1. Soak bricks in water before laying — dry bricks suck moisture from mortar and weaken the joint.
2. Butter the end and bottom of each brick with mortar before placing.
3. Press into position. Tap with trowel handle until level and aligned.
4. Standard bond patterns: running bond (offset each course by half a brick — simplest and adequate for most walls), English bond (alternating courses of headers and stretchers — stronger), Flemish bond (alternating headers and stretchers in each course — strongest but hardest to lay).
5. Check level and plumb every 3-4 courses. Use a string line for horizontal alignment.
6. Minimum wall thickness for structural: 2 bricks wide (8 inches).

## Making Bricks

If you need bricks and don't have a supply:

1. Mix clay soil and sand (roughly 1:1 to 1:3 depending on clay content). Add chopped straw for unfired bricks.
2. Press into molds (wooden frame, open top and bottom, 8×4×2.5 inches).
3. Sun-dry for 1-2 weeks until completely dry throughout.
4. For fired bricks: stack in a kiln formation (with air channels between) and fire for 24-48 hours at 1800°F+. Fired bricks are waterproof and much stronger than sun-dried.

## Arches and Lintels

Over doors and windows, you need a lintel (horizontal beam) or an arch to carry the wall load above.

**Lintel:** A stone slab, timber beam, or steel angle laid across the opening. Must extend at least 6 inches into the wall on each side. Stone lintels crack under heavy loads — timber or steel is better for wide spans.

**Arch:** Build over a temporary wooden form (centering). Lay bricks or stones in a curve with mortar between. The keystone at the top locks the arch. Remove the centering after the mortar sets (7+ days for lime mortar). An arch is stronger than a lintel and can span wider openings.
