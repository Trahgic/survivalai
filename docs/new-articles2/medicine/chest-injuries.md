Chest injuries are the second leading cause of trauma death after head injuries. The good news: the most immediately lethal chest injuries are treatable with basic skills and improvised materials.

## Broken Ribs

Most common chest injury. Caused by falls, blows, or crush. The rib itself isn't dangerous — the risk is what the broken rib damages (lung, blood vessels) and the pain preventing adequate breathing.

1. Feel along each rib for point tenderness, crepitus (grinding sensation), or deformity.
2. Do NOT wrap or bind the chest — this restricts breathing and causes pneumonia.
3. Pain management is critical — if the person can't breathe deeply because of pain, they develop pneumonia within days. Ibuprofen 400mg every 6 hours AND acetaminophen 500mg every 6 hours (stagger them 3 hours apart for continuous coverage).
4. Encourage deep breathing exercises every hour despite the pain — inhale as deeply as possible, hold 3 seconds, exhale. 10 repetitions.
5. Splint the arm on the injured side in a sling against the chest — this limits rib movement and reduces pain.

## Open Chest Wound (Sucking Chest Wound)

A hole in the chest wall that lets air into the chest cavity but outside the lung. Air accumulates and compresses the lung — the person can't breathe.

Recognize: bubbling or hissing sound from the wound, blood bubbles at the wound, increasing difficulty breathing.

1. Seal the wound immediately. Use plastic (bag, wrapper, ID card, duct tape over plastic) — anything airtight.
2. Tape on THREE sides only, leaving one edge open. This creates a flutter valve — air can escape out but can't be sucked in.
3. If no plastic available, a gloved hand sealed over the wound works temporarily.
4. If the person worsens after sealing (increasing distress, neck veins bulging), you may have created a tension pneumothorax — briefly lift the seal to let trapped air escape, then reseal.

> Commercial chest seals exist and are worth stocking. Hyfin and Asherman chest seals cost $10-15 and are purpose-built for this. Include two in your first aid kit — one for the entrance wound, one for a possible exit wound.

## Tension Pneumothorax

Air trapped in the chest cavity under pressure, compressing the lung and shifting the heart. Rapidly fatal without treatment.

Signs: severe difficulty breathing, one side of the chest doesn't move with breathing, neck veins distended, trachea shifted to one side (very late sign), rapid deterioration.

Emergency treatment (needle decompression): Insert a large-bore needle (14-gauge, at least 3 inches long) into the chest wall at the 2nd intercostal space (second rib space from the top) at the midclavicular line (straight down from the middle of the collarbone), on the affected side. Insert perpendicular to the chest wall, just above the 3rd rib (to avoid the blood vessels that run along the bottom edge of each rib). A rush of air confirms the diagnosis. Leave the needle in place or attach a flutter valve (cut finger of a glove over the needle hub).

This is an advanced skill. Practice the landmark identification on yourself: find your collarbone, find the notch at the top of your sternum, count down two rib spaces at the midpoint of the collarbone. That's the insertion point.

## Flail Chest

Three or more adjacent ribs broken in two or more places each, creating a "floating" segment of chest wall. This segment moves paradoxically — inward during inhalation, outward during exhalation.

Treatment: position the person with the injured side DOWN (gravity stabilizes the flail segment). Pad the flail segment with bulky dressings but do not tape circumferentially. Pain management is critical. This is a serious injury requiring evacuation.
