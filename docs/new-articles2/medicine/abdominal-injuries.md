Abdominal injuries are terrifying because you can't see what's happening inside. The abdomen contains organs that bleed massively (liver, spleen), organs that leak digestive enzymes (pancreas), and organs that spill bacteria (intestines). All three categories are potentially fatal without surgical repair.

## Assessment

Gently press on all four quadrants of the abdomen. You're checking for:
- Rigidity (involuntary muscle guarding — the abdomen feels board-stiff)
- Rebound tenderness (pain is worse when you release pressure than when you push)
- Distension (abdomen is swelling visibly over time — indicates internal bleeding)
- Bruising patterns: seatbelt sign (diagonal bruise), Grey Turner's sign (flank bruising), Cullen's sign (bruising around navel) — all suggest internal bleeding

Any of these signs after trauma = assume internal injury. The person needs surgical care. Your job in the field is to manage shock, position for comfort, and evacuate.

## Blunt Abdominal Trauma

From falls, vehicle impacts, kicks, or blows. The spleen (left upper quadrant) and liver (right upper quadrant) are the most commonly injured organs.

1. Do NOT give food or water — surgery may be needed.
2. Position on back with knees slightly bent (relaxes abdominal muscles, reduces pain).
3. Treat for shock: elevate legs, keep warm, monitor vital signs.
4. Monitor abdomen every 15 minutes — increasing distension or rigidity means active bleeding.
5. Nothing you do in the field stops internal abdominal bleeding. The only treatment is surgery. Evacuate.

## Penetrating Abdominal Wounds

Stab or impalement injuries.

1. Do NOT remove impaled objects — they may be tamponading (plugging) a blood vessel. Stabilize the object in place with bulky padding taped around it.
2. Control external bleeding with direct pressure around (not on) the wound.
3. Do NOT push any protruding organs back inside.
4. Cover the wound with a moist sterile dressing covered by plastic wrap to prevent drying.
5. Position for comfort (usually knees bent).
6. Start antibiotics if available — bowel contamination is almost certain with any abdominal penetration. Amoxicillin + metronidazole or ciprofloxacin + metronidazole.

## Evisceration (Organs Outside the Body)

Intestines or other organs protruding from an open abdominal wound.

1. Do NOT attempt to push organs back in.
2. Cover with a moist dressing — saline-soaked gauze is ideal, clean damp cloth works. The tissue must not dry out.
3. Cover the moist dressing with plastic wrap or a plastic bag to retain moisture.
4. Tape loosely in place. Do not compress.
5. Keep the person warm, positioned with knees bent.
6. This is a surgical emergency. Without surgery, survival is unlikely — but people have survived field evacuations of 12+ hours with proper wound coverage.

## Appendicitis

Right lower quadrant pain that starts around the navel and migrates to the lower right. Worse with coughing or movement. Nausea, low-grade fever, loss of appetite.

McBurney's point: the most tender spot is typically 1/3 of the way from the right hip bone (anterior superior iliac spine) to the navel. Rebound tenderness at this point strongly suggests appendicitis.

Without surgery: antibiotics can treat uncomplicated appendicitis in 60-70% of cases. Ciprofloxacin 500mg every 12 hours + metronidazole 500mg every 8 hours for 10 days. If the pain suddenly improves then dramatically worsens with rigidity, the appendix has ruptured — this is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate surgical care.
