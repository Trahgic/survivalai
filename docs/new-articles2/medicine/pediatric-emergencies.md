Children are not small adults. Their physiology, medication dosages, and disease progression are fundamentally different. The biggest mistake in pediatric emergency care is waiting too long to act — children compensate well until they crash suddenly.

## Vital Signs by Age

Normal values vary dramatically by age:

| Age | Heart Rate | Respiratory Rate | Systolic BP |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Newborn | 120-160 | 30-60 | 60-90 |
| Infant (1-12 mo) | 100-150 | 25-50 | 70-100 |
| Toddler (1-3 yr) | 90-140 | 20-30 | 80-100 |
| Child (4-8 yr) | 80-120 | 18-25 | 85-110 |
| Older child (9-12) | 70-110 | 14-22 | 90-120 |

A child's heart rate is the most important vital sign. Increasing heart rate is the earliest sign of distress. By the time blood pressure drops, the child is in severe shock.

## Fever Management

Fever itself is not dangerous until 105°F+ in most children. It's the body fighting infection. Treat the child, not the number.

Medications (by weight, not age):
- Acetaminophen: 15 mg/kg every 4-6 hours. Max 5 doses/24 hours.
- Ibuprofen: 10 mg/kg every 6-8 hours. NOT for infants under 6 months.
- NEVER give aspirin to anyone under 18 — risk of Reye's syndrome.
- You can alternate acetaminophen and ibuprofen every 3 hours if one alone isn't working.

> Febrile seizures (seizures from fever) in children aged 6 months to 5 years are terrifying but usually harmless. They last 1-2 minutes and stop on their own. Do not put anything in the mouth. Place on their side. Time it. Seek care if it lasts more than 5 minutes.

## Dehydration in Children

Children dehydrate faster than adults and die from it more quickly.

Signs by severity:
- Mild: thirsty, slightly dry mouth, decreased urine (fewer wet diapers)
- Moderate: sunken eyes, no tears when crying, very dry mouth, skin tents when pinched
- Severe: lethargic or unconscious, sunken fontanelle (soft spot on infant's head), no urine for 8+ hours

Treatment: ORS (oral rehydration solution) — 6 level teaspoons sugar + 1/2 teaspoon salt per liter of water. Give small frequent sips, not large gulps (large volumes cause vomiting). For infants, 1 teaspoon every 1-2 minutes by syringe or spoon.

## Choking — Infant (Under 1 Year)

Do NOT do abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) on infants.

1. Place infant face-down on your forearm, head lower than chest, supporting the head.
2. Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
3. Turn infant face-up. Give 5 chest thrusts with 2 fingers on the center of the chest, just below the nipple line.
4. Alternate back blows and chest thrusts until the object is expelled or the infant becomes unconscious.
5. If unconscious: start infant CPR (30 compressions, 2 breaths). Check mouth for visible object before each breath.

## CPR — Infant and Child

Infant (under 1): 2 fingers on center of chest, compress 1.5 inches deep, 100-120/min. Cover both mouth and nose with your mouth for breaths. 30:2 ratio.

Child (1-8): one or two hands on center of chest, compress 2 inches deep, 100-120/min. Pinch nose, mouth-to-mouth. 30:2 ratio.

## Weight Estimation

When you don't have a scale (and all pediatric doses are weight-based):

- Average 1 year old: 10 kg (22 lbs)
- Average 2 year old: 12 kg (26 lbs)
- Ages 2-12: weight in kg ≈ (age × 2) + 8
- Example: 6 year old ≈ (6 × 2) + 8 = 20 kg
