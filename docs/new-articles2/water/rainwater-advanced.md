Basic rainwater collection is covered in the main rainwater article. This article covers larger-scale permanent systems that can supply a household year-round.

## Calculating Your Supply

The formula: Catchment area (sq ft) × rainfall (inches) × 0.623 = gallons collected.

Example: a 1,000 sq ft roof in an area with 40 inches of annual rainfall collects 1,000 × 40 × 0.623 = 24,920 gallons per year. That's 68 gallons per day — enough for a household if managed carefully.

A 2,000 sq ft roof with 30 inches of rain: 37,380 gallons/year = 102 gallons/day. Comfortable for a family of four.

## Collection Components

**Gutters:** Standard K-style aluminum or vinyl gutters work. Improvised: split bamboo, halved PVC pipe, or bent sheet metal. Minimum 1/4 inch slope per foot of run toward the downspout.

**Downspout:** Carries water from gutters to storage. Standard 2×3 or 3×4 inch downspouts. Larger is better — heavy rain overwhelms small downspouts.

**First-flush diverter:** The first rain off a roof carries bird droppings, dust, pollen, and debris. A first-flush system diverts the first 1-2 gallons per 100 sq ft of roof away from your storage.

Simple first-flush: a capped vertical pipe (3-4 inch PVC, 3-4 feet tall) in the downspout line before the storage tank. The dirty first water fills this pipe. When the pipe is full, a floating ball seals it and clean water flows past to the tank. Between rains, a slow-drip valve at the bottom of the pipe empties it for the next rain.

**Screen/filter:** A mesh screen (window screen or finer) at the tank inlet keeps leaves, insects, and debris out. Clean monthly.

## Storage Options

| Type | Capacity | Pros | Cons |
|------|----------|------|------|
| Plastic barrel (55 gal) | 55 gal each | Cheap, portable, easy to find | Small, need many for serious supply |
| IBC tote (275 gal) | 275 gal each | Common, stackable, affordable | UV degrades plastic — shade them |
| Polyethylene tank | 500-10,000 gal | Purpose-built, UV-resistant | Cost, transport |
| Ferrocement cistern | Any size | Buildable on-site from wire mesh and cement | Labor-intensive, needs cement |
| Underground cistern | Any size | Cool temperature, hidden, no UV | Excavation required, pumping needed |

For a survival homestead, aim for 2,500-5,000 gallons of storage minimum — this bridges dry spells of 2-4 weeks.

## Ferrocement Cistern (Build Your Own)

The most practical large-tank option when you can't buy a manufactured tank.

1. Build a cylindrical frame from welded wire mesh (concrete reinforcing mesh). 6 feet diameter × 4 feet tall = roughly 850 gallons.
2. Apply layers of cement plaster (1 part Portland cement, 3 parts sand, water to stiff paste) to both sides of the mesh. Build up 1-1.5 inches total thickness.
3. Apply in thin coats, letting each coat set before adding the next. 3-4 coats over 3-4 days.
4. Cure by keeping moist for 7 days (cover with wet burlap or plastic).
5. Seal interior with a food-safe cement sealer or multiple coats of lime wash.
6. Cost: roughly $200-400 in materials for 1,000 gallons. Lasts decades.

## Treatment for Drinking

Rainwater from a clean roof through a first-flush diverter and screen filter is relatively clean but NOT sterile. Before drinking:

1. Settle: let water sit 24 hours. Sediment drops to the bottom.
2. Filter: gravity filter (ceramic, sand, or commercial) removes particulates and most bacteria.
3. Disinfect: chlorine (2 drops of 8.25% bleach per gallon) or UV (SODIS method — clear bottles in sun 6 hours).

For non-drinking uses (irrigation, washing, laundry, toilet flushing) — use directly from the tank with no treatment.
