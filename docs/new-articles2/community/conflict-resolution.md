In survival situations, the most dangerous threat is often inside your group, not outside it. Stress, sleep deprivation, hunger, and fear amplify every personality conflict. Groups that can't resolve internal disputes fall apart — and isolated individuals die at many times the rate of functioning groups.

## Why Conflicts Escalate in Survival

Normal social controls disappear. People are scared. Resources feel scarce even when they're adequate. Sleep deprivation makes everyone irrational. The power structure is unclear. Old grievances resurface under stress. People who were casual acquaintances are suddenly trapped together 24/7.

The most dangerous period is days 3-14: the initial emergency adrenaline has worn off, routines aren't established, and the reality of the situation hits. This is when groups fracture.

## Prevention: Structure Kills Conflict

Most conflicts come from ambiguity — who decides, who works, who gets what.

1. **Establish clear roles immediately.** Not democracy — functional roles. Someone manages water, someone manages food, someone manages security, someone manages shelter. People with jobs have purpose and fewer grievances.
2. **Set up fair work rotation.** The worst morale killer is perceived unfairness. Post a visible schedule. Everyone works, everyone rests, everyone eats the same.
3. **Daily group meeting.** 10 minutes, every morning. Status, plans, concerns. People who feel heard complain less.
4. **Decision-making protocol.** For routine decisions: the person with the relevant role decides. For major decisions (whether to move, how to ration, security posture): the leader decides after hearing input. Trying to consensus-vote everything paralyzes the group.

## When Conflict Happens

1. **Address it immediately.** Unresolved conflict festers and spreads. The longer you wait, the more people take sides.
2. **Private conversation first.** Pull the parties aside individually. Listen. Let them vent. Acknowledge their feelings without agreeing or disagreeing. "I understand you're frustrated about the food rotation."
3. **Bring them together.** Each person states their concern without interruption. The other listens. Then swap. Often, being heard is enough.
4. **Focus on interests, not positions.** "You want more food" is a position. "You're worried you don't have enough energy to do your job" is an interest. Interests can be solved creatively. Positions are zero-sum.
5. **Find a concrete solution and implement it immediately.** Don't just agree to "try harder." Change the schedule, reassign the task, adjust the ration — something specific.

## Dealing with Difficult People

**The complainer:** Give them a job that matters and check in daily. Idle people complain. Busy people with purpose don't.

**The hoarder:** Address it publicly and gently. Establish that group resources are group resources. A private stash undermines trust for everyone.

**The bully:** Confront directly, privately first. "That behavior isn't going to work here." Have backup. If behavior continues, the group must act collectively — isolation or expulsion as a last resort.

**The checked-out person:** Depression and learned helplessness are common in survival. Give them small achievable tasks. Pair them with someone positive. Don't let them isolate. Monitor for suicidal indicators.

## The Leader's Job

The leader's most important function is not making decisions — it's maintaining group cohesion. A mediocre decision everyone supports beats a perfect decision that splits the group. When the leader makes unpopular calls (rationing, dangerous tasks, discipline), explain the reasoning clearly. People accept hard decisions when they understand why.
