Desert survival follows different rules than any other environment. The priorities invert: shade and water come before everything else. Without water, you die in 2-3 days. With water but no shade, you die nearly as fast from heat exhaustion.

## The Numbers

Water requirements in desert heat: 1 gallon per day minimum at rest in shade. 2+ gallons if walking. In 110°F+ direct sun with exertion, you can lose 1 quart of sweat per hour.

At rest in shade with no water, survival time by temperature:
- 90°F: ~3 days
- 100°F: ~2 days
- 110°F: ~1.5 days
- 120°F: ~1 day

These are RESTING in SHADE. In direct sun with exertion, halve these numbers.

## If You Have a Vehicle

Stay with it. A vehicle provides shade, is visible from the air, contains materials for signaling, and may have water in the radiator (coolant is toxic — don't drink it, but you can use it for cooling).

1. Stay in or under the vehicle during the day. Set up shade with tarps, floor mats, or blankets if the interior is too hot.
2. Signal from the vehicle: mirrors, fire, hood up, bright objects spread on the ground.
3. Travel at night only if you decide to move.

## Travel Rules

If you must move, travel at night, early morning, or late evening only. Rest in shade during the heat of the day (10 AM - 4 PM).

1. Cover all skin — loose, light-colored clothing reflects heat and reduces sweat evaporation loss. A wet bandana on the head and neck is highly effective.
2. Breathe through your nose, not your mouth — mouth breathing loses more moisture.
3. Eat minimally — digestion uses water. If water is limited, don't eat at all. Protein-heavy foods require the most water to digest.
4. Do not ration water — drink when thirsty. Your body needs it now, not later. Rationing just makes you weaker sooner.
5. Walk on hard ground. Walking in sand dunes uses 2-3x more energy.
6. Follow drainage patterns downhill — they lead to water, vegetation, and civilization.

## Finding Water in Desert

- Vegetation lines: any green line of trees or brush in an otherwise brown landscape marks subsurface water. Dig at the base of the vegetation, 1-3 feet down.
- Dry riverbeds: dig in the outside bend (deeper curve) of a dry wash, 2-4 feet down. Water collects under the bend.
- Rock formations: look for seeps, damp patches, and collected water in crevices after rain. Check shaded north-facing rock faces.
- Animal trails: they converge toward water sources. Follow trails downhill.
- Insects: bees fly in straight lines to water. Ants marching up a tree may be heading to a water-filled hollow.
- Solar still: dig a hole 3 feet across, 2 feet deep. Place a container in the bottom. Cover with clear plastic. Weight the center of the plastic so it sags over the container. Condensation drips into the container. Output: 1-3 cups per day — not enough to survive on alone but supplements other sources.

## Heat Illness Progression

Heat cramps → heat exhaustion → heat stroke. Each stage is worse. Treat early.

**Heat cramps:** Muscle spasms in legs, abdomen, arms. From salt loss. Treatment: rest in shade, drink salted water (1/4 teaspoon salt per quart), stretch affected muscles.

**Heat exhaustion:** Heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, headache, dizziness, cool clammy skin, fast weak pulse. Treatment: shade, lie down, elevate legs, cool with wet cloths, sip water with electrolytes. Recovery takes 24-48 hours.

**Heat stroke:** Hot DRY skin (sweating stops), temperature above 104°F, confusion, staggering, seizures, unconsciousness. THIS IS A DEATH EMERGENCY. Cool aggressively: immerse in water, pour water over entire body, fan, pack ice (if available) at neck, armpits, groin. Do not give fluids to an unconscious person.
