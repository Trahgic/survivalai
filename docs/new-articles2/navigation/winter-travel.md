Winter travel is slower, more dangerous, and more exhausting than any other season. Deep snow, short days, extreme cold, and the constant energy drain of thermoregulation make every mile harder. The rewards: frozen rivers become highways, tracks in snow are easy to follow, and water is everywhere (as snow).

## Clothing System

Layering is not optional — it's the only system that works.

1. **Base layer:** Wicking material against skin. Wool or synthetic. NEVER cotton — cotton kills in cold weather. When cotton gets wet (from sweat), it loses all insulation and sucks heat from your body.
2. **Insulation layer:** Fleece, down, or wool. Traps dead air. Multiple thin layers beat one thick layer because you can vent without removing everything.
3. **Shell layer:** Wind and waterproof outer. Blocks wind (which strips heat) and keeps precipitation off insulation.

Manage sweat: open vents, remove layers BEFORE you start sweating. Wet insulation is useless insulation. The mantra: "Be bold, start cold." You should feel slightly chilly when starting to move. If you're comfortable standing still, you'll be soaking wet in 20 minutes of walking.

## Snowshoe Travel

Without snowshoes, you sink thigh-deep in soft snow and exhaust yourself in minutes. Improvised snowshoes extend your range from hundreds of yards to miles.

Simple frame snowshoe:
1. Cut two green branches (birch, willow, ash) about 4 feet long and 1 inch thick. Bend each into a teardrop/oval shape and tie the ends together.
2. Lash cross-members: 2-3 sticks across the frame for rigidity.
3. Weave cord, strips of hide, or rope in a rough grid pattern across the frame. Tighter weave = better flotation in soft snow.
4. Binding: attach to your boot with cord that allows your heel to lift (you don't walk flat-footed in snowshoes — your toes pivot at the binding).

## Avalanche Safety

Avalanches kill more backcountry travelers in winter than any other hazard.

Danger signs: recent heavy snowfall (12+ inches in 24 hours), wind-loaded slopes, warming temperatures, cracking or "whumpfing" sounds when walking on snow, evidence of recent slides nearby.

Dangerous terrain: 25-45 degree slopes (steeper ones shed snow naturally, shallower ones don't slide). Convex slopes are more dangerous than concave. Open slopes without anchors (trees, rocks) are most dangerous.

If caught: try to swim toward the surface. As the slide slows, create an air pocket in front of your face with your arms. Clear space around your mouth immediately. Once the snow stops, you cannot move — it sets like concrete. You have 15-30 minutes of air in your pocket.

## Ice Travel

Minimum ice thickness for safe travel:
- Walking: 4 inches of clear blue ice
- Snowmobile/ATV: 8 inches
- Vehicle: 12-15 inches

Ice near moving water, springs, inlets, and outlets is thinner. Dark spots indicate thin ice. Snow-covered ice is weaker than clear ice (snow insulates and slows freezing).

Test with an ice chisel, axe, or auger as you go. If water wells up through the hole, the ice is too thin — go back the way you came.

If you fall through: don't panic. Turn to face the direction you came from (that ice held you). Kick hard and pull yourself up onto the ice with arms spread wide. Roll away from the hole — don't stand up. Get to shelter and fire immediately — hypothermia sets in within minutes.

## Energy and Calories

Cold weather burns 3,000-5,000 calories per day even with moderate activity. Your body runs its furnace full blast just to maintain core temperature. Eat calorie-dense food frequently — every 2 hours. Fat is the most efficient cold weather fuel: nuts, butter, pemmican, chocolate. Hot liquids provide direct warmth and morale.
