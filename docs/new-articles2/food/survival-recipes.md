These recipes have kept soldiers, explorers, and indigenous peoples alive for centuries. They store for months to years without refrigeration and pack maximum calories into minimum weight and volume.

## Pemmican (Stores for Years)

The original energy bar. A mixture of dried meat powder and rendered fat, optionally with dried berries. Calorie-dense, shelf-stable, nutritionally complete.

1. Dry lean meat (venison, beef, bison, elk) — cut into thin strips and dry completely. Jerky-dry is not enough; it should be brittle and snap cleanly. Oven at 170°F for 6-10 hours, or smoke/sun-dry until crackling dry.
2. Grind the dried meat into powder. A blender works; without one, pound in a cloth bag with a rock until it's fluffy powder.
3. Render fat: cut raw animal fat into small pieces, heat slowly on low until liquid fat separates from cracklings. Strain through cloth. The clear liquid fat is tallow (beef) or lard (pork). Should be pure and clean-smelling.
4. Mix meat powder and rendered fat at roughly 1:1 ratio by weight. The mixture should be just wet enough to hold together when squeezed.
5. Optional: add dried berries (blueberries, cranberries, chokecherries) — up to 10% by volume.
6. Press into bars, balls, or pack into containers. Seal airtight.
7. Stores 1-5 years at cool room temperature. Indefinitely if kept cold.

One pound of pemmican has roughly 3,000 calories. It's the most calorie-dense portable food you can make.

## Hardtack (Stores for Decades)

Simple unleavened crackers made from flour, water, and salt. Civil War hardtack has been found edible after 100+ years.

1. Mix 2 cups flour, 1/2 teaspoon salt, 1/2 to 3/4 cup water. Knead into a stiff dough — should not be sticky.
2. Roll out 1/2 inch thick.
3. Cut into 3×3 inch squares.
4. Poke holes through each piece with a fork or nail in a grid pattern (holes prevent puffing and help with drying).
5. Bake at 250°F for 4 hours, flipping once. Should be completely dry, pale, and rock-hard.
6. Store in airtight container.

To eat: soak in water, broth, or coffee for 15-30 minutes, or fry in fat. Do not try to bite into dry hardtack — you will break a tooth.

## Portable Soup (Bouillon Concentrate)

18th-century military ration. A dried meat broth concentrate that dissolves in hot water.

1. Boil bones and meat scraps in water for 12-24 hours, adding water as needed. Strain.
2. Continue boiling the strained broth until it reduces to a thick syrup.
3. Pour into a flat pan and let cool — it should set into a firm gel.
4. Cut into squares and dry completely in sun or low oven.
5. The dried squares dissolve in hot water to make instant broth. Stores for months.

## Bannock (Trail Bread)

Quick camp bread cooked over a fire.

1. Mix 2 cups flour, 1 tablespoon baking powder (if available), 1/2 teaspoon salt, 2 tablespoons fat (lard, butter, oil).
2. Add water until dough just holds together. Do not over-mix.
3. Pat into a 1-inch thick disc.
4. Cook on a flat rock near the fire, on a stick wrapped with dough, or in a greased pan — 8-10 minutes per side until golden and cooked through.
5. Eat warm. Doesn't store as long as hardtack but tastes infinitely better.

## Jerky

1. Cut lean meat across the grain into strips 1/4 inch thick, 1 inch wide.
2. Optional marinade: soy sauce, salt, pepper, garlic for 4-12 hours.
3. Dry at 160°F for 4-8 hours until the strips bend and crack but don't snap. They should not be moist inside.
4. Store in airtight container. Properly dried jerky lasts 1-2 months at room temperature, 6+ months vacuum-sealed.
