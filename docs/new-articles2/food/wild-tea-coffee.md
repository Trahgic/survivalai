Hot beverages are a survival morale booster that's easy to overlook. When coffee and tea supplies run out, dozens of wild plants make serviceable replacements — some with actual caffeine, most with useful medicinal properties, all better than plain hot water.

## Coffee Substitutes

None of these contain caffeine, but they produce a dark, bitter, roasted drink that fills the coffee craving.

**Dandelion root coffee:** Dig dandelion roots (fall roots are largest). Wash, chop into 1/4 inch pieces, roast in a dry pan or oven at 300°F until dark brown and brittle — 30-60 minutes. Grind and brew like coffee. Strong, earthy, slightly bitter. The closest wild substitute to real coffee.

**Chicory root:** Same process as dandelion. Chicory produces a richer, more coffee-like flavor. Historically blended with real coffee to stretch supplies — still used in New Orleans-style coffee.

**Acorn coffee:** Leach acorns first (boil in several changes of water until not bitter), then roast until dark brown, grind, brew. Mild nutty flavor.

**Roasted barley/grain:** Any grain roasted dark and brewed produces a coffee-like drink. This is the basis of commercial substitutes like Postum.

## Caffeinated Wild Plants

**Yaupon holly (Ilex vomitoria):** The only caffeinated plant native to North America. Despite the alarming Latin name (which was a taxonomist's joke), properly prepared yaupon does not cause vomiting. Harvest leaves, dry them, roast lightly or use fresh. Steep in hot water. Contains caffeine comparable to green tea. Found throughout the southeastern US.

**Pine needle tea:** Not caffeinated but extremely high in vitamin C (5x more than oranges by weight). Use fresh green needles from pine, spruce, or fir. Steep in hot water 10-15 minutes. Pleasant, citrusy flavor. Do NOT use yew needles — they're toxic.

## Herbal Teas (No Caffeine)

**Mint/wild peppermint:** Grows near water throughout North America. Steep fresh leaves. Settles the stomach, aids digestion. One of the most pleasant wild teas.

**Chamomile:** Small daisy-like flowers. Mild, apple-like flavor. Calming, helps with sleep and anxiety.

**Rose hip tea:** The red fruit that remains on rose bushes after petals drop. Very high in vitamin C. Crush and steep. Tart, fruity.

**Bee balm/bergamot:** Purple-red tubular flowers. Minty-citrus flavor. Used by Oswego people — hence "Oswego tea." Mild antibacterial properties.

**Sassafras root bark:** Dig small roots, scrub clean, steep the bark. Root beer flavor. Traditional spring tonic. Note: safrole in sassafras is a suspected carcinogen in large quantities — occasional use is fine, don't make it your daily drink.

**Nettle tea:** Steep dried stinging nettle leaves (stinging compounds break down with drying or heat). Rich in iron and minerals. Earthy, green flavor. One of the most nutritious wild teas.

**Linden/basswood flowers:** Harvest flowers in early summer. Sweet, honey-like tea. Mildly sedating.

## Preparation Tips

Use clean water brought to a rolling boil. Steep leaves and flowers 5-10 minutes. Steep roots and bark 15-20 minutes (simmer, don't just steep). Sweeten with honey, maple syrup, or birch syrup if available. Most wild teas improve with a second steeping of the same plant material.
