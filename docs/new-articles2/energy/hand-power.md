A healthy adult can sustain about 75 watts of output for extended periods (pedaling a bicycle) and can burst to 300-400 watts for short efforts. That's not much in electrical terms — but it's enough to charge batteries, run LED lights, power a radio, and keep small electronics alive.

## Bicycle Generator (Best Option)

A bicycle driving an alternator or DC motor is the most efficient way to convert human power to electricity.

1. Mount a bicycle on a stand so the rear wheel spins freely (a bicycle trainer stand, or improvise with a frame that elevates the rear axle).
2. Press a DC motor or car alternator against the rear tire so the tire drives the motor shaft by friction. Or use a belt/chain drive for better efficiency.
3. A car alternator needs to spin at 2,000+ RPM to produce meaningful output — you'll need a gear ratio (small wheel on the alternator, driven by the large bike wheel).
4. Output: a strong cyclist pedaling steadily produces 50-100 watts. That's enough to charge a 12V battery, run LED lights, or charge phones and radios.
5. Charge a 12V battery and use the battery to run devices — this smooths out the intermittent human input into steady power.

## Hand-Crank Generator

Smaller output but simpler build and useful for short charging sessions.

1. A small DC motor (from a cordless drill, car window motor, or salvaged appliance) run in reverse acts as a generator.
2. Attach a handle to the shaft. Crank by hand.
3. Output: 5-20 watts depending on motor and crank speed. Enough to charge a phone in 1-2 hours of cranking.
4. Add a rectifier (4 diodes in a bridge configuration) if the motor produces AC. Add a voltage regulator to protect devices.
5. Commercial hand-crank radios and flashlights use this principle — stock a few as backup.

## Gravity Battery

Store energy as elevated weight, release it slowly to drive a generator.

1. Lift a heavy weight (bucket of rocks, water jug) to a height using a rope and pulley.
2. As the weight descends slowly, the rope drives a generator through gears.
3. Energy stored = weight × height. A 50 lb weight raised 10 feet stores about 0.2 watt-hours — not much. You need significant weight and height for useful energy.
4. More practical as a clock mechanism (drives a mechanical timer) than as electrical generation.

## Practical Applications

What can you actually power with human energy?

| Device | Watts | Human Power Needed |
|--------|-------|--------------------|
| Phone charging | 5-10W | 15-30 min cranking or pedaling |
| LED light | 1-5W | Easy sustained pedaling |
| Ham radio (receive) | 2-5W | Easy sustained |
| Ham radio (transmit) | 10-50W | Hard pedaling during transmit |
| Laptop | 30-60W | Maximum sustained pedaling |
| Electric drill | 500W+ | Not feasible with human power |

The key insight: human power works for electronics and lighting. It does not work for heating, cooking, or power tools. Use fire for heat, use human power for information and light.
