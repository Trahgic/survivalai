Planting at the wrong time is the most common gardening failure. Every crop has a temperature range, a frost tolerance, and a required growing season length. This guide covers the major food crops and when to plant them relative to your last frost date.

## Find Your Last Frost Date

The last spring frost date and first fall frost date define your growing season. In the continental US:

- Deep South (zones 8-9): last frost Feb 15-Mar 15, first frost Nov 1-Dec 1. Growing season: 240-270 days.
- Mid-South/Mid-Atlantic (zones 6-7): last frost Apr 1-May 1, first frost Oct 1-Nov 1. Growing season: 160-200 days.
- Northern states (zones 4-5): last frost May 1-Jun 1, first frost Sep 15-Oct 15. Growing season: 120-160 days.
- Far north (zones 2-3): last frost Jun 1-15, first frost Sep 1-15. Growing season: 80-120 days.

## Cool Season Crops (Frost Tolerant)

Plant 2-4 weeks BEFORE last frost date. Can handle light frost (28-32°F).

| Crop | Plant Before Last Frost | Days to Harvest | Notes |
|------|------------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Peas | 4-6 weeks before | 60-70 | Direct sow. Trellis for climbing varieties. |
| Lettuce | 2-4 weeks before | 45-60 | Succession plant every 2 weeks. Bolts in heat. |
| Spinach | 4-6 weeks before | 40-50 | Very cold-hardy. Best in spring and fall. |
| Kale | 4-6 weeks before | 55-75 | Survives down to 10°F. Sweetens after frost. |
| Radishes | 2-4 weeks before | 25-35 | Fastest food crop. Good morale booster. |
| Carrots | 2-3 weeks before | 65-80 | Slow to germinate. Keep soil moist. |
| Onions | 4-6 weeks before | 90-120 | Sets or transplants. Long-day vs short-day matters by latitude. |
| Potatoes | 2-4 weeks before | 75-120 | Cut seed potatoes into pieces with 2-3 eyes each. |
| Cabbage | 4-6 weeks before (transplant) | 70-100 | Start indoors 6-8 weeks before transplant date. |
| Broccoli | 4-6 weeks before (transplant) | 60-80 | Prefers cool weather. Side shoots after main head. |
| Beets | 2-4 weeks before | 50-65 | Both roots and greens are edible. |
| Turnips | 2-4 weeks before | 40-60 | Fast-growing, stores well. |

## Warm Season Crops (Frost Kills Them)

Plant AFTER all frost danger has passed — at or after last frost date. Soil temperature should be above 60°F.

| Crop | Plant After Last Frost | Days to Harvest | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Tomatoes | 1-2 weeks after | 65-85 | Transplants. Stake or cage. |
| Peppers | 2 weeks after | 65-80 | Need warm soil. Slow to start. |
| Squash (summer) | 1-2 weeks after | 50-65 | Direct sow. Very productive. |
| Squash (winter) | 1-2 weeks after | 80-110 | Stores for months. Butternuts are easiest. |
| Beans (bush) | 1 week after | 50-60 | Direct sow. Fix nitrogen in soil. |
| Beans (pole) | 1 week after | 60-70 | Need trellis. Produce longer than bush. |
| Corn | 2 weeks after | 70-90 | Plant in blocks (4x4 minimum) for pollination. |
| Cucumbers | 2 weeks after | 55-65 | Direct sow. Trellis saves space. |
| Melons | 2-3 weeks after | 80-100 | Need long warm season. Mulch heavily. |
| Sweet potatoes | 3-4 weeks after | 90-120 | Plant slips. Need warm soil and long season. |
| Okra | 2-3 weeks after | 55-65 | Loves heat. Very productive. |

## Fall Planting

Many cool-season crops grow better in fall than spring — fewer pests, steadier moisture, sweeter flavor after frost.

Count backward from your first fall frost date. Plant fall crops at least that many days plus 14 (for slowing growth in shorter days) before first frost.

Example: kale needs 60 days. First frost October 15. Plant by: October 15 minus 60 minus 14 = August 1.

## Succession Planting

Don't plant everything at once. For crops you eat fresh (lettuce, radishes, beans), plant a new batch every 2-3 weeks. This extends your harvest over months instead of getting everything at once and watching half of it rot.

## Saving Seeds for Next Year

The most critical long-term skill. Let your best plants go to seed. Collect seeds when dry. Store in a cool, dark, dry place. Seeds from open-pollinated (heirloom) varieties come true. Seeds from hybrid varieties (F1) may not resemble the parent plant. Prioritize saving open-pollinated seed stock.
