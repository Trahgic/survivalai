Generators convert fuel into electricity on demand. They're the fastest way to get significant power running — no sun required, no wind required, no infrastructure needed. The tradeoff: they consume fuel (which runs out), they're noisy (which attracts attention), and they require maintenance (which demands parts and knowledge).

## Fuel Types

### Gasoline

**Pros:** Most common generator fuel. Generators are cheap and widely available. Fuel available everywhere (pre-disaster).

**Cons:** Gasoline goes stale in 3–6 months without stabilizer. Highly flammable and explosive (vapor ignites easily). Hard to store long-term safely. Engine carburetor clogs from old gas — the most common generator failure.

**Storage:** Add fuel stabilizer (Sta-Bil or similar) at purchase. Store in approved containers (red gas cans), away from heat and ignition sources, in a ventilated area. Rotate stock every 6 months. Ethanol-free gas stores longer than E10/E15 blends.

### Diesel

**Pros:** More fuel-efficient than gasoline (20–30% more runtime per gallon). Diesel fuel stores longer (12+ months). No spark ignition system — fewer ignition-related failures. Diesel generators are typically more durable and longer-lasting.

**Cons:** Diesel generators are heavier, more expensive, and louder. Diesel gels in cold weather below 10–15°F (-10 to -12°C) — add anti-gel additive in winter. Harder to start in extreme cold.

**Storage:** Diesel stores well for 12–18 months in sealed containers. Add biocide (diesel fuel treatment) annually to prevent microbial growth in the tank.

### Propane (LP Gas)

**Pros:** Stores indefinitely — doesn't degrade like gasoline or diesel. Burns cleaner (less carbon buildup on engine). No carburetor to clog. Quieter operation. Available in standard tanks (20 lb BBQ tanks to 500+ gallon bulk tanks).

**Cons:** Lower energy density than gasoline or diesel — uses more fuel per hour for the same wattage. Propane generators are less common and slightly more expensive. Tanks are bulky and heavy. Performance drops in extreme cold (reduced vapor pressure).

### Dual-Fuel / Tri-Fuel

Some generators run on gasoline AND propane (dual-fuel) or gasoline, propane, AND natural gas (tri-fuel). Conversion kits are available for many generators. Having multiple fuel options is valuable — run gasoline while it's fresh, switch to propane for long-term.

## Generator Sizing

**Running watts vs. starting watts:** Motors and compressors draw 2–3x their running wattage when starting. Your generator needs enough starting watts to handle the highest single-start demand plus all running loads.

**Common loads:**
- LED lights: 10–20W each
- Phone charger: 10–25W
- Laptop: 50–100W
- Refrigerator: 100–400W running, 1,200W starting
- Well pump: 750–1,500W running, 2,200W starting
- Microwave: 1,000–1,500W
- Space heater: 1,500W
- Power tools: 500–1,500W running

**Sizing rule:** Add up all loads you'll run simultaneously. Choose a generator rated at 125% of that total for running watts, and verify the starting watts cover your highest-start appliance.

**Common generator sizes:**
- 2,000W inverter generator: Lights, phone, laptop, small appliances. Quiet. 1 gallon per 4–8 hours.
- 3,500W: Adds a refrigerator and a few more loads. 1 gallon per 3–5 hours.
- 5,000–7,000W: Runs most of a household minus AC and electric heat. 1 gallon per 2–4 hours.
- 10,000W+: Full household backup. 1 gallon per 1–2 hours.

## Operation

### Setup

1. Place the generator outdoors — NEVER inside a building, garage, or enclosed space. Carbon monoxide kills.
2. Position at least 20 feet from any window, door, or vent. Exhaust drifts and enters buildings.
3. Place on a flat, dry, level surface.
4. Ground the generator if using for extended periods or powering a building's wiring (drive a ground rod and connect with a wire to the generator's grounding lug).
5. Use heavy-duty extension cords rated for the wattage you're drawing. Undersized cords cause voltage drops and overheating.

> **CRITICAL:** Carbon monoxide from generator exhaust kills hundreds of people per year during power outages. You CANNOT smell it, see it, or taste it. Never run a generator indoors, in a garage (even with the door open), in a basement, or under a carport. Keep it outside with exhaust pointing away from the building. If anyone develops headache, dizziness, or nausea while a generator is running, shut it down and ventilate immediately.

### Load Management

If your generator is smaller than your total demand, rotate loads:

1. Run the refrigerator for 2 hours (cools down), then shut it off.
2. Switch to well pump, fill water storage, shut off.
3. Switch to battery charger for your solar bank.
4. Run lights and communication in the evening.

A small generator used strategically provides far more value than trying to run everything at once.

### Fuel Consumption

Running a generator at 50% load is roughly 25–30% more fuel-efficient per watt than at 100% load. Don't under-load your generator dramatically (below 30% load causes carbon buildup and glazing of cylinder walls), but don't max it out continuously either. The sweet spot is 50–75% of rated capacity.

## Maintenance

### Before Each Use

1. Check oil level. Low oil destroys the engine in minutes. Most generators have a low-oil shutoff — don't rely on it.
2. Check fuel level and fuel quality. Smell the gas — stale gas has a varnishy smell. Drain and replace if stale.
3. Check air filter. A clogged air filter reduces power and increases fuel consumption.
4. Check for fuel leaks around connections and the carburetor.

### Regular Maintenance

- **Oil change:** Every 50–100 hours of operation or at least annually. Use the weight specified in the manual (typically 10W-30).
- **Air filter:** Clean or replace every 50 hours.
- **Spark plug:** Check and gap or replace every 100 hours.
- **Fuel filter:** Replace annually or every 200 hours.
- **Valves:** Adjust every 200–300 hours if you have the specs and tools.
- **Exercise the generator** monthly for 30 minutes under load, even if you don't need power. This prevents fuel system varnishing, lubricates seals, and charges the starting battery (if equipped).

### Carburetor Issues (Gasoline)

The most common failure: stale gasoline varnishes the carburetor jets, preventing fuel flow. The generator cranks but won't start or runs rough.

**Fix:**
1. Drain the carburetor bowl (small bolt or screw at the bottom of the carburetor).
2. Clean the main jet (a tiny brass nozzle inside the carburetor) with a thin wire, compressed air, or carburetor cleaner spray.
3. If that doesn't work, remove the carburetor, disassemble, and soak all metal parts in carburetor cleaner for 24 hours. Clear all passages with compressed air or wire.
4. Reassemble, fill with fresh fuel, and try again.

**Prevention:** Run the carburetor dry before storage. Turn off the fuel valve and let the engine die from fuel starvation. This leaves the carburetor empty — no fuel to varnish.

## Security Considerations

Generators are loud and attract attention. In a grid-down scenario:

- **Noise reduction:** Inverter generators are significantly quieter (50–60 dB) than conventional generators (70–80+ dB). If noise is a concern, invest in an inverter model.
- **Sound barriers:** Position the generator behind a wall, in a pit, or behind stacked materials. Don't enclose it (ventilation and CO risk) but use baffles to direct sound away from roads and neighbors.
- **Run schedule:** Don't run 24/7. Run during hours when ambient noise (wind, rain) masks the sound. Nighttime generator noise carries much farther than daytime.
- **Fuel security:** A generator without fuel is useless weight. Secure your fuel supply. Don't advertise that you have fuel or power.
