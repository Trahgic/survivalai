When stored fuel runs out, you can produce your own from biological materials. None of these methods are as convenient as pulling up to a gas station, but they can keep engines, generators, and lamps running indefinitely using renewable inputs — vegetable oil, wood, grain, and manure.

## Biodiesel

Diesel fuel made from vegetable oil or animal fat through a chemical reaction (transesterification). Runs in any diesel engine with no modifications.

### Process

1. **Start with oil.** Waste cooking oil (filtered), fresh vegetable oil (soybean, canola, sunflower, palm), or rendered animal fat. The oil must be dry — water ruins the reaction. Heat to 250°F (120°C) briefly to drive off moisture if needed.
2. **Prepare the methoxide.** Dissolve sodium hydroxide (lye — available as drain cleaner, must be 100% NaOH) in methanol (wood alcohol — available as HEET fuel-line antifreeze in the yellow bottle). Ratio: 3.5 grams NaOH per liter of oil, dissolved in 200 mL of methanol per liter of oil. Stir until fully dissolved.

> **WARNING:** Methanol is toxic — absorbed through the skin, inhalation of vapors causes blindness. Sodium hydroxide causes severe burns. Wear gloves, eye protection, and work in a well-ventilated area. This is real chemistry with real hazards.

3. **React.** Heat the oil to 130°F (55°C). Slowly pour the methoxide into the oil while stirring vigorously. Continue stirring for 1 hour. The NaOH catalyzes a reaction where methanol replaces the glycerin backbone of the oil molecules, creating fatty acid methyl esters (biodiesel) and glycerin.
4. **Settle.** Pour the mixture into a clear container and let it sit for 8–24 hours. Two layers form: biodiesel on top (amber, thin), glycerin on the bottom (dark, thick).
5. **Drain the glycerin** from the bottom. It has uses — soap making, fire starter, compost.
6. **Wash the biodiesel.** Gently mist warm water over the surface, let it settle through the biodiesel and collect on the bottom. Drain. Repeat 3–4 times until the wash water is clear. This removes residual soap, methanol, and catalyst.
7. **Dry.** Heat to 150°F (65°C) and hold for 15 minutes, or let sit uncovered in warm sun. Any remaining water causes problems in injectors.

### Quality Test

Good biodiesel is clear amber, not cloudy. It should pass the "27/3 test": at 27°C (81°F) it should be clear within 3 hours of being at that temperature. Cloudy biodiesel has water or soap contamination — wash again.

### Yield

1 gallon of vegetable oil produces roughly 1 gallon of biodiesel. You lose volume to glycerin and washing.

## Wood Gas (Gasification)

Wood gas is produced by heating wood in a low-oxygen environment. The resulting gas (a mixture of carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and methane) can run internal combustion engines — both gasoline and diesel with modifications. Millions of vehicles ran on wood gas during WWII fuel shortages.

### How It Works

1. Wood (or any dry biomass — charcoal, nutshells, corn cobs) is loaded into a sealed reactor.
2. A controlled amount of air enters from below (enough for partial combustion, not enough for full burning).
3. The heat from partial combustion breaks down the remaining wood into flammable gases.
4. Gas exits the top of the reactor, passes through a cooling and filtering system (to remove tar, ash, and moisture), and feeds into an engine's air intake.

### Simplified Gasifier Design

**Downdraft gasifier (FEMA design — published by the US government for wartime fuel production):**

1. **Reactor:** A steel drum or cylinder (55-gallon drum works). Fill with dry wood chunks (1–3 inch pieces).
2. **Fire zone:** A constriction (narrowing) partway down the reactor where the highest temperatures occur. This is where tars crack into usable gas.
3. **Air inlet:** Controlled air enters at the fire zone through nozzles (called tuyeres). Limit the air to 25–30% of what a full fire would need.
4. **Gas outlet:** Hot gas exits below the fire zone, passes through the remaining charcoal bed (which filters it), then exits the reactor bottom.
5. **Cooling:** The gas must cool before entering an engine. Run it through a pipe or radiator exposed to air. Hot gas damages engine components.
6. **Filtering:** Pass cooled gas through a filter (cloth, fine mesh, or a container of sawdust/wood chips) to remove particulates.
7. **Engine connection:** Plumb the clean, cool gas into the engine's air intake with a mixing valve. The engine runs on a mixture of wood gas and air. Power output drops 30–50% compared to gasoline because wood gas has lower energy density.

### Limitations

- Requires dry wood — wet wood produces excess tar that clogs everything.
- Tar management is the biggest challenge. Tars condense in cool pipes and gum up engines.
- Power output is reduced compared to gasoline or diesel.
- Requires continuous feeding and monitoring.
- CO in wood gas is lethal — never operate in an enclosed space. CO poisoning is the primary safety risk.

## Ethanol (Alcohol Fuel)

Ethanol (grain alcohol) can be produced by fermenting sugars and distilling the result. It runs in gasoline engines with minor adjustments (larger jets or fuel volume increase).

### Fermentation

1. **Feedstock:** Any sugar or starch source — fruit, grain (corn, wheat, barley), sugar cane, sugar beets, potatoes, honey.
2. **Mash:** For grain: cook cracked grain in water to convert starch to sugar (mashing). For fruit: crush and add water. For sugar: dissolve in warm water.
3. **Yeast:** Add brewer's yeast, baker's yeast, or turbo yeast. Yeast consumes sugar and produces ethanol and CO2.
4. **Fermentation:** Keep the mash at 70–85°F (21–29°C) in a sealed container with an airlock (allows CO2 out but no air in). Fermentation takes 3–14 days. It's done when bubbling stops.
5. **Result:** "Wash" at 8–15% alcohol by volume. Similar to beer.

### Distillation

To get fuel-grade ethanol (at least 80% alcohol, ideally 90%+), you must distill the wash.

1. Heat the wash in a sealed boiler. Ethanol boils at 173°F (78°C), water at 212°F (100°C). By heating to between these temperatures, ethanol vapor rises while water stays behind.
2. The vapor passes through a condenser (a coiled tube cooled by cold water). Vapor condenses back to liquid ethanol.
3. Collect the condensate. The first small amount ("foreshots") contains methanol — discard it (toxic). The next portion ("heads") is harsh — set aside. The main run ("hearts") is your fuel-grade ethanol. The last portion ("tails") has more water — set aside or redistill.
4. A single distillation yields roughly 60–70% ethanol. A second distillation (redistilling the hearts) gets you to 85–90%.
5. Above 95% (azeotrope), you can't improve with simple distillation — it requires molecular sieves or other dehydration methods.

### Using Ethanol as Fuel

- Ethanol has about 67% the energy density of gasoline. Fuel consumption increases roughly 30–40%.
- E85 (85% ethanol, 15% gasoline) runs in most fuel-injected gasoline vehicles with no modifications.
- Pure ethanol in carbureted engines requires larger jets (20–30% more fuel flow). Re-jet the carburetor or adjust the mixture screw.
- Ethanol is corrosive to some rubber seals and older fuel system components. Modern vehicles handle it fine. Older engines may need fuel system rubber replaced with ethanol-compatible materials.

## Biogas (Methane)

Methane produced from decomposing organic material (manure, food scraps, plant waste) in an anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment. Burns in stoves, lamps, and engines.

### Simple Biodigester

1. **Container:** A sealed drum, tank, or plastic bag. Must be airtight except for the gas outlet.
2. **Feed:** Mix manure (cow, pig, chicken — fresh) with water at roughly 1:1 ratio. Add food scraps, plant waste, or other organic material.
3. **Load the digester** and seal it.
4. **Bacteria** naturally present in the manure break down the organic material anaerobically, producing methane (50–70%) and carbon dioxide (30–50%).
5. **Gas collection:** Pipe the gas from the top of the digester to a storage bag or directly to a burner.
6. **Retention time:** 20–40 days in warm climates. Slower in cold. The digester works continuously — add fresh material regularly and drain processed slurry (which is excellent fertilizer).

**Production rate:** A small household digester (200 gallons) fed with the daily manure from 2–3 cows produces roughly 30–50 cubic feet of biogas per day — enough for 3–4 hours of cooking.

### Safety

- Methane is explosive in concentrations of 5–15% in air. Never check for leaks with a flame. Use soapy water (bubbles indicate leaks).
- Hydrogen sulfide (rotten egg smell) is also produced in small amounts. Toxic in concentration. Ensure outdoor ventilation around the digester.
- The digestate (processed slurry) is pathogen-reduced but not sterile. Handle as you would compost.
