Keeping a vehicle running when there's no mechanic matters more than you'd think. A working vehicle means mobility, shelter, power generation (alternator), climate control, and hauling capacity. Most roadside breakdowns are simple problems — flat tires, dead batteries, overheating, loose connections. You don't need to rebuild an engine. You need to handle the top 10 failures that strand people.

## Flat Tire — Change It

The single most common vehicle failure. Every driver should be able to do this in under 15 minutes.

1. Pull to a firm, flat surface away from traffic. Turn on hazard lights. Set the parking brake.
2. Get the spare tire, jack, and lug wrench from the vehicle (usually under the trunk floor or bolted under the truck bed).
3. **Before jacking:** Loosen the lug nuts 1/4 turn while the tire is still on the ground. Turn counterclockwise. If they're very tight, stand on the wrench or use a pipe for leverage. Don't remove them yet.
4. Place the jack under the vehicle's designated jack point (check the owner's manual — usually the reinforced frame rail near the flat tire). On trucks, use the axle or frame.
5. Jack the vehicle up until the flat tire is 1–2 inches off the ground.
6. Remove the lug nuts, pull the flat tire off the hub.
7. Mount the spare tire, hand-tighten the lug nuts.
8. Lower the vehicle until the tire is touching the ground but not bearing full weight.
9. Tighten lug nuts fully in a star pattern (opposite corners, not going around in a circle). This seats the wheel evenly.
10. Lower fully, remove the jack, and recheck lug nut tightness.

**Spare tire notes:** Most cars have a compact spare ("donut") rated for 50 mph max and 50–70 miles. Full-size spares are better if you have room. Check spare tire pressure periodically — they lose air sitting in the trunk.

### Repairing a Flat (No Spare)

A tire plug kit is cheap and works for punctures in the tread area. Not for sidewall damage.

1. Find the puncture — look for the nail, screw, or embedded object. If it's still in the tire, leave it for now.
2. Remove the object with pliers.
3. Ream the hole using the rasp tool from the plug kit — push it in and out several times to roughen and size the hole.
4. Thread a plug strip through the eye of the insertion tool. Coat with rubber cement from the kit.
5. Push the insertion tool straight into the hole until about 1/2 inch of plug sticks out.
6. Pull the tool straight out — the plug stays in.
7. Trim excess plug flush with the tread.
8. Inflate the tire and check for leaks with soapy water (bubbles = still leaking).

## Dead Battery — Jump Starting

1. Position a running vehicle close enough for jumper cables to reach, but don't let the cars touch.
2. **Red cable first:** Connect one red clamp to the dead battery's positive (+) terminal. Connect the other red clamp to the good battery's positive (+) terminal.
3. **Black cable:** Connect one black clamp to the good battery's negative (-) terminal. Connect the other black clamp to an unpainted metal surface on the dead car's engine block — not the dead battery's negative terminal. This grounds the circuit and keeps any spark away from the battery (hydrogen gas risk).
4. Start the running vehicle. Wait 2–3 minutes.
5. Start the dead vehicle. If it cranks slowly, wait another few minutes for the dead battery to charge.
6. Once started, remove cables in reverse order: black from the dead car first, black from the good car, red from the good car, red from the dead car.
7. Drive the now-running vehicle for at least 20 minutes to recharge the battery.

**Battery won't hold a charge:** The battery may be permanently dead. Test with a multimeter — a healthy charged battery reads 12.4–12.7 volts. Below 12.0 volts, it's either discharged (charge it) or failed (replace it). Check terminals for corrosion — white/green crusty buildup on the posts. Clean with a wire brush and baking soda solution.

## Overheating

Temperature gauge in the red or steam coming from under the hood. Pull over immediately — continuing to drive an overheating engine can warp the cylinder head, blow the head gasket, or seize the engine. These are expensive and hard to fix.

### Immediate Steps

1. Turn the heater to full hot and fan to maximum. The heater core is a small radiator — this pulls heat from the engine.
2. If the temperature doesn't drop, pull over and turn the engine off.
3. **Do not open the radiator cap when the engine is hot.** The coolant is pressurized and above boiling temperature. It will erupt and cause severe burns. Wait at least 30 minutes.
4. Check the coolant reservoir (the plastic overflow tank connected to the radiator). If it's empty, the system is low on coolant.

### Finding the Leak

Once cool, open the radiator cap slowly (use a rag for protection). Look for:

- **Hose failure:** Inspect upper and lower radiator hoses for cracks, bulges, or wet spots at clamp connections. Squeeze them — they should feel firm, not mushy. Tighten clamps. Temporary hose repair: wrap the damaged area with duct tape or rubber and hose clamps.
- **Radiator leak:** Look for wet spots or green/orange stains on the radiator face. Small leaks can be temporarily sealed with commercial radiator stop-leak, or in an emergency, cracking an egg into the radiator — the egg white cooks and plugs small holes. This is a temporary fix only.
- **Water pump:** A leaking water pump usually weeps from a small hole at the bottom of the pump housing. Replacing a water pump requires draining the cooling system and removing the drive belt.

### Refilling Coolant

Use a 50/50 mix of antifreeze and water. In an emergency, straight water works — it just doesn't protect against freezing or boiling as well. Never run an engine with no coolant at all.

## Alternator Problems

The alternator charges the battery while the engine runs. If it fails, the battery drains and eventually the car dies — usually after 30–60 minutes of driving.

**Symptoms:** Battery warning light on the dashboard. Dimming headlights. Electrical accessories weakening. Engine dies and won't restart (battery drained).

**Quick test:** With the engine running, disconnect the negative battery cable (carefully, don't short it). If the engine immediately dies, the alternator isn't producing power. If it keeps running, the alternator is working and the problem is elsewhere.

**Drive belt:** The alternator is driven by a belt from the crankshaft. If the belt is broken, cracked, glazed, or loose, the alternator can't spin. Check tension — the belt should deflect about 1/2 inch when pressed firmly. Tighten or replace.

Replacing an alternator in the field requires basic tools (wrenches, socket set) and a replacement unit. It's usually bolted to the engine with 2–3 bolts, plus the electrical connections and drive belt.

## Fuel System Issues

### No Fuel Reaching Engine

1. Check the fuel gauge. Is there gas?
2. Listen for the fuel pump when you turn the key to ON (not start). You should hear a brief whirring/buzzing from the rear of the vehicle. No sound = dead fuel pump or blown fuse.
3. Check the fuel pump fuse and relay in the fuse box (locations vary — check the owner's manual or the fuse box cover diagram).

### Fuel Line Leak

Gasoline smell + visible wetness under the vehicle. This is a fire hazard.

1. Don't start the engine.
2. Locate the leak. Rubber fuel lines deteriorate and crack at connections and bends.
3. For small leaks at connections: tighten the clamp. For cracked rubber line: cut out the damaged section and splice in new fuel-rated hose with clamps on both ends. Don't use regular rubber hose — it dissolves in gasoline. Use fuel-rated hose only.
4. For hard fuel line (metal tubing) leaks: clean the area and wrap tightly with fuel-resistant tape as a temporary fix. Proper repair requires cutting and flaring new tubing or using a compression fitting.

## Belt and Hose Failures

### Drive Belt (Serpentine Belt)

One belt drives the alternator, power steering, A/C compressor, and water pump. If it breaks, you lose all of these. The engine won't overheat immediately (it takes the water pump failing to cause that), but you'll lose power steering and charging.

Keep a spare belt in the vehicle. Changing it requires routing around the pulleys (diagram is usually on a sticker under the hood) and releasing the tensioner (a spring-loaded pulley — push it with a wrench to release tension, slide the belt on, then release).

### Heater Hoses and Bypass Hoses

Smaller hoses that can burst and dump coolant fast. If a heater hose bursts and you can't fix it, you can bypass it: disconnect both ends from the heater core fittings, and connect them to each other with a coupler or just loop and clamp. You lose the heater but the engine stays cool.

## Emergency Toolkit for Vehicles

Keep these in every vehicle:

- Lug wrench and jack (should be factory-supplied)
- Jumper cables (at least 12 feet)
- Basic wrench set (8mm–19mm covers most cars)
- Socket set with ratchet
- Screwdrivers (flat and Phillips)
- Pliers and wire cutters
- Duct tape and electrical tape
- Zip ties (assorted sizes)
- Spare drive belt
- Tire plug kit and portable tire inflator (12V plug-in)
- 1 quart motor oil
- 1 gallon premixed coolant
- Multimeter (for electrical diagnosis)
- Fire extinguisher
- Rags, gloves, flashlight

> **When to stop driving:** If the engine is making a metallic knocking sound, if oil pressure drops to zero, if the temperature gauge pegs and stays, or if you see metal shavings in the oil — stop immediately. Driving through these conditions turns a repairable problem into a destroyed engine.
