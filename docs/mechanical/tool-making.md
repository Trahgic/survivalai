Every other skill in this knowledge base depends on tools. Without the ability to make and repair tools, you're stuck with what you found. Blacksmithing is the foundation — with a forge, an anvil, and a hammer, you can make knives, chisels, axes, hooks, hinges, nails, brackets, and repair almost anything made of steel.

## Building a Forge

A forge is just a contained fire with forced air. The forced air is what makes it different from a campfire — it pushes temperatures high enough to make steel soft and workable (1,500°F+ / 815°C+).

### Brake Drum Forge (Easiest Build)

1. Find a car or truck brake drum — they're thick cast iron and already bowl-shaped.
2. Weld or bolt steel legs to the bottom (3 legs, about 30 inches tall for a comfortable working height). Or set it on top of a steel table or stacked cinder blocks.
3. Drill or cut a hole in the bottom of the drum for the air inlet (about 2 inches diameter).
4. Connect a pipe from the bottom to a blower. An old hair dryer, bathroom exhaust fan, or hand-cranked blower all work. Even a length of pipe you blow through works in a pinch.
5. Line the inside of the drum with a layer of clay, sand, or refractory cement to protect the drum and hold heat better. Leave the air inlet open.
6. Fuel: charcoal is easiest to start with. Coal (bituminous/blacksmithing coal) burns hotter and longer. Hardwood charcoal you make yourself works fine (see **charcoal-making.md**).

### Using the Forge

1. Build a small fire in the center of the forge with paper and kindling.
2. Add charcoal gradually as the fire grows. Don't dump a load of cold fuel on a small fire — it'll smother.
3. Turn on the air supply at low speed. The fire should glow bright orange to yellow.
4. Place the steel in the fire and let it heat. Rotate the piece so it heats evenly.
5. For general forging, you want bright orange to yellow heat (roughly 1,700–2,100°F / 930–1,150°C).
6. For forge welding, you need yellow-white heat (2,300°F+ / 1,260°C+). More air and more fuel.
7. Don't leave steel in the fire unattended — it can burn (actually oxidize away) at very high temperatures, especially thin stock.

## The Anvil

You need a heavy, hard surface to hammer on. A real anvil is ideal but anything heavy and flat works:

- **Railroad track section** stood on end or laid flat. The most common improvised anvil. Mount it securely.
- **Large sledgehammer head** set in a stump.
- **Thick steel plate** (1 inch+) bolted to a heavy stump or bench.
- **Forklift tine section** — hardened steel, excellent improvised anvil face.

Mount your anvil at knuckle height when standing. Your arm should swing naturally and the hammer should hit flat.

## Basic Forging Operations

### Drawing Out (Making Longer and Thinner)

Heat the steel to bright orange. Hammer with overlapping blows, working from the far end toward you. Rotate the piece 90 degrees and hammer again. This stretches the steel in one direction. Used for making points, tapers, and reducing stock to a smaller cross-section.

### Upsetting (Making Shorter and Thicker)

Heat the end you want to thicken. Hold the piece vertically and hammer straight down on the top end, driving the hot steel back into itself. Or hold it against the anvil face and strike the cold end. Used to create heads on nails and bolts, and to thicken stock for forge welding.

### Bending

Heat at the bend point. Place the piece on the anvil edge with the bend line at the edge, and hammer the overhanging portion down. For tight bends, hammer over the anvil horn (the pointed end). For gentle curves, bend gradually over a rounded form.

### Punching and Drifting

Punch a hole through hot steel using a punch (a tapered steel rod). Place the hot workpiece over the pritchel hole (the round hole in the anvil face) and drive the punch through. Then insert a drift (a tapered tool the shape of the desired hole) and drive it through to enlarge and shape the hole.

### Cutting

Score hot steel with a hardy (a chisel that fits in the anvil's square hole) or a hot cut (a handled chisel struck with a hammer). Cut most of the way through, then break off the waste piece. You can also cut cold steel with a cold chisel and hammer, but it's slower and duller.

## Making a Knife (Starter Project)

A simple knife is the classic first forging project. Use a piece of high-carbon steel — an old file, leaf spring, coil spring, or lawnmower blade.

1. **Forge the profile:** Heat the steel to bright orange. Hammer it into a rough blade shape — taper one end for the blade, leave the other end thicker for the handle tang. Draw out the blade to about 3/16 inch thick. Don't try to make the edge thin yet.
2. **Refine the shape:** Clean up the profile with lighter hammer blows. Straighten any warps by laying the blade flat and tapping.
3. **Normalize:** Heat the blade to medium red (non-magnetic — test with a magnet; steel loses magnetism at the critical temperature around 1,475°F / 800°C for most carbon steels). Let it cool in still air. Do this 2–3 times. Normalizing refines the grain structure and relieves stress from forging.
4. **Grind/file the edge:** With the blade cold, grind or file the edge geometry. For a working knife, grind a flat bevel from the spine about halfway to the edge, leaving the edge itself about the thickness of a dime. Don't sharpen to a final edge yet — it'll burn off during hardening.
5. **Harden:** Heat the blade evenly to non-magnetic temperature (even cherry red glow, test with magnet). Immediately quench in oil (vegetable oil, motor oil, ATF — not water, which can crack high-carbon steel). Plunge edge-first, straight down, and move it gently back and forth. The steel is now glass-hard and very brittle.
6. **Temper:** Clean the surface with sandpaper so you can see the color change. Heat gently — an oven at 400°F (200°C) for 1 hour is ideal. Without an oven: hold the blade above the forge coals at a distance, watching the surface color change. For a knife, you want straw yellow to light bronze (around 375–425°F / 190–220°C). This reduces hardness slightly but dramatically increases toughness so the edge won't chip.
7. **Final sharpening:** Now grind and hone the final edge (see **sharpening.md**).
8. **Handle:** Shape a handle from hardwood. Drill a hole for the tang, set with epoxy or drive the hot tang into the handle (it'll burn its own hole — let it cool and seat tightly). Pin through with a brass rod or nail for security.

## Heat Treating Steel

Heat treatment is what makes the difference between soft steel that bends and hard steel that holds an edge.

### Hardening

Heat to critical temperature (non-magnetic, cherry red to orange depending on alloy) and quench rapidly. This locks the carbon atoms in a hard crystalline structure (martensite).

- **High-carbon steel (files, springs, old tools):** Oil quench. Water quench risks cracking.
- **Mild steel (A36, rebar, structural):** Can't be meaningfully hardened — not enough carbon. Don't waste time trying to harden mild steel for cutting tools.

### Tempering

After hardening, steel is too brittle for most uses. Tempering reduces hardness to a usable level.

Heat the hardened steel gently and watch the surface color change as temperature rises:

| Color | Temperature | Use |
|-------|------------|-----|
| Light straw | 375°F / 190°C | Razors, engraving tools |
| Dark straw | 425°F / 220°C | Knives, chisels |
| Bronze/brown | 475°F / 245°C | Axes, wood chisels |
| Purple | 525°F / 275°C | Springs, screwdrivers |
| Blue | 575°F / 300°C | Springs, saws |

Let cool in air after reaching the desired color. You can re-temper if you went too far — just re-harden and try again.

### Annealing (Softening)

To soften hardened steel for drilling, filing, or reshaping: heat to critical temperature and cool as slowly as possible. Bury in dry sand, ashes, or vermiculite and leave overnight. This produces the softest possible state.

## Making Common Tools

**Chisels:** Start with a round or square bar of high-carbon steel (old coil spring works well). Forge a taper at one end, harden and temper to dark straw or bronze.

**Punches:** Same as chisels but leave the working end thicker. Temper to bronze/purple.

**Hooks and S-hooks:** Mild steel rod or rebar. Heat and bend around the anvil horn. No heat treating needed.

**Nails:** Cut short lengths of mild steel rod. Heat one end and hammer a point. Flip, insert in a nail header (a thick plate with a hole slightly larger than the nail shank), and hammer a head on the other end.

**Tongs:** Essential for holding hot steel. Made from two bars with a rivet through the pivot point. The jaw shape should match what you're holding — flat jaws for flat stock, V-jaws for round stock.

**Handles and hafts:** Shape a handle from ash, hickory, or oak. Fit to the tool's eye or tang. Wedge tightly. A loose tool head is dangerous.

> **Where to get steel:** Old files, leaf springs from vehicles, coil springs, lawnmower blades, and ball bearings are all high-carbon steel suitable for tools. Railroad spikes are medium-carbon — usable for light tools but won't hold a fine edge. Rebar is usually mild steel — good for hooks, brackets, and structural work but not cutting tools.
