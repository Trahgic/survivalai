Plumbing is moving water where you want it and waste where you don't. In a grid-down situation, that might mean fixing a busted pipe, rigging a gravity-fed supply from a rain barrel, or building a simple water system from scratch. The principles are the same whether you're patching a suburban kitchen sink or plumbing a homestead.

## Find and Use Shutoff Valves

Before any repair, stop the water flow.

**Main shutoff:** Usually near the water meter or where the main line enters the building. Turn clockwise to close (righty-tighty). Gate valves (round handle, multi-turn) or ball valves (lever handle, quarter-turn). Ball valves are more reliable — gate valves can seize if unused for years. If the valve is stuck, don't force it with a pipe wrench or you'll snap the valve body. Apply penetrating oil and work it back and forth gently.

**Fixture shutoffs:** Under sinks, behind toilets, below water heaters. Small valves on the supply lines to individual fixtures. Turn these off for localized repairs without shutting down the whole house.

**No shutoff valve?** You may need to shut off at the meter or cap the line. In an emergency, a wooden plug whittled to size and driven into an open pipe slows a leak enough to work.

## Pipe Types and How to Work Them

### Copper

Found in most homes built 1960–2000. Reliable, long-lasting, but requires soldering to join.

**Soldering copper pipe:**
1. Cut with a pipe cutter (rotate around the pipe, tightening slightly each pass) or hacksaw. Deburr the inside edge with a reamer or file.
2. Clean the outside of the pipe end and the inside of the fitting with emery cloth or sandpaper until shiny.
3. Apply flux (soldering paste) to both surfaces.
4. Assemble the joint — push the pipe fully into the fitting.
5. Heat the joint with a propane torch. Apply heat to the fitting, not the pipe — the fitting is thicker and needs more heat.
6. When the flux sizzles and starts to draw toward the heat, touch solder to the joint where pipe meets fitting. The solder should melt on contact with the hot metal and get sucked into the gap by capillary action.
7. A good joint shows a thin ring of solder all the way around. Wipe excess with a damp rag while still hot.
8. Wait 30 seconds before touching (copper conducts heat).

**Emergency copper repair:** A compression fitting (two pieces that clamp the pipe with a ferrule and nut) joins copper without soldering. Push-fit fittings (SharkBite brand and similar) just push onto the pipe — no tools at all. Both are more expensive but work immediately and require zero skill.

### PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

White plastic pipe. Common for drain, waste, and vent (DWV) lines. Not used for hot water supply (it softens).

**Joining PVC:**
1. Cut square with a hacksaw or PVC cutter.
2. Deburr the cut end.
3. Dry-fit first — mark alignment so you assemble correctly. PVC cement sets in seconds.
4. Apply PVC primer (purple liquid) to both surfaces — this softens the plastic.
5. Apply PVC cement to both surfaces. Push together with a quarter-twist, hold 30 seconds.
6. The joint is pressure-ready in 15 minutes, fully cured in 24 hours.

### PEX (Cross-Linked Polyethylene)

Flexible plastic tubing, color-coded (red for hot, blue for cold). Increasingly common in modern homes. Bends around corners, doesn't burst as easily when frozen.

**Joining PEX:** Requires crimp rings and a crimp tool, or push-fit fittings (SharkBite). PEX can't be solvent-welded like PVC. It's easy to work with but you need the right fittings.

### Galvanized Steel

Found in older homes (pre-1960). Threaded connections. Corrodes from the inside over decades, reducing flow and contaminating water with rust.

**Working with galvanized:** Unscrew at threaded fittings with two pipe wrenches (one on each side of the joint, turning opposite directions). Apply Teflon tape or pipe dope to threads before reassembling. If replacing, transition to copper or PEX at the first opportunity — galvanized has a limited lifespan.

## Common Leak Repairs

### Pipe Leak (Pinhole or Crack)

**Temporary:** Rubber patch (a piece of inner tube or rubber sheet) held tight with a hose clamp or wrapped tightly with self-fusing silicone tape. This can hold for months on low-pressure lines.

**Better temporary:** Pipe repair clamp — a two-piece metal sleeve with bolts that clamps around the pipe over the leak with a rubber gasket inside. Hardware store item, installs in 2 minutes.

**Permanent:** Cut out the damaged section. Join in a new piece using couplings and the appropriate method for the pipe material (solder, cement, crimp, or thread).

### Leaking Fitting

Usually where pipe meets a valve, tee, or elbow. Tighten the fitting if threaded. If soldered, you'll need to re-solder (drain the line completely first — you can't solder a joint with water in the pipe; even a trickle of water prevents the joint from reaching soldering temperature — stuff a piece of bread into the pipe to temporarily dam the water while you solder, it dissolves when you turn the water back on).

### Leaking Faucet

**Compression faucet (two handles):** The rubber seat washer at the bottom of the stem is worn. Turn off the water, remove the handle (screw under the decorative cap), unscrew the packing nut, pull out the stem, replace the washer at the bottom. While you're in there, check the O-ring on the stem body too.

**Cartridge/single-lever faucet:** The internal cartridge is the replaceable core. Remove the handle, pull or unscrew the cartridge, take it to match a replacement. Install new cartridge in the same orientation.

## Gravity-Fed Water Systems

No pump, no electricity. Water flows downhill. If your water source is higher than your point of use, gravity does all the work.

### Design

1. **Elevation difference (head):** Every foot of elevation between the water source and the tap gives you about 0.43 PSI of pressure. A 20-foot elevation difference gives roughly 8.6 PSI — enough for a garden hose, simple tap, or drip irrigation. A shower needs about 5–10 PSI minimum. A modern house expects 40–60 PSI.
2. **Pipe sizing:** Use the largest pipe practical for the main run. Friction loss increases dramatically in small pipe over long distances. For runs over 200 feet, use at least 1-inch pipe. For shorter runs, 3/4-inch is fine.
3. **Storage tank:** Place your tank (rain barrel, IBC tote, cistern) at the highest feasible point. The higher the tank, the more pressure at the tap.
4. **Filter/screen:** Install a screen or filter at the tank outlet to prevent debris from entering the system.
5. **Valves:** Install shutoff valves at the tank and at each use point. Ball valves are best — they don't seize up.

### Pipe Route

Run pipe downhill with a consistent slope. Avoid high points in the line — air gets trapped and blocks flow (an "airlock"). If you must go over a rise, install an air release valve at the peak.

Bury pipe below frost line in cold climates (varies by region — 18 inches in the South, 48+ inches in northern states). If you can't bury deep enough, insulate with foam pipe insulation and mulch.

## Drain and Waste

### Clearing a Clog

**Plunger:** Works on toilets, sinks, and tubs. For sinks, block the overflow hole with a wet rag for full suction. Use a flange plunger (the one with the extra flap) for toilets.

**Drain snake:** A flexible metal cable you feed into the drain and rotate. It breaks through clogs that a plunger can't move. Feed it in until you feel resistance, then crank the handle and push. Pull back and push forward repeatedly.

**Baking soda and vinegar:** Pour 1/2 cup baking soda into the drain, follow with 1/2 cup vinegar, cover, wait 30 minutes, flush with hot water. Works on slow drains and grease buildup. Won't clear a solid blockage.

### Building a Simple Drain

Waste water needs to flow away from living areas and toward either a septic system, drainage field, or soak pit.

1. Slope drain pipes at 1/4 inch per foot. This is enough to keep solids moving but not so steep that water outruns the solids and leaves them behind.
2. All drains need venting — a pipe open to the air that prevents vacuum from forming in the drain line (without a vent, water draining from a sink creates suction that pulls water out of the trap below, letting sewer gas in). A vent pipe runs up from the drain line and exits through the roof or out a wall above head height.
3. Every fixture needs a trap — the U-shaped bend that holds water and blocks gas from coming back up. The water sitting in the trap is the seal. If a drain hasn't been used in a while and the trap dries out, sewer smell comes through. Pour water in to refill it.

## Improvised Fittings

When you can't get standard plumbing parts:

- **Garden hose fittings** can connect to improvised systems. Garden hose thread is 3/4 inch GHT — adapters exist to connect to standard pipe thread.
- **Automotive heater hose** (rubber) fits over barbed fittings and clamps down. Good for short runs of flexible connection.
- **Bamboo pipe:** Hollow bamboo sections joined with wrapped cord and sealed with pine pitch, beeswax, or tar. Used for centuries in Asia for irrigation.
- **PVC repair without cement:** If you're out of PVC cement, friction-fit a pipe into a fitting (sand the pipe end slightly oversize), wrap the joint with several layers of self-fusing silicone tape or rubber tape, then clamp with hose clamps. Not code but holds under low pressure.

> **Priority in a plumbing emergency:** Stop the water first (shutoff valve), assess the damage, then fix. A burst pipe at full pressure can dump hundreds of gallons in minutes, causing as much damage as the leak itself.
