Buildings collapse when they carry more weight than they're designed for, or when the materials are too weak or too small for the span. You don't need an engineering degree to build safe structures, but you do need to understand a few rules about how loads work and how wood and other materials carry them. Getting this wrong kills people — a roof that collapses under snow, a floor that gives way, a retaining wall that fails.

## How Loads Work

**Dead load:** The weight of the structure itself — the lumber, roofing material, flooring, walls. This is constant and predictable.

**Live load:** The weight of people, furniture, stored goods, and anything that moves or changes. Building codes typically assume 40 pounds per square foot (psf) for residential floor live load.

**Snow load:** Weight of snow on the roof. Varies by region — from 20 psf in moderate climates to 60+ psf in heavy snow areas. Wet heavy snow weighs roughly 5 lbs per inch of depth per square foot. Light dry snow weighs about 1.5 lbs per inch.

**Wind load:** Lateral pressure from wind. Affects walls and roof uplift. Less of a concern for heavy timber structures, critical for lightweight or tall structures.

**The safety factor:** Always build stronger than the calculated minimum. Professional engineers use safety factors of 2–3x for wood structures. If your calculation says a beam can hold 1,000 lbs, design as if it can only hold 400–500 lbs. You're not a professional engineer — err on the side of heavy.

## Lumber Sizing and Strength

Standard lumber is weaker than you'd expect because it's not the nominal size. A "2x4" is actually 1.5 x 3.5 inches. A "2x6" is 1.5 x 5.5 inches. All span tables and load calculations use actual dimensions, not nominal.

### Lumber Grades That Matter

- **#1 (Select Structural):** Fewest knots, straightest grain, strongest. Used for critical structural members.
- **#2 (Standard):** Some knots, generally straight. The most common framing lumber. Adequate for most residential construction.
- **#3 (Utility):** More knots, possible warp. Not for structural use in critical applications.

Large knots, especially on the bottom edge of a beam, dramatically reduce strength. A 1-inch knot on the bottom of a 2x8 joist can reduce its load capacity by 30–50%. When selecting lumber for beams and joists, pick the straightest, clearest (fewest knots) pieces and orient them with any crown (slight upward bow) facing up.

### Species Matter

Not all wood is equally strong. Common framing species ranked roughly by strength:

1. Douglas Fir, Southern Yellow Pine — strongest commonly available
2. Hem-Fir (Hemlock-Fir) — medium-strong
3. Spruce-Pine-Fir (SPF) — most common, adequate for standard residential framing
4. Cedar, Redwood — weak structurally, good for rot resistance

If you're salvaging lumber and don't know the species, assume the lowest strength rating and size up.

## Span Tables (Simplified)

Span is the unsupported distance between bearing points (posts, walls, or other supports). The longer the span, the larger the lumber needs to be.

### Floor Joists (40 psf live load, 10 psf dead load, #2 SPF lumber, 16" on center)

| Joist Size | Maximum Span |
|-----------|-------------|
| 2x6 | 8 ft 6 in |
| 2x8 | 11 ft 0 in |
| 2x10 | 13 ft 6 in |
| 2x12 | 16 ft 0 in |

These are code-rated maximums with proper graded lumber. In a field build with uncertain lumber quality, reduce these by 20–30% as a safety margin.

### Roof Rafters (20 psf snow load, 10 psf dead load, #2 SPF, 16" on center)

| Rafter Size | Maximum Span |
|------------|-------------|
| 2x6 | 10 ft 6 in |
| 2x8 | 14 ft 0 in |
| 2x10 | 17 ft 6 in |
| 2x12 | 21 ft 0 in |

For heavy snow areas (40+ psf snow load), reduce spans by 20–25% or increase lumber size.

### Beams (Header/Girder Supporting Floor Joists)

Beams carry concentrated loads from multiple joists. They need to be significantly larger. A doubled 2x10 (two 2x10s nailed together) can typically span about 8 feet supporting a standard residential floor load. A doubled 2x12 can span about 10 feet. For longer spans, use engineered lumber (LVL beams), steel, or add intermediate posts.

## Foundation Basics

Every structure needs to transfer its weight to the ground without sinking, shifting, or washing away.

### Pier Foundation (Simplest)

Individual concrete piers or treated wood posts set into the ground. The structure sits on top. Used for cabins, small buildings, porches, and elevated structures.

1. Dig holes to below frost line (18–48+ inches depending on region). If the foundation doesn't go below frost line, frost heave will lift and crack the structure.
2. Fill with concrete or set pressure-treated 6x6 posts directly in the ground (packed with gravel for drainage).
3. Space piers every 6–8 feet along the beam line.
4. Level the tops — all piers must be at the same elevation. Use a long straight board and a level, or a water level (a clear tube filled with water — water finds its own level).
5. Set beams on top of piers. Bolt or anchor to the piers.

### Continuous Foundation (More Permanent)

A concrete or stone wall around the perimeter of the building, extending below frost line. More material and labor than piers but distributes loads better and resists lateral soil pressure if the building has a basement.

For a concrete block foundation:
1. Dig a trench below frost line, at least 16 inches wide.
2. Pour a concrete footer in the trench — 8 inches thick, at least twice the width of the wall above.
3. Lay concrete blocks on the footer, mortared, to ground level.
4. Waterproof the exterior with tar or membrane before backfilling.

### Gravel Pad

For temporary or semi-permanent structures (sheds, small buildings, greenhouses): level the ground, lay landscape fabric, and spread 4–6 inches of compacted gravel. Not a true foundation but prevents water pooling under the structure and provides a stable base.

## Wall Framing

Standard wall framing is straightforward:

1. **Bottom plate:** A treated 2x4 laid flat on the foundation. Bolt to concrete or attach to subfloor.
2. **Studs:** Vertical 2x4s set 16 inches on center (measured from center to center). 16 inches allows standard 4x8 sheet goods (plywood, OSB) to land on stud centers.
3. **Top plate:** A 2x4 laid flat across the top of the studs. Nail through the top plate into each stud.
4. **Double top plate:** A second 2x4 on top of the first, with joints offset by at least 4 feet. This ties the wall together and provides a nailing surface for the roof system above.
5. **Headers:** Over windows and doors, the wall needs a beam (header) to carry the load from above across the opening. Standard: doubled 2x8 for openings up to 4 feet, doubled 2x10 for up to 6 feet, doubled 2x12 for up to 8 feet.

### Bracing

A rectangular frame has no rigidity — it racks (leans) under lateral force. Bracing prevents this.

- **Plywood sheathing:** The simplest and strongest bracing. A sheet of plywood or OSB nailed to the studs turns the wall into a rigid panel (shear wall). Nail with 8d nails every 6 inches along the edges and 12 inches in the field.
- **Diagonal bracing:** If you don't have sheet goods, run a diagonal board from the bottom corner to the opposite top corner. This triangulates the frame.
- **Metal strap bracing:** Thin metal straps nailed diagonally across studs. Faster than diagonal lumber but not as strong.

## Roof Framing

### Simple Gable Roof

1. **Ridge board:** A board (2x8 or larger) at the peak running the length of the building.
2. **Rafters:** Angled members running from the ridge to the wall top plates. Cut a "birdsmouth" notch (a triangular cut) where the rafter sits on the top plate — this locks it in place.
3. **Collar ties:** Horizontal members connecting opposing rafters at mid-height. Prevents the rafters from spreading outward (which pushes the walls apart).
4. **Rafter ties or ceiling joists:** Horizontal members at the bottom of the rafters connecting opposing walls. Essential for preventing wall spreading under roof load.

### Roof Pitch

Steeper roofs shed snow and rain better but use more material and are harder to build. A 4:12 pitch (4 inches of rise per 12 inches of run) is a good moderate pitch for most climates. In heavy snow areas, 6:12 or steeper sheds snow before dangerous weight accumulates.

## Common Structural Mistakes

1. **Undersized beams.** When in doubt, go bigger. Doubling a beam costs a few extra dollars/boards but the peace of mind is priceless.
2. **Point loads without support below.** If a beam carries weight down to a post, that post needs to sit on something solid — a pier, a footing, a foundation wall. A post sitting on unsupported floor sheathing will punch through eventually.
3. **Notching or drilling joists incorrectly.** You can drill holes in the middle third of a joist (the neutral axis) for wiring and plumbing. You cannot notch the bottom edge or drill near the bottom — that's where the tension forces are, and cutting there dramatically weakens the joist.
4. **No lateral bracing.** Tall walls without bracing rack. Long beams without lateral support buckle sideways. Brace everything.
5. **Ignoring the foundation.** The fanciest framing means nothing if it's sitting on dirt that washes out, heaves in frost, or compresses under load.
6. **Green (wet) lumber.** Fresh-cut lumber shrinks as it dries — joints loosen, nails pop, framing warps. Use dried lumber when possible. If you must use green lumber, expect movement and plan for it (use bolts you can retighten rather than nails).

> **The cardinal rule:** If you're not sure whether a structural member is strong enough, make it bigger. Overbuilding costs you extra lumber. Underbuilding costs you the structure — or worse. When lives depend on it, the extra material is always worth it.
