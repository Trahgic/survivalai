A bicycle is the most efficient human-powered transportation ever invented. Four times faster than walking, carries cargo, no fuel needed, and most repairs require only basic tools. In a situation without fuel, a working bicycle is worth its weight in gold.

## Flat Tire Repair

The most common bike breakdown. You'll deal with this regularly if you ride on rough terrain.

### What You Need

- Tire levers (or a flat-head screwdriver, spoon handle — anything thin and strong)
- Patch kit (patches, glue, sandpaper) or a spare tube
- Pump (hand pump, floor pump, or CO2 inflator)

### Step by Step

1. Remove the wheel. For the rear wheel, shift the chain to the smallest gear first — this makes removal and reinstallation easier. Open the quick-release lever or loosen the axle nuts.
2. Deflate the tire completely if it isn't already.
3. Insert a tire lever between the tire bead and the rim, about 2 inches from the valve. Pry the bead over the rim edge and hook the lever to a spoke.
4. Insert a second lever a few inches away and slide it around the rim to unseat one side of the tire.
5. Pull the tube out. Leave the tire on the rim (one bead is still seated).
6. Inflate the tube and listen/feel for the leak. If you can't find it, submerge in water and look for bubbles.
7. **Patching:** Rough up the area around the hole with sandpaper (about a nickel-sized circle). Apply a thin, even layer of rubber cement and let it dry until tacky (2–3 minutes — this is important, don't skip the drying). Press the patch firmly over the hole, working from the center out. Hold pressure for a minute.
8. Before reinstalling, run your fingers along the inside of the tire to feel for the object that caused the flat (thorn, wire, glass). Remove it. Also check the rim tape — if a spoke end is poking through, it'll puncture the new tube immediately.
9. Slightly inflate the tube so it holds shape. Tuck it into the tire starting at the valve. Push the valve through the rim hole.
10. Work the tire bead back onto the rim by hand. Start opposite the valve and work toward it. Avoid tire levers for reinstalling — they can pinch the tube and cause another flat.
11. Before full inflation, push the valve up into the tire and pull it back to make sure the tube isn't pinched under the bead. Spin the wheel and check that the tire is seated evenly.
12. Inflate to the pressure listed on the tire sidewall.

### Improvised Patches

No patch kit? In a pinch:
- A piece of rubber from another tube, glued with contact cement, super glue, or even rubber cement
- Duct tape on the inside of the tire over the hole (inside the tire, not on the tube — this is a boot, not a true patch, but can get you home)
- If the tube is shredded and no repair is possible: stuff the tire with grass, rags, or plastic bags and ride slowly. It's rough but it works for short distances.

## Chain Repair

A broken chain stops the bike completely.

### Temporary Fix (No Tools)

If a link breaks or pin falls out, remove the damaged links and rejoin the chain shorter. It'll work with fewer gears available. You need:

- A chain tool (a small device that pushes chain pins in and out). Carry one — they're the size of your thumb.
- Without a chain tool: a nail or small bolt the right diameter can substitute. Place the chain on a hard surface with the pin hole over a small gap (a hole in a piece of metal, or the gap between two flat surfaces). Drive the old pin out with the nail and hammer. Drive a new pin (or the same pin, carefully) back through the aligned links.

### Chain Replacement

Chains stretch over time. A stretched chain wears out the gears (which are much more expensive). Replace the chain when it measures more than 12-1/8 inches over 12 links (12 inches is the nominal length — any more than 1/8 inch of stretch means it's worn).

New chains often come long and need to be sized. Route through the derailleurs, pull both ends to the largest gear combination, and cut the chain where two ends meet with one link of overlap. This gives correct tension.

## Brake Adjustment

Brakes that don't stop the bike will get you hurt.

### Rim Brakes (Caliper or V-Brakes)

1. **Pad alignment:** The brake pad should hit the rim flat and square — not the tire (blows the tire) and not below the rim (does nothing). Loosen the pad bolt, position by hand, retighten.
2. **Cable tension:** If you have to pull the lever to the handlebar before brakes engage, the cable is too loose. Find the barrel adjuster (a knob where the cable enters the brake lever or caliper). Turn it counterclockwise to increase tension. If the barrel adjuster is maxed out, loosen the cable anchor bolt on the caliper, pull the cable tighter with pliers, and retighten.
3. **Pad wear:** Brake pads have wear indicator lines or grooves. If the grooves are gone, the pad is done. Replace before it's metal-on-metal.

### Disc Brakes

1. **Rubbing:** If the rotor rubs the pads, loosen the caliper mounting bolts slightly, squeeze the brake lever to center the caliper on the rotor, and retighten the bolts while holding the lever.
2. **Pad wear:** Remove the wheel, look into the caliper. If pad material is less than 1mm thick, replace. Pads push out with a flathead screwdriver or tire lever to insert new ones.
3. **Spongy lever (hydraulic):** Air in the brake line. Needs bleeding — this requires specific brake fluid (DOT 4 or mineral oil depending on brand) and a bleed kit. If you don't have these, tighten the lever reach adjustment to compensate temporarily.

## Wheel Truing

A wheel that wobbles side-to-side has one or more loose or broken spokes.

1. Flip the bike upside down or put it in a stand. Spin the wheel slowly and watch the gap between the rim and the brake pads (or hold a marker close to the rim as a reference).
2. Find where the rim wobbles toward one side.
3. **Tighten** spokes on the opposite side of the wobble (pulling the rim back toward center). **Loosen** spokes on the same side as the wobble. Use a spoke wrench — a small wrench that fits the spoke nipples at the rim.
4. Turn each spoke nipple only 1/4 turn at a time. Over-tightening one spoke puts stress on the rim and can crack it.
5. Keep spinning and adjusting until the wobble is acceptable.

**Broken spoke:** If a spoke snaps, tape it to an adjacent spoke so it doesn't flap around and catch in things. The wheel will wobble — true it as well as you can with the remaining spokes. Replace the broken spoke when you have a replacement and can remove the tire.

## Derailleur Adjustment

### Rear Derailleur (Most Common Issue)

If gears skip, click, or won't shift to certain gears:

1. **Cable tension:** The most common fix. Shift to the smallest rear cog. Turn the barrel adjuster at the rear derailleur (or at the shifter) counterclockwise 1/2 turn at a time. Pedal and shift through the gears. Keep adjusting until shifts are clean and the chain doesn't hesitate on any gear.
2. **Limit screws:** Two small screws on the derailleur body marked H (high) and L (low). These prevent the chain from falling off the gears.
   - H screw: Adjust while in the smallest cog. The upper derailleur pulley should align directly under the smallest cog.
   - L screw: Adjust while in the largest cog. The pulley should align directly under the largest cog.
3. **B-tension screw:** Adjusts the gap between the upper pulley and the cassette. If the derailleur is grinding against the large cogs, turn this screw clockwise to increase the gap.

### Front Derailleur

Less common. Adjust the same way — cable tension first, then limit screws to prevent the chain from dropping off the inside or outside.

## Bearing Overhaul

Bearings are in the hubs, bottom bracket (where the pedal cranks attach), and headset (where the fork turns). Grinding, clicking, or play (wobble) in any of these means the bearings need service.

1. Disassemble the bearing assembly.
2. Clean all parts with solvent (degreaser, gasoline, or kerosene).
3. Inspect ball bearings for pitting, flat spots, or discoloration. Replace if damaged.
4. Inspect bearing races (the cups and cones the balls ride in) for pitting. Light pitting is okay. Deep grooves mean replacement.
5. Pack with fresh grease — fill the race about 3/4 full.
6. Reassemble and adjust: tighten until there's no play (wobble), then back off just until the assembly spins freely. This is "cone adjustment" and takes patience.

## Improvised Parts

When you can't get replacement parts:

- **Brake cable:** Braided fishing line or thin steel wire (picture hanging wire) can substitute in an emergency. Thread through the cable housing and secure at both ends.
- **Tire boot:** A piece of a dollar bill, duct tape, or a section cut from an old tire placed inside the tire over a gash to prevent the tube from bulging through.
- **Chain link:** A piece of wire through the pin holes can hold a chain together temporarily. Ride gently.
- **Spoke:** A piece of straight wire the same gauge, threaded through the hub flange and secured at the rim with a spoke nipple or wrapped tight.
- **Handlebar tape/grips:** Strips of cloth, leather, or duct tape wrapped around the bars.

> **Maintenance schedule:** Clean and lube the chain every 100–200 miles (or whenever it's visibly dirty or squeaking). Check tire pressure before every ride. Check brake pad wear monthly. Full bearing overhaul annually or every 2,000 miles. A well-maintained bicycle lasts decades.
