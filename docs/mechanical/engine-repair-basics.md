Small engines power generators, chainsaws, water pumps, tillers, and dozens of other critical tools. They're simple compared to car engines, and most problems come down to three things: fuel, spark, and air. If the engine has clean fuel getting to the combustion chamber, a spark at the right time, and air flowing through, it runs. If any one of those three fails, it doesn't.

## Two-Stroke vs. Four-Stroke

Know which you're working on — they're maintained differently.

**Four-stroke:** Has a separate oil reservoir. You add oil through a fill cap and check it with a dipstick. Runs on straight gasoline. Most generators, lawn mowers, and water pumps are four-stroke. Quieter, more fuel-efficient, heavier.

**Two-stroke:** No separate oil system. Oil is mixed directly with the gasoline (usually 40:1 or 50:1 ratio — check the manual or markings on the engine). Most chainsaws, string trimmers, and older small equipment are two-stroke. Lighter, simpler, louder.

> **WARNING:** Running a two-stroke on straight gasoline (no oil mixed in) will seize the engine within minutes. The oil in the fuel mix is the only lubrication the internal parts get. Always mix fuel correctly.

## Engine Won't Start — Troubleshooting

Work through this in order. It's almost always one of these.

### 1. Check the Fuel

- Is there gas in the tank? Sounds obvious, but check.
- Is the fuel fresh? Gasoline goes bad in 30–90 days. Old gas turns into varnish that clogs jets and passages. If the gas smells like paint thinner instead of gasoline, dump it and use fresh fuel.
- Is the fuel valve open? Many engines have a shutoff valve between the tank and carburetor. Make sure it's in the ON position.
- Is fuel reaching the carburetor? Disconnect the fuel line at the carburetor and see if gas flows out when the valve is open. If not: clogged fuel line, clogged fuel filter, or stuck valve.

### 2. Check the Spark

- Pull the spark plug. Is it wet with fuel? If so, the engine is getting gas but not firing — the problem is electrical. If it's dry, fuel isn't reaching the cylinder.
- Check the plug's condition. Black, sooty, oily, or fouled plugs don't fire reliably. Clean with a wire brush or replace. Correct gap: usually 0.030 inches (check the manual). Set with a feeler gauge or the blade of a knife — the plug gap should just catch a standard pocketknife blade.
- Test for spark: put the plug back in the plug wire, hold the threaded part of the plug against the engine's metal housing (grounding it), and pull the starter rope. You should see a blue spark jump across the gap. No spark = ignition problem (bad coil, bad kill switch wiring, or sheared flywheel key).

### 3. Check the Air

- Is the air filter clean? A clogged air filter chokes the engine. Remove it and try starting without it (just for testing — don't run without a filter, it'll suck in debris). If it starts without the filter, clean or replace the filter.
- Foam filters: wash in warm soapy water, squeeze dry, re-oil lightly with engine oil.
- Paper filters: tap loose debris off, blow out with compressed air if available, replace if heavily soiled.

### 4. Check Compression

Pull the starter rope slowly. You should feel definite resistance as the piston compresses the fuel-air mixture. If the rope pulls easily with little resistance, the engine has low compression — usually a blown head gasket, stuck valve, or worn piston rings. These are more involved repairs.

## Carburetor Cleaning

The carburetor mixes fuel with air in the right ratio. Stale fuel gums up the tiny passages inside, and this is the single most common reason small engines don't start after sitting.

### Quick Clean (Engine Still Assembled)

1. Locate the carburetor — it's the metal piece between the air filter and the engine block, with the fuel line connected to it.
2. Remove the air filter.
3. Spray carburetor cleaner (or brake cleaner) into the carburetor throat while someone slowly pulls the starter rope. The solvent dissolves varnish in the main passages.
4. If there's a drain screw on the bottom of the carburetor bowl, open it and let old fuel drain out. Close it, add fresh fuel, try starting.

### Full Carburetor Removal and Cleaning

If the quick clean doesn't work:

1. Turn off the fuel valve. Disconnect the fuel line from the carburetor.
2. Remove the air filter and its housing.
3. Disconnect the throttle linkage and any springs (take photos before disconnecting — linkage routing is easy to forget).
4. Unbolt the carburetor from the engine (usually 2 bolts or nuts).
5. Remove the float bowl (the cup on the bottom — usually held by one bolt or screw in the center). Dump the old fuel.
6. The float is a small plastic or brass piece that controls fuel level. It pivots on a pin — don't bend it.
7. The main jet is a brass fitting in the center post inside the bowl. Unscrew it. Hold it up to light — you should see clearly through the tiny hole. If it's blocked, poke it clear with a strand of wire from a wire brush (not a drill bit — don't enlarge the hole). Blow through it.
8. Spray carburetor cleaner through every hole, passage, and orifice you can find. Let it soak 10 minutes, then blow out with compressed air or let dry.
9. Reassemble in reverse. Use a new gasket between the carburetor and engine if available — old gaskets cause air leaks that make the engine run lean.

## Spark Plug Maintenance

Spark plugs are cheap and easy to replace. If in doubt, just put in a new one.

- **Correct plug:** Check the manual or the old plug's markings. Common small engine plugs: Champion RJ19LM, NGK BR2LM, Bosch WR11E0. They're not all interchangeable — wrong reach or heat range causes problems.
- **Gap:** Set to specification with a wire-type gap gauge. Most small engines: 0.030 inches. Too wide = weak spark. Too narrow = runs but misfires under load.
- **Don't overtighten.** Snug plus 1/4 turn with a plug wrench. Stripped threads in an aluminum cylinder head are a nightmare.

## Oil Changes (Four-Stroke Only)

Change oil every 25–50 hours of operation, or at least once per season. Small engines work hard and oil degrades fast.

1. Run the engine for a few minutes to warm the oil (warm oil drains faster and carries out more contaminants).
2. Remove the drain plug (bottom of the engine) and drain into a container. Some small engines don't have a drain plug — you tip the engine and pour out through the fill hole.
3. Replace drain plug.
4. Fill with the correct type and amount. Most small engines use SAE 30 or 10W-30. Check the dipstick — don't overfill. Overfilling causes smoking, hard starting, and can blow seals.

## Fuel System Tips

**Fuel stabilizer:** If you're storing an engine for more than 30 days, add fuel stabilizer to the gas (follow label ratios). Run the engine for a few minutes to circulate the treated fuel through the carburetor. This prevents varnish formation.

**Draining for storage:** Even better than stabilizer: run the engine until it dies from fuel starvation, then drain the carburetor bowl. Starts clean next season.

**Ethanol problems:** Modern pump gas contains 10% ethanol, which attracts water and degrades rubber fuel lines and gaskets. If possible, use ethanol-free gasoline for small engines. If not available, don't store ethanol-blended fuel for more than 30 days.

**Fuel line replacement:** Rubber fuel lines crack and harden over time. Replace with the correct inside-diameter fuel line — measure the old one. Push onto fittings and secure with small hose clamps.

## Ignition System

Most small engines use a magneto ignition — a magnet on the flywheel passes a coil on the engine housing, generating the spark. No battery needed.

**No spark troubleshooting:**

1. Check the kill switch wire. Disconnect it from the ignition coil. If the engine starts now, the kill switch or its wiring is shorting to ground, killing the spark.
2. Check the coil gap. The ignition coil should sit 0.010–0.014 inches from the flywheel magnets. Set it with a business card — loosen the coil mounting screws, slide the card between the coil and flywheel, tighten the screws, then rotate the flywheel to pull the card out.
3. Check the flywheel key. A small metal key aligns the flywheel on the crankshaft so the spark fires at the right moment. If the engine backfired or hit something hard, the key can shear — the spark will fire at the wrong time and the engine won't start or runs terribly. Remove the flywheel (requires a puller tool or sometimes a bolt trick), inspect and replace the key. These are soft metal and shear by design to protect the crankshaft.

## Governor and Speed Control

Small engines have a governor — a mechanical system that keeps the engine running at a consistent speed under varying loads. If the engine surges (speeds up and slows down rhythmically), the governor linkage may be sticking or the carburetor needs cleaning. Don't adjust the governor spring or linkage unless you know what you're doing — incorrect settings can cause the engine to over-speed and throw parts.

## Common Problems and Fixes

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Won't start at all | Old fuel, clogged carb, no spark, dead plug | Work through the troubleshooting steps above |
| Starts then dies | Clogged carburetor main jet, fuel flow restriction | Clean carb, check fuel line and filter |
| Runs rough, surges | Dirty carb, air leak, dirty air filter, governor issue | Clean carb, check gaskets, clean filter |
| Smokes white/blue | Oil burning — overfilled, bad rings, tilted wrong | Check oil level, check for blown head gasket |
| Smokes black | Running rich — choke stuck on, clogged air filter | Open choke, clean air filter |
| Overheating | Low oil, dirty cooling fins, overloaded | Check oil, clean debris from cylinder fins and shroud |
| Hard to pull starter | Hydro-lock (gas in cylinder), seized engine | Remove spark plug, pull rope to clear gas. If seized: low oil damage |
| Leaking gas | Bad fuel line, loose bowl, cracked primer bulb | Replace cracked parts, tighten connections |

> **Realistic advice:** Keep a small engine toolkit with your critical equipment: spare spark plug, plug wrench, screwdriver, pliers, a can of carb cleaner, fuel line, and a quart of oil. 90% of field repairs use these items. The ability to keep a generator or chainsaw running when there's no repair shop is worth more than the tools themselves.
