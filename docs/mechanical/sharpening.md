A dull tool is a dangerous tool — it slips instead of cutting, requires more force, and tires you out faster. A sharp knife does more work with less effort and fewer accidents. Sharpening isn't a mystery — it's pushing metal against an abrasive at the right angle until the edge is thin and even.

## Sharpening Concepts

Every cutting edge is a wedge. You're making two flat surfaces meet at a thin line. The angle of that wedge determines what the tool is good for:

| Tool | Edge Angle (Total) | Why |
|------|-------------------|-----|
| Razor, scalpel | 15–20° | Maximum sharpness, very fragile |
| Kitchen knife, fillet knife | 20–25° | Sharp, moderate durability |
| General-purpose knife, hunting knife | 25–30° | Good balance of sharpness and toughness |
| Axe, hatchet | 30–40° | Tough enough to hit wood without chipping |
| Splitting maul | 40–50° | Meant to wedge, not slice |

When sharpening, you're removing metal from one or both sides until the edge meets at the correct angle. That's it.

### The Burr

As you sharpen one side, a thin strip of metal folds over the edge to the other side — this is the burr (or wire edge). It means you've sharpened all the way to the edge. You can feel it by lightly dragging your thumb across the edge (perpendicular to the blade, not along it — don't slice your thumb). Once you've raised a burr on one side, flip the tool and sharpen the other side until you raise a burr there. Then remove the burr by alternating light strokes or stropping.

## Sharpening Stones (Whetstones)

Whetstones come in different grits. Lower number = coarser = removes metal faster but leaves a rougher edge. Higher number = finer = slower cutting but smoother edge.

### Which Grit When

- **Coarse (120–400 grit):** Repairing damage, reprofiling an edge, fixing chips. Used rarely — only when the edge is damaged or the angle needs changing.
- **Medium (600–1000 grit):** Regular sharpening of dull edges. This is your workhorse stone.
- **Fine (2000–6000 grit):** Refining and polishing the edge. Makes a noticeably sharper finish.

For a working knife in a survival context, a medium stone is all you need. Fine stones are nice but not essential.

### Types of Stones

**Natural stones** (Arkansas, Japanese waterstones): Excellent but expensive and fragile.

**Synthetic stones** (aluminum oxide, silicon carbide): Cheap, consistent, widely available. Work just as well as naturals for practical purposes.

**Diamond plates:** Metal plates coated with industrial diamond. Cut fast, never dish (wear unevenly), work on any steel including very hard tool steels. Pricier but last many years.

### Using a Whetstone

1. **Setup:** Place the stone on a stable surface. If it slides, put a damp cloth underneath. Use water or honing oil on the surface (oil stones use oil, water stones use water — using the wrong one doesn't ruin the stone but works poorly). Diamond plates can be used dry or with water.

2. **Find the angle:** Hold the blade against the stone at the desired angle. For a 25° total angle, you'll sharpen each side at about 12–13°. Here's a trick: lay the blade flat on the stone (0°), then raise the spine until a coin's thickness would fit between the stone and the bevel. That's roughly 15°. Raise a bit more for 20°. Practice holding this angle consistently.

3. **Stroke:** Push the blade across the stone edge-first (as if you're trying to slice a thin layer off the stone). Maintain consistent angle and pressure. Cover the entire length of the edge in each stroke — start at the heel and draw to the tip. Use the full length of the stone.

4. **Pressure:** Moderate on the coarse stone, lighter as you move to finer stones. Too much pressure on a fine stone just slows you down and can round the edge.

5. **Count strokes:** Do an equal number on each side. 10–20 strokes per side on a medium stone is usually enough for routine sharpening. Check for a burr. Once you've raised and removed it on both sides, test the edge.

6. **Testing sharpness:** 
   - Paper test: try to slice through printer paper. A sharp knife cuts cleanly. A dull one tears.
   - Thumbnail test: gently drag the edge across your thumbnail (lightly — don't push). A sharp edge catches and grabs. A dull edge slides.
   - Shaving test: try to shave arm hair. If it catches and cuts hairs, it's sharp.

## Stropping

Stropping removes the last traces of the burr and polishes the edge. It's what takes a knife from "sharp" to "razor sharp."

Use a leather strop (a strip of leather glued to a flat piece of wood, or just a piece of belt). Drag the blade across the leather spine-first (opposite direction from sharpening — if you go edge-first you'll slice the leather). Alternate sides, 10–20 strokes each. Light pressure.

Stropping compound (chromium oxide, green paste) on the leather cuts faster and produces a finer edge. But plain leather works fine.

## Sharpening Without a Stone

When you don't have proper sharpening equipment:

**Bottom of a ceramic mug:** The unglazed ring on the bottom of most ceramic mugs is hard enough to sharpen a knife. Use the same technique as a whetstone — hold the angle, draw the blade across edge-first. It's surprisingly effective.

**Flat rock:** Sandstone, slate, and river stones with fine grain work as natural whetstones. Find a flat-ish stone with a smooth surface. Wet it and use like a whetstone. Look for fine-grained sedimentary rocks — coarse granite tears up the edge.

**Car window edge:** Roll the window down partway. The top edge of car window glass is an excellent fine sharpener. Draw the blade across the edge like a strop.

**Concrete or brick:** Very coarse — only for badly damaged edges or axes. Use like a whetstone but expect a rough finish that needs refining.

**Sandpaper on a flat surface:** Tape sandpaper to a flat board, table, or glass sheet. Start with 400 grit, work up to 1000+. This actually works very well — it's essentially a flat homemade whetstone.

## Sharpening Axes and Hatchets

Axes are sharpened differently than knives because the edge is convex (curved outward rather than flat). This convex grind makes the edge stronger for chopping.

### With a File

1. Secure the axe head in a vise or wedge it into a stump. The edge should be accessible.
2. Use a 10-inch bastard file. File into the edge (push the file toward the edge, not away from it). Match the existing bevel angle — usually about 25–30° per side.
3. Use even, full-length strokes. Count your strokes and do the same number on each side.
4. Don't try to make the edge razor sharp — axes need some thickness behind the edge for strength. A properly sharp axe bites into wood cleanly and doesn't bounce off.
5. Remove the file burr with a few light passes of a whetstone or by stropping on a piece of leather.

### With a Puck

A sharpening puck (round, dual-grit stone) is designed for axes. Hold it flat against the bevel and make circular motions. Coarse side first, then fine. Wet the puck.

### Axe Edge Repair

If the edge has chips or a rolled section:

1. Use a coarse file to remove material back past the damage. This may require removing a lot of steel — be patient.
2. Reshape the bevel evenly on both sides.
3. Finish with the fine side of a puck or whetstone.

Badly damaged axes can also be ground on a belt sander or bench grinder, but go slowly and dip in water frequently to prevent overheating. Blue discoloration on the edge means you've drawn the temper — the steel has softened and won't hold an edge. If you see blue, you need to grind past it into unaffected steel.

## Sharpening Saws

Saws require specialized techniques because each tooth is a tiny cutter.

### Crosscut Saw (Cuts Across the Grain)

Teeth are filed at an angle (alternating left and right, called "set"). Each tooth acts like a small knife.

1. Secure the saw in a vise with the teeth just above the jaws.
2. Use a triangular (three-cornered) saw file sized to the tooth spacing.
3. File each tooth at the same angle as the existing bevel — usually about 60° across the face of the tooth.
4. File every other tooth from one side, then flip the saw and file the remaining teeth from the other side.
5. Use consistent pressure and the same number of strokes per tooth.

### Rip Saw (Cuts With the Grain)

Teeth are filed straight across (no alternating angle). Each tooth acts like a tiny chisel.

1. Same setup as crosscut.
2. File each tooth straight across, perpendicular to the blade.
3. Maintain the existing tooth shape and height.

### Setting Saw Teeth

"Set" is the slight outward bend of alternating teeth that makes the cut (kerf) wider than the blade, preventing binding. If a saw binds in the cut, the teeth have lost their set.

Use a saw set tool (a plier-like device that bends each tooth a precise amount). Bend every other tooth to one side, then flip and bend the remaining teeth to the other side.

Without a saw set tool: place the saw on a flat surface and use a center punch against each tooth to push it slightly outward. This is crude but works.

## Maintaining an Edge

A knife or tool that's sharpened regularly stays sharp with minimal effort. A neglected edge that's allowed to go dull requires heavy grinding to restore.

- **Strop** a knife every few uses — 10 strokes per side takes 20 seconds.
- **Touch up** on a fine stone every week or two of regular use.
- **Full sharpen** on medium and fine stones once a month with heavy use.
- **Repair** with a coarse stone only when there's visible damage.

> **The best edge is the one you maintain.** Don't wait until your knife won't cut. Regular light touch-ups keep a working edge indefinitely and never require you to remove much metal. A knife that's maintained well can last a lifetime. One that's only sharpened when it's dull gets ground away in a few years.
