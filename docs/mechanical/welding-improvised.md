Joining metal is one of the most valuable skills in a long-term survival situation. Broken tools, cracked brackets, split frames — metal fails, and without the ability to rejoin it, you're left with junk. You don't need a MIG welder and a 220V outlet. People joined metal for thousands of years with nothing but fire and a hammer.

## Methods Ranked by Difficulty

1. **Mechanical fasteners** (bolts, rivets, wire wrapping) — easiest, no heat needed
2. **Soldering** — low heat, soft metals and electrical work
3. **Brazing** — medium heat, strong joints on thin stock
4. **Forge welding** — high heat, no electricity, strongest traditional method
5. **Arc welding from batteries** — requires batteries and welding rod, produces real welds

## Mechanical Fastening

Before you heat anything, ask whether a bolt, rivet, or wrap would do the job.

**Bolting:** Drill holes through both pieces, run a bolt through, nut on the back. Strongest when pieces overlap. Use washers to prevent pull-through on thin stock. Lock nuts or a second nut prevent loosening from vibration.

**Riveting:** A rivet is a metal pin inserted through aligned holes in two pieces. The tail end is hammered flat, clamping the pieces together. Works with any soft metal (aluminum, copper, mild steel). You can make rivets from nail shanks — cut to length, leave enough tail to hammer into a head.

**Wire wrapping:** For emergency repair of handles, frames, and joints where a tight wrap of steel wire holds things together. Use mechanics wire or any steel wire. Wrap tightly, twist the ends with pliers. Not elegant, but it works.

## Soldering

Soldering joins metals using a filler metal (solder) that melts below 840°F (450°C). The base metals don't melt — only the solder does. It's the weakest metal joint but fine for electrical connections, tin work, copper pipe, and light-duty sheet metal.

### What You Need

- **Solder:** Lead-free solder (tin-silver or tin-copper) for water pipes and food contact. Lead-tin solder for electrical work (easier to use, flows better). Wire solder is most convenient.
- **Flux:** Cleans the metal surface so solder can bond. Rosin flux for electronics. Acid flux (zinc chloride) for sheet metal and plumbing. Use the right flux for the job — acid flux on electronics will corrode circuits.
- **Heat source:** Soldering iron for small work. Propane torch for plumbing and larger joints. A piece of copper bar heated in a fire works as an improvised soldering iron.

### Technique

1. Clean both surfaces bright with sandpaper, steel wool, or a file. Solder won't stick to dirty, oxidized, or painted metal.
2. Apply flux to both surfaces.
3. Heat the joint — not the solder. The workpiece needs to be hot enough to melt the solder on contact. If you melt solder with your iron and drip it on cold metal, it won't bond.
4. Touch solder to the heated joint. It should flow into the gap by capillary action, pulled by the heat.
5. Remove heat and let cool without moving the joint. A good solder joint is smooth and shiny.

## Brazing

Brazing uses a filler metal (brazing rod, usually brass or bronze) that melts above 840°F (450°C) but below the melting point of the base metals. It's much stronger than soldering — a properly brazed joint can be as strong as the base metal. Works on steel, cast iron, copper, brass, and dissimilar metals.

### What You Need

- **Brazing rod:** Bronze brazing rod is the most versatile. Available at welding supply and hardware stores. In a pinch, brass rod stock or even brass wire works.
- **Flux:** Brazing flux (usually borax-based paste). Household borax (20 Mule Team) works as emergency brazing flux — mix with water into a paste.
- **Heat:** Propane torch handles thin stock. MAPP gas or oxy-acetylene for heavier work. A forge or charcoal fire works for pieces you can put in the fire.

### Technique

1. Clean and fit the joint. Pieces should have close contact — brazing works by capillary action in tight gaps, not by filling big voids.
2. Apply flux paste to both surfaces and the brazing rod.
3. Heat the joint area evenly until the flux turns clear and glassy (this means you're approaching brazing temperature).
4. Touch the brazing rod to the joint — not to the flame. The rod should melt from contact with the hot metal and flow into the joint. If you have to hold the rod in the flame to melt it, the base metal isn't hot enough yet.
5. Let the filler flow through the entire joint. Add more rod as needed.
6. Cool slowly. Don't quench in water — the thermal shock can crack the joint.

### Applications

- Joining steel tubing (bicycle frames, tool handles, structures)
- Repairing cast iron (cast iron is nearly impossible to weld without special equipment, but brazes well)
- Joining dissimilar metals (steel to brass, copper to steel)
- Thin sheet metal where welding would burn through

## Forge Welding

The oldest form of welding — used for thousands of years by blacksmiths. Two pieces of steel are heated to near-melting temperature and hammered together. The atoms at the surfaces bond permanently. This produces a true metallurgical weld with no filler metal.

### Requirements

- A forge that can reach welding heat: approximately 2,300°F (1,260°C) for mild steel. The steel should be bright yellow to white-hot. See **tool-making.md** for forge construction.
- An anvil or heavy flat steel surface.
- A heavy hammer (2–3 lb cross peen or ball peen).
- Flux: borax is the traditional forge welding flux. It melts and flows over the steel at high temperature, coating the surface and preventing oxidation (scale formation). Without flux, the oxide layer prevents the weld from bonding.

### Technique

1. Shape the pieces to be joined so they have matching surfaces. For a basic splice (joining two bar ends), upset (thicken) both ends by heating and hammering them back on themselves so the weld area is slightly thicker than the stock.
2. Clean the surfaces — wire brush off any scale.
3. Heat both pieces in the forge until bright yellow to white-hot. The surface should look slightly "sweaty" or wet — this is the steel just beginning to flux.
4. Sprinkle borax on the hot surfaces. It'll bubble and melt into a glass-like coating.
5. Return to the forge and heat to full welding temperature. The flux should be liquid and the steel should be a bright lemon-yellow to white.
6. Working fast: remove both pieces, place one on the anvil, set the other on top, and strike firmly with the hammer. Start with light-medium blows in the center of the joint to tack the pieces, then work outward with heavier blows to close the joint. You have about 3–5 seconds before the steel cools too much.
7. If the weld didn't take fully, reheat to welding temperature and hammer again. Multiple heats are normal.
8. After welding, normalize the piece by heating to a medium red and letting it cool in still air. This relieves internal stress.

### What Can Go Wrong

- **Steel too cool:** The weld won't bond. You need full welding heat — anything below bright yellow won't work. Beginners consistently don't get the steel hot enough.
- **Scale in the joint:** If you skip flux, iron oxide forms on the surfaces and prevents bonding. Use borax.
- **Too slow:** The window for hammering is short. Have your anvil and hammer ready before pulling the steel from the fire. Don't walk across the shop with white-hot steel.
- **Wrong steel:** High-carbon steel and alloy steels have narrower welding temperature ranges and are harder to forge weld. Start with mild steel (A36 or equivalent).

## Arc Welding from Batteries

You can run a basic arc welder from car batteries wired in series. This produces real fusion welds — the base metal actually melts and joins. Quality depends heavily on skill, but even a crude arc weld is stronger than most alternatives.

### Setup

1. Wire 3–4 fully charged 12V car batteries in series (positive of battery 1 to negative of battery 2, and so on). This gives you 36–48 volts — enough to strike and maintain an arc with standard welding rod.
2. Connect the negative terminal of the first battery to the workpiece (ground clamp or bolt/clamp to the metal being welded).
3. Connect the positive terminal of the last battery to a welding rod holder (a pair of locking pliers or vise grips works — just insulate the handle with rubber or leather).
4. Insert a welding rod (E6011 or E6013, 3/32 inch diameter for thin stock, 1/8 inch for thicker work). E6011 runs on DC and handles dirty/rusty steel. E6013 is smoother but pickier about fit-up.

### Welding Technique

1. **Eye protection is non-negotiable.** Arc welding produces UV radiation that permanently damages eyes. Use a proper welding helmet (shade 10 minimum). If you don't have one, don't arc weld. Even brief exposure causes "arc eye" — painful corneal burns.
2. Wear long sleeves, leather gloves, and closed shoes. Spatter burns are hot enough to cause serious burns.
3. Strike the arc like striking a match — drag the rod tip across the metal. Once the arc starts, hold the rod about 1/8 inch above the surface.
4. Move slowly and steadily. The puddle of molten metal should be about twice the diameter of the rod.
5. Maintain a consistent arc length. Too far away: the arc sputters and the weld is porous. Too close: the rod sticks (break it free by twisting sharply).
6. When the rod is used down to 2 inches, stop, chip the slag off the weld with a hammer, wire brush, and insert a new rod.
7. The finished weld should have a consistent bead with no holes, cracks, or unfused edges.

> **Battery safety:** Car batteries produce hydrogen gas when charging and discharging. Weld in a ventilated area. Don't smoke near the batteries. A spark near a venting battery can cause an explosion. Wear eye protection even when not actively welding.

## Joint Types

For any joining method, the joint design matters as much as the technique:

**Butt joint:** Two pieces end-to-end. Weakest joint — the weld or braze only bonds at the contact faces. Fine for light-duty, poor for structural.

**Lap joint:** One piece overlaps the other. Much stronger than butt — more surface area for bonding. Used for sheet metal, straps, and patches.

**T-joint:** One piece meets another at a right angle. Weld or braze along the inside corner (fillet). Common for frames and brackets.

**Scarf joint:** Both ends cut at matching angles (usually 30–45 degrees) and joined along the angled face. Creates a larger bonding area than a butt joint. The standard joint for forge welding.

For structural work, always use lap or scarf joints when possible. Butt joints fail first.
