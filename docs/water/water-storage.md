Having a water source means nothing if you can't store what you collect. In any survival situation, water supply is inconsistent — rain comes and goes, streams can dry up, wells run slow. Storage bridges the gaps between supply and demand, and the difference between having a week of stored water versus a day can mean life or death.

## How Much to Store

**Minimum:** 1 gallon per person per day for drinking and basic cooking.

**Realistic target:** 3–5 gallons per person per day when you include washing, sanitation, and cooking. More in hot weather.

**Planning math:** For a group of 4 people with 2 weeks of buffer, you need 56–280 gallons depending on how much you ration. A good middle ground target is 3 gallons per person per day × number of people × days of buffer.

## Container Selection

### What Works

- **Food-grade HDPE plastic (recycling #2):** The standard. Blue 55-gallon drums, water jugs, and IBC totes. Look for "food grade" or "NSF" markings. HDPE doesn't leach chemicals into water.
- **Glass:** Inert and safe. Heavy and breakable, so impractical for large volumes, but excellent for smaller stores.
- **Stainless steel:** Excellent. Won't leach, won't break, lasts forever. Expensive.
- **Food-grade polyethylene tanks:** Purpose-built water tanks from 50 to 10,000+ gallons. Black tanks resist algae (no light penetration). These are the long-term solution.
- **Concrete cisterns:** Permanent installations. Very durable. Need to cure properly before use — fresh concrete leaches lime which raises pH.
- **Ceramic or clay pots:** Traditional and effective. Porous clay keeps water cool through evaporation. Common in tropical regions.

### What to Avoid

- **Containers that held chemicals, solvents, or non-food products.** Plastic absorbs chemicals that will leach into water for years. Even thorough cleaning doesn't make them safe.
- **Containers with BPA or non-food-grade plastics.** Especially in heat — BPA and phthalates leach more aggressively in warm conditions.
- **Galvanized metal containers** for long-term storage — zinc coating can leach at levels that affect taste and, over time, health.
- **Containers that can't be sealed.** Open water storage breeds mosquitoes, collects debris, and invites contamination.
- **Thin disposable water bottles.** Fine for short-term, but they degrade in sunlight, leak plasticizers over time, and are too flimsy for serious storage.

## Treatment for Long-Term Storage

Clean water stored in a clean, sealed container will stay drinkable for 6–12 months without treatment. Adding a small amount of disinfectant extends this indefinitely.

### Chlorine Treatment (Bleach)

Use plain, unscented household bleach (sodium hypochlorite, 5.25–8.25% concentration).

- **For treating stored water:** Add 1/8 teaspoon (8 drops) per gallon. This gives about 1–2 ppm chlorine — enough to prevent bacterial growth but low enough that it's safe to drink.
- **For treating questionable water before storage:** Add 1/4 teaspoon (16 drops) per gallon. Let stand 30 minutes before use.
- Mix well after adding bleach. The water should have a faint chlorine smell. If it doesn't, add another 1/8 teaspoon and wait 15 minutes. If it still doesn't smell like chlorine, the water has too much organic material — filter it first, then treat.

### Calcium Hypochlorite (Pool Shock)

Better for long-term preppers because the powder stores much longer than liquid bleach (bleach loses half its potency in 6–12 months, calcium hypochlorite powder lasts 10+ years sealed).

- Dissolve 1 heaping teaspoon of granular calcium hypochlorite (65–73% concentration) in 2 gallons of water to create a stock solution.
- Add 1 part stock solution to 100 parts water for treatment.
- Or roughly: a pinch (1/4 teaspoon) of dry powder per 50 gallons.
- Use only calcium hypochlorite with NO additives (no algaecides, no clarifiers, no fragrance).

## Rotation Schedule

If storing commercially bottled water or home-stored tap water:

- **Commercial bottled water:** Good for 1–2 years if stored in a cool, dark place. The water doesn't go bad — the plastic slowly degrades.
- **Home-stored treated water:** Rotate every 6–12 months. Drain, clean container, refill, re-treat.
- **Cisterns and large tanks:** Test water quality every 3–6 months. Re-treat with chlorine if levels have dropped. No need to drain and refill unless contamination is detected.

**Use and replace** is easier than scheduled rotation. Use your stored water for daily needs and refill regularly. First in, first out — always consume the oldest water.

## Storage Conditions

- **Cool:** Heat accelerates bacterial growth and chemical leaching from plastic. Ideal is 50–70°F (10–21°C). A basement or cellar is perfect.
- **Dark:** Light promotes algae growth and degrades plastic containers. Store in a dark room or use opaque containers.
- **Clean:** Store containers off the ground if possible (a wooden pallet works). This prevents moisture buildup underneath and keeps containers away from floor-level contaminants.
- **Sealed:** Every container needs a tight lid or cap. Screen any vents or overflow openings to keep insects out.
- **Stable:** Heavy water containers (a gallon weighs 8.3 pounds, a 55-gallon drum weighs 458 pounds full) need a solid surface. Don't stack full drums more than 2 high on a wooden floor.

> **WARNING:** Don't store water containers directly on concrete floors for extended periods. Concrete off-gasses chemicals that can migrate through some plastics. Place a board, pallet, or cardboard between the container and the concrete.

## Improvised Storage

When proper containers aren't available:

- **Lined pits:** Dig a hole, line with plastic sheeting (the thicker the better), fill with collected water. Cover with plastic and weight the edges. Not ideal — contamination risk is high and evaporation is a problem. But it stores volume.
- **Bathtubs:** A standard bathtub holds 40–60 gallons. Plug the drain, clean with bleach solution, fill, and cover with plastic. A WaterBOB (a bladder that fits inside a bathtub) is a purpose-built version of this if you prepared in advance.
- **Trash cans:** New, clean garbage cans with lids hold 30–44 gallons. Line with a clean trash bag for extra protection.
- **Any watertight vessel:** Pots, coolers, buckets, barrels. Clean thoroughly, treat the water, seal as best you can.

## Emergency Water Sources in Your Home

If disaster strikes before you've stored water:

- **Water heater:** 30–80 gallons. Turn off the power/gas and the water supply. Open a faucet upstairs to let air in. Drain from the valve at the bottom. Let sediment settle.
- **Pipes:** Turn off the main water supply. Open the highest faucet to let air in. Collect from the lowest faucet. You'll get a few gallons from the pipes themselves.
- **Toilet tanks:** 1.5–3 gallons per tank. Not the bowl — the tank. Safe if no chemical cleaners are in the tank.
- **Ice cubes:** Whatever is in your freezer.
- **Canned goods:** Liquid from canned vegetables, fruits, and beans is drinkable.

## Water Hauling and Transport

When your water source is distant from your camp or home:

- A single 5-gallon jerry can (the military style with a screw cap) weighs 42 pounds full. Two is a serious load for most people.
- A wheelbarrow or garden cart dramatically increases what one person can transport.
- Bicycle trailers can haul 100+ pounds of water.
- Gravity: if your water source is uphill from your camp, you can run a hose or pipe and let gravity do the work. Even 10 feet of elevation difference provides enough pressure for a slow but continuous flow.
- Designate clean containers for water only. Never use them for anything else. Label them.
