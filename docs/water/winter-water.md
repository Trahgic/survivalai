Cold weather water is deceptive. You feel less thirsty in the cold, but your body loses water at nearly the same rate as in warm weather — through breathing (you can see it as vapor), sweating under layers, and increased urination (cold causes your body to dump fluid). Winter dehydration is common, sneaky, and dangerous because people simply don't feel like drinking.

## Don't Eat Snow

This is the single most important winter water rule. Eating snow directly:

1. **Drops your core temperature.** Your body has to heat that snow from 32°F (0°C) or colder to 98.6°F (37°C). This costs significant metabolic energy — energy you can't afford in a survival situation. The caloric cost of melting enough snow to stay hydrated can actually accelerate hypothermia.
2. **Reduces your net water intake.** Snow is mostly air. A pot packed full of snow melts down to a fraction of the volume. You'd have to eat enormous amounts of snow to get meaningful hydration.
3. **Can cause mouth and throat injuries.** Very cold snow can damage the soft tissue in your mouth.

**Exception:** If you're on the verge of severe dehydration and have no way to melt snow, small amounts in your mouth (not gulping handfuls) are better than nothing. Let it melt on your tongue before swallowing. But this is a last resort.

## Melting Snow and Ice

### Fire Method (Best)

1. Start with a small amount of water in the pot if possible — even a few tablespoons. This prevents the pot from scorching and the snow from developing a burned taste.
2. Add snow gradually. Pack it lightly — don't compact it. Tightly packed snow insulates itself and melts unevenly.
3. Stir occasionally. Add more snow as it melts.
4. It takes roughly 10 liters of loose snow to produce 1 liter of water.
5. Bring to a rolling boil for purification — snow is not sterile, especially near populated areas or heavily used terrain.

### Body Heat Method

- Fill a water bottle or container with snow and tuck it inside your clothing, between layers. Your body heat melts it over several hours.
- Best placed against your torso (not directly against skin — put a layer between).
- Works for supplemental water when you can't make a fire, but it's too slow for primary hydration.
- Don't sleep with a snow-filled bottle against you — it draws heat all night and can contribute to hypothermia.

### Solar Method

- On sunny days, spread dark-colored cloth (black trash bags, dark jackets) in direct sunlight and pile snow on them.
- Dark material absorbs solar heat and melts snow faster than on light surfaces or bare ground.
- Channel the meltwater into a container.
- A shallow dark container in the sun with snow in it works too — a metal pot painted black is ideal.

## Ice vs. Snow

**Ice yields much more water per volume than snow** and requires less energy to melt.

- Old, blue or clear ice is best — it's denser and contains less air.
- New white ice is more air than water.
- Avoid sea ice unless it's old, blue, and has rounded edges — new sea ice is salty. Multi-year sea ice (identified by blue color, rounded shapes, and surface melting/refreezing patterns) has had most of its salt leached out and produces drinkable water.
- River and lake ice is usually freshwater but may be contaminated by whatever is in the water underneath. Purify after melting.

## Finding Liquid Water in Winter

Not all water freezes:

- **Springs:** Many springs run year-round because groundwater maintains a relatively constant temperature (50–55°F / 10–13°C). Look for unfrozen patches of ground, steam, or green vegetation in otherwise frozen landscape.
- **Fast-moving streams:** Moving water resists freezing. Even in extreme cold, rapids and waterfalls often remain at least partially open.
- **Under ice:** Break through lake or river ice to access liquid water below. Test ice thickness first — you need at least 4 inches of clear ice to safely stand on. Chop a hole with an axe, hatchet, or heavy rock.
- **Beaver lodges and dams:** Beaver ponds often have openings in the ice near the lodge. The water is typically cleaner than it looks.

## Preventing Water from Freezing

### Insulation

- Keep water bottles inside your clothing during the day. Inside a jacket pocket, against your body, they won't freeze.
- At night, put water bottles in your sleeping bag. Having a warm water bottle in your bag also provides heat.
- Insulate containers with socks, cloth, or stuff them inside a boot or pack.
- Wide-mouth bottles are better than narrow — narrow necks freeze first and block access.
- Insulated bottles (double-wall stainless steel like Nalgene or Hydro Flask) resist freezing for 8–12 hours even in extreme cold.

### Burial

- Bury water containers in the ground below the frost line if you're in a fixed camp. Even 12 inches of soil insulation makes a significant difference.
- In deep snow, the ground beneath may not be frozen — dig down to ground level and bury containers there.

### Heated Storage

- Keep a water container near (not on) a fire or stove overnight.
- A rock heated by the fire and placed next to your water supply radiates heat for hours.
- In a shelter with a heat source, keep all water stores as close to the heat as practical without creating a spill or fire hazard.

## Staying Hydrated in Cold Weather

- Drink at least 2–3 liters per day, more with exertion. Just because you don't feel thirsty doesn't mean you're hydrated.
- Warm liquids are better — they provide hydration plus warmth. Tea, broth, warm sugar water, heated water with a splash of anything for flavor.
- Avoid alcohol — it causes peripheral vasodilation (sends warm blood to the skin surface), which feels warm but accelerates core heat loss and increases dehydration through increased urination.
- Caffeine in moderate amounts is fine. The mild diuretic effect is offset by the fluid you're consuming with it.
- Monitor urine color. Same rules as warm weather — pale yellow means hydrated, dark yellow or brown means drink more.
- Your breath is visible water loss. In extreme cold at altitude, you can lose 1–2 liters per day just from breathing. You can't prevent this, but you need to account for it in your intake.

## Frozen Pipes and Plumbing

In an urban/suburban winter scenario where heating is lost:

1. **Prevention:** Leave faucets dripping slowly to keep water moving in pipes. Open cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls to let warm room air reach pipes. Insulate exposed pipes with towels, rags, or foam.
2. **If pipes freeze:** Apply gentle heat. Hair dryer, heat lamp, space heater aimed at the frozen section, or wrap with towels soaked in hot water. Start at the faucet end and work toward the frozen blockage so melting water has somewhere to go.
3. **Never use an open flame** on frozen pipes — fire hazard and can cause pipe joints to fail.
4. **If pipes burst:** Turn off the water main immediately. Collect any water that's still flowing. Repair with pipe clamps, rubber patches, or duct tape as a temporary fix until proper repair is possible.
