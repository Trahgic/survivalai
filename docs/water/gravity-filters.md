Gravity filters are the most practical long-term water treatment for off-grid living. No power, no pumping, no consumable chemicals — just pour water in the top and clean water comes out the bottom. A well-built biosand filter can last for years and process hundreds of gallons per day.

## How Gravity Filtration Works

Water passes through layers of material that physically trap particles and, in biological filters, use a living microbial layer to consume pathogens. Gravity provides the pressure — no electricity or hand pumping needed.

The key principle: slower is better. The longer water is in contact with the filter media, the more contaminants are removed. Fast flow means poor filtration.

## Biosand Filter (BSF)

The gold standard of improvised water treatment. Used by millions of people worldwide. Properly built, it removes 90–99% of bacteria, 99%+ of protozoa (Giardia, Crypto), 70–80% of viruses, and most turbidity. Combining a BSF with chlorine treatment after filtration gives near-complete pathogen removal.

### How It Works

A concrete or plastic container holds layers of progressively finer sand and gravel. Dirty water is poured in the top. As it slowly drains through, three things happen:

1. **Mechanical filtration:** Particles get trapped between sand grains.
2. **Biological layer (schmutzdecke):** A living biofilm develops on the top sand surface after 2–3 weeks of regular use. This microbial community actively consumes bacteria and pathogens. This is why biosand filters improve with use — the biofilm needs time to establish.
3. **Adsorption:** Dissolved contaminants stick to sand grain surfaces.

### Building a Biosand Filter

**Container:** A concrete box (roughly 36 inches tall × 12 inches square), a tall 5-gallon bucket, a large PVC pipe (8–12 inch diameter), or a food-grade plastic drum. Needs a spout or drain hole near the bottom.

**Layer construction (bottom to top):**

1. **Outlet pipe:** Install a pipe or tube at the bottom that rises up the inside wall to about 2 inches above the top sand layer. This maintains a standing water level above the sand that keeps the biological layer alive. The outlet exits through the container wall near the bottom.

2. **Underdrain gravel (bottom 2 inches):** Coarse gravel, roughly 1/2–3/4 inch diameter. Supports the sand and prevents it from clogging the outlet.

3. **Separator gravel (next 2 inches):** Smaller gravel, roughly 1/4 inch diameter. Transition layer between coarse gravel and fine sand.

4. **Filter sand (next 18–20 inches):** Fine sand, ideally 0.15–0.35 mm grain size (about the texture of table salt or slightly coarser). This is the most important layer. Sand that's too coarse won't filter effectively. Sand that's too fine clogs and flows too slowly.

5. **Standing water (top 2 inches):** Maintained by the outlet pipe height. Protects the biofilm from drying out.

6. **Diffuser plate:** A plate, piece of sheet metal, or the bottom of a cut container placed above the sand with a few inches of clearance. When you pour water in, it hits the diffuser instead of disturbing the biofilm directly.

**Sand preparation:** Wash the sand before use. Put it in a bucket, add water, stir aggressively, pour off the dirty water. Repeat 5–10 times until the rinse water runs clear. This removes silt and clay that would clog the filter.

### Operation

1. Pour water into the top, onto the diffuser plate. Fill to about 4 inches above the sand level.
2. Water drains through by gravity. Flow rate should be about 0.4 liters per minute (roughly a slow stream). If it's faster, the sand may be too coarse.
3. Collect filtered water from the outlet.
4. Use the filter daily. The biofilm needs regular feeding (incoming pathogens and nutrients) to stay alive. If you stop using it for more than a week, the biofilm dies and takes 2–3 weeks to re-establish.
5. **Never let the sand dry out.** The standing water above the sand must always be present. If it dries, the biofilm dies.

### Maintenance

- When flow rate drops below useful levels, the surface is clogged. Remove the diffuser plate, gently scrape off the top 1/2 inch of sand, and discard it. Replace with clean sand. Flow rate will restore. The biofilm will re-establish in 1–2 weeks.
- Don't disturb the deeper sand layers. The biology and structure down there is doing the heavy lifting.
- Frequency: depends on how turbid your input water is. Pre-filtering through cloth before adding to the BSF dramatically reduces maintenance.

## Ceramic Filter

Ceramic pot filters (like the common Potters for Peace design) are simple and highly effective. A porous ceramic pot sits inside a larger container. Water seeps through the ceramic walls by gravity, leaving bacteria and protozoa behind.

### How to Use

1. Place the ceramic pot (or candle element) in the upper chamber of a two-bucket system.
2. Fill the upper chamber with untreated water.
3. Water slowly seeps through the ceramic into the lower collection bucket.
4. Flow rate is typically 1–3 liters per hour.
5. Collect clean water from a spigot on the lower bucket.

### Performance

- Removes 99.9%+ of bacteria and protozoa.
- Does NOT reliably remove viruses (pore size is too large). Combine with chlorine treatment for complete protection.
- Flow rate decreases as the filter clogs. Scrub the ceramic surface with a clean brush under running water to restore flow. Don't use soap.

### Improvised Ceramic Filter

If you have access to clay and a kiln (or large fire):
1. Mix fine clay with a combustible material (sawdust, rice hulls, coffee grounds) at roughly 50/50 by volume.
2. Shape into a pot or disk.
3. Fire at high temperature (900–1100°C). The combustible material burns away, leaving microscopic pores in the ceramic.
4. The result is a porous ceramic element that filters water by gravity.
5. Optionally, paint the inside with colloidal silver solution for antibacterial properties.

This is a significant project but produces a filter that lasts for years.

## Charcoal/Activated Carbon Filter

Charcoal adsorbs (binds to its surface) organic chemicals, chlorine, pesticides, and many dissolved contaminants that sand and ceramic filters miss. It also improves taste significantly.

### Making Charcoal

Charcoal is wood burned in a low-oxygen environment. See charcoal-making.md for detailed instructions. For water filtration:

1. Use hardwood charcoal (oak, maple, hickory). Avoid softwoods (pine, cedar) — they contain resins.
2. Crush the charcoal into pieces ranging from rice-grain to pea-sized. Too fine clogs, too coarse doesn't filter well.
3. Rinse the crushed charcoal with clean water to remove ash and dust.

**Activated carbon** is charcoal that's been treated to increase its surface area and adsorption capacity. Commercial activated carbon is steam-treated. Improvised activation: soak charcoal in a 25% calcium chloride solution (or lemon juice) for 24 hours, then rinse and bake at high heat. Not as effective as commercial activation but better than plain charcoal.

### Building a Charcoal Filter

**Simple bucket version:**

1. Drill or punch small holes in the bottom of a bucket.
2. Place a layer of clean cloth or screen over the holes.
3. Add 3–4 inches of fine gravel.
4. Add 6–8 inches of crushed charcoal.
5. Add 3–4 inches of clean sand on top.
6. Place a cloth on the very top as a pre-filter.
7. Set this bucket on top of a clean collection bucket.
8. Pour water through, collect from below.

**Performance:** Removes taste, odor, chlorine, many organic chemicals, and some heavy metals. Does NOT reliably kill bacteria or viruses — use in combination with boiling or chemical treatment.

**Replacement:** Charcoal loses its adsorption capacity over time as the pore surfaces fill up. Replace every 2–4 weeks with heavy use, or when you notice taste/odor coming through again.

## Multi-Stage Filter

The most effective improvised system combines multiple methods in sequence:

1. **Pre-filter:** Cloth or fine mesh to remove large debris and turbidity.
2. **Biosand filter:** Removes bacteria, protozoa, and remaining particles.
3. **Charcoal filter:** Removes chemicals, taste, and odor.
4. **Chemical treatment (chlorine) or boiling:** Kills remaining viruses and bacteria that made it through.

This four-stage approach produces water quality comparable to commercial treatment plants using nothing but sand, charcoal, and a few drops of bleach.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the wrong sand.** Beach sand is often too coarse and may contain salt. River sand is usually ideal. Crushed rock is too angular and packs too tight.
- **Disturbing the biofilm.** Pouring water directly onto the sand surface, moving the filter, or letting it dry out kills the biological layer.
- **Expecting instant results.** A new biosand filter needs 2–3 weeks for the biofilm to establish. During this period, it filters particles but has limited biological activity. Treat the output with chlorine during the break-in period.
- **Skipping pre-filtration.** Pouring muddy water directly into a fine filter clogs it fast. Always pre-filter through cloth for turbid water.
- **Relying on a single method.** No single improvised filter removes everything. Layer your defenses.
