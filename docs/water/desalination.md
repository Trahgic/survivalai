If you're near the ocean or have only brackish (slightly salty) water available, desalination is your path to drinkable water. The basic principle is simple: separate the water from the salt. In practice, this means either evaporating the water and collecting the condensation (distillation) or forcing it through a membrane that blocks salt (reverse osmosis). Without commercial equipment, distillation is your main option.

## Why You Can't Just Drink Seawater

Seawater is about 3.5% salt. Your kidneys can only produce urine that's less salty than seawater. To process the salt from 1 liter of seawater, your kidneys need about 1.5 liters of freshwater. You end up more dehydrated than before you drank it. Drinking seawater accelerates death from dehydration — it doesn't slow it.

Brackish water (slightly salty, like some well water or river mouths) is less dangerous but still causes diarrhea and increased dehydration at higher concentrations. If water tastes noticeably salty, it needs treatment.

## Solar Still — Coastal/Beach Version

The simplest desalination method. Uses sun heat to evaporate seawater and collect the freshwater condensation.

### Ground Pit Solar Still

1. Dig a pit 3 feet wide and 2 feet deep above the high tide line on a beach.
2. Place a clean container in the center of the pit.
3. Pour seawater into the pit around the container (not into it). You can also place wet seaweed, salt-soaked cloth, or damp sand around the container as moisture sources.
4. Cover the pit with clear plastic sheeting. Seal the edges tightly with sand or rocks.
5. Place a small stone on the plastic directly above the container to create an inverted cone.
6. Sunlight heats the enclosed air, evaporating the seawater. Pure water condenses on the underside of the plastic and drips into the container. The salt stays behind in the pit.
7. Production: 0.5–1.5 liters per day in good sun. Build multiple stills if possible.

**Tips for better output:**
- Black lining in the pit absorbs more heat (use a dark garbage bag beneath the seawater).
- Larger pits produce more water.
- Refill the seawater daily — once the pit dries out, production stops.
- Place a small tube from the container out through the sealed edge so you can suck water out without disturbing the still (optional but convenient).

### Above-Ground Solar Still

More efficient than a pit still and easier to maintain.

1. Build a shallow tray (baking sheet, shallow pan, cut-open soda cans lined with black plastic) and fill with seawater.
2. Place the tray in full sun, elevated slightly at one end.
3. Cover with a sheet of glass or clear plastic angled so condensation drips down the underside into a collection channel at the low end.
4. The glass/plastic should not touch the seawater surface — leave 2–4 inches of air gap.
5. Insulate the bottom and sides of the tray to retain heat (cardboard, cloth, foam).
6. Condensation runs down the angled cover into a clean container at the lower edge.
7. This design can produce 2–4 liters per day per square meter of collection surface in strong sun.

## Heat Distillation

Faster than solar stills but requires fuel. This is the most reliable field desalination method.

### Basic Setup

1. Fill a pot with seawater (don't fill more than 2/3 full — it needs room to boil without splashing into the collection system).
2. Bring to a boil. The steam is pure freshwater — the salt stays in the pot.
3. Capture the steam and condense it. This is the engineering challenge.

### Steam Collection Methods

**Lid drip method (simplest):**
1. Place an inverted lid (concave side up) on the pot.
2. Put cold water, ice, or a cold wet cloth on top of the inverted lid. This cools the lid and promotes condensation on its underside.
3. Steam hits the cold lid, condenses into droplets, and drips down to the center of the concave lid.
4. Tilt the lid periodically to pour collected freshwater into a container. Or rig a small cup hanging from the lid handle inside the pot (above water level) to catch drips continuously.
5. Production: slow but steady. Replace the cooling material on the lid frequently.

**Tube/hose method (better):**
1. Seal the pot with a lid that has a hole. Insert a length of metal or heat-resistant tubing (copper pipe is ideal, but any tubing that won't melt works).
2. Run the tube downhill from the pot into a collection container.
3. Cool the tube — coil it through a bucket of cold water, bury it in wet sand, or drape it with wet cloths. The steam condenses inside the cooled tube and drips out as freshwater.
4. This is essentially a homemade distillation apparatus. It works very well with copper tubing because copper conducts heat efficiently, promoting condensation.
5. Seal the connection between the tube and the lid with clay, dough, wet cloth, or whatever you have to prevent steam from escaping.

**Kettle method:**
If you have a kettle with a spout, just direct the steam from the spout through a length of tubing into a cold surface or container. A tight cloth wrapped around the spout and leading to a tube works for kettles without a good spout shape.

> **WARNING:** Don't let the pot boil dry. Concentrated salt deposits burn and can crack the pot. Refill with seawater before the level gets below 1/4 full. Also, the first collected water may be slightly brackish — taste-test it. If salty, your collection system is getting splash contamination from boiling water, not just steam. Reduce the boil to a gentle simmer.

## Brackish Water Treatment

Water that's slightly salty (brackish) rather than full seawater needs less aggressive treatment.

- **Dilution:** If you have some freshwater, mixing it with mildly brackish water can bring the salt level below the taste and health threshold. Not a real solution but buys time.
- **Solar still or distillation:** Same methods as seawater but produces results faster because there's less salt to separate from.
- **Beach well filtration:** Dig a well above the high tide line. Sand naturally filters seawater and each pass through sand reduces salt content somewhat. The water from a beach well is often brackish but much less salty than the ocean. Multiple filtration passes (pumping the water from one well into the sand near another) can reduce salt further.
- **Charcoal filtration:** Activated charcoal does NOT remove salt (salt molecules are too small for adsorption), but it removes organic contaminants that often accompany brackish water sources.

## What Desalination Doesn't Remove

Distillation removes almost everything — salt, minerals, bacteria, viruses, chemicals. The output is essentially pure water. This means:

- It may taste flat. Adding a tiny pinch of clean salt or mineral-rich substance improves the taste.
- Long-term use of exclusively distilled water without mineral supplementation can cause electrolyte deficiency. Add a small amount of salt and, if available, other mineral sources to your diet.
- Solar stills and slow distillation produce cleaner water than rapid boiling distillation, which can carry over some volatile chemicals with the steam.

## Production Expectations

Be realistic about how much freshwater you can produce:

| Method | Daily Output | Fuel/Resources Needed |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| Ground pit solar still | 0.5–1.5 L | Plastic sheet, sun |
| Above-ground solar still | 2–4 L per sq meter | Glass/plastic, tray, sun |
| Heat distillation (pot + lid) | 1–3 L | Fuel for fire |
| Heat distillation (tube condenser) | 3–8 L | Fuel, tubing, cooling water |

A single person needs a minimum of 2 liters per day. In hot coastal conditions, more like 3–4. Plan your desalination capacity accordingly — you may need multiple stills running simultaneously.
