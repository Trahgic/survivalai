You can't see most of what makes water dangerous. Crystal-clear stream water can carry Giardia. Murky puddle water might be safe after treatment. Testing water helps you decide what treatment it needs and whether a source is worth using at all.

Without a lab, your testing options are limited but still useful. The goal isn't laboratory precision — it's catching obvious problems before they make people sick.

## The Five Senses Check

Your first and fastest test. Not foolproof, but catches many problems.

### Visual

- **Clarity:** Hold a container of water up to light. Clean water should be clear or nearly clear. Cloudy, murky, or colored water has suspended particles (turbidity) that may include pathogens and that interfere with chemical and UV disinfection. Turbid water needs filtering before treatment.
- **Color:** Yellow, brown, or tea-colored water usually contains dissolved organic matter (tannins from decaying vegetation). Not necessarily dangerous, but should be treated. Green water has algae. Red or orange water may contain iron (usually harmless) or rust. Blue-green surface films can indicate toxic cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) — avoid this water.
- **Surface film:** An oily sheen on still water can mean petroleum contamination. If you stir the water and the sheen breaks into separate droplets, it's likely petroleum. If it breaks into fragments but stays together, it's natural organic film (harmless).
- **Particles:** Visible debris, sediment, insects, or worms. Filter before use.

### Smell

- **No smell:** Good sign. Most safe water is odorless.
- **Rotten egg smell:** Hydrogen sulfide. Common in stagnant water or certain groundwater. Often not dangerous in small amounts but indicates anaerobic (low-oxygen) conditions where harmful bacteria thrive. Aerate the water (pour back and forth between containers) and treat.
- **Chemical or gasoline smell:** Possible chemical contamination. Do not drink. Find another source.
- **Chlorine smell:** Someone treated this water, or it's from a municipal supply. Usually safe, possibly over-treated.
- **Musty or earthy smell:** Usually algae or decaying organic matter. Treatable.
- **Sweet or fruity smell:** Can indicate certain chemical contaminants. Use with caution, treat aggressively.

### Taste (only after visual and smell checks pass)

- Take a tiny sip, swish, and spit. Do not swallow.
- **Metallic taste:** Iron, manganese, or copper. Common in well water. Usually not dangerous in moderate amounts.
- **Salty taste:** Mineral content is high or seawater intrusion. May cause increased thirst and diarrhea if sodium is very high.
- **Bitter taste:** Possible magnesium or other mineral contamination. Not usually dangerous but unpleasant.
- **Soapy taste:** Alkaline water. Could indicate chemical contamination.
- **No unusual taste:** Best result you'll get from a taste test.

> **WARNING:** Never taste-test water that has an unusual color, oily sheen, chemical smell, or was collected near industrial sites, mining operations, or agricultural fields (pesticide risk). Some contaminants are toxic in tiny amounts and tasting won't save you.

## Biological Contamination

The biggest threat in most survival water sources. Bacteria, viruses, and parasites cause diarrheal disease, which kills through dehydration.

### What You're Looking For

- **Bacteria:** E. coli, Salmonella, Cholera, Campylobacter. Too small to see.
- **Protozoa:** Giardia, Cryptosporidium. Cause severe diarrhea. Resistant to chlorine treatment (Crypto especially). Filtration is the best defense.
- **Viruses:** Hepatitis A, Norovirus, Rotavirus. Extremely small — pass through most filters. Killed by boiling, UV, and chemical treatment.

### Testing Without a Lab

You can't identify specific pathogens in the field, but you can assess risk:

**Source assessment:** Where the water comes from tells you a lot.
- Deep well water: lowest biological risk (ground filters it)
- Spring water: low risk if emerging from rock
- Fast-flowing stream: moderate risk
- Slow or stagnant water: high risk
- Water downstream of human or animal activity: very high risk
- Floodwater: assume heavily contaminated

**Observation test:** Watch the water source for 15–30 minutes. Animal droppings near the shore? Livestock upstream? Human waste visible? These are strong indicators of fecal contamination.

**The handkerchief test:** Pour water through a clean white cloth. Examine what's trapped. Visible organisms (worms, insect larvae), excessive sediment, or organic debris indicate high biological load. The water needs aggressive treatment.

### Commercial Test Kits

If you stocked test kits pre-disaster:

- **Coliform/E. coli test strips:** Dip in water, wait for color change. Indicates fecal contamination. Available at pool supply and camping stores.
- **pH strips:** Test acidity/alkalinity. Safe drinking water is pH 6.5–8.5. Outside this range doesn't necessarily mean danger but suggests the water is unusual.
- **Chlorine test strips:** Verify your chemical treatment is at the right level. Target 0.2–2 ppm free chlorine.
- **Water quality test kits:** Multi-parameter kits test for bacteria, lead, pesticides, nitrates, and more. Relatively cheap (~$15–30 pre-disaster) and worth stockpiling.

## Chemical Contamination

Harder to detect than biological contamination and harder to treat.

**Agricultural runoff:** Nitrates, phosphates, pesticides, herbicides. Common near farmland. Clear water can be loaded with chemicals. Nitrates are particularly dangerous for infants (blue baby syndrome). No simple field test exists — if you're near agricultural land, assume contamination and use activated carbon filtration if available.

**Industrial contamination:** Heavy metals (lead, mercury, arsenic, chromium), solvents, petroleum products. Often indicated by unusual colors, smells, or dead vegetation/animals near the source. Do not use water from near industrial sites, mines, or chemical storage areas unless no other option exists.

**Natural mineral contamination:** Some groundwater contains naturally high levels of arsenic, fluoride, or dissolved minerals. Long-term use can cause health problems. Arsenic is tasteless and odorless — impossible to detect without testing.

> **NOTE:** Boiling water does NOT remove chemical contaminants. It actually concentrates them (the water evaporates, the chemicals stay). For chemically contaminated water, distillation or activated carbon filtration is needed.

## Making the Call

In most survival situations, the decision is simple:

1. Is the water source obviously contaminated (chemical smell, industrial site, visible pollution)? → Find another source.
2. Is the water from an unknown or risky biological source? → Treat it using any method from purification-methods.md. Boiling is the most reliable.
3. Is the water clear and from a reasonably clean source? → Treat it anyway. You can't see Giardia.
4. Is this the only water available? → Treat it as best you can with what you have. Dehydration kills faster than most waterborne illnesses.

The general rule: assume all untreated water is contaminated. Treat everything. Be more cautious with still/stagnant water than flowing water. Be most cautious downstream of any human activity.
