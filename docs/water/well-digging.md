A well is one of the most reliable long-term water sources you can create. It taps into groundwater — water stored in the spaces between rock and soil underground. Once you have a working well, you're no longer dependent on rain, surface water, or hauling water from a distance.

## Finding the Right Location

Not every spot has accessible groundwater, and some locations are better than others.

**Good signs:**
- Low ground — valleys, flats, and areas at the base of hills where water collects underground
- Near existing surface water (streams, ponds) but on higher ground — the water table is often close to the surface here
- Areas with water-loving vegetation (willows, cattails, dense grass)
- Where neighbors have successful wells (ask about their depth — this gives you an estimate of your water table depth)

**Bad locations:**
- At least 100 feet (30 meters) from any latrine, septic system, animal pen, or waste dump. Contamination travels through soil.
- At least 50 feet from any building foundation
- Uphill from contamination sources — water flows downhill underground too
- Rocky ground where digging is impractical
- Flood zones where the well head can be submerged and contaminated

## Estimating Water Table Depth

Before digging, get some idea of how far down you'll need to go.

- Check nearby wells, streams, or springs — the water table is roughly at the elevation of the nearest surface water.
- In river valleys and floodplains: often 5–20 feet.
- On plains: 10–50 feet or more.
- In hilly terrain: variable — follow water indicators.
- Dig a test hole first. If you hit moisture within 10–15 feet, a hand-dug well is practical. If the water table is deeper than 25–30 feet, consider a driven point well instead.

## Hand-Dug Well

The oldest well construction method. Practical down to about 25–30 feet (deeper gets dangerous without proper shoring). Best in soft or sandy soil.

### Materials Needed

- Shovels, picks, and a bucket with rope for hauling dirt
- Casing material: concrete rings, stacked stones, or a 55-gallon drum with bottom cut out
- Gravel for the well bottom
- Concrete or mortar for the apron (surface seal)
- A cover or well cap

### Procedure

1. Mark a circle 3–4 feet in diameter. Larger is easier to work in but requires more casing material.
2. Dig straight down, keeping the walls as vertical as possible. In soft or sandy soil, install casing (concrete rings, stacked stone, or other lining) as you go to prevent collapse.
3. Bucket the dirt out using a rope and helper at the top. As the hole gets deeper, set up a tripod with a pulley over the hole.
4. When you hit the water table, keep digging 3–5 feet into it. This ensures the well has storage capacity and doesn't go dry during dry spells. The bottom of the well should sit well into the water-bearing layer.
5. Place 6–12 inches of clean gravel at the bottom. This acts as a filter and prevents silt from muddying the water.
6. If using perforated casing (holes or slots in the bottom section), this allows water to seep in from the sides as well as the bottom.
7. Seal the top 10 feet of the well with solid (non-perforated) casing and pack clay or concrete around the outside. This prevents surface water from contaminating the well.

### Surface Protection

This is critical — most well contamination enters from the surface, not from groundwater.

1. Build a concrete or packed-clay apron around the well head, sloping away from the opening in all directions. At least 3 feet wide, 4–6 inches thick.
2. Raise the well casing at least 1 foot above ground level so surface water can't flow in.
3. Install a cover or cap. A wooden or metal lid that keeps rain, debris, animals, and children out.
4. Build a drainage channel away from the apron so spilled water doesn't pool around the well head.
5. Never store chemicals, fuel, or waste near the well.

## Driven Point Well (Sand Point Well)

Faster and easier than digging, works in sandy or soft soil, reaches 20–50 feet.

### How It Works

A pointed, screened pipe (well point) is literally hammered into the ground until it reaches the water table. Water enters through the screen, and you pump it up.

### Materials

- Well point: a hardened steel point with a screened section (available at hardware stores, or improvised from a steel pipe with holes drilled in the bottom 2 feet and wrapped with fine mesh screen)
- Galvanized steel pipe in 5-foot sections with threaded couplings
- Drive cap (protects the threads while hammering)
- A post driver, sledgehammer, or improvised weight for driving
- A hand pump or foot valve with suction hose

### Procedure

1. Start a pilot hole with a post-hole digger or auger, about 2–3 feet deep.
2. Place the well point in the hole.
3. Thread on the first pipe section. Place the drive cap on top.
4. Drive the pipe into the ground using a sledgehammer or drop weight. Hit the drive cap, not the pipe threads. Straight, centered blows — a crooked well binds and is useless.
5. When the first section is nearly flush with the ground, remove the drive cap, thread on another pipe section, replace the cap, and continue.
6. Every few feet, pour water down the pipe. If it drains away quickly, you've reached the water table. Keep driving another 3–5 feet to get the screen fully into the water-bearing layer.
7. If water sits in the pipe without draining, you haven't reached the water table yet.
8. Attach a hand pump at the top. Prime the pump and start pumping. The water will likely be muddy at first — pump until it clears (usually 15–30 minutes).

> **WARNING:** Suction pumps only work to about 25 feet of vertical lift (that's physics — atmospheric pressure can only push water up about 25 feet). If your water table is deeper than 25 feet, you need a deep-well pump that pushes from the bottom rather than pulling from the top.

## Well Maintenance

- Pump the well regularly. Unused wells can silt up.
- If water becomes muddy, pump hard for 15–30 minutes to clear it. If it stays muddy, the screen may be damaged or silted.
- Test water quality periodically — see water-testing.md.
- Keep the area around the well clean. No standing water, no waste, no animal access.
- In freezing climates, insulate the pump head and above-ground pipes. Drain the pump mechanism before hard freezes to prevent cracking.

## Well Disinfection

If your well may be contaminated (flooding, animals in the well, illness in the group):

1. Calculate the volume of water in the well: depth of water (in feet) × well diameter (in feet) squared × 5.9 = gallons.
2. Mix 1 quart of unscented household bleach per 100 gallons of well water.
3. Pour it in. Pump until you smell chlorine at the spigot.
4. Let it sit for 12–24 hours.
5. Pump until the chlorine smell is gone (run it away from gardens — chlorinated water kills plants).
6. Wait 1–2 days, then test. If contamination signs return, there's an ongoing source you need to find and eliminate.

## When a Well Isn't Practical

- Bedrock too close to the surface for digging or driving
- Water table deeper than 50 feet without powered drilling equipment
- Contaminated groundwater from industrial sites, mining, or natural mineral deposits
- Heavy clay soil that doesn't yield water even when saturated (clay holds water but doesn't release it)

In these cases, rainwater harvesting (see rainwater-harvesting.md) or surface water treatment may be your better option.
