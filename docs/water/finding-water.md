Finding water is the second most urgent survival priority after immediate physical safety. You can go roughly 3 days without water in moderate conditions — less in heat, altitude, or exertion. The clock starts ticking immediately, so knowing where to look and how to read the landscape matters.

Every water source you find should be treated as contaminated until you purify it. See purification-methods.md for treatment options.

## Reading the Terrain

Water flows downhill and collects in low points. That's the fundamental rule.

**Valleys and drainages:** Follow terrain downhill. Dry creek beds, gullies, and ravines channel water during rain and often have water underground even when the surface is dry. Dig in the outside bend of a dry creek bed — water pools deeper on the outside of turns.

**Rock formations:** Water collects in cracks, depressions, and at the base of cliffs. After rain, check rock crevices and potholes. Sandstone and limestone are porous — they absorb rain and release it as springs and seeps. Granite and other hard rock force water to the surface.

**Springs and seeps:** Where water emerges from the ground naturally. Look for unusually green vegetation on a hillside, wet ground, or a line of vegetation following a contour. Springs at the base of hills where permeable rock meets impermeable rock are among the cleanest natural water sources.

**Convergence points:** Where two ridges meet heading downhill, water channels come together and the likelihood of finding surface water increases.

## Vegetation Indicators

Plants need water. Their presence, density, and species tell you what's happening underground.

- **Dense, green vegetation in an otherwise dry landscape** almost always marks a water source — underground stream, spring, or high water table.
- **Cottonwood, willow, and sycamore trees** in North America grow near water. If you see a line of cottonwoods in a dry landscape, follow them — there's water at their roots.
- **Cattails and reeds** mean standing water or a very high water table. If you see cattails, water is within a few feet of the surface.
- **Seep willow, salt cedar (tamarisk), and mesquite** in desert environments indicate groundwater.
- **Grass** in an otherwise barren area can mean a shallow water table.

> **WARNING:** Vegetation alone doesn't tell you if the water is safe to drink. It only tells you water is present. Always purify.

## Animal and Insect Signs

- **Converging animal tracks** heading in the same direction — follow them downhill. They're heading to water.
- **Bird flight patterns at dawn and dusk.** Grain-eating birds (pigeons, doves, finches) fly toward water in the evening and away from it in the morning. They fly low and direct when heading to water.
- **Bees** are a strong indicator. Bees rarely range more than 3 miles from a water source.
- **Flies and mosquitoes** in large numbers mean standing water within a few hundred yards.
- **Frogs and their calls** at night confirm standing water nearby.

## Digging for Water

When there's no surface water visible but the terrain suggests it's underground:

1. **Dry creek beds:** Dig at the lowest point of the outside bend. In many dry streams, water sits 1–3 feet below the surface. Let the hole fill, then scoop the clearest water from the top.
2. **Base of hills and cliffs:** Where the slope meets flat ground, especially if the soil type changes. Dig at the boundary.
3. **Dune valleys:** In sandy terrain, the low points between dunes often have the highest water table.
4. **Near vegetation:** Dig within 10 feet of water-indicating plants. Go down until you hit wet sand or soil.
5. **Beach wells:** On ocean beaches, dig above the high tide line in the first dune. A shallow well here often produces brackish but drinkable water.

> **NOTE:** Water collected from digging is usually murky. Let it settle for 30 minutes, then pour off the clear water on top. It still needs purification.

## Condensation and Dew Collection

### Dew Collection

1. Before dawn, drag a clean absorbent cloth through tall grass or over bushes.
2. Wring the cloth into a container.
3. Production: 0.5–1 liter per hour in good conditions.
4. Dew is essentially distilled water — usually safe to drink without treatment.

### Vegetation Bag (Transpiration)

1. Place a clear plastic bag over a leafy branch of a non-poisonous tree.
2. Tie the bag's opening tightly around the branch.
3. Position so the lowest corner hangs down with a small stone inside as a collection point.
4. Production: 0.1–1 liter per day depending on branch size, sunlight, and humidity.
5. Move the bag to a fresh branch every 2–3 days.

### Solar Still (Below-Ground)

1. Dig a hole about 3 feet across and 2 feet deep in a sunny spot.
2. Place a container in the center.
3. Add non-potable water or green vegetation around (not in) the container for extra moisture.
4. Cover with clear plastic sheeting, seal edges with dirt.
5. Place a small stone on the center of the plastic to create an inverted cone above the container.
6. Production: 0.5–1.5 liters per day in good conditions. In very dry conditions, the energy spent digging may cost more water in sweat than the still produces.

## Specific Environments

### Desert
- Base of mountain ranges where water drains and collects.
- Dig at bends in dry washes where vegetation grows.
- Rock pockets (tinajas) in canyon walls collect rainwater that persists for weeks.
- Morning condensation on metal surfaces can be wiped and collected.

### Forest
- Follow drainages downhill to streams and springs.
- Birch and maple trees can be tapped for sap in late winter/early spring — bore a 2-inch deep hole, insert a spout. 1–2 liters per day per tree.
- Rain collection using tarps or broad leaves.

### Mountain
- Melt snow and ice — don't eat directly. See winter-water.md.
- Glacial streams are usually clean but may contain rock flour — let settle first.
- Mountain springs at rock layer transitions are often the cleanest water in nature.

### Urban/Suburban
- Hot water heater tanks hold 30–80 gallons of drinkable water. Turn off supply to prevent contaminated water entering, drain from bottom valve.
- Toilet tanks (not bowls) hold 1.5–3 gallons if no chemicals added.
- Swimming pools — drinkable after letting chlorine off-gas for 24 hours, then purify normally.
- Canned fruits and vegetables contain water.
- Avoid water from radiators, waterbeds, and fire sprinkler systems.
