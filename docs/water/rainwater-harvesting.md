Rain is free, relatively clean water falling from the sky. A moderate rainfall on a modest roof produces more water than most people realize. A 1,000 square foot roof in a 1-inch rainstorm collects roughly 623 gallons. Even a 10x10 tarp yields 62 gallons from the same storm.

## How Much Water Can You Collect?

**Catchment area (sq ft) × rainfall (inches) × 0.623 = gallons collected**

Multiply by 0.75–0.85 for real-world efficiency (splashing, first-flush losses, evaporation).

## Basic Collection System

You need three things: a catchment surface, a way to channel water, and storage.

### Catchment Surface

**Roofs** are the best catchment. Material matters:
- Metal roofing (galvanized steel, aluminum, tin): Best. Smooth, sheds debris, doesn't leach chemicals.
- Slate or tile: Good. Inert materials.
- Asphalt shingles: Usable but less ideal. Can leach petroleum compounds, especially when new. Filtration and treatment needed.
- Treated wood, lead-painted surfaces, tar-coated roofs: Avoid if possible. Leach harmful chemicals.

**Tarps and plastic sheeting** work for improvised collection. Stretch tight at an angle so water flows to a single collection point.

### Gutters and Channels

- Commercial gutters and downspouts are ideal.
- Improvised: split PVC pipe, bamboo halves, sheet metal bent into a channel, or a length of tarp folded into a trough.
- Angle with a slight slope (1/4 inch drop per foot) toward the collection point.
- Screen the inlet with mesh or cloth to keep leaves and debris out.

### First-Flush Diverter

The first water off a roof after a dry spell is the dirtiest — bird droppings, dust, pollen, debris. Diverting this dramatically improves water quality.

1. Attach a vertical standpipe (2–4 inch diameter) to your downspout before the storage tank.
2. Size it to hold about 1 gallon per 100 square feet of roof.
3. First water fills the standpipe. Once full, water flows to your storage tank.
4. A small drain hole at the bottom lets the standpipe empty slowly between storms.

**Simple alternative:** Disconnect your downspout at the start of rain. Let it run on the ground for the first 5–10 minutes, then reconnect.

## Storage

### Container Options

- **55-gallon drums:** Use food-grade HDPE plastic (recycling number 2). Blue drums are typically food-grade. Never use drums that held chemicals.
- **275-gallon IBC totes:** Good capacity, stackable. Verify they held food products originally.
- **Cisterns:** 500–10,000+ gallon tanks. Concrete, fiberglass, or polyethylene. The long-term solution.
- **Improvised:** Clean garbage cans with lids, bathtubs, lined pits.

### Storage Rules

1. Keep containers opaque or covered. Light promotes algae growth.
2. Keep sealed with screened overflow outlets. Open water breeds mosquitoes.
3. Elevate containers if possible. Even 3–4 feet gives gravity-fed pressure for a spigot.
4. Use oldest water first (first in, first out).
5. For long-term storage, treat with 1/8 teaspoon unscented household bleach per gallon.
6. Clean containers annually — drain, scrub with dilute bleach (1 tablespoon per gallon), rinse.

## Treatment

Rainwater from a clean roof through a first-flush system is relatively clean, but not sterile. Filter through cloth to remove particulates, then treat using any method from purification-methods.md.

For ongoing use, a gravity filter (see gravity-filters.md) provides continuous treated water.

## Scaling Up

For a group or homestead:

1. Connect multiple roof surfaces to common storage.
2. Use a multi-tank system: first tank as settling tank, overflow to clean storage.
3. Calculate daily needs: 1 gallon per person minimum for drinking/cooking, 5–10 gallons including washing/sanitation.
4. Size storage to bridge dry periods — dry spell days × daily usage = minimum storage capacity.

## Common Problems

**Mosquitoes:** Screen all openings, seal lids. A few drops of cooking oil on unsealed tanks suffocates larvae.

**Algae:** Caused by light. Keep containers dark and covered. Not toxic but clogs filters.

**Freezing:** Drain exposed pipes before winter. Buried cisterns below frost line won't freeze.

**Overflow:** Install an overflow pipe directing excess water away from your foundation.
