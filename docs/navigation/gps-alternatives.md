GPS failure — dead batteries, broken screen, no satellites — is a reality in any extended survival scenario. These techniques let you navigate reliably without electronics, using only a compass (or even without one) and your understanding of terrain.

## Dead Reckoning

Navigating from a known position by tracking direction and distance traveled. You "reckon" where you are based on where you started, what direction you moved, and how far you went.

### Process

1. **Establish a known position** on the map. Your starting point, a landmark you've identified, or a position fixed by triangulation.
2. **Set a compass bearing** to your destination or next waypoint.
3. **Walk the bearing**, counting paces to track distance (see compass-and-map.md for pace count calibration).
4. **Plot your progress** on the map as you go. "I've walked 600 meters on a bearing of 270 degrees from the trail junction — that puts me about here."
5. **Reset at each waypoint.** Every time you reach an identifiable feature (stream crossing, hilltop, trail junction), confirm your position on the map and take a fresh bearing to the next waypoint.

### Accuracy

Dead reckoning errors accumulate. A 3-degree compass error over 1 kilometer puts you about 50 meters off target. Over 5 kilometers, you're 250 meters off — easily enough to miss a camp, a trail, or a water source. This is why resetting at known points is critical.

## Terrain Association

Instead of following compass bearings blindly, you match what you see around you to what the map shows. "The map shows a saddle between two ridges 800 meters ahead. I can see that saddle right there — I'm on track."

### Reading the Ground

- **Ridgelines:** Follow the high ground. Ridges are visible from a distance and show clearly on topo maps as contour lines extending outward from higher elevations.
- **Valleys and drainages:** Follow the low ground. Water features are the most reliable map-to-ground correlation because they don't move and they're usually marked on maps.
- **Saddles:** The low point between two higher peaks. Distinctive on the ground and on maps (an hourglass shape in the contour lines).
- **Spurs:** Ridges extending from a main ridgeline. Count the spurs along a ridge on the map, then count them as you pass them on the ground.

### When Terrain Association Works Best

- In mountainous or hilly terrain with distinctive features
- When visibility is good (you can see surrounding terrain)
- When you have a detailed topo map (1:24,000 or 1:50,000)

### When It Fails

- Flat terrain with no distinguishing features (plains, desert, thick forest)
- Poor visibility (fog, heavy rain, dense canopy, darkness)
- In these conditions, fall back on dead reckoning (compass and pace count)

## Handrail Features

A handrail is a linear terrain feature running roughly parallel to your direction of travel that you can follow without constant compass work. Walking alongside a handrail is faster, easier, and less error-prone than following a bearing through featureless terrain.

**Common handrails:**
- Streams and rivers
- Ridgelines
- Roads and trails
- Power lines
- Fence lines
- Field edges
- Valley bottoms
- Shorelines

**How to use:** Identify a handrail on the map that runs near your desired route. Travel alongside it. You always know your approximate position — you're somewhere along this line. When you need to leave the handrail to reach your destination, take a compass bearing from your known position on the handrail to your target.

## Catching Features (Backstops)

A catching feature is a large, unmissable terrain feature that tells you when you've gone too far. It "catches" you before you overshoot your target.

**Examples:**
- A river perpendicular to your direction of travel
- A road crossing your path
- A distinct ridgeline
- A lake or coastline

**How to use:** Before you move, identify a catching feature on the map beyond your destination. "If I hit the river, I've gone too far and my camp is somewhere behind me." This prevents you from walking past your target without realizing it.

## Aiming Off

If your target is on a linear feature (a cabin on a river, a camp at a trail junction), don't aim directly at it. Instead, deliberately aim to one side — intentionally miss left or right.

**Why:** If you aim directly and miss (which you will — dead reckoning is never perfect), you won't know which side of the target you're on. You'll stand at the river and not know whether to go upstream or downstream.

**If you aim off intentionally to the right**, when you hit the river you KNOW the target is to your left. Turn left and follow the river to it. Problem solved.

Aim off 5–10 degrees from direct bearing. Enough to guarantee you're on the intended side despite navigation error.

## Deliberate Offset

Similar to aiming off but for reaching a specific point on a straight path. Walk a deliberate offset — add extra distance or bearing adjustment to ensure you approach from a known direction.

## Attack Point

An attack point is a large, easy-to-find feature near your final destination that you navigate to first, then use as a launching pad for a short, precise bearing to the actual target.

**Example:** Your camp is in dense forest and hard to find directly. But there's a distinctive hilltop 300 meters east of camp. Navigate to the hilltop (big target, easy to find), then take a precise bearing west for 300 meters to reach camp.

This turns one long, error-prone navigation leg into one easy leg (to the attack point) plus one short, precise leg (to the target).

## Night Navigation

Navigating at night is slow and error-prone. Avoid it when possible. When you must:

1. **Move slower.** You miss terrain features in darkness. Halve your expected pace.
2. **Use stars for direction** when visible (see navigation-without-tools.md). Realign frequently — stars shift with Earth's rotation.
3. **Follow terrain features by feel.** A ridgeline underfoot is detectable even in total darkness. A stream can be followed by sound.
4. **Use a flashlight sparingly and with red filter if possible.** White light destroys your dark adaptation (20–30 minutes to recover). Red light preserves night vision.
5. **Pace count is essential.** In the dark, terrain features are harder to identify — distance measurement becomes your primary navigation tool.
6. **Stay on paths and trails** if available. Off-trail night navigation in rough terrain is dangerous and slow.

## Navigation in Featureless Terrain

Deserts, plains, dense forests, and snowfields all lack the terrain diversity that makes map-based navigation work. In these environments:

1. **Compass and pace count are your primary tools.** You can't terrain-associate when everything looks the same.
2. **Mark your backtrail.** Stack rocks, break branches, drag a stick behind you in sand. In featureless terrain, losing your backtrail means losing your only reference point.
3. **Use celestial navigation** when compass is unavailable.
4. **Create your own landmarks.** Build a cairn at your camp or critical waypoints.
5. **In dense forest:** follow drainages downhill to reach water features that are on your map. This gives you a known position to navigate from.
6. **In desert:** navigate at dawn/dusk when temperature is manageable and shadows create terrain contrast that disappears in midday glare.
