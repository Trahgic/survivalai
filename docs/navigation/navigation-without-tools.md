When you have no compass, no GPS, and no map, you can still determine direction using the sun, stars, moon, and natural indicators. These methods have guided travelers for thousands of years. None are as precise as a compass, but they'll keep you moving in the right direction.

## Sun Methods

### Shadow Stick (Most Reliable Daytime Method)

Works anywhere the sun is visible. Determines east-west direction with reasonable accuracy.

1. Find a straight stick 2–3 feet long. Push it vertically into level ground so it casts a clear shadow.
2. Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone or scratch in the dirt. This is your first mark.
3. Wait 15–20 minutes. The shadow tip will have moved.
4. Mark the new shadow tip position. This is your second mark.
5. Draw a straight line through the two marks. **This line runs approximately east-west.** The first mark is west, the second mark is east (the shadow moves from west to east as the sun moves east to west).
6. A line perpendicular to the east-west line gives you north-south.

**Longer waits give more accuracy.** If you can wait 2–3 hours between marks, the east-west line becomes very precise. The method works best around local noon — less reliable in the first and last hours of daylight.

### Watch Method

If you have an analog watch (or know the current time and can imagine a clock face):

**Northern Hemisphere:**
1. Point the hour hand at the sun.
2. The midpoint between the hour hand and 12 o'clock is roughly south. (Before noon, count clockwise from the hour hand to 12. After noon, count counterclockwise.)

**Southern Hemisphere:**
1. Point the 12 o'clock mark at the sun.
2. The midpoint between 12 and the hour hand is roughly north.

**Adjust for daylight saving time** — use standard time (subtract 1 hour from DST).

This method is approximate — accuracy decreases at higher latitudes and near the equator.

### Sun Direction (General)

In the Northern Hemisphere:
- The sun rises in the east (roughly — varies by season)
- The sun sets in the west
- At solar noon (when the sun is highest), it's due south
- In summer, the sun rises north of east and sets north of west
- In winter, the sun rises south of east and sets south of west

In the Southern Hemisphere, reverse the north/south references — the sun at noon is due north.

## Star Navigation

### Polaris (North Star) — Northern Hemisphere

Polaris sits almost directly above the North Pole. It doesn't move — everything else rotates around it. Finding Polaris gives you true north.

**Finding Polaris:**
1. Find the Big Dipper (Ursa Major). It's one of the most recognizable star patterns — four stars forming a bowl and three forming a handle.
2. Locate the two stars at the far edge of the bowl (away from the handle). These are the "pointer stars."
3. Draw an imaginary line through these two pointer stars, extending about 5 times the distance between them. It points directly to Polaris.
4. Polaris is a medium-bright star at the tip of the handle of the Little Dipper (Ursa Minor).
5. Face Polaris. You're facing north. South is behind you. East is to your right. West is to your left.

**If the Big Dipper isn't visible** (it can be below the horizon at some times/latitudes), use Cassiopeia — a W-shaped constellation on the opposite side of Polaris from the Big Dipper. The center star of the W points roughly toward Polaris.

### Southern Cross — Southern Hemisphere

There is no bright pole star in the southern sky. The Southern Cross (Crux) is used to find south instead.

1. Find the Southern Cross — four bright stars in a cross shape, the long axis being about 6 degrees long.
2. Extend the long axis of the cross about 4.5 times its length in the direction the cross "points" (away from the brighter star at the top of the cross).
3. This point is approximately the south celestial pole. Drop a vertical line from this point to the horizon — that's south.

### Orion — Works in Both Hemispheres

Orion's belt (three stars in a row) rises due east and sets due west everywhere on Earth. If you see Orion's belt rising or setting, you have a reliable east-west reference.

### Star Movement (General)

Stars rise in the east and set in the west, just like the sun. Pick any star near the horizon and watch it for 15–20 minutes:
- If it moves up and to the right, you're facing roughly south (Northern Hemisphere)
- If it moves up and to the left, you're facing roughly north
- If it rises straight up, you're facing east
- If it sinks straight down, you're facing west

## Moon Methods

### Crescent Moon

A crescent moon can give you a rough south bearing in the Northern Hemisphere:

1. Draw an imaginary line connecting the two tips (horns) of the crescent.
2. Extend this line downward to the horizon.
3. Where it meets the horizon is approximately south (Northern Hemisphere) or north (Southern Hemisphere).

This is a rough method — accuracy varies with moon position and phase.

### Moon Rise/Set

The moon follows roughly the same east-west path as the sun:
- Rising moon = east
- Setting moon = west
- Full moon at its highest point = south (Northern Hemisphere) / north (Southern Hemisphere)

## Natural Indicators

These are general tendencies with many exceptions. Use them as supporting evidence, not primary navigation.

**More reliable:**
- **Prevailing wind patterns.** If you know the prevailing wind direction in your region, flags, bent trees, and wind-formed snow drifts indicate direction consistently.
- **Stream and river flow.** In most of North America, rivers generally flow south, southeast, or southwest toward the ocean. Not reliable in all terrain but a useful general tendency.

**Less reliable (use with caution):**
- **Moss on trees.** The old saying "moss grows on the north side" is unreliable. Moss grows where it's moist and shaded — that's often the north side of trees in the Northern Hemisphere, but not always. In forests, moss grows all around trees.
- **Spider webs.** Tend to face south (toward the sun) in the Northern Hemisphere. Loosely reliable in open areas.
- **Snow melt patterns.** Snow melts faster on south-facing slopes (more sun exposure) in the Northern Hemisphere. South-facing slopes have less snow and thinner coverage.
- **Vegetation density.** South-facing slopes in the Northern Hemisphere tend to have less dense, more drought-adapted vegetation. North-facing slopes are often more heavily forested.
- **Ant hills.** Some species build on the south side of trees or rocks (warmer). Regionally variable.

## Maintaining Direction While Traveling

Without a compass, staying on course requires constant vigilance:

1. **Pick landmarks ahead on your line of travel.** Walk to them. Pick the next one. This prevents drift.
2. **Look back frequently.** Your backtrail should be a straight line. If it's curving, you're drifting (most people drift to the right if right-handed, left if left-handed).
3. **Use terrain features as handrails.** Follow a ridgeline, stream, or trail that runs in your direction. These keep you on course without constant direction-finding.
4. **Mark your trail.** Broken branches, stacked rocks, scratches on trees. This lets you backtrack if needed and confirms you haven't been walking in circles.
5. **At night, choose two aligned stars ahead.** Walk toward the closer one. When it drifts out of alignment with the farther one, realign with a new pair. Stars shift as the Earth rotates — adjust every 15–20 minutes.
