Signaling only works if you're in a place where someone might see or hear it. The best signal in the world is useless in a location nobody is searching. Prioritize visibility — get to high ground, open areas, or known travel routes before investing effort in signals.

## Three Means Three

The international distress signal in almost every medium is three of anything:
- Three fires in a triangle
- Three whistle blasts
- Three gunshots
- Three flashes of light
- Three blasts of a horn

One of something is natural. Two might be coincidence. Three is a deliberate signal. Space them evenly — approximately 100 feet apart for visual signals, 3–5 seconds apart for sound signals.

## Signal Fires

The most effective long-range visual signal. Visible for miles by day (smoke) and night (flame).

### Daytime — Smoke Signals

1. Build a hot, well-established fire in an open, elevated area.
2. Once burning strongly, add green vegetation (leaves, pine boughs, green grass) or damp material. This produces thick white smoke.
3. In snowy or overcast environments where white smoke blends in, add rubber, oil-soaked rags, or plastic to produce dark/black smoke instead (toxic — stay upwind).
4. Three separate smoke columns (fires spaced 100 feet apart in a triangle) is the standard distress signal.

### Nighttime — Flame

1. Build the fire as large and bright as possible on elevated, visible ground.
2. Dry wood produces maximum flame height with minimum smoke.
3. Three fires in a triangle pattern.
4. Have extra fuel ready to build the fire quickly when you hear or see potential rescuers.

### Preparation

Build signal fires in advance and keep them covered (tarps, bark) to protect from rain. Stage dry tinder and kindling underneath so they light in seconds. When an aircraft or vehicle appears, you may have only minutes to get your signal going.

## Signal Mirror

A mirror or any reflective surface can project a flash of light visible up to 50 miles on a clear day. The single most effective daytime signaling device in clear weather.

### How to Aim a Signal Mirror

1. Hold the mirror near your face with one hand.
2. Extend your other hand at arm's length toward the target (aircraft, ship, distant position).
3. Form a V with two fingers on the extended hand.
4. Angle the mirror to catch the sun. The reflected flash creates a bright spot.
5. Adjust the mirror angle until the bright spot (which you'll see on your extended hand or on the ground near you) lands on your V/target.
6. Sweep the reflected light back and forth across the target's position in a slow arc. Three flashes, pause, three flashes.

### Improvised Mirrors

- Any mirror or mirror fragment
- CD or DVD (highly reflective)
- Aluminum foil stretched flat over cardboard
- Polished metal (pot bottom, can lid, belt buckle, knife blade)
- A phone screen in the sun (even a dead phone)

## Ground-to-Air Signals

Large symbols stamped, dug, or built on the ground that are visible to aircraft.

### Standard ICAO Ground Symbols

Use these if possible — SAR (search and rescue) crews know them:

- **V** = Require assistance
- **X** = Require medical help
- **I** = Require medicine/supplies
- **→** = Traveling in this direction
- **N** = No (negative)
- **Y** = Yes (affirmative)

### Making Ground Signals

- **Size matters.** Each symbol should be at least 10 feet long and 3 feet wide. Bigger is better — 20+ feet is visible from high altitude.
- **Contrast is everything.** The symbol must contrast with the surrounding ground. On snow: stomp out the symbol and fill with dark material (branches, dirt). On dark ground: use light materials (stripped bark, light rocks, clothing). On green grass: scrape to bare earth.
- **Location:** Place in the largest, most open area available. Clearings, meadows, ridgetops, beaches. Not under tree canopy.
- **Shadow helps.** Dig the symbols 1–2 feet deep if possible — the shadow in the trench creates contrast visible from directly above.
- **Maintain them.** Wind, snow, and rain degrade ground signals. Check and repair daily.

## Whistle

A whistle carries much farther than the human voice — up to a mile in favorable conditions vs. a few hundred yards for shouting. It also requires less energy and can be sustained for hours without losing your voice.

- Three short blasts = distress (pause, repeat)
- A whistle made from plastic, metal, or bone carries better than shouting
- Blow hard — short, sharp blasts
- Improvised whistle: cut a short section of thin, hollow tube (bamboo, reed, bone, pen tube). Notch it properly and it produces a high-pitched tone.

## Sound Signals

- **Gunshots:** Three shots spaced evenly. Effective in wooded areas where visual signals are blocked. Use ammunition sparingly — you may need it.
- **Banging on metal:** A rhythmic beat on a pot, car hood, or any resonant metal carries well through forest.
- **Horn or alarm:** Car horn (short battery life — use sparingly), air horn, whistle.

Sound doesn't carry well into wind, uphill, or across bodies of water. Position yourself where the sound carries toward likely search areas.

## Electronic Signals (If Available)

- **Cell phone:** Even without signal bars, a phone may connect to emergency services (911 calls route through any available tower, not just your carrier). Try from high ground.
- **Two-way radio:** Transmit on emergency frequency 156.8 MHz (Channel 16 marine) or 121.5 MHz (aviation emergency). Say "MAYDAY" three times, your position if known, number of people, and nature of emergency.
- **Personal Locator Beacon (PLB):** Activates a satellite distress signal with GPS coordinates. Searchers respond directly to your location. If you have one, use it.
- **Flashlight at night:** Flash SOS in Morse code toward any visible lights: three short, three long, three short (... --- ...). Or just flash continuously in the direction of potential rescuers.

## Attracting Attention from Vehicles

If you're trying to flag down a passing vehicle or boat:

- Stand in the open, wave both arms overhead. One arm looks like a casual wave — two arms is clearly deliberate.
- Use any brightly colored material as a flag — clothing, tarp, sleeping bag.
- At night, wave a flashlight or torch.
- On roads, create a barrier or obvious signal — rocks across the road, an SOS spelled in branches, bright material tied to a tree at the roadside.

## When NOT to Signal

In some survival scenarios (conflict zones, hostile territory), signaling attracts the wrong attention. Evaluate your situation:
- If rescue services exist and are looking for you: signal aggressively
- If the environment is hostile or you're evading threats: stay concealed and move toward safety instead of signaling
- In ambiguous situations: signal selectively — only when you've identified the approaching party as friendly
