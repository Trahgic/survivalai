Reading terrain — understanding what the ground is doing and what it means — is the foundation of overland navigation. A good terrain reader picks efficient routes, avoids hazards, and knows where they are on a map by matching what they see to what the contour lines show.

## The Five Major Terrain Features

Every landscape is built from these five shapes. Learn to recognize them on a map and on the ground.

### Hill

A high point surrounded by lower ground on all sides. On a map: concentric contour circles, smallest at the top. On the ground: ground rises as you approach from any direction. The highest contour ring is the approximate summit elevation, but the actual peak could be up to one contour interval higher.

### Valley

Low ground between hills or ridges, typically with a stream running through it. On a map: U-shaped or V-shaped contour lines with the closed end pointing uphill (water flows from the V toward the open end). On the ground: ground descends from both sides toward a central low point.

### Ridge

A line of high ground with lower ground on both sides. Like an upside-down valley. On a map: U-shaped contour lines with the closed end pointing downhill (away from higher ground). On the ground: you can walk along the top with ground falling away on both sides.

### Saddle

A low point on a ridge between two higher points. On a map: an hourglass shape in the contour lines — two hills with a dip between them. On the ground: the lowest easy crossing point when traveling perpendicular to a ridge. Military and hiking routes frequently use saddles for ridge crossings.

### Depression

A low point surrounded by higher ground — basically a hole. On a map: contour lines with hash marks (tick marks) pointing inward. On the ground: ground drops as you approach from any direction. Can be natural (sinkholes, volcanic craters) or man-made (quarries, bomb craters).

## Three Supplemental Features

### Spur

A finger-like extension projecting from a ridge or hill. On a map: contour lines form a U or V pointing away from higher ground. On the ground: a ridge that extends outward from the main ridgeline, often with drainages on both sides. Spurs make good terrain handrails — follow them uphill to reach the main ridge.

### Draw

The opposite of a spur — a small valley or channel where water flows down a hillside. On a map: contour lines form a U or V pointing uphill. On the ground: a concave channel in the hillside. Draws collect water and funnel it downhill. In steep terrain, draws can become chutes — avoid them in rain or snowmelt conditions.

### Cliff

A near-vertical or vertical face. On a map: contour lines merge into a single thick line or nearly touch. On the ground: obvious. What matters on the map is recognizing cliffs before you walk up to one — contour lines touching or nearly merging mean "you cannot walk here."

## Slope Analysis

### Determining Steepness

Count the contour lines crossed over a given horizontal distance. More lines = steeper slope.

**Rough guide (20-foot contour interval):**
- 1 contour line per inch on a 1:24,000 map ≈ gentle slope (walkable easily)
- 3–4 lines per inch ≈ moderate slope (strenuous walking, switchback territory)
- 5+ lines per inch ≈ steep (hands and feet may be needed, high fall risk)
- Lines touching or merging = cliff or near-cliff

### Reading Slope on the Ground

- **Convex slope:** Curves away from you — you can't see the base from the top. Dangerous because hazards (cliffs, water) are hidden below the convex break.
- **Concave slope:** Curves toward you — you can see the entire slope from top to bottom. Generally safer for travel.
- **Uniform slope:** Constant grade. Predictable.

## Watershed and Drainage

### Finding Water from Terrain

Water follows terrain perfectly and predictably:

1. All water flows to the lowest point.
2. Draws and valleys are collection points — look for streams here.
3. The steeper the surrounding terrain, the more reliably water will be present in the valleys.
4. Watershed divides (ridgetops) separate drainages. Rain falling on one side of a ridge feeds one stream system; rain on the other side feeds a different one.
5. Larger valleys with more tributary draws usually have more reliable water.

### Drainage Pattern Reading

On a map, trace the blue lines (streams) and the valley contour patterns:
- Where small streams merge into larger ones tells you the general downhill direction
- Following drainages downstream leads to progressively larger water features (creek → stream → river)
- In most inhabited regions, following water downstream leads to civilization

## Route Planning Off-Trail

### Choosing the Best Route

The shortest distance between two points is rarely the best route on foot. Terrain dictates the practical path.

**Prefer:**
- Ridgelines — well-drained, good visibility, often have game trails. Walking a ridge is usually easier than crossing it.
- Saddles — the easiest way to cross from one drainage to another
- Valley floors — relatively flat, near water. But can be thick with vegetation and swampy.
- Spurs — natural ramps between ridges and valleys
- Existing game trails — animals pick efficient routes through terrain. Follow them.

**Avoid:**
- Straight-line routes through steep terrain — switchback instead
- Dense vegetation in valley bottoms (thick brush, bogs, deadfall)
- Draws and gullies in steep terrain (unstable footing, flash flood risk)
- Traversing across steep slopes (side-hilling) for extended distances — exhausting and dangerous
- Cliff bands — if contour lines on the map are nearly merging, find a way around

### Estimating Travel Time

The 30-minute rule: on trail, plan 30 minutes per mile. Off trail in moderate terrain, plan 45–60 minutes per mile. In rough terrain (steep, dense vegetation, snow), plan 60–90 minutes per mile.

Add time for:
- Elevation gain: add 30 minutes per 1,000 feet of climbing
- River crossings: add 15–30 minutes each
- Dense vegetation: double your time estimate
- Group with children or injured members: double or triple

### Contouring

Instead of going straight up and over a hill, contour around it — maintain your current elevation by following the hillside. This costs horizontal distance but saves the vertical effort of climbing and descending. On a map, contouring means walking roughly parallel to the contour lines rather than perpendicular.

When to contour: when the detour distance is less than roughly 3x the direct distance over the hill. Beyond that, it's faster to just climb.

## Terrain Hazards to Recognize

**On the map:**
- Contour lines crossing a river/stream (waterfall or rapids)
- Closely spaced contours ending at water (cliff into lake/river)
- Green-to-white transition near water (marsh or bog at the edge of forest)
- Closed contour rings in flat terrain (sinkholes — common in limestone country)

**On the ground:**
- Undercut riverbanks (collapse risk)
- Talus slopes (loose rock fields below cliffs — unstable footing, ankle injuries)
- Cornices on ridgelines in winter (overhanging snow that collapses without warning)
- Avalanche chutes — bare, stripped vegetation paths on steep slopes
- Deadfall zones — areas of tangled fallen trees (extremely slow, injury risk)
