Traveling in a survival situation burns calories, water, and time — your three most limited resources. Every mile you walk costs you. Bad travel planning wastes all three. Good planning gets you where you need to go with the least expenditure of resources and the highest margin of safety.

## Should You Stay or Go?

This is the first and most important travel decision. Staying put is usually safer than moving.

**Stay if:**
- Someone knows where you are and will send help
- You have shelter, water, and reasonable safety
- You're injured or the group has injured members
- Weather is severe or deteriorating
- You don't know where you are or where to go
- Your vehicle or shelter is visible to potential rescuers

**Go if:**
- No one knows where you are
- No rescue is expected
- Your current location is unsafe (fire, flood, hostile threat)
- You know the direction and approximate distance to help
- You have adequate water and energy for the journey
- Weather conditions are favorable

If you decide to go, leave a clear sign at your departure point: note with date, direction of travel, number of people, and condition. Cairns, arrows on the ground, or a note in a visible spot.

## Distance Estimation

### On a Map

Use the map scale. For 1:24,000 maps, 1 inch = 2,000 feet. Measure straight-line distance, then add 20–30% for terrain (trails switchback, you go around obstacles, straight lines through terrain are fiction).

### Without a Map

**Visual estimation (hard to do accurately):**
- If you can see details of a person's face: under 100 yards
- If you can see individual windows on a building: about 1 mile
- If you can see a building but not details: 2–5 miles
- If a mountain fills your view but features are hazy: 10–20 miles

Humans consistently underestimate distances to objects that are large, elevated, brightly lit, or across open ground. Overestimate distances to objects that are small, below you, in shadow, or obscured.

### Travel Time Estimation

**On trail:**
- Flat: 3 mph / 20 minutes per mile
- Rolling terrain: 2 mph / 30 minutes per mile
- Mountain trail with switchbacks: 1–2 mph

**Off trail:**
- Easy terrain (open forest, meadow): 1–2 mph
- Moderate terrain (brush, uneven ground): 0.5–1 mph
- Difficult terrain (dense brush, steep slopes, deadfall): 0.25–0.5 mph
- Snow (unbroken): 0.5–1 mph with snowshoes, 0.25 mph without

**Add for elevation:**
- 1 hour per 1,000 feet of elevation gain (Naismith's Rule)
- Descent is usually faster but steep downhill is hard on the body — add time for steep descents

**Group speed = slowest member's speed.** Always.

## Energy and Calorie Budget

Walking burns roughly 300–500 calories per hour depending on terrain, pack weight, and speed. A full day of travel (8 hours moving) burns 2,500–4,000 calories beyond your resting metabolic rate.

**If food is limited:**
- Reduce daily travel distance
- Travel during cool hours to reduce water loss
- Minimize pack weight (every pound on your back costs extra calories per mile)
- Rest frequently — 10 minutes per hour of walking
- A human can cover roughly 5–10 miles per day on starvation rations if well-hydrated
- Without food, your effective travel range before incapacitation is roughly 3–5 days (depending on prior condition and terrain)

**Water budget:**
- Plan 1 liter of water per 2 hours of travel in moderate conditions
- In heat: 1 liter per hour
- Identify water sources along the route before departure. If the route has no water, carry enough or choose a different route.

## Pack Weight

### How Much Is Too Much

- **25% of body weight** is a reasonable maximum for sustained travel. For a 180 lb person, that's 45 lbs.
- **33%+ of body weight** significantly increases injury risk, slows travel dramatically, and burns excessive calories.
- Every pound you carry costs roughly 2% more energy per mile. Cutting 10 lbs from your pack over a 20-mile route saves significant calories and time.

### What to Carry

Prioritize in this order:
1. Water (or means to get it — purification/filter)
2. Navigation (map, compass)
3. Shelter (tarp, emergency blanket)
4. Fire starting (lighter, ferro rod, tinder)
5. First aid basics
6. Food (calorie-dense, lightweight — nuts, jerky, energy bars)
7. Knife/multitool
8. Signaling (whistle, mirror)

Leave everything else. Every item should justify its weight.

## River Crossings

Rivers are the most dangerous terrain feature for travelers. More people die in river crossings than from almost any other wilderness hazard.

### Assessment

1. **Don't cross if:** Water is above waist-deep and fast, the bottom isn't visible, debris is floating by, or the crossing is at a narrow point where water is channeled and fast.
2. **Look for a wide, shallow crossing.** Rivers are typically shallowest where they're widest. Look upstream and downstream for a better crossing point.
3. **Cross at a riffle** (a shallow area where water runs over gravel with visible ripples) rather than a pool (still, deep water).
4. **Never cross above a waterfall, rapids, or strainer** (fallen tree in the water that traps you against it).

### Crossing Technique

1. **Remove boots** and put them in your pack (or tie them around your neck). Crossing in boots fills them with water and adds weight. Wear camp shoes, sandals, or bare feet — you need to feel the bottom.
2. **Loosen your pack straps.** If you go under, you need to shed the pack instantly. An unbuckled hip belt and loosened shoulder straps let you dump it.
3. **Face upstream** and move sideways, one step at a time. Facing downstream, the current pushes against the back of your knees and folds you.
4. **Use a walking stick** or sturdy pole. Plant it upstream for a third point of contact. Tripod stance — always have two contact points while moving the third.
5. **Move diagonally downstream.** Ferry angle — aim upstream at 45 degrees while the current pushes you downstream. You end up roughly straight across.
6. **In a group:** Link arms or use a pole held between members. The strongest person goes upstream. Move as a unit.

### After a Fall

If you get swept away:
1. Let go of everything that isn't attached to you.
2. Roll onto your back, feet pointing downstream. Your feet are your bumpers — they hit rocks instead of your head.
3. Angle toward the near bank using backstroke-type arm movements.
4. Do NOT try to stand up in fast, knee-deep water — the current will fold you and push you under. Swim or scoot to shallow water, then stand.

## Night Movement

Avoid if possible. Night movement is slow, injury-prone, and navigating is difficult.

### When You Must

- Move at half your normal pace or slower.
- Use terrain features for guidance — follow a road, trail, ridgeline, or stream by feel and sound.
- Let your eyes dark-adapt for 20–30 minutes before moving. Avoid looking at any light source.
- Move from shadow to shadow, stopping to listen frequently.
- The ground ahead matters more than the distance ahead. Watch your feet, not the horizon.
- Stay together. Losing group members in the dark is easy and dangerous. Use a buddy system, verbal checks, or linked cord.

### Navigation at Night

- Use stars if visible (see navigation-without-tools.md).
- Follow handrail features by feel and sound — you can hear a stream even when you can't see it.
- Count paces for distance.
- If the terrain becomes too difficult or dangerous, stop and wait for daylight. A bad night bivouac is better than a broken ankle.

## Rest Planning

### The 50/10 Rule

Walk 50 minutes, rest 10 minutes. This rhythm is sustainable for a full day of travel. During rest stops: drink water, eat a small snack, check feet for blisters, check compass bearing, and reassess your route.

### Blisters

The number one travel-stopping injury. Prevent them:
- Break in boots before any major travel
- Keep feet dry (change socks during rest stops if wet)
- Apply moleskin or tape to hot spots BEFORE they become blisters
- If a blister forms: don't pop small ones. For large, painful blisters, sterilize a needle, drain from the base, leave the skin intact, cover with moleskin or tape
- A person with bad blisters travels at 50% speed or less. Prevention is everything.

### When to Stop for the Day

- 2 hours before dark in unfamiliar terrain (time to find a site, build shelter, start fire)
- When the weakest member is struggling (pushing past exhaustion causes injuries and bad decisions)
- When weather is deteriorating
- When you reach a good campsite with water — the next one might be hours away
