A map and compass are the most reliable navigation tools in existence. They don't need batteries, satellites, or signal. They work in any weather, any terrain, anywhere on Earth. Learning to use them well makes you functionally impossible to lose.

## Reading a Topographic Map

Topographic (topo) maps show terrain in three dimensions on a two-dimensional surface using contour lines.

### Contour Lines

Every contour line connects points of equal elevation. The vertical distance between consecutive contour lines (the contour interval) is printed in the map legend — commonly 20, 40, or 80 feet.

**Reading terrain from contour lines:**
- **Close together:** Steep slope. The closer the lines, the steeper the terrain.
- **Far apart:** Gentle slope or flat ground.
- **Concentric circles:** A hill or peak. The smallest circle is the summit.
- **V-shapes pointing uphill:** A valley or drainage. Water flows down the center of the V.
- **V-shapes pointing downhill:** A ridge or spur extending from higher ground.
- **Closed depression:** Concentric circles with hash marks pointing inward. This is a hole or depression, not a hill.

### Map Colors (Standard US Topo Maps)

- **Brown:** Contour lines (elevation)
- **Blue:** Water (streams, lakes, rivers, marshes)
- **Green:** Vegetation (forests, dense cover)
- **White (in green areas):** Clearings, open ground
- **Black:** Man-made features (buildings, roads, trails, boundaries)
- **Red/Pink:** Major roads, survey lines, urban areas

### Scale

The map scale tells you the relationship between map distance and real-world distance. Common scales:

- **1:24,000** (7.5-minute USGS quad) — 1 inch on the map = 2,000 feet on the ground. About 2.6 inches per mile. The most useful scale for foot navigation.
- **1:50,000** — 1 inch = about 4,167 feet. Military standard.
- **1:100,000** — Good for planning, not as useful for navigation on foot.

## Using a Compass

### Parts of a Baseplate Compass

- **Magnetic needle:** Red end points to magnetic north. Always. That's the only job it has.
- **Rotating bezel (housing):** The numbered ring (0–360 degrees) that rotates around the needle.
- **Orienting arrow:** Printed inside the bezel housing. You rotate the bezel to align this with the magnetic needle.
- **Direction of travel arrow:** Printed on the baseplate, pointing away from you. This is the direction you walk.
- **Index line:** Where the bezel meets the baseplate at the direction of travel arrow. Read your bearing here.
- **Baseplate:** Transparent for placing on a map. Has ruler markings for measuring map distances.

### Taking a Bearing (Direction to a Visible Target)

1. Hold the compass level in front of you.
2. Point the direction of travel arrow at your target (a mountain, a landmark, a tree).
3. Rotate the bezel until the orienting arrow aligns with the red end of the magnetic needle ("red in the shed" — put the red needle inside the red orienting arrow outline).
4. Read the bearing at the index line. This is the compass bearing to your target in degrees (0/360 = north, 90 = east, 180 = south, 270 = west).

### Walking a Bearing

1. Set your bearing on the compass (rotate the bezel to the desired number at the index line).
2. Hold the compass level in front of you. Rotate your body until the magnetic needle aligns with the orienting arrow (red in the shed).
3. The direction of travel arrow now points along your bearing. Walk that direction.
4. Pick a landmark ahead on your line of travel (a distinctive tree, rock, hilltop). Walk to it. Then take a new sighting along the same bearing to the next landmark. This prevents drift.

## Declination

**Magnetic north is not true north.** The magnetic pole is in northern Canada, not at the geographic North Pole. The difference between magnetic north (where your compass points) and true north (where map grid lines point) is called declination.

Declination varies by location and changes slowly over time. In the eastern US, declination is west (magnetic north is to the west of true north — add the declination to convert magnetic to true). In the western US, declination is east (subtract).

**The declination value is printed on every topo map** in the legend, along with the date. For older maps, the declination may have shifted — check the current value for your area if precision matters.

### Adjusting for Declination

**Map to field (you have a bearing from the map, need to walk it):**
- East declination: subtract from map bearing to get compass bearing
- West declination: add to map bearing to get compass bearing

**Field to map (you took a bearing with the compass, need to plot it on the map):**
- Reverse the above.

**Memory trick:** "East is least, west is best" — for map-to-field conversion, subtract east declination, add west.

Some compasses have adjustable declination — set it once and the compass automatically corrects. Worth the extra cost.

## Triangulation (Finding Your Position on a Map)

If you're lost but can see identifiable landmarks, you can find your location.

1. Identify two (preferably three) landmarks you can see AND find on the map. Mountain peaks, buildings, towers, river bends — anything distinct and mapped.
2. Take a compass bearing to the first landmark.
3. Convert the bearing to a true bearing (adjust for declination).
4. On the map, place the compass on the landmark. Rotate the map or draw a line from the landmark back along the reciprocal bearing (your bearing + or - 180 degrees). You are somewhere along this line.
5. Repeat with the second landmark. Where the two lines cross is your approximate position.
6. A third bearing narrows the fix. In practice, the three lines form a small triangle (the "cocked hat") — you're somewhere inside it.

## Pace Counting (Measuring Distance on Foot)

Pace counting tells you how far you've walked — critical for knowing where you are on a map.

### Establishing Your Pace Count

1. Measure a known distance — 100 meters (about 328 feet) or 100 yards. Use a football field, a measured trail, or pace off a mapped distance.
2. Walk the distance at your normal travel pace, counting every time your LEFT foot hits the ground (each count = two steps = one pace).
3. Do it three times and average the result. Most people get 62–68 paces per 100 meters on flat ground.
4. Record your pace count. Memorize it.

**Adjustments:**
- Uphill: add 5–10% to your pace count
- Thick vegetation: add 10–15%
- Snow, sand, or rough terrain: add 10–20%
- Night: add 5–10%
- Downhill: roughly the same as flat

### Using Pace Count

When walking a bearing, count your paces. Every time you hit your 100-meter count, note it (move a bead on ranger beads, transfer a pebble from one pocket to another, or just keep a mental tally). Cross-reference with the map — if the map shows a stream crossing 400 meters ahead and you've counted 4 pace sets, you should be at the stream.

## Putting It All Together — Route Planning

1. Study the map before you move. Identify your start point, destination, and key terrain features along the way.
2. Plan your route following terrain features where possible — ridgelines, stream valleys, trails, fences. These are "handrails" that keep you on course without constant compass work.
3. Identify "catching features" — large terrain features you can't miss that tell you if you've gone too far. A river, road, or ridge line perpendicular to your direction of travel catches you if you overshoot.
4. Take bearings to key waypoints along the route. Write them down.
5. Estimate distance between waypoints using the map scale.
6. Move waypoint to waypoint, using bearing and pace count between features.
7. Constantly cross-check your position against the terrain: "The map shows a saddle between two hills here. Do I see that?" If what you see matches what the map shows, you're on track.

## When You're Lost

1. Stop. Don't keep walking — you'll get more lost.
2. Think about your last known position. When were you last sure of your location?
3. Check the map against your surroundings. Can you identify any terrain feature — a stream, a ridge, a clearing — and find it on the map?
4. If you can identify even one feature, take a bearing to it and plot it on the map. You're somewhere along that line.
5. If you can't identify anything, consider backtracking to your last known position.
6. If backtracking isn't possible, use terrain to navigate to a catching feature — a road, river, or ridge that you can identify on the map.
7. Follow drainages downhill — they lead to streams, which lead to rivers, which lead to civilization in most populated regions.
