Feet are your primary transportation. Bad footwear means blisters, infections, reduced mobility, and in cold weather, frostbite. Keeping your shoes functional is a higher priority than it sounds — a person with destroyed boots is a person who can't walk, can't work, and becomes a burden on the group.

## Emergency Sole Reattachment

The most common catastrophic shoe failure: the sole separates from the upper. In modern shoes, the sole is glued — and glue fails.

### Quick Field Fix

1. Clean both surfaces — scrape off old glue, dirt, and debris with a knife.
2. Apply contact cement, shoe goo, or any flexible adhesive to both surfaces. If you have none: pine pitch (see **adhesives-and-sealants.md**) works as a temporary bond.
3. Let both surfaces tack up for 5–10 minutes (contact cement bonds on contact, so both surfaces need to be partially dry).
4. Press together firmly. Starting at the toe, work toward the heel, pressing out air bubbles.
5. Wrap the shoe tightly with cord, rubber bands, or duct tape to hold the sole under pressure while the adhesive cures. Leave overnight.

### Permanent Fix — Sewing

Glue alone fails again under stress. For a lasting repair, stitch through the sole and upper.

1. Glue first (as above) to hold alignment.
2. Use an awl (or a nail heated in a fire) to punch holes through both the sole and the upper, about 1/2 inch from the edge, spaced 1/2 inch apart.
3. Stitch through the holes using heavy waxed thread, artificial sinew, or dental floss (dental floss is surprisingly strong). Use a saddle stitch: two needles, one on each end of the thread, going through each hole from opposite directions and crossing in the middle of each hole. This creates a lockstitch that doesn't unravel if one stitch breaks.
4. Pull each stitch tight. The stitching should be snug but not so tight it tears through the material.
5. Knot the ends and tuck them between the sole and upper.

## Waterproofing

Wet feet lead to blisters, fungal infections, trench foot, and cold injuries. Keeping boots waterproof is constant maintenance.

### For Leather Boots

- **Mink oil or neatsfoot oil:** Apply with fingers, working into the leather, focusing on seams. Let absorb overnight. Makes leather supple and water-resistant. Reapply every few weeks with heavy use.
- **Beeswax:** Rub a block of beeswax over the entire boot surface. Heat with a hair dryer, heat gun, or by holding near a fire (carefully — don't scorch). The wax melts into the leather pores. Excellent water resistance.
- **Rendered tallow (animal fat):** The oldest boot waterproofing. Rub into leather thoroughly. Less durable than wax but readily available. Goes rancid eventually — reapply regularly.
- **Beeswax + oil blend:** Melt 2 parts beeswax with 1 part oil (olive, neatsfoot, or any available oil). Brush onto warm leather. Best overall DIY waterproofing.

### For Fabric/Synthetic Boots

These are harder to waterproof. Options:
- Silicone spray (if available)
- Duct tape over particularly worn areas
- Beeswax rubbed and heated into the fabric
- Plastic bags worn inside the boot as a vapor barrier (uncomfortable but dry)

### Seam Sealing

Most leaks come through seams, not the material itself. Run a bead of adhesive (shoe goo, silicone, pine pitch) along every external seam. For stitched seams, apply melted beeswax into the stitch holes with a finger.

## Insole Replacement

Worn insoles cause hot spots, blisters, and foot pain. Replacement options:

1. **Cardboard:** Trace your foot on cardboard, cut to shape. Temporary but better than nothing. Replace when it compresses flat.
2. **Felt:** Wool felt from an old blanket or hat makes an excellent insole. Cut to shape.
3. **Leather:** A piece of thin leather (2–3mm) custom-cut. The best long-term replacement — it molds to your foot and lasts.
4. **Grass or moss:** In an emergency, stuff the bottom of the shoe with dry grass, moss, or cattail fluff. Provides cushioning and insulation. Replace daily — it compresses and gets damp.
5. **Foam pad (salvaged):** Yoga mats, sleeping pads, or packing foam cut to shape.

## Lace Replacement

When laces break:
- Paracord (best — strong, long-lasting, and the inner strands can be extracted for other uses)
- Leather strips cut thin (1/4 inch wide, cut from any scrap)
- Braided plant fiber (see **rope-and-cordage.md**)
- Dental floss braided thick
- Wire (last resort — uncomfortable and abrasive)

## Making Moccasins

When manufactured shoes are gone, moccasins are the simplest functional footwear to make from raw materials.

### Simple One-Piece Moccasin

1. **Material:** Brain-tanned leather is ideal (soft, flexible). Any soft leather works — salvaged garment leather, thin rawhide softened by working, or thick fabric in a pinch.
2. **Pattern:** Stand on the leather and trace your foot with 2 inches of extra margin all around. This is your sole and sides in one piece.
3. **Cut out** the pattern.
4. **Fold up** the leather around your foot. The extra margin becomes the sides.
5. **Gather and stitch** the toe: pinch the front together, fold the excess into small pleats, and stitch closed with an overcast stitch using heavy thread or sinew.
6. **Stitch the heel:** Bring the back edges together, overlap slightly, and stitch.
7. **Add a cuff** if desired: stitch a strip of leather around the ankle opening. This keeps debris out and provides a place for laces.
8. **Punch lace holes** around the ankle opening. Thread a lace to cinch the moccasin around the foot.

### Adding a Sole

Moccasins wear through quickly on rocky or rough terrain. Add a separate harder sole:

1. Cut a sole piece from thicker leather (heavy rawhide, thick cowhide, or even a piece of rubber tire tread).
2. Trace your foot for the sole shape. Cut about 1/4 inch larger than your foot.
3. Stitch the sole to the bottom of the moccasin using an awl and heavy thread. Saddle stitch for durability. Keep stitches close to the edge and tight.
4. Seal the seam with pitch or beeswax for water resistance.

## Blister Prevention and Treatment

Blisters are the most common foot problem and can be completely disabling if neglected.

### Prevention

1. **Proper fit.** Shoes that are too tight create pressure. Shoes that are too loose create friction from sliding. With socks on, you should be able to wiggle your toes and have about a thumb's width between your longest toe and the front of the shoe.
2. **Break in boots gradually.** New boots cause blisters because the leather hasn't molded to your foot yet. Wear them for increasing periods over 1–2 weeks before relying on them for heavy use.
3. **Dry socks.** Change socks at least once a day if possible. Wet socks dramatically increase friction and blister risk.
4. **Address hot spots immediately.** If you feel a hot spot (warmth and mild burning), stop and apply tape, moleskin, or a smooth bandage over the area before a blister forms. Once a blister develops, treatment is longer and more painful.
5. **Sock choice.** Wool socks (even in summer) wick moisture better than cotton. Cotton holds water against the skin and multiplies blister risk.

### Treatment

**Intact blister (fluid-filled):**
1. Clean the area with soap and water or antiseptic.
2. If the blister is small and not painful, leave it intact — the fluid is sterile and the skin roof protects against infection. Cover with a bandage.
3. If it's large, in a weight-bearing area, or preventing walking: sterilize a needle (flame, alcohol), puncture at the base of the blister (lowest point), gently press fluid out through the hole. Don't remove the skin roof — it's a natural bandage.
4. Apply antibiotic ointment if available. Cover with a bandage.
5. Cut a donut-shaped pad from moleskin or folded cloth and place it around (not on) the blister. This lifts the shoe pressure off the damaged skin.

**Open blister (skin torn):**
1. Clean with clean water.
2. Trim any loose dead skin with clean scissors — hanging skin catches and tears further.
3. Apply antibiotic ointment. Cover with a non-stick bandage.
4. Change the dressing daily. Watch for infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, or red streaks spreading from the wound.

> **Your boots are a survival tool, not an accessory.** Maintain them as seriously as you'd maintain a weapon or a water filter. Check for sole separation weekly. Waterproof monthly. Address hot spots and loose fits immediately. In a collapse scenario, a new pair of good boots is one of the most valuable items you can find.
