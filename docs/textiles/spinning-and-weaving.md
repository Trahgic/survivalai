Making cloth from raw fiber is slow. There's no getting around that. But it's how every civilization clothed itself before industrial mills, and the basic tools are simple enough to build from scrap wood. If manufactured clothing runs out and can't be replaced, spinning and weaving are how you keep people covered.

## Spinning — Fiber to Yarn

Spinning twists loose fibers into a continuous thread or yarn. The twist locks the fibers together by friction. More twist = stronger thread (to a point).

### Drop Spindle (Simplest Tool)

A weighted stick that spins like a top, twisting fiber as it rotates. People have used these for at least 9,000 years. You can build one in 10 minutes.

**Building a drop spindle:**
1. Find a straight stick or dowel, about 12 inches long and 1/4–3/8 inch diameter.
2. Make a whorl (weight): a flat disk with a hole in the center. A thick wooden disk (3–4 inches diameter, 1/4–1/2 inch thick), a clay disk, a flat stone with a drilled hole, or even a CD all work.
3. Push the stick through the center of the whorl so it sits 1–2 inches from the bottom. Friction-fit or glue in place.
4. Cut a small notch or drive a tiny hook into the top of the stick. This catches the yarn.

**Spinning technique:**
1. Tie a leader yarn (an 18-inch piece of existing yarn or strong string) around the shaft just above the whorl. Bring it up and through the hook at the top.
2. Take a prepared fiber bundle (roving — a loosely organized strip of clean, carded fiber). Overlap the end of the fiber with the leader yarn.
3. Give the spindle a clockwise spin (drop it — it hangs in the air spinning).
4. As it spins, draft the fiber — pull a thin amount from the roving and let the twist travel up into it. The twist locks the fibers together.
5. Don't let the twist go into the undrafted fiber supply — pinch at the junction to control twist travel.
6. When the spindle reaches the floor (or slows too much), stop. Wind the spun yarn around the shaft above the whorl. Loop through the hook again, and resume spinning.
7. Practice. A lot. The first yarn will be lumpy, uneven, and frustrating. After a few hours, it gets smoother.

**Speed:** An experienced spindle spinner produces about 100–200 yards per hour of medium-weight yarn. That's enough for a pair of socks per week.

### Plying

Single-ply yarn is weak and tends to curl. Plying — twisting two or more singles together in the opposite direction — makes stronger, more balanced yarn.

1. Wind two bobbins or balls of singles yarn.
2. Spin the spindle counterclockwise while feeding both strands together.
3. The opposing twist locks them into a round, strong, balanced yarn.

## Weaving — Yarn to Cloth

Weaving interlocks two sets of threads at right angles: warp (lengthwise, under tension) and weft (crosswise, woven through).

### Simple Frame Loom

The easiest loom to build. Produces narrow fabric suitable for scarves, bands, straps, and small panels.

1. Build a rectangular frame from lumber — any sturdy wood. Size: 12–24 inches wide, 24–36 inches tall for a starter loom.
2. Drive small nails or screws along the top and bottom edges, 1/4 inch apart. These are the warp guides.
3. String the warp: tie yarn to the first nail, carry it to the corresponding bottom nail, around, back up to the next top nail, and so on. Keep tension firm and even.
4. Weave the weft: thread a shuttle (a flat stick with yarn wound around it, or just a blunt needle) over and under alternating warp threads. At the end of the row, go around the edge warp and return, going under the threads you went over and vice versa.
5. Push each weft row snug against the previous one with a comb, fork, or your fingers.
6. Continue until the loom is full.

### Backstrap Loom

Used across Central and South America for thousands of years. One end of the warp ties to a tree or post, the other to a belt around the weaver's waist. Body tension controls the warp. No frame needed — completely portable. Can produce fabric up to about 18 inches wide.

### Rigid Heddle Loom

A step up from a frame loom. The rigid heddle is a frame with alternating slots and holes that lifts half the warp threads at once, creating a "shed" (opening) for the weft to pass through. This doubles weaving speed.

Rigid heddle looms can be purchased or built from wood. They produce usable fabric up to about 24 inches wide.

### Floor Loom

The traditional large loom with foot-operated treadles that lift warp threads in complex patterns. Produces wide fabric quickly. Building one is a significant woodworking project but well within the capability of a competent carpenter with hand tools.

## Fiber Preparation

Raw fiber needs processing before spinning:

**Wool:** Shear the sheep → wash the fleece (hot soapy water to remove lanolin and dirt, rinse thoroughly) → pick (open up clumps by hand) → card (pull between two paddles covered with wire teeth to align fibers into a smooth roving). Carding is tedious but essential for even spinning.

**Cotton:** Pick bolls → remove seeds (a cotton gin is ideal; by hand is extremely tedious) → card into rolags.

**Plant fibers (flax, hemp):** See **natural-fibers.md** for the retting and processing steps.

## Fabric Types

**Plain weave:** Over one, under one. The simplest and strongest. Most work clothing historically was plain weave.

**Twill weave:** Over two, under one (or variations), offset each row. Creates diagonal lines. Produces a denser, more durable fabric. Denim and canvas are twills.

**Tabby:** Another name for plain weave.

## Realistic Expectations

Making cloth by hand is extremely labor-intensive. Before the Industrial Revolution, producing one shirt's worth of fabric (about 3 yards of 45-inch wide cloth) took roughly 50–100 hours of combined spinning and weaving time. That's why clothing was precious — a single shirt represented weeks of work.

Focus repair and preservation skills first. Reserve spinning and weaving for long-term situations where manufactured textiles are truly unavailable.

> **Start with spinning.** Even if you never weave, handspun yarn is useful for knitting, crocheting, repair thread, lacing, and cordage. A drop spindle fits in a pocket and costs nothing to make. Practice spinning whenever you have idle time.
