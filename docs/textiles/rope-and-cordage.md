Rope is one of the most fundamental tools humans ever developed. You need it for shelter construction, snares, fishing, climbing, hauling, tying, lashing, tool hafting, clotheslines, and a hundred other daily tasks. When commercial rope and paracord run out, you make your own from whatever fibrous material is available.

## How Cordage Works

Cordage is fibers twisted together. The twist locks the fibers by friction — each fiber is held in place by the pressure of its neighbors. More twist = more strength (up to a point — overtwisting makes cordage brittle and kinky).

**Two-ply cordage** is the standard: two bundles of fiber twisted individually in one direction, then twisted around each other in the opposite direction. The opposing twists lock together so the rope holds itself.

## Reverse Wrap Method (The Core Technique)

This is the most common hand method for making two-ply cordage. Once you learn it, you can make rope from any fiber source.

1. **Prepare fibers.** Gather long, flexible fibers. Strip, soak, or pound them as needed to separate individual fibers. Longer fibers make stronger rope with fewer splices.
2. **Start the twist.** Take a bundle of fibers about twice the desired thickness of one ply. Find the center (or slightly off-center — offset is fine). Twist the center tightly until it kinks and folds over on itself, creating a loop. This loop is your starting point.
3. **Hold the kinked loop** between the thumb and forefinger of your non-dominant hand. You now have two legs (bundles) hanging down.
4. **Twist the far leg** (the one farthest from you) away from you (clockwise if using your right hand) with your dominant hand. Give it several tight twists.
5. **Wrap it toward you** — bring the twisted leg over and in front of the near leg, so the legs swap positions.
6. **Now the other leg is the far leg.** Twist it away from you, then wrap toward you.
7. **Repeat** — twist, wrap, twist, wrap. The individual plies are twisted clockwise, but the two plies wrap around each other counterclockwise. These opposing forces hold the rope together.
8. **Maintain consistent tension** and twist. If one ply gets loose, the rope weakens at that point.

### Adding Fiber (Splicing In)

No fiber lasts forever. When a ply gets thin, you need to splice in new material:

1. Before the current fiber runs out completely, lay a new bundle of fiber alongside the thinning ply, overlapping by at least 3–4 inches.
2. Continue twisting — the new fiber integrates into the existing ply. The friction of the twist holds the splice.
3. Don't splice both plies at the same point. Offset splices by at least 2 inches so you never have two weak spots in the same place.
4. The splice point will be slightly thicker. With practice, you can taper the fibers so the transition is smooth.

## Fiber Sources

| Rawhide strips | Excellent | From any animal hide | Cut thin strips, twist |
| Sinew | Excellent | From any large animal | Dry, pound, separate fibers |

### Identification Tips

Look for plants with long, stringy fibers in their stems or bark. Test by breaking a dried stem — if stringy fibers hold the pieces together instead of snapping cleanly, that plant is a cordage candidate. The longer the fibers, the fewer splices needed, and the stronger the finished rope.

### Processing Plant Fibers

Most plant fibers need some preparation:

1. **Harvest at the right time.** Most bast fibers are strongest when the plant is mature but before the stem gets woody. Late summer to fall for most temperate plants.
2. **Dry the stems.** Hang or lay flat for 1–2 weeks.
3. **Ret (optional but helpful).** Soak dried stems in water for several days to loosen the outer bark and woody core from the usable inner fibers. Check daily — over-retting weakens fiber.
4. **Break and separate.** Crush the stems by rolling over a hard edge, then peel away the outer bark and snap out the woody pith. The remaining inner bark fibers are your cordage material.
5. **Soften.** Roll the fibers between your palms or pound them gently to separate individual strands and make them more flexible.

## Three-Ply Rope

Stronger and rounder than two-ply. Made by twisting three plies together. Use the same reverse-wrap principle but manage three legs instead of two. It takes more practice but produces superior rope for heavy-duty applications.

## Braided Cordage

Braiding (plaiting) three or more strands produces flat, flexible cordage that doesn't kink. Standard three-strand braid: right over center, left over center, repeat. Braided cordage is easier to make than twisted but generally weaker per diameter.

Four-strand round braid produces round cordage. More complex to learn but produces excellent quality.

## Rope from Scavenged Materials

In an urban or semi-urban environment, don't overlook manufactured materials:

- **Plastic bags:** Cut into strips (cut in a spiral for one continuous strip from each bag), stretch slightly to make them curl into a round cord, then twist or braid. Surprisingly strong.
- **Clothing strips:** Cut old cotton t-shirts into 1-inch strips. Braid or twist three strips together. Not as strong as plant fiber but readily available.
- **Electrical wire:** Strip the insulation, use the copper core for fine lashing. Bundle multiple strands for thicker cordage.
- **Zip ties and cable ties:** For temporary lashing. Not true rope but useful for quick fastening.
- **Duct tape:** Twist into a rope. Tear a long strip, twist tightly, and it forms a functional cord. Not durable long-term but strong in the short term.
- **Dental floss:** Surprisingly strong. Braid multiple strands for usable light cordage.

## Testing Cordage Strength

Before trusting your life to handmade rope:

1. Tie it to a fixed point at chest height.
2. Pull progressively harder while watching for:
   - Individual fiber failure (strands popping)
   - Splice slippage (plies pulling apart at a splice point)
   - Overall elongation (stretching beyond useful length)
3. Double the required strength for any application involving human weight. If you need to support 150 lbs, the rope should comfortably hold 300 lbs.

**Never use natural cordage for life-safety climbing without extensive testing.** Natural rope is variable in quality, degrades with moisture and UV, and fails without warning. Use commercial rated climbing rope for anything involving significant falls.

## Care and Storage

- **Dry before storing.** Wet natural fiber cordage rots within days.
- **Coil loosely.** Tight coils under tension cause kinks and weak points.
- **Keep out of direct sunlight** when not in use. UV degrades plant fibers.
- **Inspect before critical use.** Run the rope through your hands, feeling for thin spots, fiber damage, and weakened sections.
- **Replace regularly.** Natural cordage has a limited lifespan — weeks to months of active use depending on conditions. When it starts feeling dry, brittle, or significantly thinner than when made, replace it.

> **Make cordage whenever you have idle time.** Sitting around a fire? Twist cordage. On watch? Process fibers. Cordage is always useful, always consumed, and never in surplus for long. Building a stock of ready-made rope means you have it when you need it — not spending an hour making it while your shelter is blowing apart.
