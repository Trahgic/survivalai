When you can't buy new clothes, you fix the ones you have. A repaired shirt lasts years. An ignored tear becomes an unwearable rag in weeks. Basic sewing is one of the most practical skills you can have — it takes 10 minutes to learn the stitches and a lifetime to appreciate how much clothing you save.

## Essential Sewing Kit

Keep this together in a small waterproof container:

- Needles (assorted sizes — small for fine fabric, large for heavy canvas and leather)
- Thread (heavy-duty polyester is strongest; cotton works but breaks more easily)
- Scissors or a razor blade
- Pins or safety pins
- Buttons (assorted sizes — salvage from worn-out clothing)
- Patches (denim, canvas, or any heavy fabric scraps)
- Thimble (saves your fingers when pushing through heavy fabric)
- Seam ripper (or use a razor blade carefully)

If you don't have a commercial kit, improvised sewing needles can be made from thorns, bone slivers, or wire (file a point, flatten the eye end, drill or punch a tiny hole for thread). Improvised thread: dental floss, fishing line, thin wire, or plant fiber cordage.

## Basic Stitches

### Running Stitch

The simplest stitch. Push the needle in and out of the fabric in a straight line, making evenly spaced stitches. Used for temporary repairs, gathering fabric, and basic seams.

Works for: quick fixes, hems, closing simple tears along a straight line.

Weakness: not very strong. The thread pulls through under stress. Use for low-stress areas only.

### Backstitch

The strongest hand stitch. After each forward stitch, you go backward and insert the needle at the end of the previous stitch, creating a continuous line with no gaps.

1. Push needle up through fabric.
2. Insert it one stitch-length BEHIND where it came up.
3. Bring it up one stitch-length AHEAD of where it first came up.
4. Insert it back at the point where the thread first came up.
5. Repeat — each stitch overlaps the previous one.

Works for: structural seams, areas under stress, replacing machine stitching on seams that have come apart.

### Whip Stitch (Overcast)

Used to join two edges of fabric together, especially when reattaching patches or closing the edges of a tear that's been pulled apart.

1. Hold the two edges together.
2. Push the needle through both layers from back to front.
3. Bring the thread over the edge and push through again, 1/4 inch along.
4. Repeat. The thread wraps over the edge in a spiral pattern.

### Blanket Stitch

Decorative and functional edge stitch that prevents fraying on cut fabric edges. Also used to attach patches.

1. Bring needle up through fabric from behind, 1/4 inch from the edge.
2. Loop the thread under the needle point before pulling through.
3. Pull snug — the thread creates a neat interlocking loop along the edge.
4. Repeat, spacing stitches 1/4 inch apart.

## Common Repairs

### Patching a Hole

1. Cut a patch 1 inch larger than the hole in all directions. Round the corners — square corners catch and peel.
2. Pin the patch over the hole on the inside of the garment (for strength) or outside (for visibility — some people prefer the aesthetic).
3. Fold under 1/4 inch of the patch edges for a clean finish, or leave raw if using non-fraying fabric like canvas.
4. Stitch around the patch edge using a running stitch, backstitch, or blanket stitch. Place stitches 1/4 inch from the patch edge and 1/4 inch apart.
5. For heavy-use areas (knees, elbows, seat), use iron-on patches if available, or sew with a double row of stitches.

### Repairing a Seam

Seams split when the thread breaks but the fabric is still intact. This is the easiest repair.

1. Turn the garment inside out to access the seam.
2. Starting 1/2 inch before the split, backstitch along the original seam line.
3. Continue through the split and 1/2 inch past the other end.
4. Tie off with 2–3 knots by looping the thread through the last stitch.

### Replacing a Button

1. Position the button where it was (or over the buttonhole to check alignment).
2. Thread a needle with doubled thread. Knot the end.
3. Push up through the fabric and through one hole in the button. Pull through.
4. Push down through the next hole and through the fabric.
5. Repeat through both holes 4–6 times. For a 4-hole button, make an X pattern or parallel lines.
6. Before tying off, wrap the thread around the thread shank (the thread bundle between button and fabric) 3–4 times. This creates a gap that lets the buttonhole slide over the button. Tie off on the back side.

### Zipper Fix

**Slider won't close the teeth:** The slider is worn and not pressing the teeth together tightly enough. Gently squeeze both sides of the slider with pliers to tighten. Don't overdo it — you'll crack the slider.

**Teeth separating behind the slider:** Same fix — the slider needs tightening.

**Missing teeth:** The zipper is done. Replace it if you can (seam-rip the old one out, sew in a replacement) or convert to buttons.

**Slider pulled off:** Thread it back onto the teeth from the bottom. If the metal stop at the bottom is missing, add a few stitches of heavy thread across the teeth at the bottom to act as a new stop.

### Darning (Repairing Worn Fabric)

For areas where fabric has worn thin or developed small holes — common in socks, elbows, and knees.

1. Stretch the thin/holed area over a smooth round object (a darning egg, a light bulb, a ball, or a smooth rock).
2. Using a needle and thread, make parallel rows of running stitch across the damaged area, extending 1/2 inch into strong fabric on all sides.
3. Turn 90 degrees and weave a second set of parallel stitches perpendicular to the first, going over and under alternating threads. This creates a woven patch of thread that fills the damaged area.
4. Keep stitches close together — you're essentially weaving new fabric into the existing garment.

## Extending Clothing Life

- **Reinforce stress points before they fail.** Double-stitch crotch seams, add knee patches to work pants before they wear through, reinforce button areas.
- **Wash less frequently.** Every wash cycle degrades fabric. Spot-clean stains. Air clothes between wearings. Reserve full washing for when they're actually dirty.
- **Dry out of direct sun.** UV degrades most fabrics over time. Line-dry in shade when possible.
- **Store clean and dry.** Dirty fabric attracts insects. Damp fabric molds.
- **Layer rather than replacing.** A worn shirt under a jacket is fine. Save the good clothing for the outer layer.

> **Thread and needles are critical supplies.** A single spool of heavy-duty thread and a packet of assorted needles weighs almost nothing and extends the life of your entire wardrobe by years. Stock extras. They're irreplaceable once they're gone and nearly impossible to manufacture from scratch.
