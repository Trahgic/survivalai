Dyeing isn't just cosmetic. Color-coding clothing by role (medical, security, work crew) helps organization. Camouflage matters for hunting and security. And frankly, wearing the same faded gray clothes for months kills morale. Natural dyes are free, available everywhere plants grow, and work well on natural fibers.

## How Dyeing Works

Dye molecules bond to fiber. Some dyes bond directly (substantive dyes). Most need a mordant — a chemical bridge that locks the dye to the fiber. Without a mordant, most plant dyes wash out in the first rain.

Natural fibers take dye well: wool, cotton, linen, hemp. Synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon) don't absorb natural dyes at all.

## Mordants

### Alum (Potassium Aluminum Sulfate)

The most common and safest mordant. Produces bright, clear colors. Available at pharmacies (sold as a pickling spice), or extracted from certain clay deposits.

**Use:** Dissolve 2–4 tablespoons of alum per gallon of water. Simmer fabric in the mordant bath for 1 hour. Remove, wring gently, proceed to dyeing. Don't rinse between mordanting and dyeing.

### Iron (Ferrous Sulfate)

Darkens and "saddens" colors — greens become deeper, yellows shift toward olive, reds toward brown. Creates a chemical reaction with tannins in the dye.

**Improvised iron mordant:** Fill a jar with rusty nails, steel wool, or iron scraps. Cover with a 50/50 mix of water and vinegar. Let sit 1–2 weeks. The resulting rust-water is your iron mordant.

**Use:** Add 1–2 teaspoons per gallon of dye bath, or dip mordanted fabric in the iron solution after dyeing.

### Copper

Shifts colors toward green/blue tones. Made by soaking copper scraps (pennies, wire, pipe) in vinegar for 2 weeks.

### Tannin

Not technically a mordant but acts as one for cotton and linen, which don't absorb mordants as well as wool. Pre-soak cotton in a tannin bath (strong tea, oak bark decoction, or sumac berry solution) before mordanting with alum.

## Dye Sources

### Browns and Tans

| Source | Color | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Black walnut hulls | Rich dark brown | The strongest natural dye. Stains everything permanently — no mordant needed. Use green hulls for darkest color. |
| Oak bark | Warm tan to brown | Decoct bark 1–2 hours |
| Tea (black) | Warm tan | Simple steep. Multiple dippings for deeper color |
| Coffee grounds | Brown | Simmer grounds in water. Moderate colorfastness |
| Onion skins (brown) | Warm brown | Outer papery skins, simmer 30 min |

### Yellows and Golds

| Source | Color | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Onion skins (yellow) | Bright gold | Yellow onion skins with alum mordant. One of the most reliable natural dyes |
| Turmeric | Bright yellow | Strong color but fades in sunlight. Good for items stored indoors |
| Goldenrod flowers | Yellow-gold | Harvest in full bloom, simmer 1 hour |
| Marigold flowers | Yellow | Use fresh or dried. Alum mordant |
| Dandelion flowers | Pale yellow | Large quantity needed. Alum mordant |

### Reds and Pinks

| Source | Color | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Pokeberry | Pink to magenta | Use ripe berries. Fades over time. Alum mordant |
| Beet | Pink-red | Looks dramatic in the dye bath but fades significantly. More of a stain than a true dye |
| Madder root | True red to rust | The classic historical red dye. Grow it — perennial, harvest roots after 3 years. Alum mordant. Heat slowly, never boil |
| Avocado pits and skins | Soft pink | Surprisingly good. Simmer pits/skins 1 hour. Alum mordant |

### Blues and Purples

| Source | Color | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Indigo (Indigofera or woad) | Deep blue | The only reliable natural blue. Requires a fermentation vat — complex chemistry but incredible results. Historical importance can't be overstated |
| Red cabbage | Blue-purple | pH dependent: acidic = pink, alkaline = blue-green. Fades fast |
| Elderberry | Purple-gray | Use ripe berries. Alum and iron mordants produce different shades |
| Blueberry | Purple-blue | Fades. Better than nothing for temporary color |

### Greens

True green is hard to achieve naturally. The best method: dye yellow first (onion skins, goldenrod), then overdye with indigo blue. The combination produces a reliable green.

Iron mordant on goldenrod or other yellow dyes also shifts toward olive green.

### Black

Black is the hardest natural color. Methods:

1. **Iron + tannin:** Soak fabric in a strong tannin bath (oak galls, sumac, or oak bark), then dip in iron mordant solution. Multiple cycles deepen the color toward black.
2. **Black walnut hulls** with extended soaking produce very dark brown approaching black.
3. **Logwood** (if available) with iron mordant produces true black. Historical standard for black cloth.

## The Dyeing Process

### Preparation

1. **Scour the fabric.** Wash thoroughly with soap to remove oils, dirt, and sizing that block dye absorption. For wool: hot soapy water, don't agitate (wool felts with heat + agitation). For cotton/linen: boil in soapy water for 30 minutes.
2. **Mordant.** Simmer in mordant solution for 1 hour (see above). Some dyers mordant overnight at a lower temperature for deeper penetration.
3. **Make the dye bath.** Chop or crush plant material. Simmer in water for 30–60 minutes (don't boil — high heat browns most dyes). Strain out plant material. You want a concentrated liquid several shades darker than your target color.

### Dyeing

1. Wet the mordanted fabric (dye penetrates wet fabric more evenly).
2. Submerge in the dye bath. Push down to eliminate air bubbles.
3. Heat gently to a simmer (not a boil). Stir occasionally with a stick.
4. Simmer for 30–60 minutes. For deeper color, leave fabric in the cooling dye bath overnight.
5. Remove, wring gently, rinse in cool water until water runs mostly clear.
6. Dry in shade. Direct sunlight fades fresh dye.

### Colorfastness Tips

- Alum mordant produces the most lightfast results with most dyes.
- An afterbath of vinegar (for protein fibers like wool) or salt water (for plant fibers like cotton) can improve wash-fastness slightly.
- Some dyes are inherently fugitive (they fade no matter what): beet, blueberry, red cabbage, turmeric in sunlight. Use these for items that won't get heavy sun exposure.
- The most permanent natural dyes: walnut (needs no mordant), madder (with alum), indigo (chemically bonds differently from other dyes), and oak gall/iron black.

## Printing and Resist Dyeing

**Block printing:** Carve a design into a flat piece of wood. Dip the carved surface in thickened dye paste (dye mixed with flour paste or gum), press onto fabric.

**Resist dyeing (batik concept):** Apply melted beeswax to areas you want to remain undyed. Dye the fabric. Remove the wax by scraping and/or ironing between layers of absorbent cloth. The waxed areas retain the original color.

**Tie-dye:** Tie, fold, or bunch fabric with string before dyeing. Tied areas resist dye penetration, creating patterns. Simple but effective for visual variety.

> **Prioritize function over beauty.** Dyeing for camouflage (brown, green, tan from walnut, goldenrod + iron) and role identification (colored arm bands, headbands) has immediate tactical value. Decorative dyeing is a morale booster when basic needs are met. Both matter — just in different phases.
