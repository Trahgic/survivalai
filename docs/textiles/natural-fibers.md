When manufactured thread, yarn, and rope run out, natural fibers are your replacement. They grow everywhere. The skill is knowing which plants produce usable fiber and how to process it.

## Plant Fibers

### Flax (Linen)

The premier plant fiber for clothing. Linen is strong, cool against skin, dries fast, and gets softer with use. Flax grows in temperate climates and matures in about 100 days.

**Processing:**
1. Harvest when the lower stems turn yellow and seeds are brown. Pull entire plants by the roots — don't cut (cutting wastes stem length).
2. Remove seeds by pulling stems through a coarse comb (rippling).
3. **Ret the stems.** This is the critical step — retting is controlled rotting that loosens the outer bark and pectin glue so the inner fibers can be separated. Two methods:
   - **Dew retting:** Spread stems thinly on a field or grass for 2–4 weeks. Turn every few days. Morning dew and rain promote bacterial action that breaks down the pectin. Done when the bark peels easily from the fiber.
   - **Water retting:** Bundle stems and submerge in a pond, stream, or trough for 5–10 days. Faster and more consistent, but it stinks and pollutes the water. Check daily — over-retting weakens the fiber.
4. Dry the retted stems thoroughly.
5. **Break** the stems — crush them with a wooden brake (a hinged pair of boards) to fragment the woody core.
6. **Scutch** — scrape the broken core material away from the long fibers using a wooden blade or dull knife.
7. **Hackle** — comb through a row of upright nails or pins (a hackle) to separate and align the fibers. The long, smooth fibers that pass through are "line" — the highest quality. The shorter tangles left behind are "tow" — usable for rougher yarn and cordage.
8. Spin the fiber on a drop spindle or spinning wheel.

### Hemp

Very similar processing to flax. Produces strong, slightly coarser fiber. Historically used for rope, canvas, sacking, and durable work clothing. Where legal to grow, hemp is one of the highest fiber-yield plants per acre.

### Stinging Nettle

Often overlooked but produces excellent fiber similar to flax. Nettles grow wild throughout temperate regions, often abundantly along streams, disturbed ground, and forest edges. The stinging hairs neutralize with drying, boiling, or processing.

**Processing:** Harvest mature stems (late summer). Ret, break, scutch, and hackle same as flax. Nettle fiber is fine, strong, and silky when well-processed.

### Bark Fibers

The inner bark (bast) of many trees produces usable fiber for cordage, rough weaving, and lashing:

- **Basswood (linden):** Peel strips of inner bark, soak in water for weeks, separate fibers. Makes strong, flexible cordage.
- **Tulip poplar:** Similar to basswood.
- **Cedar bark:** Western red cedar bark was the primary fiber source for Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples — clothing, baskets, rope, mats.
- **Mulberry:** Inner bark produces paper-like material (tapa cloth in Pacific Island cultures).

### Cattail and Rushes

Cattail leaves can be woven into mats, baskets, and rough fabric when fresh and flexible. Dried cattail fluff is used as insulation and tinder. Rushes are similarly woven into mats and seats.

### Cotton

Grows only in warm climates (200+ frost-free days). Requires intensive labor for harvesting and seed removal. Processing is similar to other fibers: pick, seed, card, spin. Cotton is softer and more comfortable than plant bast fibers but weaker and harder to produce at small scale.

## Animal Fibers

### Wool

The most practical animal fiber for hand processing. Sheep shearing, washing, carding, and spinning are straightforward skills (see **spinning-and-weaving.md**).

Wool advantages: warm even when wet, naturally fire-resistant, elastic, durable, takes natural dyes well.

Other animals producing spinnable fiber: goats (cashmere, mohair), rabbits (angora), alpaca, llama, and dogs (seriously — many breeds produce spinnable undercoat fiber).

### Sinew

Dried tendon from large animals (deer, elk, cattle). Not a textile fiber but invaluable for sewing heavy materials — leather, hide, bark. Stronger than any thread you can spin from plant fiber. Dried sinew is pounded to separate into individual fibers, then moistened and twisted into thread. It's self-adhesive when wet and shrinks tight as it dries.

## Fiber Comparison

| Fiber | Strength | Softness | Water Resistance | Processing Difficulty | Availability |
|-------|----------|----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Flax (linen) | Very high | Medium | Good (dries fast) | Hard (long process) | Grow or wild |
| Hemp | Very high | Low-medium | Good | Hard | Grow (where legal) |
| Nettle | High | Medium-high | Moderate | Hard | Wild, abundant |
| Cotton | Medium | High | Poor (absorbs, slow dry) | Moderate | Grow (warm only) |
| Wool | Medium | High | Good (warm when wet) | Moderate | Requires sheep |
| Bark fiber | Medium | Low | Low | Easy-moderate | Forest trees |
| Sinew | Very high | Low | Moderate | Easy | Any large animal |

> **Start with what you have.** Before processing wild plants, check what's growing around you. Nettles might be 50 feet away. Flax can be planted in spring and harvested by fall. Wool requires sheep but every fleece produces enough yarn for several garments. Match your fiber strategy to what's locally available rather than trying to grow something exotic.
