Navigation Methods

Finding North — Polaris

Find the Big Dipper. The two stars at the end of the "cup" (Dubhe and Merak) point directly to Polaris. Extend the line between them about 5× their spacing. Polaris sits within 1° of true north — directly above the North Pole. Its altitude above the horizon equals your latitude.

Finding South — Southern Cross

Below latitude 25°N, find the Southern Cross (Crux). Extend the long axis of the cross 4.5× its length toward the horizon. That point is due south. Or: find where a line from the Cross intersects a perpendicular from the midpoint of the Pointer Stars (Alpha and Beta Centauri).

Latitude from Polaris

Measure Polaris's angle above the horizon using your fist at arm's length (≈10° per fist width). That angle equals your latitude. Example: Polaris at 4 fist-widths above the horizon ≈ 40°N latitude.

East-West from Star Movement

Watch any star for 15 minutes. If it rises — you're facing east. If it falls — west. If it moves right — south. If it moves left — north. Stars near the horizon show this most clearly.

Time from Stars

The Big Dipper rotates counter-clockwise around Polaris once every 24 hours (actually 23h 56m). If you know the date and the Dipper's position, you can estimate the time within about 30 minutes using a star clock method.

Fist Measurements

At arm's length: pinky tip ≈ 1° · three middle fingers ≈ 5° · closed fist ≈ 10° · spread hand (thumb to pinky) ≈ 20°. Use these to measure angular distances between stars and above the horizon.
